I would it might not Wife, the tediouſneſſe Will be the moſt part mine, that vnderſtand The bleſſings I haue in thee, ſo to part That driues the torment to a knowing Heart, But as thou ſay'ſt, we muſt giue way to need All liue awhile aſunder, our deſires Are both too fruitfull for our barren fortunes. How aduers runs the deſteny of ſome Creatures, Some onely can get riches and no Children, We onely can get Children and no riches, Then 'tis the prudents part to checke our willes, And till our ſtate riſe, make our Bloods lye ſtill. 'Life euerie yeere a Child, and ſome yeeres two, Beſides, drinkings abroad, that's neuer reckon'd, This geere will not hold out. Honeſt Wife I thanke thee, I ne're knew The perfect treaſure thou brought'ſt with thee more Then at this inſtant minute. A Man's happy When he's at pooreſt that has match't his Soule As rightly as his Body. Had I married A ſenſuall Foole now, as 'tis hard to ſcape it 'Mongſt Gentlewomen of our time, ſhe would ha' hang'd About my Necke, and neuer left her hold Till ſhe had kiſt me into wanton buſineſſes, Which at the waking of my better Iudgement I ſhould haue curſt moſt bitterly, And layd a thicker vengance on my act Then miſerie of the Birth, which were enough If it were borne to greatneſſe, whereas mine Is ſure of beggerie, though it were got in Wine. Fulneſſe of Ioy ſheweth the goodneſſe in thee, Thou art a matchleſſe Wife, Farwell my Ioy. I'le ſee thee often, Talke in mirth, and play at kiſſes with thee, Any thing Wench but what may beget Beggers, There I giue o're the Set, throw downe the Cards, And dare not take them vp. This does not onely make her honeſtie perfect, But her diſcretion, and approues her Iudgement. Had her deſire beene wanton, they'd beene blameleſſe In being lawfull euer, but of all Creatures I hold that Wife a moſt vnmatched treaſure, That can vnto her fortunes fixe her pleaſure, And not vnto her Blood, this is like wedlocke, The feaſt of marriage is not Luſt but Loue, And care of the eſtate, when I pleaſe Blood, Meerely I ſing, and ſucke out others, then 'Tis many a wiſemans fault, but of all Men I am the moſt vnfortunate in that game That euer pleas'd both Genders, I ne're play'd yet Vnder a Baſtard, the poore Wenches curſſe me To the Pit where e're I come, they were ne're ſerued ſo, But vs'd to haue more words then one to a bargaine, I haue ſuch a fatall Finger in ſuch buſineſſe I muſt forth with't, chiefely for Countrey Wenches, For euerie Harueſt I ſhall hinder Hay-making, I had no leſſe then ſeuen lay in laſt Progreſſe, Within three weekes of one anothers time. How Snaphance? I'le haue the Parſons Hand too, or I'le not yeeld to't. Faith when ſhe will I'le haue a bout with her. True, Lawyers vſe ſuch bouts as other Men doe, And if that be all thy griefe, I'le tender her a Husband, I keepe of purpoſe two or three Gulls in pickle To eat ſuch Mutten with, and ſhe ſhall chuſe one. Doe but in courteſie faith Wench excuſe me, Of this halfe yeard of Fleſh, in which I thinke it wants A Nayle or two. Faith I am poore, doe a charitable deed Wench, I am a younger Brother, and haue nothing. I haue no dwelling, I brake vp Houſe but this morning, Pray thee pittie me, I am a good Fellow, faith haue beene too kind To people of your Gender, if I ha'te Without my Belly, none of your Sexe ſhall want it, That word has beene of force to moue a Woman. Plot — A Chaste Maid in Cheapside
Sir Walter Whorehound lives high on the expectation of an inheritance: unless Sir Oliver Kix produces an heir, his estate will pass to Sir Walter — and Sir Oliver is impotent. Sir Walter maintains Allwit and his wife, paying for everything, in return for exclusive sexual access to Mistress Allwit: he is the true father of the children presumed to be Allwit’s. But Sir Walter is ===== PAGE 327 ===== 1613 planning to discontinue the arrangement and get married: the goldsmith Yellowhammer has agreed to marry his son Tim to a Welsh gentlewoman whom Sir Walter has introduced as his niece, and Sir Walter hopes himself to marry Tim’s sister Moll. Moll has other ideas: her sweetheart Touchwood plans to elope with her, and has Yellowhammer himself make the wedding ring, umaware of the bride’s identity. The elopement is foiled, however: Yellowhammer and Sir Walter intervene just as Touchwood is putting the ring onto Moll’s finger, and Moll is taken home and locked up. Touchwood’s elder brother is poor but virile, and cannot afford to cohabit with his wife for fear that she may produce a legitimate child he will not have the means to support. He has fathered a prolific number of bastards, and refuses to acknowledge one of them when a former paramour, a country wench, brings it to him. She then disposes of the baby by duping two corrupt promoters set to prevent the illicit trade in meat during Lent: she hides the child in a basket and allows them to confiscate it as contraband. Lady Kix’s maid suggests that Touchwood Seniors excessive fertility might solve Sir Oliver’s problems in begetting an heir; Sir Oliver agrees to pay him £400 for the job. Mistress Allwit gives birth to another of Sir Walter’s children. Tim Yellowhammer arrives home from Cambridge and is sent to see his mother at the christening party; he is priggish about being in female company, contrary to university rules, and the women are drunkenly embarrassing. Encouraged to woo his fiancée, Tim finds it heavy going: he tries to converse with her in Latin, she with him in Welsh. Allwit learns of Sir Walter’s plans for Moll, and tries to forestall them by telling Yellowhammer that his prospective son-in-law is a notorious whoremonger, but Yellowhammer is unconcerned by this.
