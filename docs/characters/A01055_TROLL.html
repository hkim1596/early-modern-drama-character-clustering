I forsooth: but he sleepes like a Hare with his eyes open and that's no good signe. Heare it? I shudder, it was a strong blast, young Mistris, able to roote vp heart, liuer, lungs and all. Lady Mistris, shall I fetch a Barbour to steale away his rough beard, whiles he sleepes in's naps? He neuer lookes in a glasse, and tis high time on conscience for him to bee trimd, has not been vnder the Shauers hand almost these foure yeeres. Sir, is your stomacke vp yet? get some warme porredge in your belly, 'tis a very good settle-braine. I am Trollia your honest implement. I beseech yee heartily Sir. I feele a horrible puking my selfe. No, no, you are but a little staring—there's difference betweene staring and starke mad. You are but whymsed, yet crotchetted, conundroun'd, or so. Who I? I will be monstrous and wise immedi ately. Welcome, Gentlemen, the more the merrier, Ile lay the cloth, and set the stooles in a readinesse, for I see here is some hope of dinner now. Worshipfull Master Doctor, I haue a great deale of I cannot tell what, to say t'ee; My Lord thun ders: euery word that comes out of his mouth, roares like a Cannon: the house shooke once, my young Lady dares not be seene. There, there, there; looke to your Right Wor shipfull, looke to your selfe. Some few knocks of Friendship, some loue-toyes, some Cuffes in kindnesse, or so. He has mumbled his nose, that tis as big as a great Cod peece.

Plot summary

Plot — 420
A group of mounted knights arrive at a wedding, and one of their number explains their presence. They worship Diana for as long as they remain unmarried, but also sacrifice to Hymenaeus, god of marriage. When they were last doing so, the god spoke, telling them one of Diana’s nymphs had got married to an earl favoured by Minerva. Diana is particularly pleased to know that the couple have agreed to wait a year before consummating the marriage. The two goddesses have asked Hymenaeus to arrange for the marriage- feast to be honoured, so he sends the knights with gifts: Minerva sends the groom a garland, Diana sends the bride a veil, and Hymenaeus sends them respectively a picture of a dove and a coronet. The knights present their shields and dance with ladies from the audience.
