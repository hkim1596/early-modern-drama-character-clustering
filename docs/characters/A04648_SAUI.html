Full speech

Giue me my fanne there. Faire seruant, I'le thanke you a day hence, when the date of your salutation comes forth. I, you need not tell me that sir, I do think you do prize a rush before my loue. VVould we might heare it once. Vpon the Violl de Gambo you meane? Nay rather by the fingers. Here Seruant, if you will play, come. Nay, I cannot stay to Daunce after your Pipe. Is your Tabacco perfum'd Sir? that you sweare by the sweet Smoake? O, peace I pray you; I loue not the breath of a Wood cocks head. Not altogither so Sir; but (as it were Fatall to their fol lies that thinke to grace themselues with taking Tabacco, when they want better entertainment) you see your Pipe beares the true forme of a Woodcocks head. 'Tis best leauing of you in Admiration, Sir ▪ VVhy I thought Sir Puntarvolo, you had been gone your Voyage? 'Tis most Courtlike spoken sir; but how might we doe to haue a sight of your Dog and Cat? And not your Cat? how dare you trust her behind you Sir? Ile giue you some VVater for her eyes: when do you goe sir? VVhat's hee, gentle Monsieur Briske? not that Gen tleman? O Sir Puntarvolo, you must thinke euery man was not borne to haue my Seruant Briskes feature. O Monsieur Briske, be not so Tyranous to confine all VVits within the compasse of your owne: Not find the sparks of a Gentleman in him, if he be a Gentleman? Doe you beleeue so? Why I can find sparkes of a Gen tleman in you Sir. Like enough sir: but would I might see this wonder you talke of: may one haue a sight of him for any reasonable summe? VVhat, and shall we see him Clowne it? Beshrew me, he Clownes it properly indeed. Beautifull and it please you sir, but not lustie. O excellent: why Gallants, is this hee cannot be Decipher'd? they were very bleare-witted yfaith that could not discerne the Gentleman in him. Doe I sir? why if you had any true Court-judgement in the carriage of his eye, and that inward power that forms his countenance, you might perceiue his counterfaiting as cleere as the noone day: Alas; Nay if you would haue tried my VVit indeed, you should neuer haue told me hee was a Gen tleman, but presented him for a true Clowne indeed; and then haue seene if I could haue Decipher'd him. Nay out of doubt hee does well, for a Gentleman to imitate; but I warrant you, he becomes his Naturall carriage of the Gentleman, much better than his Clownerie. Faith as easily as may bee: not Decipher him, quoth you? VVhy Gallants, let me laugh at you a little: was this your deuice, to try my judgement in a Gentleman? VVhat if he were not a man, yee may as well say? nay if your VVorships could Gull me so indeed, you were wiser than you are taken for. O Sir, you are very pleasurable. O, you're goodly Beagles! Out, you foole you.

Plot summary

Plot — Every Man Out of His Humor
Cordatus and Mitis are brought by Asper to watch a satirical play, in which he is to be an actor, playing malicious Macilente. They keep up a running commentary between, and sometimes during, the scenes. Each character in the play has a ‘humour’ or obsession to be purged. Sogliardo wants to be a gentleman: he takes instruction from Carlo Buffone and purchases himself a coat of arms; he is eventually used to humiliate the conceited court lady Saviolina by showing that she cannot tell true gentility from false. The farmer Sordido is hoarding grain, and resists a court order to sell it because he is gambling on the price going up after a bad harvest; when this does not happen, he tries to hang himself, and repents on hearing the comments of the rustics who cut him down. Puntarvolo plays chivalric wooing games of mock- adultery with his wife, and plans to travel to Constantinople with his wife and dog, venturing money on their safe return; but he loses the inclination when Macilente poisons the dog. Fungoso tries to imitate the courtier Brisk’s fashionable suit, but is continually frustrated because Brisk keeps changing his clothes. The uxorious Deliro seeks advice on how best to please his contrary wife, Fungoso’s sister. Shift, wanting to be known for a machismo he doesn’t really have, represents himself as a robber, but the threat of hanging forces him to admit the truth. Carlo Buffone has a humour of talking: at a tavern, he performs a comic dialogue between two cups, playing both parts himself, and Puntarvolo seals his mouth with wax. The affray brings the Constable, who is minded to arrest them all but is content tv take only Brisk. Fungoso, penniless from his clothing purchases, is left to pay the tavern bill, and has to be rescued by bis brother-in-law, who also calls in the mortgage on ===== PAGE 162 ===== Brisk’s lands; the courtier is left in prison for debts totalling £13,000, curing him of his humour. Macilente is purged of his malign humour by the sight of the Queen.
