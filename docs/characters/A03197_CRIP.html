Now you supporters of decrepite youth, That mount this substance twixt faire heauen and earth, Be strong to beare that huge deformitie, And be my hands as nimble to direct them, As your desires to waft mee hence to London. My thinkes I heare the sound of rauishment. Marry and will, knew I but where, and how. What do I see? Theeues full of lust beset virginitie! Now stirre thee cripple, and of thy foure legs Make vse of one, to doe a virgin good: Hence rauening curres: what, are you at a prey? Will nothing satisfie your greedy chappes But virgins flesh? Ile teach you prey on carrion, Fight & beat them away. Packe damned rauishers, hence villaines. Giue God the glory that gaue me the power. Hereafter more of this; but tell me now The cause of these euents, the effect, and how. Y faith she is an honourable Lady, And I much wonder that her Ladiship Giues intertaine to such bad men as these. Mongrell, ile choose. Hold, forbeare. I came in rescue of Virginitie. Thanks worthy sir, may but the Cripple be, Of power to gratifie this courtesie, I then shall thinke the heauens fauour me. If I doe liue, your loue Ile recompence. Exeunt. As much to thee my humorous blossome. Who became your baile? In a wenches counter! I thought no lesse: what sirra didst thou lie in the Knights warde, or on the maisters side? Where then, in the Hole? My crutch you meane for wearing out my clothes. Finely put off wench yfaith. Yes, and heere it is. At your pleasure. She hath a wit as sharpe as her needle. In th'Exchange! I haue walkt with thee there, before the visitation of my legges, and my expence in timber, at the least a hundred times, and neuer heard thee speak to a wench. True, that thou thoughtst it had bin a shilling, ma ry thou hadst neuer an other, nor so much as a shilling more to change it. Thou talke in th'Exchange! O thou art one of those, that if an honest Maid be sent to thy chamber with her Mistris goodes, and returne as honest and chaste as the moone: Sirra, you are one of those that will slaunder the poore wenches, by speaking liberally of their pronenesse to loue; and withall, bragge how cheap you haue bought their ware metaphorically, when indeede they depart as honest as they came thither, and leaue you all the day after to sigh at the sight of an ill bargaine. When wilt thou cast off this anticke garment of o stentation? do it, do it, or by the Lord I wil impresse thy va nities, and so anotomize the very bowells of thy absurdities, that all the worlde shall take notice of thee for a foole, and shunne thee as the pox or the pestilence. Not I. I haue businesse. By this leg I will not. By this crutch but I will. The forfeit of his bond! And you want words sirra, ile teach thee words; Thou shouldst haue come to euery one of vs As thus: thou wretch, thou miser thou vilde slaue And drudge to money, bond-man to thy wealth, Apprentice to a penny, thou that hourds vp The frie of siluer pence and half-penies, With shew of charitie to giue the poore, But putst them to increase, where in short time They grow a childs part, or a daughters portion. Thou that inuents new clauses for a bond To cousin simple plainenesse: O not a Dragon, No, nor the deuils fangs are halfe so cruell As are thy clawes; thus, thus thou shouldst haue railde: The forfeit of his bond, O I could spit My heart into his face; thou blood-hound that dost hunt The deere, deere life of noble Gentrie. My busines stayes me heere. Als one, my heart shall be as light as fire: Sblood, were I endebted a hundred pound, My fortune faild, and fled as Barnards are, Not worth a hundred pence as Barnards is: I shoud be now deuising sentences And Caueats, for posteritie to carue Vpon the inside of the counter wall: Therefore Ile now turne prouident; Ile to my shoppe And fall to worke. Welcome Mistresse Flower, whats your pleasure? Sweet faire, I pittie, yet no reliefe Harbors within the closet of my soule: This Phillis beares me true affection, But I detest the humor of fond loue: Yet am I hourely solicited As now you see, and faine she would make knowne The true perplexion of her wounded heart: But modestie checking her forwardnesse Bids her be still; yet she in similies And loue-comparisons, like a good Scholler By figures makes a demonstration Of the true loue enclosed in her heart. Plot summary

Plot — The Fair Maid of the Exchange
Phyllis and Ursula are assaulted by two ruffians as they go to deliver wares to a customer at night. A cripple rescues them, and a second assault is foiled by the intervention of Frank Golding; the cripple promises to help Frank if ever he can. Frank's two brothers are both in love with Phyllis, and, though he scorns amorous passions, Frank has fallen for her too. Phyllis, however, loves the cripple, and woos him by giving him a handkerchief to embroider “for a friend’. Meanwhile, Bobbington poses as a sailor going on a voyage, and borrows money from Phyllis’s father, Flower, leaving a diamond ring with him as security. Ferdinand and Anthony overhear one another's plans to woo Phyllis, but neither knows that Frank is also a rival. Ferdinand writes to her; Anthony plans to intercept the letter and substitute his own, but Ferdinand disguises Frank as a porter and gets him hired as Anthony's messenger. Planning to double-cross them both, Frank enlists the cripple's help. The cripple supplies him with two rejection letters to take back to his brothers, as from Phyllis. They each enlist the help of one of Phyllis’s parents, ===== PAGE 378 ===== who then quarrel over which brother shall marry their daughter. _ Knowing Phyllis loves him but thinking himself unworthy, the cripple lends Frank his clothes. Frank courts Phyllis in disguise, and she gives him her ring; then. he brings the parents forged letters from his brothers withdrawing their suits. Each parent decides to:spite the other by offering Phyllis to Frank instead of the other’s favoured son-in-law. The thwarted brothers realize what has happened, but Phyllis asks to be allowed a say in her.own marriage — and chooses Frank. As the play ends, Flower is arrested for receiving stolen property — Bobbington’s ring — and sent for trial. In the sub-plot, Mall Berry is in love with Barnard, but suffers the unwanted attentions of Bowdler. Barnard is surety for a £100 debt to Mall’s father; the original borrower is dead and the debt about to fall due, but Berry refuses to allow Barnard any more time to find the money. Bowdler persists in his pursuit of Mall, using lines he remembers from Venus and Adonis. Berry engages sergeants to arrest Barnard. Mall agrees to marry Bowdler, but the cripple points out that, through a party wall, he has overheard her talking about Barnard in her sleep: he is the man she truly loves.
