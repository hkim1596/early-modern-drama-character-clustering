Great are thy fortunes Nero, great thy power Thy Empyre lymited with natures bounds; Vpon thy ground, the Sunne doth ſet, and ryſe; The day, and night are thine: Nor can the Planets, wander where they will: See that proud Earth, that feares not Caeſars name, Yet nothing of all this, I enuy thee; But her, to whom the world, vnforſt, obayes, Whoſe eyes more worth then all it lookes vpon: In whom, all beautyes Nature hath encloſ'd, That through the wide Earth, or Heauen are diſpoſ'd Yet not ſo much her greatneſſe, and eſtate My hopes disharten, as her chaſtitie. Seeke not to grieue that heart which is thine owne, In Loues ſweete fires, let heat of rage burne out; Theſe browes could neuer yet to wrinckle learne, Nor anger out of ſuch faire eyes looke forth. What ſhame is't to be maſtred by ſuch beautie? Who, but to ſerue you, comes, how wants he dutie? Or if it be a ſhame, the ſhame is yours; The fault is onely in your Eies, they drew me; Cauſe you were louely, therefore did I loue: O, if to Loue you, anger you ſo much, You ſhould not haue ſuch cheekes, nor lips to touch, You ſhould not haue your ſnow, nor currall ſpy'd; If you but looke on vs, in vaine you chide, We muſt not ſee your Face, nor heare your ſpeech: Now, whilſt you Loue forbid, you Loue doe teach. Your Nobleneſſe doth warrant me from that, Nor need you others helpe, to puniſh me, Who, by your forehead am condem'd, or free. They, that to be reuendg'd do bend their minde, Seeke alwayes recompence, in that ſame kind The wrong was done them; Loue was mine offence, In that reuenge, in that ſeeke recompence. O vnhappie Man, What comfort haue I now Petronius? Sure this meſſage of the Princes, So grieuous and vnlookt for, will appall Petronius much. It will; but him ſo much the more, That hauing liu'd to his pleaſure; ſhall forgoe So delicate a life, I doe not maruell That Seneoa, and ſuch ſowre fellowes, can Leaue that they neu'r taſted: But when we That haue the Nectar of thy kiſſes felt, That drinkes away the troubles of this life, And but one banquet make of forty yeeres, Muſt come to leaue this: but ſoft, here he is. What, be not rauiſht with thy fancies, doe not Court nothing nor make loue vnto our feares. But empty words. Nay; I had rather farre beleeue thee here, Others ambition ſuch diſcoueries ſeeke; Faith, I am ſatisfied with the baſe delights Of common men; A wench, a houſe I haue, And of my owne a garden, Ile not change, For all your walkes, and Ladies, and rare fruits. Thruſt with reports, and rumours from his ſeat. My Lord you know the Campe depends on you As you determine. I thinke ſo great a gift was neuer heard of. Galba they ſay is frugally inclinde, Will he avow ſo great a gift as this?

Plot summary

Plot — Nero (Piso's Conspiracy)
The Emperor Nero celebrates his latest triumph : not a military victory, but success in a singing and acting competition against the Greeks; he also has one of his critics executed. He gives a performance in the role of Orestes; the theatre is guarded so that the audience cannot escape, and those who do not respond with due enthusiasm are marked down for execution. Deciding that he has treated Rome too lenien:tly, he has the city burned down . The wind blows the fire back towards the palace, and his own possessions are destroyed. . Antonius loves _ the Empress Poppaea, despite Petronius' efforts to persuade him to settle for someone of lower rank. Poppaea refuses Antonius, preferring Nymphidius, who has cultivated her affections as a means to make himself Emperor. Scaevinus broaches --a conspiracy to assassinate Nero, thinking it demeaning to have _an Emperor so dedicated to artistic pursuits. Piso is recruited, and the plotters discuss the best way to assassinate Nero; but they are overheard by the freedman Milichus, who reports them to Nero. Piso commits suicide; other conspirators are arrested, and Nero institutes a purge of their sympathizers. Petronius is arrested and executed simply because Tigellinus has -a score to settle with him. A young man is executed merely for pitying Piso's death; Poppaea tries to save him because he resembles a former lover of hers, so Nero kicks her to death in a rage. News arrives from France that Vindex has revolted and chosen · Galba as Emperor. Nymphidius spreads rumours that Nero has resigned, and tries both to curry favour with Galba's agents and to buy military support for his own ambitions. Nero is condemned by the Senate and commits suicide . Nymphidius is killed by Galba's supporters.
