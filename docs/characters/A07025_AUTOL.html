What ſhould I doe? I haue no thriuing way to lye and flatter, Nor haue I ſuch dexterity of wit As you haue (bleſt be heaven) to convert Blacke into white. Nor can I promiſe The death of any by the Starres, I haue No rich mans funerall to folemnize, That left a guilt ring for my Legacie, And his old Velvet jerkin to ſurvive him. I have no ſecret boyles within my breaſt. For which I am fear'd, no ſ it in Law to follow, No accuſation 'gainſt a great man, No houſe to let to farme, o tender wife To proſtitute, or skill to corrupt others, And ſleepe amidſt their wanton Dialogues. Would you haue me Act the plagiary, and ſeeke preferment, To be the drunken ba d of ſome blacke ſtewes? And thinke my deſtin e well ſatisfied, When my ſhame feeds me, and at length expect A Legacie, bequeath'd me from ſome Bawd, In lieu of my old ſervice, or according To the proportion of my Hernia. How ſhall that be done? For that I'll make no ſcruple. What's hee? Is he not the Sonne To the rich Vſurer, that died ſo lately? When ſhall I ſee him? What ſhall I turne a Roarer? Plot — Holland's Leaguer
Six years ago, Faustina’s father sent her abroad to keep her away from her love, Fidelio, and made her swear not to marry for seven years. Now the old man is dead, she has returned to London, known only to Fidelio, but insists on keeping her oath for the last remaining year. Her brother Philautus is in love with himself, which distresses his friends and offends his wife, but gives his servant Ardelio the opportunity to steal from him by conveying away property into storage at an accomplice’s house. Fidelio plans to make him fall in love with Faustina; this will both cure him and test her fidelity. He explains it to Faustina as a plan to humiliate a lord who has cast aspersions on women. Philautus makes advances to Faustina on the assumption that all women are libidinous and enjoy being raped from time to time; but he is impressed by her militant chastity, and accedes to her demand that he prove his honour in battle. He later returns from the wars, cured of narcissism, and asks Faustina’s pardon for his earlier insolence. She identifies herself as his sister, and he gives his blessing to her marriage to Fidelio; so she agrees to abandon her oath before the seven years are out. On learning that Ardelio is a villain, Philautus dismisses him. He goes to collect the stolen goods, only to find that his partner in crime will not acknowledge that the items even exist. The ensuing argument is overheard, and the miscreants are both arrested, In the sub-plot, Agurtes intends to cheat the courtly name-dropper Trimalchio by introducing him to a young heiress - who is, in reality, his daughter and lifelong accomplice Millicent.
