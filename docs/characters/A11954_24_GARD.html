But to be commanded For euer by your Grace, whoſe hand ha's rais'd me. It's one a clocke Boy, is't not. Theſe ſhould be houres for neceſsities, Not for delights: Times to repayre our Nature With comforting repoſe, and not for vs To waſte theſe times. Good houre of night Sir Thomas : Whether ſo late? Not yet Sir Thomas Louell : what's the matter? It ſeemes you are in haſt: and if there be No great offence belongs too't, giue your Friend Some touch of your late buſineſſe: Affaires that walke (As they ſay Spirits do) at midnight, haue In them a wilder Nature, then the buſineſſe That ſeekes diſpatch by day. The fruite ſhe goes with I pray for heartily, that it may finde Good time, and liue: but for the Stocke Sir Thomas, I wiſh it grubb'd vp now. But Sir, Sir, Heare me Sir Thomas, y' are a Gentleman Of mine owne way. I know you Wiſe, Religious, And let me tell you, it will ne're be well, 'Twill not Sir Thomas Louell, tak't of me, Till Cranmer, Cromwel, her two hands, and ſhee Sleepe in their Graues. Yes, yes, Sir Thomas, There are that Date, and I my ſelfe haue ventur'd To ſpeake my minde of him: and indeed this day, Sir (I may tell it you) I thinke I haue Incenſt the Lords o' th' Councell, that he is (For ſo I know he is, they know he is) A moſt Arch-Heretique, a Peſtilence That does infect the Land: with which, they moued Haue broken with the King, who hath ſo farre Giuen care to our Complaint, of his great Grace, And Princely Care, fore-ſeeing thoſe fell Miſchiefes, Our Reaſons layd before him, hath commanded To morrow Morning to the Councell Boord He be conuented. He's a ranke weed Sir Thomas, And we muſt root him out. From your Affaires I hinder you too long: Good night, Sir Thomas. Ha's he had knowledge of it? Yes. Which Reformation muſt be ſodaine too My Noble Lords; for thoſe that tame wild Horſes, Pace 'em not in their hands to make 'em gentle; But ſtop their mouthes with ſtubborn Bits & ſpurre 'em, Till they obey the mannage. If we ſuffer Out of our eaſineſſe and childiſh pitty To one mans Honour, this contagious ſickneſſe; Farewell all Phyſicke: and what followes then? Commotions, vprores, with a generall Taint Of the whole State; as of late dayes our neighbours, The vpper Germany can deerely witneſſe: Yet freſhly pittied in our memories. My Lord, becauſe we haue buſines of more mo ment, We will be ſhort with you. 'Tis his Highneſſe pleaſure And our conſent, for better tryall of you, From hence you be committed to the Tower, Where being but a priuate man againe, You ſhall know many dare accuſe you boldly, More then (I feare) you are prouided for. My Lord, my Lord, you are a Sectary, That's the plaine truth; your painted gloſſe diſcouers To men that vnderſtand you, words and weakneſſe. Good M. Secretary, I cry your Honour mercie; you may worſt Of all this Table ſay ſo. Doe not I know you for a Fauourer Of this new Sect? ye are not ſound. Not ſound I ſay. I ſhall remember this bold Language. I haue done. What other, Would you expect? You are ſtrangely troubleſome: Let ſome o' th' Guard be ready there. Receiue him, And ſee him ſafe i' th' Tower. Dread Soueraigne, How much are we bound to Heauen, In dayly thankes; that gaue vs ſuch a Prince; Not onely good and wiſe, but moſt religious: One that in all obedience, makes the Church The cheefe ayme of his Honour, and to ſtrengthen That holy duty out of deare reſpect, His Royall ſelfe in Iudgement comes to heare The cauſe betwixt her, and this great offender. With a true heart, And Brother; loue I doe it.

Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
