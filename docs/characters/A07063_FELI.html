What then? I feare it is not possible to limme so many per sons in so small a tablet as the compasse of our playes afford. Peace, here comes the Prologue, cleare the Stage. THE wreath of pleasure, and delicious sweetes, Begirt the gentle front of this faire troope: Select, and most respected Auditours, For wits sake doe not dreame of miracles. Alas, we shall but falter, if you lay The least sad waight of an vnused hope, Vpon our weakenesse: onely we giue vp The woorthlesse present of flight idlenesse, To your authentick censure; Othat our Muse Had those abstruse and synowy faculties, That with a straine of fresh inuention She might presse out the raritie of Art; The pur'st elixed ioyce of rich conceipt, In your attentiue eares; that with the lip Of gratious elocution, we might drinke A sound carouse vnto your health of wit. But O, the heathy drynesse of her braine, Foyle to your fertile spirits, is asham'd To breath her blushing numbers to such eares: Yet (most ingenious) deigne to vaile our wants; With sleeke acceptance, polish these rude Sceanes: And if our slightnesse your large hope beguiles, Check not with bended brow, but dimpled smiles. Stand, the ground trembleth. Piero stay, for I descry a fume, Creeping from out the bosome of the deepe, The breath of darkenesse, fatall when 'tis whist In greatnes stomacke: this same smoake, call'd pride, Take heede shee'le lift thee to improuidence, And breake thy necke from steepe securitie, Shee'le make thee grudge to let Iehoua share In thy successefull battailes: O, shee's ominous, Inticeth princes to deuour heauen, Swallow omnipotence, out-stare dread fate, Subdue Eternitie in giant thought, Heaues vp their hurt with swelling, puft conceit, Till their soules burst with venom'd Arrogance: Beware Piero, Rome it selfe hath tried, Confusions traine blowes vp this Babell pride. I sell not my thought so cheap: I valewe my meditation at a higher rate. For flattery. O how I hate that same Egyptian louse; A rotten maggot, that liues by stinking filth Of tainted spirits: vengeance to such dogs, That sprout by gnawing senselesse carion. Pree thee Flauia be my mistresse. I in faith, & twere not for printing, and painting, my breech, and your face would be out of reparation. And thou, by Art, too faire to be beloued. By wits life, most sparke spirits, but hard chance. La ty dine. Confusion seize me, but I thinke thou lyest. Why should I not be sought to then aswell? Plot summary

Plot — 184
Mistress Missa is under attack from Protestants: they want to replace the Mass with a simple Lord’s Supper involving none of her magical powers. Knowledge persuades Freemouth that she is guilty of heresy and blasphemy, and he cites her before Justice. Initially Justice tries to have Freemouth himself arrested, but Knowledge informs him of a recent change in the crown’s religious policy: Protestants are no longer to be persecuted. Justice agrees to allow Freemouth to proceed against Missa, and he accuses her in Palemon’s court. She admits everything except claiming to be more powerful than God. Porphyry and Philargury undertake Missa’s defence. Porphyry denies the right of Knowledge and Freemouth, lay persons, to question ecclesiastical matters, and attempts to have the charges thrown out, or at least referred to a spiritual court; but Palemon insists that he is competent to try the case even though he is not a priest. Missa’s defence turns on the claim that the mass was ordained by Christ at the Last Supper; but Knowledge demonstrates that the scriptural passages cited contain no mention of it, and that it is not coextensive with the Lord’s Supper, which requires no vestments, can be administered by anyone rather than only a priest, and claims no power to control the weather, work magic, or save souls. 1548 Porphyry tries to argue on the principle of obedience of authority, but Knowledge retorts that authority cannot command things contrary to scripture (such as the mass). Transubstantiation is invoked and rebutted, and Knowledge argues that all sacrifices are superseded by the atonement at the crucifixion; thus the mass is unnecessary. Palemon finds against Mistress Missa. Though she deserves death, he commutes the penalty to banishment, and she goes home to her father, the Pope.
