Haue you my messages (sent to the poore) Deliuer'd with good hands, not robbing them Of any iot was theirs. Who cheate the poore, and from them plucke their almes. Pilfer from Heauen, and there are thunderbolts From thence to beate them euer, do not lye, Were you both faithfull true distributers? I'm glad you doe not, if those wretched people Tell you they pine for want of any thing. Whisper but to mine eare, and you shall furnish them. My booke and Taper. Thy voyce sends forth such musicke, that I neuer Was raisht with a more celestiall sound, Were euery seruant in the world like thee, So full of goodnesse, Angels would come downe To dwell with vs, thy name is Angelo, And like that name thou art, get thee to rest, Thy youth with too much watching is opprest. Bee nye me still then, In golden letters downe ile set that day Which gaue thee to me, little did I hope To meete such worlds of comfort in thy selfe, This little pretty body, when I comming Forth of the temple, heard my begger-boy, My sweete fac'd godly begger-boy, craue an almes, Which with glad hand I gaue, with lucky hand, And when I tooke thee home, my most chast bo some Me thought was fil'd with no hot wanton fire, But with a holy flame, mounting since higher On wings of Cherubines then did before. Now Sir, you may goe on. Sir, he is more indebted, to you for praise, then you to him that owes it. Could gold buy you To speake thus for your friend, you Sir are worthy Of more then I will number, and this your language Hath power to win vpon another woman, Top of whose heart, the feathers of this World Are gaily stuck, but all which first you named, And now this last, his loue to me are nothing, I am guilty of a shame I yet neuer knew, Thus to hold parley with you, pray Sir pardon. If one immodest accent Flye out, I hate you euerlastingly. Sir, for your fortunes were they mines of gold, He that I loue is richer; and for worth, You are to him lower then any slaue Is to a Monarch. Can I, with wearing out my knees before him Get you but be his seruant, you shall boast y'are equall to a King. Oh kneele not Sir to me. Still on the Bread of pouerty let me feed. Which of your powerfull gods, Your gold, your siluer, brasse, or woodden ones? That can, nor do me hurt, nor protect you, Most pittied women, will you sacrifice To such, or call them gods or goddesses, Your Parents would disdaine to be the same, Or you your selues? O blinded ignorance, Tell me Caliste by the truth I charge you, Or any thing you hold more deere, would you To haue him deifide to posterity, Desire your father an Adulterer, A Rauisher, almost a Paracide, A vile incestuous wretch? Plot summary

Plot — The Virgin Martyr
An imperial edict orders the extirpation of Christianity from the Roman Empire, and the re-establishment of the worship of the pagan gods. In Caesarea, Theophilus works to that end, abetted by Harpax; his many successes so far include the forced apostasy of his own daughters, once Christians but now votaresses of Jupiter. Diocletian gives his daughter Artemia the right to choose her own husband, and she selects the unwilling Antoninus, who loves the Christian Dorothea and asks for time to consider. Artemia remains in Caesarea as her father’s deputy. Harpax tells Theophilus that Antoninus is having an illicit relationship with Dorothea, and Theophilus suborns her servants Spungius and Hircius to smuggle him into her home so that he can witness a meeting between them. Dorothea and Antoninus are arrested. Theophilus sets his daughters, Dorothea’s childhood friends, to convert her to paganism; but instead Dorothea persuades them to revert to Christianity. When they demonstrate their new faith by spitting at an idol of Jupiter, their outraged father kills them. Antoninus is taken ill. His father brings Dorothea to his sick-bed in the hope that he will rape her and so be cured. He refuses, and Dorothea is taken for torture and execution. Destitute after losing the gold Theophilus gave them, Spungius and Hircius are engaged by Harpax to be the torturers. They flog her, but miraculously she feels nothing; they are wrongly suspected of botching the job out of regard for their former mistress, and hanged. After praying that Antoninus’ love for her be transformed into love for God, Dorothea is beheaded. Antoninus drops dead at the sight. Artemia gets her second choice of husband: the co-Emperor Maximinus. Dorothea’s faithful page Angelo, whom she took in as a beggar, turns out to be an angel, and brings Theophilus a gift from her: a basket of fruit and flowers to persuade him of the joys of the Christian heaven. Harpax comes in his true form, a devil, to claim Theophilus, but is frightened off by a floral cross in the basket. Theophilus is converted, orders the release of all Christian prisoners, and gives them ships in which to flee persecution. He is arrested by the authorities and asks to die on the worst of his extensive collection of torture instruments. He is racked but will not recant; Harpax is balked of his aim, and Dorothea and the other martyrs receive him into heaven.
