Full speech

Come sister, come, wee were not borne to stand, t'is against the nature of our sexes kinde: come, sit, and tell me, how many suters you haue, and which you most doe loue? and I will tell you all mine, and which I most respect. Well I haue a suter too, if hee had as much witte as liuing, it may bee I should finde in my heart to loue him. No Sister no, tis not he, for I thinke his wit cannot cal his wealth Maister, nor his wealth his wit, and yet they are both Seruants to a foole. Tis the person and conditions I respect, and not face, for euery Boy has a good face, and its not worth a hayre. No Sister no, my loue is more worthier then words canne vtter: I cannot simpathize his rare perfections with any earthye substance: this Globe of durt produceth nothing worthye of his comparison, so soules perfection so refines his body, as you would thinke an Angell were his sire: his discourse, behauiour, and humanitie, attracts to him my soules felicitie. Nay who is yours? speake first. If it be mine, my heart will breake: mine is Maister Ruffell. In troth tis he. I am content Gent at your intreaties vpo ̄ conditions. But you shall be sworne to them. Secondly, you shall neuer make Tobacco your I doll, taking it in a morning before you say your prayers. Thirdly, you shall neuer in the praise of Tobacco disclose or dispraise by the way of making comparisons, the secrets of Ladies, or Gentlewomen, as repeating their distil'd waters, their censing oyles, or their smoothing vn guents. Fourthly, you shall neuer come with your squibs & smoke-squirts amongst Ladies and Gentlewome ̄ , fling ing out fume at your Nostrels, as a whale doth salt-water, vnlesse you be intreated by them. Fiftly, you shall presently conuey your selfe out of our company, neuer to come more neere vs, vnlesse you be sent for. No neuer: so, let him kisse the pipe. Ile hold my life hee is in loue with some waiting Gentlewoman. You haue bin very welcome Gentleme ̄ . I like it well, come lets about it presently. You see sir, we are not a horsebacke. We would willingly serue two nere friendes, be cause we are brothers, and indeed two twins, and therefore are loath to be parted. Shame to thy sex, no more. Sir do'y want a Seruant? O Lord what shall I doe? Tis partly as youle vse me. Well. For one of them the earth did neuer beare a wor thier creature. Which is that? Why sister, haue you so oft confest that his all perfection'd spirit poyntes him out for ver tue herselfe to imitate, and yet doy' aske now which is that? Then tis true, wee confesse to thee wee are both wenches, and the loue of these two Gentlemen, Sparke and Ruffel hath made vs leaue our selues to waite on them which by misfortune we did misse. I can man a punck to a play, or slaunder any Gentle woman as well as anie Innes a Court puny, I can as well as he, sweare such a Lady is in loue with me, and such a Citti zens Daughter would haue come to bed to mee, when all shall be as true as thy Ladies are honest. Thy Ladies are as common as any Tauerne doore. Doe? Why complaine them to the Magistrates, and preuent the murder.

Plot summary

Plot — The Fleer
Before the start of the action, Piso deposed Antifront, whose daughters thereupon fled from Florence. Antifront now travels in search of his missing children. He arrives in London under the alias Fleer, and meets some gentlemen. One of them, Spark, gets him a job with two of his countrywomen, a pair of Florentine courtesans working in London. Antifront is dismayed to discover that they are his missing daughters. Two sisters, Susan and Nan, are interested in Spark and his friend Ruffle, who are among the courtesans’ many clients. They dress up as boys and try to get the gentlemen to employ them as pages; Spark and Ruffle refuse, and the ‘pages’ end up working for two other clients, Sir John Havelittle and Piso junior, the son of the Florentine usurper. ‘Fleer’ wants to engineer a marriage for his daughters, selecting Havelittle and Piso as the eligible bachelors. The daughters prefer Spark and Ruffle, but are refused; taking offence, they ask Piso and Havelittle to kill the gentlemen, and suggest poison as the safest method. The plot is overheard by ‘Fleer’ and the ‘pages’. ‘Fleer’ gets himself another job looking after an apothecary’s shop, and sells Piso and Havelittle a poison which is really a sleeping potion. Spark and Ruffle dine with Piso and Havelittle and apparently die. The hosts are at once arrested on suspicion of poisoning and are committed for trial. Fleer is sent on an errand to the senior judge, who is unable to attend through illness, and sends word back for the other two judges to proceed without him. Given a ring to authenticate the message, Fleer instead uses it to get himself deputized as the third judge. He produces a letter which establishes that the guilt extends to the daughters as well as the poisoners, and all four are sentenced to death. News arrives that Piso’s father has died, making him the new Duke of Florence; but-he tells the Florentines to recall the deposed Antifront instead, and offers to make amends for his father’s treason by marrying Antifront’s elder daughter. Antifront reveals his identity and says Piso can do just 1606 that; his younger daughter will marry Havelittle. He produces the murder victims, still alive, and bestows them on Nan and Susan.
