Full speech

Yonder's Simplicity whom I hate deadly, H'as held the world too long; hee's but a foole, A toy will coozen him: if I once fasten on't, Ile make it such a Nurcery for Hell, Planting black Soules in't, it shall ne'r be fit For honesty to set her simples in. I must speake faire to the Foole. Lasse, who has put that load? that carriage On poore Simlcity? had they no mercy? Pretty, kind, louing worme; come, let me helpe it. The world, sweet heart, is full of cares and troubles, No match for thee, thou art a tender thing, A harmelesse quiet thing, a gentle foole, Fit for the fellowship of Ewes and Rammes: Goe, take thine ease and pipe; giue me the burthen, The clog, the torment, the heart-breake, the world; Heere's for thee, Lambe, a dainty Oaten Pipe. I thinke, some serpent sure has lickt him ouer, And giuen him only craft enough to keepe, And go no farther with him; all the rest Is innocence about him, truth and bluntnesse. I must seeke other course; for I haue learn'd Of my Infernall Sire, not to be lazy, Faint or discourag'd at the tenth repulse: Me thinks that world Simplicity now huggs fast, Do's looke as if't should be Deceit's at last. Sir, hee's a foole, the world belongs to you, Y'are mighty in your worth and your command, You know to gouerne, forme, make lawes, and take Their sweet and precious penalty, it befits A mightinesse like yours, the world was made For such a Lord as you, so absolute A Maiesty in all Princely noblenes As your selfe is; but to lye vse-lesse now, Rusty or lazy in a Fooles preheminence, It is not for a glorious worth to suffer. Now my hope ripens fairely. Pray let me case your Maiesty. Still repulst? I must finde ground to thriue on. Captaine, 'tis you that haue the bloudy sweats, You venture life and limmes 'tis you that taste The stings of thirst and hunger, Yet lets another carry away the World, Of which, by right, you are the only Master, Stand curtsing for your pay at your returne Perhaps with woodden legs to euery Groome That dares not look full right vpon a sword, Nor vpon any wound, or slit of honor? Giue't me sir to lay vp, I am your Treasurer, In a poore kinde. Is it possible? Can I yet set no footing in the World? I'm angry, but not weary; Ile hunt out still: For, being Deceit, I beare the Diuels name, And he's known seldome to giue ore his game. Shall the Land-Captaine, sir, vsurpe your right, Yours that try thousand dangers to his one, Rocks, shelues, gulfs, quick ▪ sands, hundred hundred horrors, That makes the Land-men tremble when th'are told; Besides the enemies encounter, I knew 'twould come at last. And shall your Purser helpe you? I will not leaue yet; Destruction playes in me such pleasant straines, That I would purchase it with any paines. Ile feede vpon the Muck on't, that a while Shall satisfie my longings, Wealth is knowne The absolute step to all promotion. This is no place for vs.

Plot summary

Plot — The World Tossed at Tennis
The royal palaces of Richmond and St James now have a rival, Denmark House. Richmond is afraid that the newcomer will take all Prince Charles’s attention, leaving them neglected; but Denmark House assures them that each is suitable for a particular season (herself for winter, St James’s for spring, and Richmond for summer), and that the Prince will rotate among them equally. An unemployed scholar and an unemployed soldier consider their options. Their patroness Pallas descends, argues that the complete man combines both their professions, art and arms together, and invokes Jupiter's help. Jupiter presents the Muses and the Nine 1620 Worthies as an illustration of the virtue of former times. Time weeps at the encroaching vice of the present; he finds himself continually abused and hated. Deceit and pride have damaged the world in its latter days, and Jupiter summons the five coloured starches as illustrations of the absurdity of modern fashion. White Starch claims precedence over the others, but Yellow wins acclaim. Simplicity arrives to see what has become of the world (represented on stage by a globe), and is not reassured by his observations. Deceit wants to cozen him of the world, Simplicity to protect it from Deceit’s bad influence. Deceit successively gains the confidence of a series of men with worldly power: a king, a land- captain, a sea-captain, a flamen, and a lawyer. Each in turn is persuaded that the world should belong to him: the King takes it from Simplicity, and the Land- Captain from the King (through a display of superior military power); the Sea-Captain threatens the Land- Captain with naval cannon and they agree to share the world; but the Flamen takes it from them, urging them to look to their souls, and the Lawyer in turn takes it from the Flamen, insisting on his role in maintaining proper social discipline. On each occasion, Deceit asks for his share of the world, and is rebuffed at every turn (though the Lawyer gives him all the money in the world): none of the worldly powers is susceptible to corruption. A dance ensues in which the various powers pass the world between themselves; Deceit and the Devil repeatedly attempt to possess it, and fail. The world ends up in the hands of the King. With the world now at ease, Jupiter tells the Soldier and Scholar that they have only themselves to blame if they are unemployed, and encourages them to go and get jobs.
