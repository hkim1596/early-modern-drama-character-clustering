Full speech

I wonder most at the Marquesse her brother, who is alwaies vehemently forward to haue her de sires graunted. Shee's doubtlesse very chaste and vertuous. Shee may aske but one, may she? We shall haue wenches now when we can Catch'em, and we transgresse thus. Berlady we are like to haue a long Lent out, Flesh will be flesh: now Gentlemen I had rather Haue angred all the gods then that blinde Gunner. I remember once the people did but flight him In a sacrifice: and what followed? Women kept their houses, grew good huswiues, Honest forsooth, was not that fine? Wore their owne faces, Though they weare gay cloathes without suruaying: And which was most lamentable, They lou'd their husbands. And I too. You shall see within these Few yeeres a fine confusion i'the countrey, marke it: Nay, and we grow for to depose the Powers, And set vp Chastitie againe, well I ha done. A fine new Goddesse certainely, whose blessings Are hunger and hard bedds. Well, let come what will come, I am but one, and as the plague falles, Ile shape my selfe: If women will be honest, Ile be Sound, if the god be not too vnmercifull, Ile take a little still where I can get it, And thanke him, and say nothing. Weele with you Sir: And Loue so fauour vs, As we are still thy seruants. Come my Lords, Lets to the Duke, and tell him to what folly His doting now has brought him. The God begins his vengeance; what a sweet youth he has sent vs here, with a pudding ins belly? Yes, yes, let it alone. O God! I knew her when I haue beene offred her to be brought to my bed for fiue pound: whether it could haue beene performd or no, I know not. I thinke better then her mother. But pray you tell mee, Why is the Prince, now ripe and full experient, Not made a dore in the State? Sir we are pleas'd to wayte: when is it? If she should proue good now, what wert like? How blacke the day begins! Faith Ile go starue my selfe, or grow diseas'd to shame the hangman; for I am sure hee shall be my Herald, and quarter me. Ile make a mourner. Tis true, and thus it was; His houre was come To lose his life, he ready for the stroke, Nobly, and full of Saint-like patience, Went with his Guard: which when the people saw, Compassion first went out, mingled with teares, That bred desires, and whispers to each other, To doe some worthy kindnes for the Prince. And ere they vnderstood well how to doe, Fury stept in, and taught them what to doe, Thrusting on euery hand to rescue him, As a white innocent: then flew the rore Through all the streetes, of Saue him, saue him, saue him; And as they cryde, they did for catching vp Such sudden weapons as their madnesse shew them. In short, they beat the Guard, & tooke him from em, And now march with him like a royall Army.

Plot — Cupid's Revenge
Leontius, King of Lycia, promises his daughter Hydaspes anything she asks for on her birthday. She wants him to ban the cult of Cupid in Lycia: the god’s naked statues are to be taken down and burnt, and Cupid-worship to be made a capital offence. Leontius agrees, persuaded that the religion is the cause of national immorality. His nephew Ismenus demurs: he will promulgate the Duke’s orders, but will himself take no hand in obeying them. As his temples are dismantled, Cupid declares vengeance on the kingdom. The Lycian royal family begins to behave with inappropriate amorousness. Hydaspes fixates on Zoilus, a deformed dwarf, and asks to be allowed to marry him; her father’s response is to have him beheaded, and she pines to death. Prince Leucippus falls for Bacha, a strumpet only interested in his wealth and power, but, questioned by his father, denies sexual misbehaviour and praises her virtue and chastity. Leontius is so impressed that he sends Leucippus away ===== PAGE 378 ===== and woos Bacha for himself, affecting youthful clothing and behaviour. Bacha becomes Queen of Lycia. When Leucippus returns to court, Bacha tries to seduce him again. He refuses to cuckold his father, and, annoyed at his refusal, she plots his death; this will leave her daughter Urania as the only heir to the throne. She makes Leontius jealous of his son’s popularity with the people and courtiers, and persuades him that Leucippus is plotting his assassination. The court parasite Timantus tells Leucippus that the King is to discuss his arrest at a council, and arranges to place him where he can eavesdrop and be forewarned of the charges. It is a ruse: Leucippus is arrested and sentenced to death, and Urania is proclaimed heir to the throne. At the execution, the citizens rise in rebellion and rescue the Prince from the scaffold. As Leontius sickens, Bacha schemes to marry Urania to a foreign prince; but she is in love with Leucippus, locates him through the good offices of Ismenus, and follows him posing as a page. Bacha engages Timantus to murder Leucippus. He finds the Prince by intercepting a letter from Ismenus; but his attack fails when Urania interposes her own body and is mortally wounded. Timantus is killed, but reveals her true identity before he dies. Ismenus brings Leucippus news that Leontius is dead. He declares that he will leave Bacha to heaven’s justice, but she stabs him.
