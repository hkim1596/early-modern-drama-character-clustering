So much hee hath out stript mee. Oh Master Geraldine, Flattery in Men's an adiunct of their sex, This Countrie breeds it, and for that, so farre You needed not to haue trauell'd. Sir, my Husband Hath tooke much pleasure in your strange discourse About Ierusalem and the Holy Land; How the new Citie differs from the old, What ruines of the Temple yet remayne, And whether Sion, and those hills about, With these Adiacent Townes and Villages, Keepe that proportioned distance as wee read; And then in Rome, of that great Piramis Reared in the Front, on foure Lyons Mounted, How many of those Idoll Temples stand, First dedicated to their Heathen gods, Which ruined, which to better vse repayred, Of their Panthaeon, and their Capitoll, What Structures are demolish't, what remaine. How now Sister, This is a fashion that's but late come vp, For maids to court their husbands. Ya'ue my leaue, To giue it full expression. My apprehension, Can giue him no more true expression, Then that he first appeares, a Gentleman, And well conditioned. I haue nor Eyes, To search into the inward Thoughrs of Men, Nor euer was studied in that 'Art, To iudge of Meus affection by the face; But that which makes me best opinion'd of him, Is, That he's Companion, and the Friend Beloued of him, whom you so much commend, The Noble Master Geraldine, Hee pretends Loue to my sister Pru. I haue obseru'd him, Single her out, to priuate conference. But Loue in these kindes, should not be compel'd, Forc'd, nor Perswaded; When it freely Springs, And of it selfe, takes voluntary Roote, It Growes, it Spreads, it Ripens, and brings soorth, Such an Vsurious Crop of timely Fruit, As crownes a plentious Autume. How fell they out at first? But in all this, How did the Women scape? Tis the care of Fathers; and the weakenesse Incident to youth, that wants experience. What's the Proiect? Wee intreat you, Possesse vs oth' Nouell, You are Modest. Why say wee be who should be iealous of vs? This is not first of many hundred Nights, That wee two haue beene priuate, from the first Of our acquaintance, when our Tongues but clipt Our Mothers-tongue, and could not speake it plaine, Wee knew each other; As in slatnre, so Increast our sweet Societie; Since your trauell, And my late Marriage, Through my Husbands lone, Mid-night hath beene as Mid-day, and my Bed-chamber, As free to you, as your owne Fathers house, And you as welcome too 't. Most trew, it is withall an Argument, That both our vertues are so deepe imprest In his good thoughts, hee knowes we cannot erre. And she no lesse, whom either Beauty, Youth, Time, Place, or opportunity could tempt, To iniure such a Husband. I know your meaning, It was once voyc'd, that wee two should haue Matcht, The World so thought, and many Tongues so spake, But Heauen hath now dispos'd vs otherwayes; And being as it is, (a thing in me, Which I protest, was neuer wisht, nor sought) Now done, I not repent it. Troath they had, I should haue beene your trusty Treasurer. If they should not, Wee might proclaime, they were not Charitable, Which were a deadly fin but to conceiue. As to one, that in my Bosome hath a second place, next my deere Husband. Presume on that already, but perhaps, You meane to stretch it further. You aske the thing, I was about to beg; Your tongue hath spake mine owne thoughts. As I hope Mercy. Nay Master Geraldine, one Ceremonie rests yet vnperform'd, My Vow is past, your oath must next proceed, And as you couet to be sure of me, Of you I would be certaine. No doubt; but Lone's still Iealous, and in that To be excused; You then shall sweare by Heauen, And as in all your future Acts, you hope To thriue and prosper; As the Day may yeeld Comfort, or the Night rest, as you would keepe Entire, the Honour of your Fathers house, And free your Name from Scandall and Reproach, By all the Goodnesse that you hope to enioy, Or ill to shun— Till that day come, you shall reserue your selfe A single man; Conuerse nor company With any Woman, Contract nor Combine, With Maid, or Widow; which expected houre, As I doe wish not haste, so when it happens, It shall not come vnwelcome; You here all, Vow this. Th'art then a Deuill, that presents before mee My horrid sins; perswades me to dispaire; When hee like a good Angel sent from Heauen, Besought me of repentance; Swell sicke Heart, Euen till thou burst the ribs that bound thee in; So, there's one string crackt, flow, and flow high, Euen till thy blood distill out of mine eyes, To witnesse my great sorrow. Plot summary

Plot — The English Traveller
Geraldine has recently returned from foreign travels, to find that, during his absence, a childhood friend, whom he was expected to marry, married old Wincott instead; she did not seek the marriage, but has no complaints. Having no children of his own, Wincott treats Geraldine as a close friend, and is trusting enough to leave the young man alone with his wife. Geraldine agrees with her that, should they both survive Wincott, he will be her second husband, and he agrees to remain single until then. Dalavill spreads rumours that Geraldine and Mistress Wincott are lovers. Concerned at the possible scandal, Old Geraldine tests his son by proposing that he should marry; Geraldine’s refusal is taken as confirmation of the affair. Geraldine protests his innocence, but to prove it, and silence the gossip, his father insists that he should never again visit the Wincotts. His absence grieves Wincott, and Geraldine agrees to explain himself at a secret midnight meeting. He is pressed by Wincott to stay the night but, unable to sleep, decides to pass the time looking innocently at Mistress Wincott in her bed. Outside her room he overhears her talking to Dalavill: they are lovers. Disillusioned, Geraldine plans to leave England. Wincott lays on a feast to mark his departure, and everyone is invited. At the party, Geraldine confronts Mistress Wincott with what he knows. She is mortified, rejects Dalavill, faints, and dies, having written to her husband a confession. Dalavill flees the house. Wincott confirms Geraldine as his heir. In the other plot, Young Lionel lives with riotous prodigality while his father is away, abetted by some of the servants, and fills the house with whores and drunken gallants. When Old Lionel returns unexpectedly, intending to stay at home in future, Young Lionel and his associates panic and hide inside the house - leaving themselves with the problem of how to get out before Old Lionel finds them. The steward Reignald takes matters in hand, and tells them to make no sound, and pretend the house is deserted. Old Lionel finds his house apparently shut up, and Reignald explains that it has been abandoned: a previous owner committed a murder there, and now the house is haunted. When the former owner denies this, Old Lionel attempts to have him arrested. Meanwhile, Reignald is harried by a usurer to whom Young Lionel owes money.
