Full speech

Remember, Sir, this is your wedding day, And that triumph belongs onely to your wife. If you be noble let me cut off his head. Yes: does any man thinke Ile goe like a sheepe to the slaughter? Hands off my Lord, your Lordship may chance come vnder my hands: If you doe, I shall shew my selfe a Citizen, and reuenge basely. Signior Claridiana. A friend to thee, if thy intents be iust & honorable. My name is Rogero: do'st thou know me? I know thee valiant Spaniard, and to thee Murders more hatefull, then is sacrilege Thy actions euer haue been honourable. I neuer wrong'd thee Spaniard, did I? speake I'le make thee satisfaction like a souldiour, Tell him all the Plot. A true Italian, and a Gentleman: Thy rage is treacherie without a cause. Murder should keepe with basenesse, not with merit: I'le answere thee to morrow by my soule, And cleare thy doubts, or satisfie thy will. My friend; is it the rigour of the law I should be tied thus hard, Ile vndergoe it: If not, prethee then slacken; yet I haue deseru'd it, This murder lies heauie on my conscience. O whore, I little thought to see you in this taking, I am gouernour of this castle of cornets, My graue will be stumbl'd at, thou adultrat whore, I might haue liu'd like a Marchant. Peace, thou art verie quicke with me. A bastard, a bastard, a bastard: I might haue liu'd like a gentleman, And now I must die like a Hanger on: Shew trickes vpon a woodden horse, And runne through an Alphabet of scuruie faces: Doe not expect a good looke from me. I haue confest it, to my ghostly father, And done the Sacrament of penance for it. What would your highnesse more? Amen, Amen. O that a man had the instinct of a Lyon, He knowes when the Lionesse place fals to him: But these solaces, these women, They bring man to gray haires before he be thirtle. Yet they cast out such mistes of flatterie from their breath, That a mans lost againe ▪ sure I fell into my marriage bed drunke: Like the Leopard, well with sober eyes would I had auoided it; Come graue and hide me from my blasted fame; Exeunt Ambo with officers. O that thou could'st as well conceale my shame. Marry and that Lethergie seize you, reade againe. Stretch not my patience Mahomet. Had I knowne this I would haue poison'd thee in the Chalice, This morning, when we receaued the Sacrament. Thus tis to lye with another mans wife: He shalbe sure to heare on't againe. But we are friends, sweet ducke, kisse her. And this shall be my maxime all my life, MAN neuer happy is till in a wife. O my Liege, I had rather rore to death with Phaleris Bull, then Darius -like, to haue one of my wings extend to Atlas, the other to Europa. What is a Cuckold learne of me, Few can tell his pedigree, Nor his subtill nature conster, Borne a man, but dyes a monster. Yet great Antiquaryes say, They spring from our Methusala, Who after Noahs flood was found, To haue his Crest with branches crown'd. God in Edens happy shade, This same creature made. Then to cut off all mistaking, Cuckolds are of womens making. From whose snares, good Lord deliuer vs.

Plot summary

Plot — The Insatiate Countess
Suitors visit Isabella, who is in open mourning for her dead husband, but secretly glad to be free of his jealousy and restrictions. She accepts Count Roberto’s suit and they are married the following day; but when she dances with Count Rogero in the wedding masque, her affections turn. She woos Rogero by letter and elopes with him to Pavia; Roberto enters holy orders. At Pavia, Rogero is visited by his friend Gniaca; Isabella falls in love with the newcomer, and feigns illness to give herself an opportunity to seduce him. After bedding Gniaca, she then feigns penitence to free herself from Rogero. The spurned lover takes his revenge by circulating bitter satires on Isabella’s promiscuity, so she sets on Gniaca to kill him. In the attempt, Gniaca is reconciled with his former friend, so Isabella seduces Don Sago, colonel in the Spanish power occupying Pavia, to murder both Rogero and Gniaca. Sago kills Rogero, and is arrested, but pardoned by the Duke of Medina. Isabella’s attempt to bribe the Duke fails to secure her freedom, and she is beheaded after a scaffold reconciliation with Roberto. In the sub-plot, the enemies Claridiana and Mizaldus are both married on the same day. They quarrel when they meet, and Mizaldus’s wife Abigail and Lady Lentulus, who are close friends, compare notes and swap places: each receives her own husband in the other’s bedroom. Meanwhile their friends Mendosa and Lady Lentulus, attracted to one another, try to circumvent the latter’s vow not to admit a man through her front door: he climbs up to her window by rope ladder, but falls into the street, where the watch find him injured and send him to a surgeon. Claridiana and Mizaldus are arrested in each other’s house; appalled at the thought of being cuckolded, they both confess to murdering Mendosa. Mendosa in turn tries to save Lady Lentulus’s honour with a false confession that he was attempting to burgle her house. In court, the Duke establishes that Claridiana and Mizaldus are telling incompatible stories about the ‘murder’, and they are sentenced to death in jest; in Mendosa’s case, however, the sentence is genuine. Claridiana and Mizaldus are determined to die until confronted with evidence of their wives’ chastity; with reconciliation comes reprieve.
