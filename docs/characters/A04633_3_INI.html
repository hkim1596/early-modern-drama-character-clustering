Full speech

What is he, calls vpon me, and would seeme to lack a Vice ▪ Ere his words be halfe spoken, I am with him in a trice; Here, there, and euery where, as the Cat is with the mice: True vetus Iniquitas. Lack'st thou Cards, friend, or Dice? I will teach thee cheate, Child, to cog, lye, and swagger, And euer and anon, to be drawing forth thy dagger: To sweare by Gogs-nownes, like a lusty Inuentus, In a cloake to thy heele, and a hat like a pent-house. Thy breeches of three fingers, and thy doublet all belly, With a Wench that shall feede thee, with cock-stones and gelly. Child of hell, this is nothing! I will fetch thee a leape From the top of Pauls -steeple, to the Standard in Cheepe: And lead thee a daunce, through the streets without faile, Like a needle of Spaine, with a thred at my tayle. We will suruay the Suburbs, and make forth our sallyes, Downe Petticoate-lane, and vp the Smock-allies, To Shoreditch, Whitechappell, and so to Saint Kathernes. To drinke with the Dutch there, and take forth their patternes: From thence, wee will put in at Custome-house key there, And see, how the Factors, and Prentizes play there, False with their Masters; and gueld many a full packe, To spend it in pies, at the Dagger, and the Wool sacke. Nay ▪ boy, I wil bring thee to the Bawds, and the Roysters, At Belins gate, feasting with claret-wine, and oysters, From thence shoot the Bridge, childe, to the Cranes i'the Vintry, And see, there the gimblets, how they make their entry! Or, if thou hadst rather, to the Strand downe to all, 'Gainst the Lawyers come dabled from Westminster-hall And marke how they cling, with their clyents together, Like Iuie to Oake; so Veluet to Leather: Ha, boy, I would shew thee. Child of hell, be thou merry: Put a looke on, as round, boy, and red as a cherry. Cast care at thy posternes; and firke i'thy fetters, They are ornaments, Baby, haue graced thy betters: Looke vpon me, and hearken. Our Cheife doth salute thee, And least the coldyron should chance to confute thee, H' hath sent thee, grant-paroll by me to stay longer A moneth here on earth, against cold Child, or honger Mount, dearling of darkenesse, my shoulders are broad: He that caries the fiend, is sure of his loade. The Diuell was wont to carry away the euill; But, now, the Euill out-carries the Diuell. O mee!

Plot — The Devil Is an Ass
. _ Pug the junior devil is keen to visit earth. Satan svarns him that his ideas of effective devilment are half a century out of date, but grants him leave to spend the day in London, made flesh and subject to all the frailties and limitations of the human body. A ready-made body is available, in the shape of a recently hanged cutpurse, but Pug will have to make his own arrangements for clothes. Another proviso is that he must serve the first man he meets. Unfortunately for Pug, he turns out to be a twit: Fabian Fitzdottrel. Fitzdottrel longs to see the devil, and agrees to accept Pug as an unpaid servant because he gives his mame as Devil. The same obsession makes Fitzdottrel eager to see a new play, The Devil is an Ass, but he needs some finery to show off as he sits on the stage. Wittipol is eager to sleep with Fitzdottrel’s wife, and offers him a suitable cloak in exchange for fifteen minutes’ uninterrupted conversation with the lady. He uses the time to proposition her, and engages her interest even though she has been forbidden to reply. Fitzdottrel is introduced to Merecraft, a purveyor of imaginative financial schemes, who offers to make him rich and a duke through land reclamation in the eastern fens. Fitzdottrel agrees to meet with him, leaving Pug to ensure that nobody attempts to visit his wife. Pug instead brings her a follow-up message from Wittipol, then makes advances to her on his own account, but is caught by Fitzdottrel and gets a beating. Wittipol’s friend Manly has a room which abuts Mistress Fitzdottrel’s gallery, which he uses to have a second meeting with her. This is much more intimate than the first, despite being conducted through the windows of adjacent buildings. Pug gets back into favour by tipping off Fitzdottrel, who puts a stop to the assignation and threatens to take up another woman to share his expectations and be his Duchess. Merecraft suggests that what Fitzdottrel’s wife needs is to be taught proper manners, and recommends a widow who knows the ways of the Spanish court, who might be persuaded to undertake the task if sent an expensive ring as a gift, and who is entirely fictitious. His accomplice Engine proposes hiring an actor to impersonate her, but Merecraft worries that his business methods might be leaked to a playwright and end up on stage; in the event, Engine finds a gentleman to take on the role instead, who happens to be Wittipol.
