Who's that abroad ſo late? Charity ſhould be in bed at Mid-night. Hee's gone from home, and that's a reaſon too We ſhut up doores thus early; ſay, what are you? Religious men, and keepe unlawfull houres? 'Twere ſuſpitious for me, but a lone woman, he abroad, To entertaine men of your ranke and place, VVhoſe luſts have all bin queſtion'd, and have drawne Good women of beſt rumour and report, Into foule ſcandall; therefore pardon me, This night you get no entrance. Good, excuſe mee. Curſe upon them, No night but this to trouble me in? being ingaged To better fare by appointment. VVell Frier, you ſhall, But this provided, I will locke you up In a cloſe Garret, and my ſelfe keepe the Key To avoid ſuſpition; you ſhall have freſh ſtraw, But other Bedding none, no fire nor Candle, Beere, Ale, nor any ſuch commodity, Now in my Husbands abſence; ſo, pray enter, And this I doe for Charity. I have onely a Cock-loft, pleaſe you gentle Friers, To make beſt uſe of that and ſome freſh ſtraw, Beſt harbour that my Husbands abſence yeelds Y'are welcome to. Pleaſe you enter, but yet no further then Ile locke you in Ile keepe the Key my ſelfe. Whoſe there? Not Maſter Conſtable? Have you diſpoſ'd your Watch? Not too loud, meane time I ſoftly will ſneake downe The Staires, and ſoftly let you in. I was in feare you would have broke with me, But now I ſee y'are currant. Piping hot, Ile goe fetch Salt and Trenchers. Now let's be merry, but in any caſe Speake not too loud, leaſt we ſhould wake the Friers. Two Abbey Lubbers that are lockt up faſt, Of whom I could not rid me; but I thinke I have fitted them, they have neither light nor bed, Nor any other Comfort. Doe you't, I am no Carver. If I begin, may I ne're eate more. If that ſhould be my Husband? You were ſham'd, I quite undone; he knockes againe; Upon my life 'tis he. My Husbands voice, who's there? Creepe beneath the Bed. Now to convey theſe victuals hence, and put out the fire, Lord Husband is it you? who lookt for you ſo late? Theſe ſhall into the Cupboord, if the ſmell Betray's not w'are ſafe. I am comming, good lye cloſe, and if I can get him Once to bed, I have a tricke to ſhift you. Lord Husband that you'le venter whom ſo late, So many Knaves abroad? What, talke of Victuals now? is this a time of night? Had I knowne But of your comming home I had provided, But now I pray to bed, this cold ſo troubles me. You ſee the fire's quite out. Would houſe and all were fir'd, So ſome of us were out on't. No hurt man; nay, pray will you to bed? Two Friers ſhut from their Covents, wanting harbour, Begg'd lodging with ſuch importunity They would not be deny'd; at length I pittied them, And yet to make them ſure, I lockt them faſt Up in the Straw-loft, and ſee, here's the Key, Th'are faſt enough for ſtarting. Frier Bernard, and Frier Iohn. Call them up? what time ſhall we to bed then? To your bellies pots and all. Not ſo much as a cantell of Cheeſe, or cruſt of bread, That can this night be come by (for your throates, Ile rather ſee you choakt.) What a leering eye the Frier caſt towards the Cupbord? We ſhall have now ſome fooling. You ſee to pleaſe you, we can doe any thing. Pox on the Frier, have you theſe trickes? Your Divells take you; you know where's the beſt liquor. The Divell take all ſuch ſmell-feaſts. A poyſon on thee, would it might prove ſo to thee, It was never provided for thoſe chopps. But curſe on thine, and on thy ſtomacke too. I could eate thee. More ſcurvy trickes Frier Iohn, I may live to cry quit tance with you. Ile after him ▪ and leaſt he ſhould beare downe part Of the houſe, Ile let him out of doores. Wondrouſly. Ten daies, no more, Part of that time that Slightall hath bin Mad, My daughter paſt her ſences. No, onely one by Chamber ſtands remote, And in that after Midnight there's a noiſe, That troubles the whole houſe of howlings, ſhrickes, Fire-workes and Crackers, as if the great ſquib-maker Were new come from beyond Sea. Onely my deſperate Husband, who, one night, Would be ſo fooliſh venturous as lodge there, But did returne ſo frighted, told ſuch wonders, Still fright me to remember. Heaven reſt her ſoule, my mother. A Divell ſir like me? Here's now my Husband, he can ſpeake it all. And now art found. Made me againe thy Mother, and this worke, Your ever patient and obedient wife. For reverence of your Order. Sicke in her Chamber, where ſhe keepes her Bed, And dares not thence remove.

Plot summary

Plot — A New Trick to Cheat the Devil
Anne Changeable wants to marry Slightall, with her father’s consent, but her mother persuades her that marriage to Lord Scales will be better for her social standing. Slightall takes rejection badly: he turns to misogyny and voluptuous hedonism. One of his servants, Roger, tries unsuccessfully to dissuade him from squandering his money, while the other, Geoffrey, helps make an arrangement with a usurer to supply him with a further £1,000 — the usurer’s objective being to acquire his land. Lord Scales visits Anne, and she finds that he is not a good kisser: Slightall is the better catch in every respect save his lack of a title; but when she tries to reactivate the romance, Slightall rejects her. He has been ruined by prodigality and pays off his servants; Geoffrey is taken on by Lord Scales, but Roger declines a similar offer. Anne insults everyone who persuaded her to jilt Slightall, but this only makes Lord Scales more attracted to her. Changeable promises Anne that he will get Slightall back for her. Driven out of his wits with melancholy, Slightall conjures up Beelzebub, and makes a contract: the devil will give him enough money to pay his debts in exchange for the right to take his soul when he no longer owes anyone anything. On the day his debt to the usurer falls due, he turns out to have ample money to pay, so the usurer gets his money back rather than the lands he coveted. Asked by Anne to give Slightall some financial support, Lord Scales finds his offer rejected with contempt. The Changeables’ house is troubled by a female spirit; Slightall agrees to help exorcise it by spending the night in the haunted room. When he does so, he is visited by the ghost in Anne’s form. Beelzebub comes for Slightall’s soul, but Slightall demonstrates that he is not yet clear of debt: he paid the usurer with money loaned by Beelzebub. The devil accepts the situation and offers to marry him to the she-devil who haunts the Changeables’ house. Anne’s mother and Lord Scales give their consent, and watch as a friar performs the ceremony. In fact it is not a ghost in Anne’s form, but Anne herself; and ‘Beelzebub’ was really Changeable: Anne has finally got her man back. In the sub-plot, two friars are detained until late by charitable visitation, and find themselves locked out of their abbey. They ask Nan to give them a bed for the night.
