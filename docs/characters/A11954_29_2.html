Full speech

Thou art going to Lord Timons Feaſt. Farthee well, farthee well. Why Apemantus ? Away vnpeaceable Dogge, Or Ile ſpurne thee hence. He powres it out: Plutus the God of Gold Is but his Steward: no meede but he repayes Seuen-fold aboue it ſelfe: No guift to him, But breeds the giuer a returne: exceeding All vſe of quittance. Long may he liue in Fortunes. Shall we in? Ile keepe you Company. But beleeue you this my Lord, that not long agoe, one of his men was with the Lord Lucullus, to borrow ſo many Talents, nay vrg'd extreamly for't, and ſhewed what neceſſity belong'd too't, and yet was deny'de. I tell you, deny'de my Lord. I, to well. Moſt true; the Law ſhall bruiſe 'em. He has made too much plenty with him: He's a ſworne Riotor, he has a ſinne That often drownes him, and takes his valour priſoner. If there were no Foes, that were enough To ouercome him. In that Beaſtly furie, He has bin knowne to commit outrages, And cherriſh Factions. 'Tis inferr'd to vs, His dayes are foule, and his drinke dangerous. How? I alſo wiſh it to you: I thinke this Honorable Lord did but try vs this other day. It ſhould not be, by the perſwaſion of his new Fea ſting. In like manner was I in debt to my importunat bu ſineſſe, but he would not heare my excuſe. I am ſorrie, when he ſent to borrow of mee, that my Prouiſion was out. Euery man heares ſo: what would hee haue borro wed of you? A thouſand Peeces? He ſent to me ſir—Heere he comes. The Swallow followes not Summer more willing, then we your Lordſhip. My Noble Lord. My moſt Honorable Lord, I am e'ne ſick of ſhame, that when your Lordſhip this other day ſent to me, I was ſo vnfortunate a Beggar. If you had ſent but two houres before. All couer'd Diſhes. I pray you vpon what? This is the old man ſtill. It do's: but time will, and ſo. Know you the quality of Lord Timons fury? Did you ſee my Cap. Lord Timons mad. As we do turne our backes From our Companion, throwne into his graue, So his Familiars to his buried Fortunes Slinke all away leaue their falſe vowes with him Like empty purſes pickt; and his poore ſelfe A dedicated Beggar to the Ayre, With his diſeaſe, of all ſhunn'd pouerty, Walkes like contempt alone. More of our Fellowes. It is nois'd He hath a maſſe of Treaſure. True: for he beares it not about him: 'Tis hid. 'Tis his deſcription. Ile beleeue him as an Enemy, And giue ouer my Trade. They confeſſe Toward thee, forgetfulneſſe too generall groſſe; Which now the publike Body, which doth ſildome Play the re-canter, feeling in it ſelfe A lacke of Timons ayde, hath ſince withall Of it owne fall, reſtraining ayde to Timon, And ſend forth vs, to make their ſorrowed render, Together, with a recompence more fruitfull Then their offence can weigh downe by the Dramme, I euen ſuch heapes and ſummes of Loue and Wealth, As ſhall to thee blot out, what wrongs were theirs, And write in thee the figures of their loue, Eu to read them thine. And ſhakes his threatning Sword Againſt the walles of Athens. And enter in our eares, like great Triumphers In their applauding gates. Our hope in him is dead: let vs returne, And ſtraine what other meanes is left vnto vs In our deere perill. We ſtand much hazard, if they bring not Timon. So did we wooe Transformed Timon, to our Citties loue By humble Meſſage, and by promiſt meanes: We were not all vnkinde, nor all deſerue The common ſtroke of warre. Nor are they liuing Who were the motiues that you firſt went out, (Shame that they wanted, cunning in exceſſe) Hath broke their hearts. March, Noble Lord, Into our City with thy Banners ſpred, By decimation and a tythed death; If thy Reuenges hunger for that Food Which Nature loathes, take thou the deſtin'd tenth, And by the hazard of the ſpotted dye, Let dye the ſpotted. What thou wilt, Thou rather ſhalt inforce it with thy ſmile, Then hew too't, with thy Sword. Throw thy Gloue, Or any Token of thine Honour elſe, That thou wilt vſe the warres as thy redreſſe, And not as our Confuſion: All thy Powers Shall make their harbour in our Towne, till wee Haue ſeal'd thy full deſire.

Plot summary

Plot — Timon of Athens
; Timon gives freely of his riches to his acquaintances: every little gift will trigger disproportionate largesse by way of requital. He intervenes financially to secure Ventidius’ release from a debtors’ prison, and settles money on his servant Lucilius to enable him to marry the girl he loves, a wealthy man’s daughter. He entertains guests at a lavish feast at which a masque of Amazons is performed. But his creditors. are alarmed by his uncontrolled spending, and call in his debts, which amount to more than the value of his entire estate. He imagines that he can still call on the former recipients of his liberality, but they make their excuses and leave him in difficulties; he is failed even by Ventidius, now a rich man after his father’s death. Timon lays on a second feast, and invites his unreliable friends; they think normal service has been resumed, but are discomfited to find that the menu consists of water alone. Timon drives them off with abuse and leaves Athens in misanthropic fury. Alcibiades attempts to save a friend from execution for murder, but the Senate dismisses the appeal; when he persists, they banish him. In the forest, Timon digs for roots to eat, but finds only buried gold. He meets Alcibiades, marching against Athens with his soldiers, and gives him gold to ensure the city’s destruction; he also pays the army’s whores to spread venereal disease. He encourages a band of thieves to prey upon mankind, but his words persuade them to rethink their way of life. His faithful steward seeks him out and offers to share his own savings; Timon rewards him for his honesty but does not change his low opinion of the rest of humanity. News gets round that Timon still has gold, and hangers-on arrive, hoping this means his bankruptcy was only a bluff to test his friends; he drives them violently away. The Senate asks Timon to return to Athens as its tuler, to defend it against Alcibiades, but Timon, who is busy writing his own epitaph, wishes annihilation on them, and recommends suicide as their only means of escape. Alcibiades’ messenger to Timon finds only his tomb. Athens surrenders, and Timon’s epitaph is read.
