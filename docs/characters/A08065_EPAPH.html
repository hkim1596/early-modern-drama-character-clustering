Yes my Lord, and much beyond. The very doing neuer was ſo liuely As now this counterfeyting. They had beene ſtones, whom that could not haue mon'd. By Ioue, thinke you are the God himſelfe Come from aboue, to ſhew your hidden arts; And fill vs men with wonder of your skill. I ſweare by thee O Caeſar; Then whom no power of Heauen I honour more, No mortall voice can paſſe, or equall thine. Happy are they that heare it. Haue you put Caius in, I ſaw him frowne One that in whiſpering oreheard What pitie 'twas, my Lord, that Piſo died. Helpe ſhe dies. One that by blood, and rapine would repaire His ſtate conſum'd in vanities, and luſt. Faith, the right nature of a coward to ſet light Dangers that ſeeme farre off. Piſo was here, Ready to enter at the Preſence doote, And dragge him out of his abuſed chaire, And then he trembled; Vindex is in France, And many woods, and ſeas, and hills betweene. Strange, ſtrange indeed, for had he but come vp, And taken the Court, in that affright and ſtirre, While vnreſolu'd for whom, or what to doe, Each on the other had in iealouſie (While as apaled Maieſtie not yet Had time to ſet the countenance) he would Haue hazarded the royall ſeat. But for a ſtranger with a naked prouince, Without allies, or friends ith' ſta e to challenge A Prince vpheld with thirty Legions Rooted in foure diſcents of Anceſtors, And foureteene yeares continuance of raigne, Why it is — I pray thee Tigellinus, what furie's this? What ſtrange euent, what accident hath thus Orecaſt your countenances? O you vnſearched wiſedomes, which doe laugh, At our ſecuritie, and feares alike; And plaine to ſhew our weakeneſſe, and your power Make vs contemne the harmes, which ſureſt ſtrike When you our glories, and our pride vndoe, Our ouerthrow you make rediculous too. exeunt. Slow making counſels, and the ſliding yeere Haue brought me to the long foreſeene deſtruction Of this miſled yong man; his State is ſhaken, And I will puſh it on; reuolted France; Nor the coniured Prouinces of Spaine, Nor his owne guilt, ſhall like to me oppreſſe him; I to his eaſie yeelding feares proclaime New German mutenys, and all the world Rowſing it ſelfe in hate of Neroes name; I his diſtracted counſels doe diſperce With freſh deſpaires, I animate the Senate And the people, to ingage them paſt recall In preiudice of Nero, and in briefe, Periſh he muſt, the fates and I reſolue it; Which to effect, I preſently will goe, Proclaime a Donatiue in Galbaes name.

Plot summary

Plot — Nero (Piso's Conspiracy)
The Emperor Nero celebrates his latest triumph : not a military victory, but success in a singing and acting competition against the Greeks; he also has one of his critics executed. He gives a performance in the role of Orestes; the theatre is guarded so that the audience cannot escape, and those who do not respond with due enthusiasm are marked down for execution. Deciding that he has treated Rome too lenien:tly, he has the city burned down . The wind blows the fire back towards the palace, and his own possessions are destroyed. . Antonius loves _ the Empress Poppaea, despite Petronius' efforts to persuade him to settle for someone of lower rank. Poppaea refuses Antonius, preferring Nymphidius, who has cultivated her affections as a means to make himself Emperor. Scaevinus broaches --a conspiracy to assassinate Nero, thinking it demeaning to have _an Emperor so dedicated to artistic pursuits. Piso is recruited, and the plotters discuss the best way to assassinate Nero; but they are overheard by the freedman Milichus, who reports them to Nero. Piso commits suicide; other conspirators are arrested, and Nero institutes a purge of their sympathizers. Petronius is arrested and executed simply because Tigellinus has -a score to settle with him. A young man is executed merely for pitying Piso's death; Poppaea tries to save him because he resembles a former lover of hers, so Nero kicks her to death in a rage. News arrives from France that Vindex has revolted and chosen · Galba as Emperor. Nymphidius spreads rumours that Nero has resigned, and tries both to curry favour with Galba's agents and to buy military support for his own ambitions. Nero is condemned by the Senate and commits suicide . Nymphidius is killed by Galba's supporters.
