Gracious my father feare not, I rest most dutious to your dispose. Consort of Musique. You are most welcome to our long desiring Father, to vs you are come.— Is this your fathers true proportion? Shewes a picture. And the best grac't. I wonder he would send a counterfeit to mooue our loue, My Lord, please you to scent this flower. This hath beene such as you are, hath beene sir; they say in England, that a farre fam'd Frier had guirt the Island round with a brasse wall, if that they could haue catched, Time is, but Time is past, left it hill clipt with aged Neptunes arme. Yet blushes at it when she rises. But is your Fathers age thus fresh, hath yet his head so ma ny haires? More say you? Right sir, for this hath none, is his eye so quicke as this same peece makes him shew? This modell speakes aboue fortie. Your father hath a faire Solicitor, And be it spoke with virgin modestie, I would he were no elder, not that I doe flie His side for yeares, or other hopes of youth, But in regard the malice of lewd tonges Quicke to depraue on possibilities, (Almost impossibilities) will spread Dulcimel & Tiberio conferre priuatly. Rumors, to honour dangerous. And as I told you sir, Tiberio being sent, Grac't in high trust as to negotiate His royall fathers loue, if he neglect The honour of this faith, iust care of state, And euerie fortune that giues likelyhood To his best hopes, to drawe our weaker heart To his owne loue (as I protest he do's) I am of yeares, apt for his loue, & if he should proceed in priuate vrgent sute, how easie twere, To win my loue, for you may say (if so Your wisedome please) you find in me A very forward passion to inioy him, And therefore you beseech him seriously Straight to forbeare, with such close cunning arte, To vrge his too well graced suite: for you (If so your Lordship please) may say I told you all. Ther's a shippe of fooles going out, shall I prefer thee Nymphodoro, thou maist be maisters mate, my father hath made Dondilo Captain, els thou should'st haue his place. May I rest sure thou wilt conceale a secret? How may I rest truely assur'de? Why, canst thou not conceale a secret? O Philocalia that difficultie onely inflames me, when the enterprise is easy, the victory is inglorious, no let my wise aged, learned, intelligent Father, that can interpret eies vn derstand the language of birds, interpret the grumbling of dogs & the co ̄ ference of cats, that can read euen silence, let him forbid all enterviewes, all speeches, all tokens, all messages, all (as he thinkes) humaine meanes, I will speake to the Prince, court the Prince, that he shall vnderstand mee, nay I will so stalke on the blind side of my all knowing fathers wit, that do what his wisedome can, he shall be my onely mediatour, my onely messenger, my onely honourable spokesman, hee shall carrie my fauours, he shall amplifie my affection nay hee shall direct the Prince the meanes the very way to my bed, hee and onely he, when hee onely can doe this, and onely would not doe this, hee onely shall doe this. Pish Philocalia, tis against the nature of loue, not to bee violent. Constancy? constancy and patience are vertues in no liuing creatures but Centinels and Anglers: heres our father. Nay I protest what ere he faine to you (as he can faine most deepely) And as I saide, royally wise, and wisely royall Father— Tho he can faine, yet I presume your sense is quick e nough to find him. But other talke for vs were farre more fit, For see heere comes the prince Tiberio. Deare father in all dutie, let me beseech your leaue, that I may but— Yet let me, (the loiall seruant of simplicitie.) Heauens forbid such insolence, yet let me denounce my harty hatred. Tho't be but in the Princes eare, (since fit's not maide ̄ s blush to raile aloude. Let mee but check his heate. And take him downe deare father, from his full pride of hopes. O my princely father now or neuer let your princely wisedome appeare. My Lord the Prince. O Lord what wisedome our good parents neede, to shield their chickens from deceipts, & wiles of kite like youth. Alas had not your grace beene prouident, a very Nestor in aduise and knowledge, ha, where had you poore Dulci mel beene now, what vaines had not I beene drawne in to? Father doe you see that tree, that leanes iust on my chamber window? O sir, but note the policie of youth, marke but the stratagems of working loue, The prince salutes me, and thus greetes my eare. Although he knew, I yet stood wauering, what to elect, because though I affected, yet destitute of meanes to in ioy each other, impossibilitie of hauing, might kill our hope and with our hope desires to inioy. Therefore to auoid all faint excuses, and vaine feares, thus he deuised: to Dulcimels chamber window. A well growne plain tree spreads his hap pie armes, by that in depth of night one may ascend, (dispight all fathers iealousies and feares) into her bed. You shall prouide a Priest (quoth hee) in truth I pro mist & so you well may tell him, for I temporized and onelie held him off. With full intention to disclose it all, to your preuenting wisedome. O Sir in that is al, when but this night? This very night when the court reuels had o're wakt your spirites, and made them full of sleepe, then— Royally wise, and wisely royall father. I humbly thanke your worthy piety, that through your only means I haue obtained so fit, louing & desired a hus band.

Plot summary

Plot — Parasitaster, or The Fawn
After failing to persuade his son Tiberio to marry, Hercules, the widowed Duke of Ferrara, plans to marry Princess Dulcimel of Urbino, and sends Tiberio to negotiate the treaty. Hercules goes to Urbino himself, assumes the alias Faunus, meets Nymphadoro and Herod, and hears some unflattering remarks about himself in his other persona, Hercules. He gets them to introduce him at court, and becomes a favourite with the Duke of Urbino. Dulcimel would rather marry Tiberio than Hercules, and contrives to woo him using her father, Duke Gonzago, as an intermediary. She brings him a scarf which she has embroidered with a message, and tells him that it was sent by Tiberio; the outraged Gonzago gives it to the young man, thinking he is returning an unwanted would-be lover's gift. She then reports to ===== PAGE 176 ===== Gonzago that Tiberio intends to climb up the tree by her bedroom window and surreptitiously marry her. Gonzago repeats the plan back to Tiberio, revokes his diplomatic status, and orders him to leave Urbino. Instead, Tiberio. puts the plan into effect and marries Dulcimel in her bedroom. Faunus: observes the foolish behaviour of the courtiers at Urbino. Herod cuckolds his brother, the impotent Sir Amoroso, and gets his sister-in-law with child — in effect, dispossessing himself of the estate by producing an heir apparent. Donna Zoya, who bas not slept with her husband Zuccone for four years, feigns pregnancy, and the gossip spreads rapidly through the court, each person telling the next on condition that they keep it strictly to themselves. Zuccone repudiates her, then regrets it on learning she is not really an adulteress, but she will only take him back on new terms which give her the upper hand in the marriage. Faunus lays on a court entertainment: a parliament of Cupid to consider the many grievances lovers have sent to heaven. Cupid’s statutes are read, and several of the foolish courtiers are tried. Nymphadoro is convicted as a multiple philanderer and sent to the ship of fools, Herod for falsely boasting of love, and Granuffo for affecting silence to seem wiser than he is. Sir Amoroso (charged with wooing on false pretences) and Zuccone are spared. The last fool to be exposed is Gonzago, for procuring the seduction of his own daughter. Resuming his own identity, Hercules thanks Gonzago for securing Tiberio’s marriage.
