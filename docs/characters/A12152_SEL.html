And yet your loue is from your heart Ile warrant. Well sir, I know you haue Rethoricke, but I Can without art giue you a finall answer. Y'are ouer-weake to let your passions sway you: If I knew any thing I had of yours, I would not doe you that iniustice, sir, To let it stay with me, and for your loue I cannot pay it backe againe with mine, Either release the debt, or I shall dye in't, Your suite is fruitlesse, hopelesse, pardon me, farewell. Brother, you forget your selfe. Must? My duty, sir, shall speake it. Young Infortunio. Sir, I haue often heard you talke of her, But neuer knew that sister well. I humbly thanke you. Deare father, low as earth I tender you The duty of a daughter, I haue heard you Not with a carelesse eare, that liberty You haue bestow'd on me, for which I owe All that I am, doth make me confident You will not be offended, if I tell you My loue is vertuous, were it otherwise, I should elect as you premonish youth, And prodigall blood: And father, I thinke here I shew my selfe your daughter, nor am I Without good president too: how many fine Young noble Ladies, in this Faiëry Ile, Haue matcht with reuerend age? and liue as they Were borne from Natures puritie, free from staine Of sensuall imputation, by their Loues, Deriuing heauenly honours to themselues 'Boue merit of equality. I would not leaue Rufaldo for a world Of rash vntemperate youth, beleeue it sir. With what agility he moues himselfe, As he were made of ayre? let weaknes taxe Our inequality, I haue a minde Can easily contemne what the worlds malice Out of its owne first guiltines can throw Vpon our loues, 't shall be enough for me Thus to conuince the world of so much basenes, Lodg'd in luxurious thoughts, by my chast thoughts. Rufaldo, thou art mine, all time, me thinkes Is slow, till we be actually possest Of mutuall enioying. Stay, who's this The Welshman that deliuers his affection At second hand to me. Sir, I am bound to you for the affection You cast on me, 'tis farre aboue my merit. Loue you, sir? I know not How to be so inhumane, not to loue you, Your parts deserue a nobler obiect, I am not Worthy so much opinion of your loue, But wherein I may doe you seruice sir, You shall command Selina. Thus farre I haue past without discouery: the mor ning is auspicious to my flight. Selina, what an alteration hath a day made in thee, that to preuent thy so desired marriage, thou art thus lost in a masculine habit, and dost flie him? thou didst so much loue aged Rufaldo, in what a Le thargy wert thou falne, Selina? Whither had reason so withdrawne it selfe? I could not make distinction of a man, From such a heape of age, aches and rheume: Sure I was mad, and doth encrease my fury, To thinke with what a violence I ran To imbrace such rottennesse. O, my guilty soule Doth feele the punishment of the iniury I did to Infortunio of late, Of whom as I despaire, so shall the world, Euer to know againe haplesse Selina. This is the morne the sacred Rites should tie Me to Rufaldo, ripe in expectation, But like Ixion he shall graspe a Cloud, My empty cloathes at home, Selina thus Is turn'd a Shepheard, and will try her fortune; Hard by the Shepheards haue their shady dwellings, There let Selina end her haplesse daies, Father and all farewell. Thus as Felice My other Sister, Ile weare out my life, Farre from your knowledge: sacred loue commands Reuenge and iustice for my cruelty, And reson now awak'd shall lead me to it, Thus I am safe, I goe to finde out that, Will meete me euery where, a iust sad fate. I had thought the woods and such wilde groues as these, Had beene the house of rapine, and could not Afford humanity, beasts, and men like them, Are wont to make such places desolate: Did Nature make you thus at first, and are We that haue Cities, house, and Ciuill Lawes More rude then you? or hath all vertue chose You as diuine earth to dwell vpon? Shepheardesse, indeed I am in loue With your wild Kingdome here, and would not be A King abroad, if I might be a subiect With such faire Nymphes as you. No more, good Shepheards, you vndoe a boy With the opinion of his happinesse: If a few Iewels I haue brought with me, May find acceptance here, I shall bestow them As freely as your loues haue falne on me. Nay then, Ile force 'em on you, I haue left To purchase a Flock with you. But Shepheardesse, or sister if thou wilt, Oh would thou wert, I preethee call me brother, Hath Loue a part among you, tell me pray, What punishment inflict you on false loue? But sure you are exempt from such A misery: what then Is her reward, that out of peeuishnesse Contemnes the honest passion of her Louer, Insults vpon his vertue, and doth place Vnworthily her affection? Oh, is there not within the power of Art, How to restore this Gentleman? He will be worthy of your care herein, And should he be, which I cannot imagine, Ingratefull to your skill, I would reward it, And call you mother, or my sister for it. Indeed he is the dearest friend I had: And if my blood were powerfull to restore him, Ide spend it like a prodigall. I know Selina. Come, I will fetch Selina to you, if you will sleepe. But you said you would sleepe: when slept you last? A thousand blessings on you. Come, sir, goe with me, and when you haue slept, Ile fetch Salina to you. Will you goe with me? Oh, stay: that brought Infortunio hither, You haue now told your selfe, distraction brought you. You owe this Shepheardesse for your restore, Whose skill heauen made so happy. This Lad can tell you that, better then I, But if his sorrow will not let his tongue Deliuer it, Ile tell you sir; you were in loue. Sir, you remember her too well, vnlesse She would deserue it better. Why should you be so passionate? let once reason so late recouered, teach you loue your selfe, reseru'd for nobler fortune. Doe you beleeue him sir? Be not deiected, sir, you haue a fate Doth smile vpon you, I haue a little skill. In that this Gentleman seem'd to haue some knowledge, I must needs crosse his iudgement, and pronounce You are more happy. Let not ought your soule annoy, You that virgin shall emoy, That you first lou'd, who doth waite To make your wishes fortunate, And ere Sun twice declines to West, You may be with marriage blest. Then with one Argument I can easily Take off your wonders, looke on thy Selina, That on the wedding morne, forsooke Rufaldo, Touch'd with a sense of thy indignities, Thus to obscure me from all curious search And inquisition, but not hoping euer To be made thine, now if true loue maintaine The opinion you pretend, thus in your armes I powre my selfe. Shall I not be beleeued then for my selfe? am I refu sed now? Since then you put me, sir, to proue my selfe, let me not be lost, I will not call you what I desire, nor name you sister: giue me leaue to finde my selfe, I know not where I am yet: my brother Antonio gon? what fury hath assum'd Selina's shape? I haue it, my Bother's lost: Ile send a shepheard in Antonio's name, To inuite my Father hither, and that Incubus. I vow not to forsake these Plaines, till I Possesse my selfe, or be reiected quite, Suspend thy passions then awhile, Selina, To morrow is the Shepheards Holiday, Which they solemnize with rurall pleasures, 'Twill draw them sooner: ha, are they gone? I will not leaue 'em, with this thread I shall Tread o're the Labyrinth, and discouer all. Faire Nymph, vouchsafe the honor to dance with me. We know you are Selina. You are a thiefe. You haue robd Selina. Antonio. I am Selina by my hopes of heauen. I haue no brother but Antonio. If thou beest Antonio, what made thee assume my habit? Hees here, and with him Gasparo and my sister the lost Felice. Keepe your face constant: the Musicke calls. I am Selina, sir. As I to be possest By Infortunio here.

Plot summary

Plot — The School of Compliment (Love Tricks)
Selina and Hilaria have each attracted the attention of several suitors. Selina’s brother Antonio is one of those interested in Hilaria; and Hilaria’s recently widowed father Rufaldo, who disapproves of Antonio and would rather she married the wealthy Bubulcus, would nevertheless like Selina for his second wife. Antonio has his friend and servant distract Rufaldo while he sneaks in to visit Hilaria; they do it by persuading Rufaldo that he has been miraculously rejuvenated. Selina’s father is happy to let her have her own way in ===== PAGE 140 ===== her love-life, because her sister Felice absconded after he interfered in hers. He proposes that she should be cautious about marrying Rufaldo, but she declares enthusiasm for the idea and asks for a hasty wedding; on the appointed day, however, she disappears and goes to live in the country disguised as a shepherd. Felice’s abandoned suitor Gasparo sets up a school of compliment to teach people refined behaviour. His clients include: Bubulcus, who wants to learn how to woo Hilaria and scorn Antonio; Delia, a chambermaid keen to brush up her etiquette: a judge who wants to learn how to address the jury; Medulla, a social-climbing widow; an old countryman who wants to turn his oafish son into a courtier; a quarrelsome gentleman; Ingeniolo, a justice’s clerk in love; and Jenkin, an amorous Welshman in search of poetry to turn a shepherdess’s head. The search for Selina sends several people off to the country, including her true love Infortunio. Antonio pretends to go too, and appears not to return; in fact he impersonates Selina and ‘marries’ Rufaldo. On the wedding night, the old man is horrified to learn that his new ‘wife’ will not sleep with him, and also has the physical strength to enforce ‘her’ will on him. Anxious not to lose ‘her’ altogether, he asks ‘her’ at least to live in his house and sleep with Hilaria; Antonio readily agrees. Bubulcus boasts to ‘Selina’ and Hilaria that he has killed the missing Antonio in a duel, stupidly forgetting that Selina is Antonio’s sister; ‘she’ fetches officers, and Bubulcus is arrested for murder. The real Selina has fallen in with a community of shepherds. Among them is her missing sister Felice, who uses herbal remedies to cure Infortunio of his love-melancholy for Selina.
