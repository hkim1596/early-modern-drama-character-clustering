Nay good Chucke. Deny me, by my honour You take no pitty on me. I shall swoune Assoone as you are absent, aske my Man else, You know he dares not tell a lie. And alls to make you merry When you come home. Old, Ducke to me You are young Adonis. Ile kisse him for the honor of my Country, With any she in Corinth. And if neede be I haue a Couch, and a banquetting house in my Orchard, Where many a man of honour has not scorn'd To spend an afternoone. Hee's a clowne, I warrant him. Carelesse Harlotrie, Striking her. Looke too't, if a Curle fall, or winde, or Sunne, Take my Complexion off, I will not leaue One haire vpon thine head. Fie on these warres, I am staru'd for want of action, not a gamester left To keepe a woman play; if this world last A little longer with vs, Ladyes must studie Some new found Mistery, to coole one another, Wee shall burne to Cinders else; I haue heard there haue beene Such Arts in a long vacation; would they were Reueal'd to mee: they haue made my Doctor too Phisitian to the Army, he was vs'de To serue the turne at a pinch: but I am now Quite vnprouided. Leaue the last out, it smells rancke of the Countrie, And shewes course breeding, your true Courtier knowes not His Neece, or Sister from another woman, If she be apt and cunning. I could tempt now This foole, but he will be so long a working. Then hee's my Husbands Sonne; the fitter to Supply his wants, I haue the way already. I'le trie, if it will take; when were you with Your Mistris, faire Cleora. O what am I falne to! But 'tis a punishment for my lust and pride, Iustly return'd vpon me. Yes forsooth. Whom can we accuse But our selues for what we suffer? thou art iust Thou all-creating power. And miserie Instructs me now, that yesterday acknowledg'd, No Deitie beyond my lust and pride. There is a heauen aboue vs, that lookes downe With the eyes of Iustice, vpon such as number Those blessings freely giuen, in the accompt Of their poore merits: Else it could not be Now miserable I, to please whose pallat The Elements were ransack'd, yet complain'd Of Nature, as not liberall enough In her prouision of rarities To soothe my taste, and pamper my proud flesh: Now wish in vaine for bread: I that forgot I was made of flesh and blood, and thought the silke Spunne by the diligent worme, out of their intrals, Too course to cloathe mee; and the softest Downe Too hard to sleepe on; that disdain'd to looke On vertue being in ragges; that stop'd my nose At those that did not vse adulterate arts To better nature; that from those, that seru'd me, Expected adoration, am made iustly The scorne of my owne Bond-woman. Temper'd, I say, with mercie. Enter at one dore Leosthenes Ti magoras at the other Officers with Pisander and Timandra. 'Twill best become you. I will not change thee For twenty boistrous young things without Beards. These bristles giue the gentlest Tittillations, And such a sweet dew flowes on them, it cures My lippes without Pomatum; heres a round belly, 'Tis a Downe pillow to my backe. I sleepe So quietly by it; and this tunable nose (Faith when you heare it not) affords such musicke, That I curse all night Fidlers. As I liue I am iealous. Yes, and I haue reason, Knowing how lusty and actiue a man you are. You haue forgot what you did in your sleepe, And when you wakd cald for a Cawdle. I long to see the man of warre Cleora Archadamus Daughter goes, and rich Olimpa, I will not misse the showe. Troth I like em the better. I cannot endure to haue a perfum'd Sir Stand cringing in the hammes; licking his lips, Like a Spaniell o're a Firmenty pot, and yet Has not the boldnesse to come on, or offer What they know we expect. Musing Cliora? She has giuen it you. Let him kisse his Drumme, Ile saue my lips, I rest on it. He shall finde We are a Common-wealth. Haue at vs. I am sicke, the man Speakes poniards, and diseases. Plague vpon it, She has got the start of vs. I could e'ne burst With enuy at her fortune. There are none but our slaues left, Nor are they to be trusted; some great women (Which I could name) in a dearth of Visitants, Rather then be idle, haue beene glad to play At small game, but I am so queasie stomack't, And from my youth haue beene so vsde to Dainties, I cannot taste such grosse meate; some that are hungrie Draw on their shoomakers, and take a fall From such as mend Mats in their Galleries; Or when a Taylor settles a Petticoate on, Take measure of his Bodkin: fie vpon't, 'Tis base; for my part, I could rather lie with A Gallants breeches, and conceaue vpon 'em, Then stoope so low. Plot summary

Plot — The Bondman (The Noble Bondman)
Carthage has territorial designs on Sicily, so Corinth sends its general, Timoleon, to take charge of the defence of Syracuse. On arrival, he criticizes the Sicilians for appointing unworthy and inexperienced senators and for general corruption. He decrees that all private wealth should be sequestered to pay for the war. It is an unpopular measure until Cleora public- spiritedly gives up her jewels, forcing the others to follow her example. Cleora has several suitors, including Pisander of Thebes; but her brother Timagoras, keen for her to marry his friend Leosthenes, slandered Pisander to induce their father to send him away. Now that the young men are going to war, Leosthenes is jealous about what she may get up to in his absence, so she promises literally not to see or say anything until his return. The slaves of Syracuse take advantage of the absence of the army and rebel, enslaving their former masters. The ringleader is Cleora’s slave Marullo, who claims to be motivated by unrequited passion for his mistress, but, when he gains access to her, disclaims any intention of raping her and receives in return a wordless acknowledgement of her regard for him. He encourages the former slaves to treat their former masters well, since their positions may again be reversed. News arrives of a victory over the Carthaginians, with particular military distinction achieved by Leosthenes. The rebellious slaves man the city’s walls and gates to keep out the returning army; from the walls, Marullo declares their grievances and demands their freedom. Timoleon realizes they will have to fight their way into the city, but the slaves put up a valiant resistance until cowed by the sight of their masters’ whips. Marullo flees to Cleora’s house, where he is captured. Cleora tells Leosthenes what happened, and asks him to use his influence to have Marullo spared, but this only makes Leosthenes jealous again. Cleora’s father gives her freedom to choose her own husband; he respects Marullo for his restraint in not ransacking the house during the revolt. Cleora realizes she must marry either Leosthenes or Marullo: both are worthy of her, but she is bound to disappoint one. On hearing that Marullo has been ill-treated by his captors, she rushes off to visit him in prison. Leosthenes and Timagoras follow, alerted by her maid, and find them alone together.
