I am tyr'd Sir, And nere shall foot it home. Nor can you taxe me, Sir, I hope, for want of duty to deserve These favours from you. Make me understand Sir, What 'tis you point at. And who gave you Commission to deliver Your verdict, Minion? Sir, I know them By publique fame, though yet I never saw them; And that oppos'd antipathy betweene Their various dispositions, renders them The generall discourse and argument; One part inclining to the Scholar Charles, The other side preferring Eustace, as A man compleat in Courtship. To be plaine, Sir, (Since you will teach me boldnesse) As they are Simply themselves, to neither; Let a Courtier Be never so exact, Let him be blest with All parts that yeeld him to a Virgin gracious, If he depend on others, and stand not On his owne bottomes, though he have the meanes To bring his Mistresse to a Masque, or by Conveyance from some great ones lippes, to taste Such favour from the kings; or grant he purchase, Precedency in the Country, to be sworne A servant Extraordinary to the Queene; Nay, though he live in expectation of Some huge preferment in reversion; If He Want a present fortune, at the best Those are but glorious dreames, and onely yeeld him A happinesse in posse, not in esse; Nor can they fetch him silkes from th'Mercer; nor Discharge a Taylors bill; nor in full plenty (Which still preserves a quiet bed at home) Maintaine a family. Troth (if he be nothing else) As of the Courtier; all his Songs, and Sonnets, His Anagrams, Acrosticks, Epigrammes, His deepe and Philosophicall discourse Of natures hidden secrets, makes not up A perfect husband; He can hardly borrow The Starres of the Celestiall crowne to make me A tire for my head; nor Charles Waine for a Coach, Nor Ganimede for a Page, nor a rich gowne From Juno's Wardrobe, nor would I lye in (For I despaire not once to be a mother) Under heavens spangled Canopy, or banquet My guests and Gossips with imagin'd Nectar, Pure Orleans would doe better; no, no, father, Though I could be well pleas'd to have my husband A Courtier, and a Scholar, young, and valiant, These are but gawdy nothings, If there be not Something to make a substance. A full estate, and that said, I've said all, And get me such a one with these additions, Farewell Virginity, and welcome wedlocke. I am not So punctuall in all ceremonies, I will bate Two or three of these good parts, before Ile dwell Too long upon the choice. A mad old Gentleman. I finde no fault, Better things well done than want time to doe them. Uncle, why are you sad? Can he speake, Sir? And does he speake well there? He is a man, Then conversation me thinkes— I like thy noblenesse. And me thinkes bravely, This the meere Scholar? I wonder; Is he your brother, Sir? Speake not so softly Sir, tis very likely. No certaine, Sir, I have heard nothing from you but things excellent. They have rich linings Sir. I would your brother— But touch'em inwardly, they smell of Copper. And one hand seale the match, Ime yours for ever. Nay certainly, tis done Sir. Onely conditionall, That if he had the Land, he had my love too; This Gentleman's the heire, and hee'll maintaine it. Plot summary

Plot — The Elder Brother
Louis wishes his lazy daughter Angelina would take more exercise, but her woman Sylvia has a better idea: she should get married. Unfortunately Angelina has high standards: when Louis proposes one or other of Brisac’s sons, Charles the scholar and Eustace the courtier, she objects that neither will have the money to keep her as she would wish. Louis pursues the idea anyway, but the bookish Charles, who has only ever seen one woman, his mother, has been put off marriage by the bad examples of antiquity. Louis is willing to accept Eustace, the younger brother, so long as Brisac agrees to disinherit Charles. Much to the disgust of Charles’s uncle, Miramont, a legal instrument is drawn up for the purpose. Charles seems unconcerned by his father’s plans, until he sees Angelina, whereupon he changes his mind about women, refuses to sign away his birthright, and woos the girl himself. She finds him more attractive than Eustace, and the wedding is aborted. Brisac throws him out; Angelina goes with him, and they live together in unmarried chastity. Spurred on by his father, Eustace visits them at night and tries to seize Angelina, but Charles puts up a fight and takes Eustace’s sword from him. Brisac has arranged for Charles’s servant Andrew to marry Lily, and given the couple a farm; but in reality Lily is his mistress and, on the same night as the brothers fight, he visits her for sex. Andrew brings Miramont to the farm to see the liaison for himself, and intervenes as Brisac is undressing for bed. Brisac tries to buy Andrew’s silence with a larger grant of land before realizing that there is a second witness. Eustace borrows a sword and confronts Charles, but Charles will not give up Angelina, and they fight. The bout is interrupted by the arrival of news that Brisac has been arrested by Louis, who insists on the return of Angelina (in the same condition as before). The brothers join forces to rescue their father. Miramont points out that, if he pursues the case, Louis will both ruin Angelina’s reputation and put himself in the control of lawyers; so Louis drops the matter. Miramont pays off the officers and insists that, as a penance, Brisac should abide by his offer to enlarge Andrew’s farm.
