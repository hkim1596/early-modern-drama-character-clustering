Haue you not seene your husband since you ariued? Surely he is wondrous kind, If I had a such Doue-house as Camillo's I would set fire on't, wer't but to destroy The Pole-cats that haunt to't,—my sweet cossin. That I did my pretty cossin, Marcello see it fitted. Sister away you must not yet bee seene. I wish it may. Be gone. Void the chamber, You are welcome, will you sit, I pray my Lord Bee you my Orator, my hearts too full, I'le second you anon. Do not feare it: I'le answere you in your owne hawking phrase, Some Eagles that should gaze vpon the Sunne Seldome soare high, but take their lustfull ease, Since they from dunghill birds their pery can ceaze, You know Uittoria, You shift your shirt there When you retire from Tennis. Her husband is Lord of a poore fortune Yet she wears cloth of Tissue, She is your Strumpet, Let's not talke on thunder, Thou hast a wife, our sister, would I had giuen Both her whitehands to death, bound and lockt fast In her last winding sheere, when I gaue thee But one. True, Thy ghostly father with al's absolution, Shall ne're do so by thee. I shall not need, lust carries her sharpe whippe At her owne girdle, looke to't for our anger Is making thunder-bolts. Wee'le end this with the Cannon. Better that Then change perfumes for plaisters, Willingly. What practising your pike so yong, faire cous. Yes cousin a yong Prince Of good descretion might. Pretty cousin, Ha, without thier ransome, How then will you reward your souldiers That tooke those prisoners for you. Why then the next yeare following You'le haue no men to go with you to warre. See a good habite makes a child a man, Whereas a bad one makes a man a beast: Come you and I are friends. Call Camillo hither You haue receiued the rumor, how Count Lodowicke Is turn'd a Pyrate. Sister, this is not well my Lord, why sister, She merits not this welcome. Are you foolish? Come dry your teares, is this a modest course. To better what is nought, to raile and weepe, Grow to a reconcilement, or by heauen, I'le nere more deale betweene you. Was your husband loud. Since we departed. What? turn'd fury? Now by my birth you are a foolish, mad, And iealous woman. Was this your circle of pure Vnicornes horne, You said should charme your Lord; now hornes vpon thee, For iealousy deserues them, keepe your vow, And take your chamber. To see her come, To my Lord Cardinall for a dispensation Of her rash vow will beget excellent laughter. Where's the commission? Giue me the Signet. Haue you any children. An old tale. Vppon a time Phoebus the God of light Or him wee call the Sunne would neede be married. The Gods gaue their consent, and Mercury Was sent to voice it to the generall world. But what a pitious cry their straight arose Amongst Smiths, & Felt-makers, Brewers & Cooks. Reapers and Butter-women, amongst Fishmongers And thousand other trades, which are annoyed By his excessiue heate; twas lamentable. They came to Iupiter all in a sweat And do forbid the banes; a great fat Cooke Was made their Speaker, who intreates of Ioue That Phoebus might bee guelded, for if now When there was but one, Sunne so many men, Weare like to perish by his violent heate. What should they do if hee were married And should be get more, and those children Make fier-workes like their father, so say I, Only I will apply it to your wife, Her issue should not prouidence preuent it Would make both nature, time, and man repent it. Farewell good Marcello. All the best fortunes of a Souldiers wish, Bring you a ship-board. Is't true? O 'twas well. We shall not want his absence past sixe daies, I faine would haue the Duke Brachiano run Into notorious scandale, for their's nought In such curst dotage, to repaire his name, Onely the deepe sence of some deathlesse shame: There's small pitty in't Like mistle-tow on seare Elmes spent by weather, Let him cleaue to her and both rot together. You haue dealt discreetly to obtaine the presence, Of all the graue Leiger Embassadours To heare Vittorias triall. A Chaire there for his Lordship. A Lawyer, that pleades against you. Why you vnderstand lattin. Signior. You need not stand on't much; pray change your language, Sir your paynes Shall bee well spared, and your deepe eloquence Bee worthely applauded amongst those Which vnderstand you. Sir, Put vp your papers in your fustian bag, Cry mercy Sir, tis buckeram, and accept My notion of your learn'd verbosity. Your vnhappy husband Is dead. And by a vaulting engine. what a prodigy wast, That from some two yardes height a slender man (more, Should breake his necke? You must haue patience. No cose; they sleepe. When God shall please. Good God let her sleepe euer. Thou didst loue her. O, all of my poore sister that remaines! Take him away for Gods sake. Beleeue mee I am nothing but her graue, And I shall keepe her blessed memorie, Longer then thousand Epitaphs. Pray let's see it. I do assure your Lordship, You are a worthy member of the State, And haue done infinite good in your discouery Of these offendors. Where's Count Lodowicke? I'le lay a thousand Duckets you here news Of a Pope presently, Hearke; sure he's elected, Behold! my Lord of Arragon appeares, On the Church battlements. Wel: what of her? Let the Matrona of the Conuertites Be apprehended: fled ô damnable! How fortunate are my wishes. Why? 'twas this I onely laboured. I did send the letter T'instruct him what to doe. Thy fame, fond Duke, I first haue poison'd; directed thee the way To marrie a whore; what can be worse? Diuert mee not. Most of his Court are of my faction, And some are of my councell. Noble freind, Our danger shall be 'like in this designe, Giue leaue, part of the glorie may bee mine. Why did the Duke of Florence with such care Labour your pardon? say. I shall perswade them To stay, my Lord. Set on there to the presence There's no way More speeding than this thought on. And yet mee thinkes that this reuenge is poore, Because it steales vpon him like a theif, To haue tane him by the Caske in a pitcht feild, Led him to Florence! T'is a ridiculous thing for a man to bee his owne Chronicle, I did neuer wash my mouth with mine owne praise for feare of getting a stincking breath. I shall neuer flatter him, I haue studied man to much to do that: What difference is betweene the Duke and I? no more than betweene two brickes; all made of one clay. Onely't may bee one is plac't on the top of a turret; the other in the bottom of a well by meere chance; if I were plac't as high as the Duke, I should sticke as fast; make as faire a shew; and beare out weather equally. How haue you thriu'd? That's the miserie of peace. Onely outsides are then respected: As shippes seeme verie great vpon the riuer, which shew verie little vpon the Seas: So some men i'th Court seeme Colossusses in a chamber, who if they came into the feild would appeare pittifull. Pigmies. Hee cals for his destruction. Noble youth, I pitty thy sad fate. Now to the barriers. This shall his passage to the blacke lake further, The last good deed hee did, he pardon'd murther. What did'st thou thinke of him; 'faith speake freely. O, speake well of the Duke. How is it with the Duke? See heere he comes. There's death in's face allready. I met euen now with the most pitious sight. Your reuerend mother Is growne a very old woman in two howers. I found them winding of Marcello's coarse; And there is such a solemne melodie 'Tweene dolefull songes, teares, and sad elegies: Such, as old grandames, watching by the dead, Were wont t'out-weare the nights with; that beleeue mee I had no eies to guide mee forth the roome, They were so ore-charg'd with water. 'Twere much vncharety in you: for your sight Will adde vnto their teares. Ladie, who's this? Farewell Lodouico. If thou do'st perish in this glorious act, Ile reare vnto thy memorie that fame Shall in the ashes keepe aliue thy name.

In this, Bracciano is abetted by Vittoria’s brother Flaminio, who persuades her husband Camillo that their sleeping apart will make her more amorous. Vittoria tells Bracciano of a dream which seems to suggest the murder of Camillo and the Duchess Isabella; but the liaison is forestalled by the intervention of Vittoria’s mother, Cornelia. Flaminio inveighs against Cornelia for compromising his chances of preferment. Isabella comes to Rome to effect a reconciliation with her husband. Cardinal Monticelso and the Great Duke of Florence also put pressure on him to fulfil his responsibilities, both marital and political. In a private interview with Isabella, Bracciano vows never again to sleep with her, but she tries to limit the political damage by making the same vow herself in public. Florence and Monticelso plan to tempt Bracciano into adultery by getting Camillo out of Rome on public business, but Bracciano plots murder: Isabella is killed when she kisses a poisoned picture of Bracciano, and Flaminio breaks Camillo’s neck. Vittoria is arrested and arraigned before an ecclesiastical court; unable to prove her complicity in Camillo’s murder, it convicts her of adultery and commits her to a house of convertites. Flaminio feigns madness to evade interrogation. Lodovico witnesses Isabella’s murder. He returns to Rome illegally to bring Florence the news, but his pardon is signed after the event. He and Flaminio meet and quarrel. Florence borrows from Monticelso a list of villains, in order to select an agent of vengeance. By writing a letter feigning love for Vittoria, he induces Bracciano to contrive her escape from the house of convertites, disguised as a page. Monticelso is elected Pope; his first act is to excommunicate Bracciano and Vittoria. Florence sends Lodovico to Padua to assassinate Bracciano, and by trickery makes it appear that Monticelso supports the conspiracy. Bracciano marries Vittoria and accepts into his court Mulinassar, a Moorish soldier, who is actually Florence in disguise.
