Suffer this, sir, & suffer all, a whoreson Gull, to—, But in the meane time she makes an Asse of some body. Flat-cap heark in your eare sir, yare a flat foole, an Asse, a gull, & Ile thrum you: do you see this cambrick sir? I laugh? not I sir. Yes forsooth, his backe is but new turnd. I thanke you mistris, my back's broad enough, now my Maisters gown's on. Twere a good Comedy of errors that yfaith. That can I, but I will not. Troth Mistris nothing: not so much as a Bee, he did not hum: not so much as a bawd he did not hem: not so much as a Cuekold he did not ha: neither hum, hem, nor ha, onely starde me in the face, past along, and made hast in, as if my lookes had workt with him, to giue him a stoole. Nay let me alone to play my maisters prize, as long as my Mistrisse warrants me: Ime sure I haue his best clothes on, and I scorne to giue place to any that is inferiour in appa rell to me, thats an Axiom, a principle, & is obseru'd as much as the fashion; let that perswade you then, that lie shoulder with him for the vpper hand in the shop, as long as this chaine will mainteine it. Vmh, vmh, hum. Step sirra and fetch it, hum, hum hum. Sfoot clubs, clubs, prentices, downe with em, ah you roagues, strike a Citizen in's shop. Your head bleeds sir, cry clubs. Yes sir, weele vse em like honest men. A rescue Prentises, my maister's catch-pold. But are you mad indeed Maister? Come weele see whether he goes, if the maister be mad, we are his seruants, and must follow his steps, weele be mad caps too; Farewell mistrisse, you shall haue vs all in Bedlam. Exeunt. Here comes one of the butter flies, aske him. O yes, of all countries some, but especially mad greekes they swarme: troth mistris, the world is altered with you, you had not wont to stand thus with a paper humblie com plaining: but you're well enough seru'd: prouander prickt you, as it does many of our Cittie-wiues besides. Why youre best go see. Afraid! you had more need be ashamd: he may ra ther be afraid of you. Nay I know not that, but he talkes like a Iustice of peace, of a thousand matters and to no purpose. I, ide faine see a woman weepe out her eyes; thats as true, as to say, a mans cloake burnes; when it hangs in the water: I know youle weepe mistrisse, but what saies the pain ted cloth. Trust not a woman when she cryes, For sheele pump water from her eyes, With a wet finger, and in faster showers, Then Aprill when he raines downe flowers. I but mistrisse how easily will they find a cause, and as one of our Cheese-trenchers sayes very learnedly: As out of Wormwood Bees suck Hony, As from poore clients Lawyers firke mony, As Parsley from a roasted cunny. So tho the day be nere so sunny, If wiues will haue it raine, downe then it driues, The calmest husbands make the stormest wiues, Why thats well done, good mistris throw aside this fashion of your humor, be not so phantasticall in wearing it, storme no more, long no more.—This longing has made you come short of many a good thing that you might haue had from my Maister: Here comes the Duke. And please your grace, hees not starke mad, but one ly talkes like a young Gentleman, somewhat phantastically, thats all: theres a thousand about your court, citty and countrie madder then he. Heres a warrant ready drawne my Lord. Here my Lord. Whoop, come mistris the Duke's mad too. Exeunt. Come mistris we are in Bedlam now, mas and see, we come in pudding-time, for heres the Duke.

Plot — 1 The Honest Whore (1 The Converted Courtesan) (The Patient Man and the Honest Whore)
Count Hippolito attempts to make a scene at the funeral of the Duke’s daughter, Infelice, but is restrained. In fact, Infelice is not dead: the Duke administered a sleeping draught to prevent her from marrying Hippolito. When she wakes, he tells her Hippolito is dead and sends her away to Bergamo, then accepts Dr Benedict’s offer to poison Hippolito. Bellafront the courtesan falls in love with Hippolito when his friend and her client Matheo brings him to meet her. He mistrusts her protestation that, for him, she will become honest, and speaks harshly against whores. But she fulfils her promise: dismisses her pander Roger (who quickly finds alternative employment) and sends away her former clients, including Matheo. On a Monday, she goes to visit Hippolito, disguised as a page; she finds him in solemn meditation (since Infelice supposedly died on a Monday), and he rejects her again. Dr Benedict tells the Duke that he has been poisoned, and is banished for it; but in fact he has done no such thing, and tells Hippolito that Infelice is not only alive, but waiting at the madhouse to marry him. Meanwhile, Fustigo returns to Milan to find that his sister has married Candido, a man apparently incapable of anger. This is demonstrated in a wager between three gentlemen who try to make him lose his temper: first Castruccio poses as a customer and makes unreasonable demands, which Candido meets even though it means spoiling a piece of cloth; then Fluello steals a beaker from the household, for which Candido has them arrested, but then genially invites them to dinner. Viola also wants to make her husband angry, and has Fustigo pose as a swaggerer and attempt to seduce her. When he tries, however, Candido’s apprentices take it upon themselves to beat him while their master is away, and Candido’s equanimity remains intact.
