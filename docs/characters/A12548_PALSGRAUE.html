Full speech

Sicke at this instant now to be infirme, When the English King hath his kinde Letter sent: For mee to place this honourable prince, The Duke of Sauoy in the Empires rule: That Nation my great Grandfather did loue. And since I came to vnderstand their valour, I held them the Prime Souldiers of the world: And thinke no Martiall Tutor fittes a prince, But hee that is a true borne Englishman. Ill comes this Letter, and your Grace at once, A worser time then this you could not choose. Though I am chiefe Elector of the seuen, And a meere Caesar now the Chayre is voyde: Sicknesse hath weakened all my powers so much, I shalbe slighted as a worthlesse thing. What Spaynes King? Oh that I were as I was wont to bee, Before this dangerous sicknesse was my Foe, No Christian King that came to mee for ayde But hee should speed. But now recouered. And hither come to beate you out of Spaine. I hold my thunder here, & my right arme Has vigor in it, when you feele my blowes To giue you cause to call them Thunderboltes, If there be any in this Martiall Troope That with a Soldiours face, has a bold heart, And dares auerre that this religious prince Is not the lawfull and true King of Spaine, I will make good his Title by the sword, And against that prowde combattant oppose My selfe as challenger to fight for him. I lost him in the preasse, his snowy steed Was crimsond ouer with the blood of men, And Lyon-like he fought with all his strength, But since the Emperour is my prisoner, I shall die lesse regard the Dukes escape. When fortune is dispos'd? 〈…〉 a man. Valour and foresight are of no effect: Releast by Saxon, and his Keepers liue; You are not as I thought you, valiant men; But worse then these that runne away for feare. He should haue made passage through my heart, Ere scapt from me by Saxon or his plots: But now it is too late to follow him; And the whole Field is made a liquid Sea, Sinke may they both into the crimson fenne. But why should they sinke, you deserue it best: From hencefoorth Ile nere take a Spaniards part, Except he had a farre more valiant heart, At whose hart aime you now, that you are come? To iustifie an Embassage against mee? Cannot. Since mildnesse cannot temper your stern wrath, But that your splene must vomit vpon mee. Ile teach you Syr to haue your tongue lockt vp. By taking off your locke. Pulls off his locke. Now by the honour of my Fathers house, Saxon Ile meete thee in the Realme of France, In the Kings Court, or place where thou wert borne, So I may haue good Hostage, and faire play. This Saxons braue, giues courage to vs all. But Ile requite it with a Germaine braule. Tis such a Dreame youl neuer wake ̄ from? To talke of this strange admiration, Which like the night houers on euery eye: Know that I haue deluded you with hopes Vaine, like those villaines, which my sword did kill, And by a Letter to the King deliuered, Sent by your Queene, to be reueng'd for lust, I causde his Maiestie to enter thus. What can you sweare? Away with them. Choose your owne Weapons, and Ile meete you streight. Sound trumpets, & the destinies guide all. Plot summary

Plot — The Hector of Germany, or The Palsgrave, Prime Elector
. Defeated in his attempt to seize power in Spain, Henry the Bastard attempts to get himself elected Holy Roman Emperor; he secures the support of three of the seven Electors, promising that Saxony will be the power behind his throne. But the other Electors, including the sickly Palsgrave, prefer the Duke of Savoy’s candidature, so the Bastard uses force: he defeats the opposing Electors in battle, imprisons two of them, and has himself crowned, then breaks his word and excludes Saxony from power. News of the ===== PAGE 311 ===== 1613 usurpation cures the Palsgrave, who prepares to mobilize an army. Spain is the theatre of war for their first confrontation. The Palsgrave is victorious, and the Bastard is captured, but rescued by Saxony. The warring potentates seek alliances against one another: the Palsgrave goes to England, the Bastard to France. The Palsgrave competes in an English tournament and is installed in the Order of the Garter. King John of France is reluctant to fight the English again (they have too good a track record of victory in France), and hopes to use diplomacy to secure Edward III’s neutrality. Refused the chance to conduct the mission, Saxony goes to England in disguise, but is identified by the Palsgrave, who dishonours him by pulling off a lock of his hair. Diplomacy fails, and Edward and the Palsgrave invade France, dispatching troops to free the imprisoned Electors. The Palsgrave defeats and kills Saxony in single combat. King John withdraws his support from the Bastard, and Savoy is formally crowned Emperor. In the sub-plot, Lord Fitzwaters is betrothed to Floramell, but discovers that she loves his son. He witnesses a secret assignation between the lovers in the garden of Lord Clinton, Floramell’s father. Clinton orders Young Fitzwaters off his land; Lord Fitzwaters goes further and banishes him from England. Floramell goes with him, leaving in her place a cross-dressed page, whom Lord Fitzwaters ‘marries’. The lovers are separated in a shipwreck: Floramell is washed up on the French coast and becomes a maid of honour at court; Young Fitzwaters is marooned on a barren rock and subsists on a fish diet until he is rescued by Saxony. King John is attracted to Floramell and offers to make way for her by having the Queen murdered. Floramell will not consent to adultery or murder, but agrees to give him a single kiss.
