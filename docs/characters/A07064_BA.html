Marry forsooth, Ile carrie for my deuice, my grand fathers great stone-hors, flinging vp his head, & ierking out his left legge. The word; Wighy Purt. As I am a true knight, wil't not bee most retort and obtuse, ha? Verely, sir Gefferey had a monstrous strange dream the last night. For mee thought I dreamt I was asleepe, and me thought the ground yaun'd and belkt vp the abhominable ghost of a misshapen Simile, with two vgly Pages; the one called master, euen as going be fore; and the other Mounser, euen so following after; whil'st Signior Simile stalked most prodigiously in the midst. At which I bewrayed the fearefulnesse of my nature: and being readie to forsake the fortresse of my wit, start vp, called for a cleane shirt, eate a messe of broth, and with that I awakt. When my beard is on, most noble prince, when my beard is on. In truth, one tolde me that my wit was balde, & that a Meremaide was halfe fish, and halfe fish: and therefore to speake wisely, like one of your counsell, as indeede it hath pleased you to make me, not onely being a foole, of your counsell, but also to make me of your counsell, being a foole; If my wit be bald, and a Mermaid be halfe fish and halfe cunger, then I must be forced to conclude the tyring man hath not glewd on my beard halfe fast, enough. Gods bores, it wil not stick to fal off. O Lord Duke, I would be sorie of that. Many men can vtter that which, no man, but themselues can conceiue: but I thanke a good wit, I haue the gift to speake that which neither any man els, nor my selfe vnderstands. Wise? O no. I haue a little naturall discretion, or so: but for wise, I am somewhat prudent: but for wise, ô Lord ▪ I will make him come, most retort and obtuse, to you presently. I thinke, sir Ieffrey talks like a counseller. Go to, gods neaks, I thinke I tickle it. Patheticall, and vnuulgar; words of worth, ex cellent words. In sooth, Madam, I haue taken a murre, which makes my nose run most patheticallie, and vn vulgarlie. Haue you anie Tobacco? Instantlie, most vnvulgarlie, at your seruice. Truelie, here's the most patheticall rozzen. Vmh. Why, looke you Ladie, I was wade a knight on ly for my voice; & a counseller, only for my wit. Stand backe there, foole; I do hate a foole most most pathetically. O these that haue no sappe of of re tort and obtuse wit in them: faugh. I cannot disgest thee, the vnuulgar foole. Goe foole. Goe to, tis iust, the man hath wrong: go to. Why then, ô wight, alas poore knight. O, welladay, sir Gefferey. Let Poets roare, And all deplore: for now I bid you god night. A poor knight, god wot: the nose of thy knight hoode is bitten off with cold. O poore sir Geffrey, cold, cold. Nay, and you talke of reuenge, my stomack's vp, For I am most tyrannically hungry. A beuer? I haue a headpeece, a skull, a braine of proofe, I warrant yee.

Plot summary

Plot — 185
Simplicity, a new arrival in London, is horrified to find the city full of heresy, where ‘old custom’, the traditional religious observances of earlier generations, is held in contempt. Knowledge offers to explain the truth, and shows how the mass has no basis in scripture and certainly does not involve transubstantiation. He teaches Simplicity the doctrinal basis of Protestant worship: pray and fast simply, and shun popish idolatry. Simplicity is duly grateful.
