Sir, you see I have made a speedy choyse, And as swift a marriage; be it as it will, I like the man, if his qualities afflict me, I shall be happy in't. A good bargaine, I hope. Roger, Where's your Master? Not your Master, Sir. Well Sir, I shall be blest in't: But where is he? And where's that? Why Sir, all is his, and at his Dispose; who shall dare to twhart him? How now, Sweete-heart? what hast thou there? Ha, ha; prethee doe not mistake thy selfe, Nor my true purpose; I did not wed to thrall, Or binde thy large expence, but rather to adde A plenty to that liberty; I thought by this, Thou would'st have stuft thy pockets full of Gold, And throwne it at a hazard; made Ducks and Drakes, And baited fishes with thy silver flyes; Lost, and fetcht more: why this had bin my joy; Perhaps at length thou would'st have wast'd My store; why, this had bin a blessing to Good for me. I'm new to seeke for crosses, the hopes I meant Turne to despaire, and smoother in content. Let him be largely texted in your love, That all the Citty may reade it fairely; You cannot remember me, and him forget. We were alike to you in poverty. Call it not borrowed, Sir, 'tis all your owne; Here 'fore this reverent man I make it knowne, Thou art our childe as free by adoption, As deriv'd from us by conception, birth, and Propinquitie; Inheritour to our full substance. Will the Tide never turne? Was ever woman Thus burden'd with unhappy happinesse? Did I from Ryot take him, to waste my goods, And he strives to augment it? I did mistake him. I thanke yee Sir. 'Las, they are poore and leane, and being so; 〈◊〉 them not till they are fatter. The match is faire; and were that knot once tyed, 〈◊〉 send some Augels to attend the bride. I grieve for my poore Gossip, his good wife, She never met good fortune all her life, And this will breake her heart-strings: In good sooth I'l goe and comfort her. Sister, there's no way to make sorrow light But in the noble bearing; be content; Blowes given from heaven are our due punishment; All ship wracks are no drownings, you see buildings Made fairer from their Ruines; he that I married, The brother to your husband, lay, you know, On the same bed of misery, yet now Hee's ranckt with the best Citizens. Come, I will worke my husband; stay this griefe. The longest sorrow findes at last reliefe. Now Sir, your businesse. What are they, know their names. O, they are my old debtors, Speedwell and Lambskin Goe call them in, and my gentle sister Comfort your selfe and my imprison'd brother, To whom commend me give to him this gold, What good I can, I'l doe for him be bold. Now good Sir Godfry and Master Innocent. I know it well, Sir. I am content to doe so: you shall finde Nor me nor my husband carry a griping minde. Now Cuz, where's your Vncle. O feare not, I'l be your surety Sir. Vpon my trust none here shall doe you wrong; What is his businesse with the Alderman? Saw you your sad father? Not yet Sir, but here they come like honest Gentlemen To take some order for it: good Sweetheart Shall it be put to me. I thanke ye Sir; then tell me Gentlemen, What present money can you pay? We'l sheare no sheepe so close. All your two thousand pound Gentlemen we quit, For your two hundred: goe pay the money to My Cuz, and receive your two bonds canceld: Say Sir, are ye content. I must come a wooing to you Sir. For your brother; Oh 'tis unmeet For soules fram'd by one square to grow uneven, 'Tis like a warre 'mongst the great lights of heaven, One cannot lose his beauty, but the other Suffers ecclipse; so brother against brother. Would you kill a man lying at your feet: Doe good for ill. The easier then tane off. Such wheeles in childrens bosome seldome runne. Troth would it might: I gladly shall agree. To any act that tends to charity. I thanke you; how fare you brother? Had you an ague then? O, 'tis well Cozin, what make you heare I pray? Plot summary

Plot — A New Wonder, A Woman Never Vexed
Old Forster has recently made his fortune by marrying a rich widow, but they are both dissatisfied with the financial irresponsibility of his brother and son, whom ===== PAGE 17 ===== 1624 they are on the point of disowning. Robert, the son, has purloined some of his stepmother’s money to buy his uncle, Stephen Forster, out of debtors’ prison; he also gives Stephen money to buy new clothes. Stephen uses the money for gambling, and is cheated. Robert is disappointed to find him in the gaming-house, and he himself likewise gets into trouble with his stepmother for going there, even though he only went in to look for Stephen. Mistress Forster has an uncommonly even- tempered friend, another rich widow, who enjoys miraculous good fortune. When she loses her wedding ring in the river, it turns up inside a salmon bought by her maid. She meets Stephen at the gaming-house and likes him. She suggests that he should marry for money, and offers herself. He accepts, and they perform a hand-fasting. Jane is being pursued by two undesirable suitors, an old knight and a young fool. Her father, Alderman Brune, suggests Robert Forster as a third option, if he can be reconciled with his father. Brune is Old Forster’s partner in a mercantile venture, but accepts Forster’s offer to buy him out; he takes payment in cloth rather than cash, which clears Forster’s warehouses for the new merchandise due in any day. However, Forster continues to look askance at Robert. News arrives that the ships have sunk in the Thames: Forster is bankrupt, and voluntarily commits himself to prison in order to avoid his creditors. Stephen takes charge of his new wife’s property, and proves, against expectations, to be provident rather than spendthrift. He asks Robert to help him manage the estate, and adopts him as his heir, insisting only that he does nothing to help his father. However, Stephen himself pays the prison-keeper to ensure that his brother remains comfortable. When Jane’s two other suitors complain of the favour shown to Robert, Stephen produces evidence that they are in debt to the widow’s first husband (and therefore now to him), and the threat of arrest gets rid of them. The widow generously accepts their payment of £200 to discharge a debt of ten times that amount. Unable to resist his sense of filial obligation, Robert purloins the money and gives it to his father. Stephen pretends to be angry, but secretly pays off his brother’s creditors and thereby secures his release.…
