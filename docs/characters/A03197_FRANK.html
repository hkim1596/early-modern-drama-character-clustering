Stay there my horse: Whence comes this eccho of extreamitie? What doe I heare, a virgin call for helpe? Hands off dam'nd vilaines, or by heauen I sweare, Ile send you all to hell. Fight and driue them away. Now you distressed obiects, do you tell Vpon what mount of woe your sorrowes dwell. How, loue! why whats that loue? And be ore-come by him! plagude by him! Driuen into dumps by him! put downe by a boy! Master'd by loue! O, I am mad for anger: By a Boy! is there no rosemary and bayes in England To whip the Ape? by a boy! Whats heere, Phillis and loue, and loue and Phillis : I haue seene Phillis, and haue heard of loue; I will see Phillis, and will heare of loue: But neither Phillis, nor the power of loue, Shall make me bond-slaue to a womans becke. Who's here, my second brother male-content? Ile stand aside and note his passions. More louers yet! what the deuill is this loue? What brother Anthony, at praiers so hard? Tell me what saint it is thou inuocates? Is it a male, or female? howsoeuer, God blesse thee brother, th'art in a good mind, But now I remember me, thy saint is blind. I brother, blind, I heard thee talke of loue, And loue is blind they say. A voluntary motion of delight, Touching the superficies of the soule; A substance lesse diuine then is the soule, Yet more then any other power in man Is that which loues, yet neither is enforc'd, Nor doth enforce the heart of man to loue: Which motion as it vnbeseemes a man, So by the soule and reason which adorne, The life of man it is extinguished, Euen at his pleasure that it doth possesse. Tis true, fore God it is, and now me thinks, My heart begins to pittie hearts in loue: Say once more, Anthony, tell me thy griefes, Let me haue feeling of my passion, Possesse me deeply of thy melting state, And thou shalt see. No by my troth, if euery tale of loue, Or loue it selfe, or foole-bewitching beautie, Make me crosse-arme my selfe; studdy ay-mees; Defie my hat-band; tread beneath my feete Shoo-strings and garters; practise in my glasse Distressed lookes, and dry my liuer vp, With sighes enough to win an argosie. If euer I turne thus fantastical, Loue plague me, neuer pittie me at all. What? Phillis, the faire maid of the Exchange? Is she god Cupids iudge ouer mens hearts? Brother, ile haue one venny with her tongue, To breathe my wit, and iest at passion: By your leaue Mistresse Flower. I prethee tell me Phillis, I heare say, Thou keepst loue captiue in thy maiden thoughts. But shall I know it? May not your friend command as great a matter? Why, I hope you know me. Yo gods of Loue that sits aboue, and pitty Louers paine, Looke from your thrones vpon the mones, that I do now sustaine. Was euerman thus tormented with loue? Ye little birds that sit and sing Amidst the shadie valleys, And see how Phillis sweetly walkes Within her Garden alleyes; Goe pretie birds about her bowre, Sing pretie birdes she may not lowre, Ah me, me thinkes I see her frowne Ye pretie wantons warble. Go tell her through your chirping billes, As you by me are bidden, To her is onely knowne my loue, Which from the world is hidden: Goe prety birds and tell her so, See that your notes straine not too low, For still me thinke I see her frowne, Ye pretie wantons warble. Go tune your voices harmonie, And sing I am her Louer; Straine lowde and sweet, that euery note, With sweet content may moue her: And she that hath the sweetest voyce, Tell her I will not change my choice, Yet still me thinkes I see her frowne, Ye pretie wantons warble. O flie, make haste, see, see she falles Into a prety slumber, Sing rownd about her rosie bed That waking she may wonder, Say to her, tis her louer true, That sendeth loue to you to you: And when you heare her kinde reply, Returne with pleasant warblings. Auaunt delusion, thoughts cannot winne my loue; Loue, though diuine, cannot diuine my thoughts: Why to the ayre then do I idle heere Such heedles words farre off, and ne're the neere; Hie thee yong Franke, to her that keepes thy heart, There let sweet words they sweeter thoughts impart. But stay; here comes my mellancholly brothers both, Ile step aside, and heare their conference. Exit aside. Brother, say on. Sblood, and I thought you did not, Ide retire: Brother you know, I loue you as my life. By the red lippe of that daintie saint, Ile aid thee all I may. Your hand then brother for to put it on, So now tis well, come brother, whats my taske? Now fortune be my guide, this is the shop; And in good time the Cripple is at worke: God speede you sir. I would entreat you reade a letter for me. I know you doe Cripple, better then you thinke. Sir, a Gentleman of good learning, and my friend; To say the truth, t'was written for my selfe, Being some-what ouertaken with fond loue, As many men be sir. Trustie Iohn men call me sir. Nay then I see I must disclose my selfe: Sir, might I build vpon your secrecie, I would disclose a secret of import. Then Cripple know, I am not what I seem, But tooke this habite to deceiue my friend: My friend indeed, but yet my cruell foe; Foe to my good, my friend in outward show: I am no Porter, as I seeme to bee, But yonger brother to that Anthony ; And to be briefe, I am in loue with Phillis, Which my two elder brothers do affect: The one of them seekes to defeat the other: Now if that I, being their yonger brother Could gull them both, by getting of the wench, I would requite it with loues recompence. Cripple, thou once didst promise me thy loue, When I did rescue thee on Mile-end Greene, Now is the time, now let me haue thy ayd, To gull my brothers of that beauteous mayd. Saist thou me so friend, for that very word My life is thine, command my hand and sword. Ile arme my selfe with floutes and crueltie. Will you so Phillis, what a state am I in? Why I of all am furthest from her loue: Sblood, if I now should take conceit at this, Fall sicke with loue indeed, were not my state Most lamentable? I by this hand were it: Well heart, if thou wilt yeeld, looke to thy selfe, Thou wilt be tortur'd, well what remedie. Here comes my brother Anthonie, I am for him. I too late, to my griefe, Spoke with her, sir, Yfaith I thinke I haue; Heer's a letter for you, and by that You shall be iudge if I did speake with her; Now Cripple, shall we prooue your learned wit? You haue your answer, and a kind one too; Cripple ile make thee chrutches of pure siluer For this deuise, thou hast a golden wit; Now if my brother Ferdinand were heere To reade his absolution, heere he comes. Brother. I haue done both, and more then that, behold Heere is an answere to your letter brother. Farewell poore tortur'd heart; was euer knowne Two louing brothers in such miserie? Let me consider of my owne estate: What profit do I reape by this delusion? Why none; I am as farre from Phillis heart As when she first did wound me with her eyes: Cripple, to thee I come, tis thou must be My counsellor in this extreamitie. Exit. For which my loue to thee shall neuer end. Yea Mistresse Flower, it is finished. Faire Mistresse Phillis, such wanton toyes as these, Are for yong Nouices that will soone bee pleasde, The carefull thoughts that hammer in my braine, Bid me abandon wanton loue; tis vaine. Is my vngarnishd, darke, and obscure Cell, A mansion fit for all-commanding loue? No, if thou wilt sport with loue, And dally with that wanton amorous boy; Hie thee vnto the odoriferous groues. Forbeare, forbeare, admit that I should yeild: Thinke you, your father would applaud your choice. Now to employ the vertue of my shape: Faire mistresse, If heretofore I haue remorselesse beene, And not esteemd your vndeserued loue, Whereby in the glasse of your affection I see my great vnkindnesse; forgine what's past, And here I proffer all the humble seruice Your hie prizde loue doth merrit at my hands, Which I confesse is more then I vnable Can gratefie: therefore command my toyle, My trauell, yea, my life to pleasure you. That foolish boy may chance prooue to be witty: What, and the elder brothers fooles? Oh ti's pittie! What are you cursing too? then we catch no fish: Comes there any more, heeres two Snights to a dish. Well, to her both, both doe the best you can; I feare yong Franke will prooue the happier man. What, you repulst too? Phillis is too vnkinde. I take thy offering, And for the gift you gaue to me, take this, And let vs seale affection with a kisse. Now gentlemen, please you draw neere and listen to the Cripple. Know you that letter? sir, what say you to this? Sirs, a porter euen of late left them with me, To be deliuered to this Gentlewoman. So, vomit forth the rhume of all your spight, These threats of yours procure me more delight. I will assaile with expedition. Well, I will goe, but not in this disguise; Arme thee with policie Franke, Franke must be wise: Now would the substance of this borrowed shape Were here in presence, and see where he comes, Poore in the well framd limbes of nature, but Rich in kindnesse beyond comparison. Welcome deere friend, the kindest soule aliue, Here I resigne thy habite backe againe, Whereby I prooue the happiest man that breaths. Harke, I will tell thee all: Whisper in priuate. I thanke you, when I leaue to loue my wife, Heauen hasten death and take away my life. Were he the deuill, thou shalt not budge a foote. Feare not deare heart. Deere heart, reuoke these words, Here are no spirites nor deformities, I am a counterfeit Cripple now no more, But yong Franke Golaing. as I was before: Amaze not loue, nor seeme not discontent, Nor thee nor him shall euer this repent. But I did for you both; I was your Scribe, The whilst you went to see your house a fire: And you (as I remember) I did send, To see your sister drown'd at London-Bridge. Why brothers, shee's mine by her fathers gift. Thy life, thy loue, thy selfe, and all for me, For if I want but one, I then want thee. I aske but all for I deserue no more. By heauen, I aske no more: Brothers, haue done, and Dad to end all strife, Come take her hand and giue her for my wife.

Plot summary

Plot — The Fair Maid of the Exchange
Phyllis and Ursula are assaulted by two ruffians as they go to deliver wares to a customer at night. A cripple rescues them, and a second assault is foiled by the intervention of Frank Golding; the cripple promises to help Frank if ever he can. Frank's two brothers are both in love with Phyllis, and, though he scorns amorous passions, Frank has fallen for her too. Phyllis, however, loves the cripple, and woos him by giving him a handkerchief to embroider “for a friend’. Meanwhile, Bobbington poses as a sailor going on a voyage, and borrows money from Phyllis’s father, Flower, leaving a diamond ring with him as security. Ferdinand and Anthony overhear one another's plans to woo Phyllis, but neither knows that Frank is also a rival. Ferdinand writes to her; Anthony plans to intercept the letter and substitute his own, but Ferdinand disguises Frank as a porter and gets him hired as Anthony's messenger. Planning to double-cross them both, Frank enlists the cripple's help. The cripple supplies him with two rejection letters to take back to his brothers, as from Phyllis. They each enlist the help of one of Phyllis’s parents, ===== PAGE 378 ===== who then quarrel over which brother shall marry their daughter. _ Knowing Phyllis loves him but thinking himself unworthy, the cripple lends Frank his clothes. Frank courts Phyllis in disguise, and she gives him her ring; then. he brings the parents forged letters from his brothers withdrawing their suits. Each parent decides to:spite the other by offering Phyllis to Frank instead of the other’s favoured son-in-law. The thwarted brothers realize what has happened, but Phyllis asks to be allowed a say in her.own marriage — and chooses Frank. As the play ends, Flower is arrested for receiving stolen property — Bobbington’s ring — and sent for trial. In the sub-plot, Mall Berry is in love with Barnard, but suffers the unwanted attentions of Bowdler. Barnard is surety for a £100 debt to Mall’s father; the original borrower is dead and the debt about to fall due, but Berry refuses to allow Barnard any more time to find the money. Bowdler persists in his pursuit of Mall, using lines he remembers from Venus and Adonis. Berry engages sergeants to arrest Barnard. Mall agrees to marry Bowdler, but the cripple points out that, through a party wall, he has overheard her talking about Barnard in her sleep: he is the man she truly loves.
