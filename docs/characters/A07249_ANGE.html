Full speech

O! now your hearts make ladders of your eyes In shew to climbe to heauen, when your deuotion Walkes vpon crutches: where did you waste your time When the religious man was on his knees, Speaking the heauenly languague. Haue you the baskets emptied which your Lady Sent from the charitable hands, to women That dwell vpon her pitty? And went your money to the prisoners. Auant you theeues, and hollow hypocrites. Your hearts to me lie open like blacke bookes, And there I reade your doings. Too well on your sides, you are hid in gold Ore head and eares. Know I am the same, And still the seruant to your pictie, Your zealous prayers and pious deeds first wonne me (But 'twas by his command to whom you sent 'em) To guide your steps. I tride your charitie, When in a beggers shape you tooke me vp And clothd my naked limbes, and after fed (As you beleeu'd) my famishd mouth. Learne all By your example to looke on the poore With gentle eyes, for in such habits often Angels desire an Almes. I neuer left you, Nor will I now, for I am sent to carry Your pure and innocent soule to ioyes eternall, Your martyrdome once sufferd, and before it Aske any thing from me, and rest assur'd You shall obtaine it I will performe it. And in that instant when the sword seta free Your happy soule his shall haue libertie. Is there ought else? Willingly deere Mistresse.

Plot summary

Plot — The Virgin Martyr
An imperial edict orders the extirpation of Christianity from the Roman Empire, and the re-establishment of the worship of the pagan gods. In Caesarea, Theophilus works to that end, abetted by Harpax; his many successes so far include the forced apostasy of his own daughters, once Christians but now votaresses of Jupiter. Diocletian gives his daughter Artemia the right to choose her own husband, and she selects the unwilling Antoninus, who loves the Christian Dorothea and asks for time to consider. Artemia remains in Caesarea as her father’s deputy. Harpax tells Theophilus that Antoninus is having an illicit relationship with Dorothea, and Theophilus suborns her servants Spungius and Hircius to smuggle him into her home so that he can witness a meeting between them. Dorothea and Antoninus are arrested. Theophilus sets his daughters, Dorothea’s childhood friends, to convert her to paganism; but instead Dorothea persuades them to revert to Christianity. When they demonstrate their new faith by spitting at an idol of Jupiter, their outraged father kills them. Antoninus is taken ill. His father brings Dorothea to his sick-bed in the hope that he will rape her and so be cured. He refuses, and Dorothea is taken for torture and execution. Destitute after losing the gold Theophilus gave them, Spungius and Hircius are engaged by Harpax to be the torturers. They flog her, but miraculously she feels nothing; they are wrongly suspected of botching the job out of regard for their former mistress, and hanged. After praying that Antoninus’ love for her be transformed into love for God, Dorothea is beheaded. Antoninus drops dead at the sight. Artemia gets her second choice of husband: the co-Emperor Maximinus. Dorothea’s faithful page Angelo, whom she took in as a beggar, turns out to be an angel, and brings Theophilus a gift from her: a basket of fruit and flowers to persuade him of the joys of the Christian heaven. Harpax comes in his true form, a devil, to claim Theophilus, but is frightened off by a floral cross in the basket. Theophilus is converted, orders the release of all Christian prisoners, and gives them ships in which to flee persecution. He is arrested by the authorities and asks to die on the worst of his extensive collection of torture instruments. He is racked but will not recant; Harpax is balked of his aim, and Dorothea and the other martyrs receive him into heaven.
