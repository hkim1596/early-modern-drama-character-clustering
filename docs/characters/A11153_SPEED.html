Sir, let me advise you: I see you want experience, Meddle no further in this case, 'twilbe the More credit for your observation; for I finde by my Experience, you are but shallow. Sweete Virgin, to be prolix and ted ious, fits 〈◊〉 Experience; short words and large deedes are Best pleasing to women. My name is Speedwell, by my fathers Coppy. Yes, sweete Feminine, I have serv'd for it too: For I have found my nativity suited to my name, As my name is Speedwel, so have I sped Well in divers actions. Your observation is corrupt, Sir; Let me shew min Owne Tale; I say, sweete Beauty, my name is Speedwell, my God-father by his bounty being an Old Soldiour, and having serv'd in the wars as far as Bulloyne, therefore cal'd my name Godfry; a Title of large renowne; my wealth and wit has Added to those, the paraphrase of Kinghthood; So that my name in the full longitude is cal'd Sir Godfry Speedwell, a name of good experience. You say well, sweet Modesty, a good imagination is good, And shewes your good experience. Your experience may see his course education; but to The purpose, sweet Female; I doe love that Face of yours. Sir you doe interrupt, and thwart my love. Mistake me not kinde Heart. In loving your face, I love all the rest of your body As you shall finde by experience. Let your experience make a tryall. Requite one, sweete Chastity, and let it be Sir Godfry, with the correspondencie of your Love to him; I will maintaine you like a Lady, And it is brave, as I know by experience. How dare you maintaine that, Sir? I dare crosse it with my sword Sir. You have confinde him prisoner forever, I hope your experience sees hee's a harmelesse thing. Where she is leader, there will be followers. Withall my heart, sweet Virgin; Ah, ha; this place Promises well in the eyes of experience; Master Innocent, come you behinde. When I looke backe, Sir, I see your nose behinde. Sweet Lady, follow experience, Experience must beare with folly; Thou art all inno cent, And thy name is Lambskin; grave Sapience guides me, And I care not a pin for thy squibs, and thy Crackers, My old dry wood shall make a lusty bonefire, when Thy greene Chips shall lye hissing in the Chimney- Corner. Remember Mistris, I can make you a Lady by Mine owne experience. A match betweene us By this hand.— An adventurer still Sir, to this new found land. I doubt Sir, he will play the merchant with us. And heare yee, Sir, let him seeke out his equalls; For some of us are in danger to make her A Lady shortly: I know what I speake; what I speake, I'l doe; yet I'l doe nothing, but What comes from grave experience. Ha? doe you know us, Sir? You grow too bold; My experience now hath found you; You were once a tatter'd fellow, your name is Foster; have you such gold to give? O let him, I have a protection, Sir. A Serjeant? Nay, then experience must worke, Legs be strong and bold; when Serjeants waite At feasts, the cheere's but cold. I'l shift for one. We come about the old businesse, the sicknesse of the purse Lady Fayre Lady, we are your debtors, and owe you mony. Experience tels us that our bonds are forfeit, For which your husband threatned to arrest us; my Shoulders love no such clappings, I love Tobacco, but would be loth to drinke in Woodstreet. Plot — A New Wonder, A Woman Never Vexed
Old Forster has recently made his fortune by marrying a rich widow, but they are both dissatisfied with the financial irresponsibility of his brother and son, whom ===== PAGE 17 ===== 1624 they are on the point of disowning. Robert, the son, has purloined some of his stepmother’s money to buy his uncle, Stephen Forster, out of debtors’ prison; he also gives Stephen money to buy new clothes. Stephen uses the money for gambling, and is cheated. Robert is disappointed to find him in the gaming-house, and he himself likewise gets into trouble with his stepmother for going there, even though he only went in to look for Stephen. Mistress Forster has an uncommonly even- tempered friend, another rich widow, who enjoys miraculous good fortune. When she loses her wedding ring in the river, it turns up inside a salmon bought by her maid. She meets Stephen at the gaming-house and likes him. She suggests that he should marry for money, and offers herself. He accepts, and they perform a hand-fasting. Jane is being pursued by two undesirable suitors, an old knight and a young fool. Her father, Alderman Brune, suggests Robert Forster as a third option, if he can be reconciled with his father. Brune is Old Forster’s partner in a mercantile venture, but accepts Forster’s offer to buy him out; he takes payment in cloth rather than cash, which clears Forster’s warehouses for the new merchandise due in any day. However, Forster continues to look askance at Robert. News arrives that the ships have sunk in the Thames: Forster is bankrupt, and voluntarily commits himself to prison in order to avoid his creditors. Stephen takes charge of his new wife’s property, and proves, against expectations, to be provident rather than spendthrift. He asks Robert to help him manage the estate, and adopts him as his heir, insisting only that he does nothing to help his father. However, Stephen himself pays the prison-keeper to ensure that his brother remains comfortable. When Jane’s two other suitors complain of the favour shown to Robert, Stephen produces evidence that they are in debt to the widow’s first husband (and therefore now to him), and the threat of arrest gets rid of them. The widow generously accepts their payment of £200 to discharge a debt of ten times that amount. Unable to resist his sense of filial obligation, Robert purloins the money and gives it to his father. Stephen pretends to be angry, but secretly pays off his brother’s creditors and thereby secures his release.…
