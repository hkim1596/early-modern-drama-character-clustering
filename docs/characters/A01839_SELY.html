Indeed we may be thought vpon in time, When there be Countries more then there be men, We may get some preferment: sit at home And proue good boyes, and please our father well. My thoughts are two vnbridled, Baiazet, I neither can, nor will endure thy curbe, My comprest valor like a strangled fire. Breakes out in violent flames, and I must rule. Trizham and Mahomet are slipt in hast Each to their seuerall Prouince, we must stay, That are their Elders for another day; This Court will proue our scaffold where vve stand Plac't in the eye of angry Baiazet: Who thvvarts him in his fury is but dead, And in that passio s heate, off goes his head. I must not line thus. I tha ke you brother, then belike some crimes Lye heauy on my conscience, and I feare, Vnlesse I shift my station, 'twill be knowne; You thinke well of me kind Mahometes. Can I applie them then vnto my selfe? Am I so loose in manners? by heauen and earth, Thou shalt repent this deepelie. Straight I will follow you. Away fond wretches, ô that every breast Were of so dull a temper as you two. But who come's heere? Brother Corcutus whither are you bent ▪ What from the Court so soone? You goe to rule there? I am the last ▪ be prodigallin wine, Fill vp my bowle with Nectar, let it rise Aboue the goblets side, and may it like A swelling Ocean flow aboue the banckes, I will exhaust it greedily, 'tis my due. I shall obserue thy precepts, Mesithes welcome, How fare you in these dayes of discontent? My dutie bids me aske, and wish you well; I haue beene long a barren debtor to you, At length I may proue thankfull: weare my loue, 'Tis yours without refusal, a sleight gift, Yet your lookes tels me, 'twill helpe out my drift. Your kind acceptance of that recompence, Binds me more strictly to you. So one hath tooke, see where another comes: All health to Mustapha. Command my pardon, and commend my lo e To thy bright daughter: tell her I admire Her vertuous perfection; let that chaine Make me remembred often in her mind. No Cynicke complement, good Mustapha. Health follow you, And honour me; here is a third at hand. Yes, thus farre Spend me that purse of gold. Words are but empty shaddowes, but if deeds Answere their words, we cannot doubt their faith, They stoupe beneath my feete, I seeme to be As true as Ioue, but slye as Mercurie, Here comes Mesithes muttering backe againe, But step aside and we shall know his mind. He davnted me at first, but now I find The golds bright lustre made his judgement blind, Mustapha comes. I'st so? a while Ile feede thy ayrie hopes Then dash thee into nothing. Heer's a third. What we resolu'd, stands firme, but the euent Be scan'd when leasure serues, weele now preuent My brothers hopes, and by a sudden ate Vnto their liues and dayes giue equall date, To compasse a blest end: now we beginne Ioue hath offended if it be a sinne To throw a father downe: Saturne did dwell Once in the heauens, Iou threw him downe to hell. But stay. Achmetes, and our fathers friends? We thanke you friends: Your care hath fostered vp our infant hopes Beyond the pitch of expectation. We heare that Baiazet is going now From hence to Constantinople; my men Lie closely ambusht in the middle way, Close by a ruinous city, there expect A sudden on-set, but till then farewell. When we meete next, our ensignes wau'd on high, Shall shine like Meteors blazing in the skie. Soldier as thou hop'st to liue, Mocke not my thoughts with false and painted tales, Of a supposed stratagem. What wilt thou sweare? Enough, vnfold the meaning and the way By which this happy project must be wrought. Oh were thou faithfull— Th'ast wonne our credit, beare a noble mind About thee, then to find me forward trust This night when sleepe triumphant hath subdu'd Her wakefull subjects, and the midnight clocke Sounded full twelue, in this appointed place, Expect my presence, and till then adiew Our next shall be a tragicke enterview. I will jnoble thee place thee my second selfe In all my power for thy rare faith. VVhere's our Achomates? Answere thy Prince first I say, what art thou? But he that weares it is a Saint, and such am I. Achomates thou lyest, this night shall proue I shrinke not to vnmake what I haue done.

Plot summary

Plot — The Raging Turk, or Bajazet the Second
Bajazeth’s youngest son Corcutus has _ himself crowned Emperor, but quickly repents and resigns the crown to his father. Understanding Bajazeth to have promised him the succession, he retires to academic life. Bajazeth’s younger brother Zemes also covets the crown and plots with the King of Armenia to seize it. The Armenian army is mobilized, but routed by the Turks under Achmetes. In battle, Achmetes encounters Zemes and fights him; Zemes pretends to have been killed, then flees to Rome, where he takes refuge with Pope Alexander. Achmetes divorces his wife for adultery and so offends her father, the Pasha Isaac. Isaac tells Bajazeth that Achmetes negligently allowed Zemes to escape and then boasted of killing him. At a banquet, Bajazeth gives Achmetes a robe, which turns out to be black. In Turkish practice, giving a black robe signifies that the recipient is condemned to death without recourse to law, but Achmetes is rescued by the janissaries, who threaten Bajazeth and force him to rescind the sentence. Bajazeth fetches home the Turkish army to counter Achmetes’ control of the janissaries, and buys time by proposing a war with Rome for harbouring Zemes. In fact, he has written asking the Pope to kill Zemes, and Alexander has agreed to do so at a convenient time; he is spared the labour when Zemes drops dead of guilt. Meanwhile Bajazeth’s plans for war are opposed by two of his sons; he has them strangled and stabs Achmetes himself, but soon regrets the murders. Bajazeth’s son Selimus is ambitious and buys the pashas’ support for his plans to depose Bajazeth; he then leaves the court and goes to Tartary. Tired of the cares of state and the danger of assassination, Bajazeth declares his intention to abdicate in favour of one of his sons, and seeks the people’s opinion as to which they prefer. Privately he intends to give the crown to his second son, Achomates, and plots to dispose of the rivals: Mahomates must be murdered and Selimus killed in battle; Corcutus, ensconced in academe, is considered to be of no account. With Mahomates duly assassinated, Bajazeth proclaims Achomates his successor, but the people declare they will have no emperor but Bajazeth himself.
