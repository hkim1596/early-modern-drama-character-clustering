So I with Cicero did conferre at length, Who I perceiue the preſent ſtate deteſts, And though that age deminiſht haue his ſtrength, In him a will t'auenge his country reſts. By Lepidus inuited this laſt night, Whilſt Caeſar went to ſuppe, and I with him, Of all deaths ſhapes to talke we tooke delight, So at the table to beguile the time. And whilſt our iudgements all about were tride, Straight Caeſar (as tranſported) to the reſt With a moſt ſodaine exclamation cride, O, of all deaths vnlookt for death is beſt. For from our ſelues it ſteales our ſelues ſo faſt, That euen the mind no fearefull forme can ſee, Then is the paine ere apprehended paſt, All ſowres ere taſted would diſgeſted be. The threatned deſtinie thus he diuinde, It would appeare diuinely being inſpirde, For now I hope that he ſhall ſhortly finde, That forme of death which he himſelfe deſirde. There where the Senate minds this day to ſit, Stand all prepar'd t'approach where danger dwells, And for the ſacrifice when all is fit, Ile bring an offring conſecrated elſe. LOng-lookt for time that ſhould the glory yeeld, Which I through Neptunes truſtleſſe raigne haue ſought, And through the duſt of many a bloodie field, As by all dangers worthy to be bought. Thy comming now thoſe lowring ſhadowes cleares, That did th'horizon of my hopes ore-caſt, This day defrayes the toyles of many yeares, And brings the haru'ſt of all my labors paſt. The Senators a meſſenger haue ſent, Moſt earneſtly entreating me to come, And heare my ſelfe diſcernd by their conſent, To weare a crowne ouer all excepting Rome. Thus they deuiſe conditions at this houre, For him, of whom Mars hath made them the prey; As ſubiects limite could their ſoueraignes powre, That muſt haue minde of nought but to obey. But hauing pacifide thoſe preſent things, I minde to leade my valorous legions forth, To th'orientall realmes, adoring kings That can exhibite honors due to worth. Than ſwimme my thoughts in th'Ocean of delight, Whilſt on the pillow of ſoft praiſe repoſde, Thoſe eies to gaze vpon my glories light, That enuy op'ned, admiration cloſde.

Plot summary

Plot — Julius Caesar
Juno remains angry that a Trojan adjudged Venus the most beautiful goddess. Through the centuries, she has sought to avenge herself by harrying the Trojans’ 1607 descendants, the Romans, most recently by fomenting a civil war. But the victor of that war was Caesar, who traces his descent from Venus; he has conquered much of the world and established his ascendancy in Rome, but Juno now intends to work his overthrow. Caesar explains to Antonius that he wants to be a king not for personal ambition but to comply with a Sibylline prophecy which makes monarchy a precondition for a Roman victory in the east. Antonius warns him that there are Romans who desire his death. Cicero laments the loss of Rome’s liberty, and Decius Brutus agrees that, though Caesar’s past actions have been honourable, he should have demobilized his armies now that there is no further external threat. Brutus and Cassius agree that they will not participate in any ceremony aimed at making Caesar a king. Brutus is Caesar’s friend, but reasons that he must assassinate the tyrant for the public good. His wife Portia wrings the secret of the conspiracy out of him. Brutus and Cassius recruit conspirators, both patriots and men who have a personal grudge against Caesar. However, they exclude Cicero and agree not to kill Antonius. Caesar’s wife Calpurnia tries to persuade him not to go to the Senate on the Ides of March — an astrologer has foretold his death, and she herself has had bad dreams - but Decius Brutus induces him to change his mind. The conspirators assassinate Caesar. Antonius makes a show of friendship with them, but is obviously planning reprisals. A messenger brings Caesar’s wife Calpurnia a full account of the circumstances of the murder.
