Full speech

It will well become you. Such as refuse to offer sacrifice To any of our gods, put to the torture. Grub vp this growing mischiefe by the roots, And know when we are mercifull to them, We to our selues are cruell. I would know No meane in thine could this be true. Haste good Sempronius, draw vp our guards, And with all ceremonious pompe receiue The conquering army. Let our garrison speake Their welcome in lowd shouts, the citie shew Her state and wealth. Sacred Casar; If your imperiall Maiestie stand pleasd To showre your fauours vpon such as are The boldest champions of our religion, Looke on this reuerend man, to whom the power Of serching out, and punishing such delinquents, Was by your choyce committed, and for proofe He hath deseru'd the grace imposd vpon him, And with a fayre and euen hand proceeded Partiall to none, not to himselfe, or those Of equall neerenesse to himselfe, behold This paire of Virgins. Welcome, foole, thy fortune, Stand like a blocke when such an Angell courts thee. Spiritlesse villaine, How I am tortur'd, by the immortall gods I now could kill him. Well exscusde. And here as your selfe We will obey and serue her. Those fellowes trouble vs. Bridle your rage sweete Princesse. So insolent, base Christian. Confusion on thee, For playing thus the lying Sorcereste. I curse thy basenesse. I can no longer, Fret out my life with weeping at thee villaine; sirra, Would when I got thee, the high thunder hand Had strucke thee in the wombe. Albeit the reuerence Iowe our gods and you, are in my bosome Torrents so strong, that pitty quite lies drownd From sauing this yong man, yet when I see What face death giues him, and that a thing within me, Sayes 'tis my sonne, I'am forc'd to be a man, And grow fond of his life, which thus I beg. She's a Witch, A sorceresse Theophilus, my sonne Is charm'd by her enticing eyes, and like An image made of waxe, her beames of beauty Melt him to nothing; all my hopes in him. And all his gotten honours finde their graue In his strange dotage on her. Would when first He saw and lou'd her, that the earth had opend And swallowd both aliue. Not any, though the Princesse were appeasd, All title in her loue surrenderd vp, Yet this coy Christian, is so transported With her religion, that vnlesse my sonne (But let him perish first) drinke the same potion And be of her beleefe, sheele not vouchsafe To be his lawfull wife. Let her be sent for, Prosper in your good worke, and were I not To attend the Princesse, I would see and heare How you succeede. Giue them your Ring To leade her as in triumph if they win her Before her highnesse. Your grace hindeys Euer your humblest Vassals. That want strength To stand themselues? Is he with child, a Mid wife: To me it shall not. Ile be no subiect to the greatest Caesar Was euer crown'd with Lawrell, rather then cease To be a father. Plot summary

Plot — The Virgin Martyr
An imperial edict orders the extirpation of Christianity from the Roman Empire, and the re-establishment of the worship of the pagan gods. In Caesarea, Theophilus works to that end, abetted by Harpax; his many successes so far include the forced apostasy of his own daughters, once Christians but now votaresses of Jupiter. Diocletian gives his daughter Artemia the right to choose her own husband, and she selects the unwilling Antoninus, who loves the Christian Dorothea and asks for time to consider. Artemia remains in Caesarea as her father’s deputy. Harpax tells Theophilus that Antoninus is having an illicit relationship with Dorothea, and Theophilus suborns her servants Spungius and Hircius to smuggle him into her home so that he can witness a meeting between them. Dorothea and Antoninus are arrested. Theophilus sets his daughters, Dorothea’s childhood friends, to convert her to paganism; but instead Dorothea persuades them to revert to Christianity. When they demonstrate their new faith by spitting at an idol of Jupiter, their outraged father kills them. Antoninus is taken ill. His father brings Dorothea to his sick-bed in the hope that he will rape her and so be cured. He refuses, and Dorothea is taken for torture and execution. Destitute after losing the gold Theophilus gave them, Spungius and Hircius are engaged by Harpax to be the torturers. They flog her, but miraculously she feels nothing; they are wrongly suspected of botching the job out of regard for their former mistress, and hanged. After praying that Antoninus’ love for her be transformed into love for God, Dorothea is beheaded. Antoninus drops dead at the sight. Artemia gets her second choice of husband: the co-Emperor Maximinus. Dorothea’s faithful page Angelo, whom she took in as a beggar, turns out to be an angel, and brings Theophilus a gift from her: a basket of fruit and flowers to persuade him of the joys of the Christian heaven. Harpax comes in his true form, a devil, to claim Theophilus, but is frightened off by a floral cross in the basket. Theophilus is converted, orders the release of all Christian prisoners, and gives them ships in which to flee persecution. He is arrested by the authorities and asks to die on the worst of his extensive collection of torture instruments. He is racked but will not recant; Harpax is balked of his aim, and Dorothea and the other martyrs receive him into heaven.
