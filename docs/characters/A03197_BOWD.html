What? dost thou meane thy loue pretie foole? I know thou camst to the drawer. Am not I the propperer man? Will you get vp and ride? Come, come, leaue your iesting, I shall put you down. By this hand, I shall. Go to, you are a woman. I haue seene as faire. As faire as Mall Berry. As M. Bowdler. Go thou down then. Faith sir, the modest behauior of this Gentlewoman, Hath insinuated my company. Faith, I shall put you downe in talke, you were best to yeeld. Meane you by me? you gull me not? For if you did, I would intoxicate my head. Meane you by mee? you gull me not? Well then faire Mall, you loue me as you say. All in good time you will do, else you lie, Will you not? Barnard, she gulles me still. Call her againe, Barnard. I know it, but a kind of honest blood Tilts in my loynes, with wanton appetites, She bade me doe a message to the Drawer, And I will doe it; there will come a day, When Humfry Bowdler, will keepe holiday, Then Mall looke to your selfe, see that you be sped, Or by this light Ile haue your maidenhead. When wilt thou spit out this serpents tong of thine? Sweete rogue, whats the matter? Sweet rascall, where, O doe not kill my soule With such delayes, tell me kinde rogue, O tell me where it is. Come Barnard, lets to the dancing, lets tickle it to night, For to morrow thy heeles may be too heauy. Cripple, will you along? Fare-well then dogge of Israell, farewell. Exeunt. O Lord sir, it is your most eleuated humor to be mer ry, to be concise, set vp the coller, and looke thus with a dou ble chinne, like Diogenes peering ouer his Tub, is too cymi call, the signe of Melancholly, and indeede, the meere effect of a salt rhume. You gull me not. Sirra dogge, how didst thou like my last caper, and turne a the toe? It was my yesterdayes exercise. What purgation, you filthy curre? Now as I am a Gentleman My Tutor was not witting of the same; And in my opinion t'will doe excellent: O this aire! heer's a most eloquious aire for the memorie, I could spend the third part of my Armes in siluer, To be encountred by some good wit or other. Peace Cripple, silence, name her not, I could not indure the carrire of her wit, for a million, shee is the onely shee Mercury vnder the heauens; her wit is all spirit; that spirit fire, that fire flies from her tongue, able to burne the radix of the best inuention; in this Element shee is the abstract and breefe of all the eloquence since the incarnation of Tullies I tell thee Cripple, I had rather incounter Hercules with blowes, than Mall Berry with words: And yet by this light I am horribly in loue with her. Now haue I no more blood than a bull-rush. See, see, that glorious angell doth approach, What shall I doe? I would it were come to that, I win her! by heauen, I am not furnish'd of a courting phrase, to throw at a dogge. By this light, I had not thought the clod had had so nimble a spirit: but Cripple farewell, Ile to Mall Berry, come Barnard along with me. As a feather, sweet Rogue, as a feather: Haue I not good cause, sweet Mall, sweet Mall, Hath she not causde the same: well, if I liue, sweet wench, Either by night or day I will requite your kindenesse. Mall, thou art mine, by thine owne consent; How saist thou Mall ? But that is not sufficient Mall, if thou art content Mall, heer's a Rogue hard by, a friend of mine, whom I will acquaint with our loues, and hee shall bee partaker of the match. Come then sweet Mall, weele to the Drawer, There to dispatch what I further intend. I will not Mall. Now you lame Rogue, where is this maidens worke? my wiues worke you rascall, quicke, giue it her? I yee halting Rascall, my wiues worke; shee's my wife before God and Ralfe, how saist thou Mall, art thou not? A good wench Mall, ifaith, now will I to thy fa ther for his good wil, Cripple, see you remember what is past, for I will call thee in question for a witnesse if neede require, farewell curre, farewell dogge. Exit. Bowdler and Ralfe. Zounds two Cripples, two dogs two curres, tis won derfull! Gentlemen, patience is your fairest play. Prouided! why am not I thy Menelaus? Is it euen so, is Hellen stolne by Paris ? Then thus in armes will Menelaus mourne, Till Troy be sack'd, and Hellena returne. Exit

Plot summary

Plot — The Fair Maid of the Exchange
Phyllis and Ursula are assaulted by two ruffians as they go to deliver wares to a customer at night. A cripple rescues them, and a second assault is foiled by the intervention of Frank Golding; the cripple promises to help Frank if ever he can. Frank's two brothers are both in love with Phyllis, and, though he scorns amorous passions, Frank has fallen for her too. Phyllis, however, loves the cripple, and woos him by giving him a handkerchief to embroider “for a friend’. Meanwhile, Bobbington poses as a sailor going on a voyage, and borrows money from Phyllis’s father, Flower, leaving a diamond ring with him as security. Ferdinand and Anthony overhear one another's plans to woo Phyllis, but neither knows that Frank is also a rival. Ferdinand writes to her; Anthony plans to intercept the letter and substitute his own, but Ferdinand disguises Frank as a porter and gets him hired as Anthony's messenger. Planning to double-cross them both, Frank enlists the cripple's help. The cripple supplies him with two rejection letters to take back to his brothers, as from Phyllis. They each enlist the help of one of Phyllis’s parents, ===== PAGE 378 ===== who then quarrel over which brother shall marry their daughter. _ Knowing Phyllis loves him but thinking himself unworthy, the cripple lends Frank his clothes. Frank courts Phyllis in disguise, and she gives him her ring; then. he brings the parents forged letters from his brothers withdrawing their suits. Each parent decides to:spite the other by offering Phyllis to Frank instead of the other’s favoured son-in-law. The thwarted brothers realize what has happened, but Phyllis asks to be allowed a say in her.own marriage — and chooses Frank. As the play ends, Flower is arrested for receiving stolen property — Bobbington’s ring — and sent for trial. In the sub-plot, Mall Berry is in love with Barnard, but suffers the unwanted attentions of Bowdler. Barnard is surety for a £100 debt to Mall’s father; the original borrower is dead and the debt about to fall due, but Berry refuses to allow Barnard any more time to find the money. Bowdler persists in his pursuit of Mall, using lines he remembers from Venus and Adonis. Berry engages sergeants to arrest Barnard. Mall agrees to marry Bowdler, but the cripple points out that, through a party wall, he has overheard her talking about Barnard in her sleep: he is the man she truly loves.
