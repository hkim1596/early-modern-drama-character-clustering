And we are praid vnto, our sonnes are gentleme ̄ , what resteth then, but we saile nearer to the point? Why all we haue. Fairest of beauties, loue her seemely selfe, For thy two eies are Cupids, which doe shoote From thy inamor'd bow, shafts all of gold, Headed with mettall of immortall proose. In thy faire bosome liues two hearts relenting, Thine penetrable, through attoning pittie, Mine longing by desire to scale the fort Of loues faire presence, make me happie, Signe to my sute, but ye, for tis thy censure Makes me thus bold, pronounce faire judgement, Either of life or death, I that plead loue, Doubly deuoted, challenge from thy dietie A maiden answere, let it come bright fire, To trie the substance of my loues resolue. Neglect me so. Then whom you wil, call you this modestie to be so waspish? giuen to slight men off with inciuillitie, giue me leaue to remember. You sir. If euer you vs'd speede, be swift as lightning, Shoote as the starres in their celestiall sphears, Go and returne as Paris did from Greece, With that immatchles Hellen, tell the Priest It must be done, he will beleeue your haste, Because twas quickned with the former grant, And promised by the knight himselfe. Wheres the knight? If euer you were swift be nimble now, what haye married, tide this knot? A gallant one, much cost is threatned. And the good old knight vnbuckles from his backe, the libe rall loade of honour, dos proclaime triumphs, and welcome vnto all, calles for his wife, charges her care, commands his seruant Iames to inuite his guests, which in a rolle stands quo ted, theres a new come pothecarie, and he bribes, euen grace her selfe in this assembly, and dos promise his furtherance in the businesse, on the sodaine you are mist, daggers and di uels the knight cries wheres my daughter? one vp ascends to search the chambers, another runs to seeke for this lost daughter. I knowing more then much in this her absence, singled my selfe to warne you of his search, hether will hee come, for he feares yong Filbon, missing the futers, calling for the marchant, I answered not (being absent) in this heat: the welch man, where is he? none can be found, cries out he knowes not what, and all his word is now, a plott, a plott, a plott. And I will see the rest, what will you sir doe? Thanke ye sir. My sonne return'd, surrenders to your doombe his life, for yours so lately lost, deputed in your daughter, for she is dead sir, buried in the ile of Scillie.

Plot summary

Plot — The Two Maids of More-Clacke
Before the action, James Humil wrote to his wife from what he took to be his deathbed. Believing herself widowed, she made swift arrangements for her remarriage to Sir William Vergir, with the support of her son, who hoped to marry one of Sir William’s daughters, Now, on her way back from the wedding, she sees James in the street. She loses her glove, and offers a reward for its discovery: the person who finds . it will have the honour of deciding the festivities in celebration of her second marriage. This is a ruse to enable her to see James, who has found the glove. She tells him that, though she genuinely thought him dead, she contracted her second marriage as a means of paying his debts and securing a desirable match for ===== PAGE 471 ===== 1608 their son. She takes on James as a servant, and tells her second husband that she will only consummate the marriage once Young Humil is married to his elder daughter, Mary. Since both daughters are already engaged, Sir William tries to put off the fiancés by setting impossible conditions: Toures will only be allowed to marry Mary after she has died and come back to life; Young Filbon will get Tabitha only if he becomes a woman. Filbon sends a letter to Tabitha via Touch, Sir William’s servant, but the letter is found and Touch dismissed; Tabitha becomes estranged from her father. Toures visits Mary disguised as a tinker, and they go off to Putney to get married. Tabitha has two other suitors ~ Auditor, Toures’s father, and the Welsh knight Sir Robert, who is really Touch in disguise — but she tells them she is only interested in Filbon. The creation of Sir Robert is a ruse to allow Filbon access to her, despite her father’s opposition: when he releases her to be married to the Welsh knight, Touch hands over the role to Filbon. Toures and Mary are married, and escape by ship, but Mary falls ill during a storm at sea. She appears to die and is buried with all her possessions in a trunk on the beach in the Scilly Isles. The Governor of the Scillies digs up the trunk, thinking it booty, and finds her inside, still alive; he happens to be her uncle. Humil finds his mother in bed with James, and tells Vergir. He is unable to substantiate the charge of adultery because, by the time Vergir arrives to see for himself, James has left the bed (but is still in the room).
