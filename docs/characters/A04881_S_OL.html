Well, wheres your wife. Ile giue her some-what, though I loue not thee. And whats he. A Ruffian Knaue a is. I am indeed faire maide, My stile is Knight: come let me kisse your lips. Not so, The foolish knaue ha's beene accounted wilde, And so haue I, but I am now come home, And so will he. Nay sigh not. For you shall finde him louing and me thankfull. And were it not a scandall to my honour, To be consenting to my sonnes attempt, You should vnto my house, meane while take this, As pledge and token of my after loue: How long since dyed your father. Well sirra, loue this woman, And when you are man and wife bring her to me, Shee shall be welcome. I must be gone, I must a wooing too. We old men haue our cretchets, our conundrums, Our fegares, quirks and quibibles, As well as youth, Iustice Tutchim I goe To hunt no Buck, but prick a lusty Doe, I go in truth a wooing. Iustice not so: by her there hangs a Tale. She ha's a daughter. I likewise haue a sonne, A villanous Boy, his father vp and downe, What should I say, these Veluet bearded boyes, Will still be doing, say what we old men can, Has done the deed, has peirst the vessells head, And knowes by this the vintage. Maides may be maides to night and not to morrow. Women are free and sell their maiden-heads, As men sell cloth, by yard and handfull, But if you chaunce to see your Sister widdow, Comfort her teares and say her daughters matcht, With one that has a knocker to his Father, An honest Noble Knight. All this and more. Not I by heauen. O God haue you no roome Beyond this Chamber, has sworne to kill me, And pisse in my very visnomy, Not affraid, But of all men I loue not to meddle with a Drunkard: Haue you any Rome backwards. O God a comes. Hold Captaine. Good Captaine hold. 'Tis not your beard shall cary it, down with the rogue. Why so I did. What would you haue me do when I was downe, And hee stood thundring with his weapon drawne, Ready to cut my throte. Good meate the belly fils, good wine the braine, Women please men, men pleasure them againe, Ka me, ka thee, one thing must rub another, English loue Scots, VVelshmen loue each other. That may not be, For excomunications then will flee. Why is there a fee belonging to it. Whom haue wee heere what my mad-headed sonne What makes he here so late? say I am gone, And I the whilest will step behind the hangings. Now fresh and youthfull as the month of May, Ile bid my Bryde good morrow, Musitions on, Lightly, lightly, and by my knighthoods spurre, This yeere you shall haue my protection, And yet not buy your liuery coates your selues: God morrow Bride, fresh, fresh, as the month of May, I come to kisse thee on thy wedding day. Plot summary

Plot — Ram Alley, or Merry Tricks
The rich heiress Constantia Somerfield disguises herself as a page and enters the employ of her beloved, Boucher, intending to keep an eye on his fidelity. The rich widow Taffeta drops a handkerchief to get Boucher’s attention, and invites him into her house. Despite a prophecy that a widow will endanger his life, soul, lands, and reputation, Boucher is attracted to her. He has several rivals, including the old knight Sir Oliver Smallshank and Captain Puff, who claims marital rights in Taffeta and threatens to attack anyone who shows any sexual interest in her. Sir Oliver comes to woo Taffeta, but hides under her farthingale when Puff arrives. Puff assaults her, and Sir Oliver is forced out of his hiding place. Seeing the advantages of being a courtier’s wife, Taffeta agrees to marry him. Sir Oliver’s younger son, Will, is in debt, and his lands are mortgaged to Throat the lawyer. He pretends to have eloped with Constantia, but the woman he introduces to his family is actually his whore, Frances. He leaves her with Throat while he goes to arrange the wedding, asking Throat to confirm his cover story: that he has expectations of becoming a lord. Throat tells her the truth, that Will is a penniless fortune-hunter, and woos the lady for himself. When Will takes her, ostensibly to a nocturnal marriage ceremony, Throat has them attacked in the darkened streets. They are separated from Frances, whom Throat then marries himself. Will arranges to have himself arrested for debt when he comes looking for his putative fiancée; Throat is initially unwilling to help him, but, encouraged by Frances, agrees to pay the debt and return Will’s mortgage if he will sign a legal document renouncing all prior claims to Constantia Somerfield. Will agrees, and gets his lands back and some of his debts paid. Constantia’s mother, Lady Somerfield, comes to town, and is told that Will has abducted her daughter. Throat introduces himself as her son-in-law, and is arrested. Meanwhile Frances steals away with her new husband’s jewels. She is arrested at a tavern, and thinks it a ruse to get her away from Throat’s man; when the debt turns out to be real, she tries to sweet-talk the sergeant into releasing her. She is redeemed by her accomplice, Beard, only to be ‘rescued’ from him by Will's elder brother, who still thinks her Constantia and intends to marry her himself.
