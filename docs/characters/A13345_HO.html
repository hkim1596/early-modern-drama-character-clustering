What hath not my young lord Wealthy been here this morning? I that a ſhall Peter, ſhe cannot be matched to greater honour and riches in all this Country; yet the peeuiſh girle makes coy of it, ſhe had rather affect a Prodigall, as there was Hadit, one that by this time cannot be otherwiſe then hang'd, or in ſome worſe eſtate, yet ſhee would haue had him, but I prayſe my ſtarres ſhee went without him though, I did not without 's lands 'twas a rare mortgage Peter? But is it poſsible your ſiſter ſhould be ſtolne, ſure ſome of the houſhold ſeruants were confederates in't. Then my lord your father is farre impatient. Troth my lord when you pleaſe, ſhee's at your diſpo ſure, and I reſt much thankfull that your Lordſhip will ſo high ly honour me, ſhe ſhal haue a good portion my lord, though no thing in reſpect of your large reuenues; call her in Peter, tel her my moſt reſpected lord Welthies here, to whoſe preſence I will now commit her, and I pray you my Lord, proſecute the gaine of her affectation with the beſt affecting words you may, and ſo I bid good morrow to your lordſhip So, haue you layd the keyes of the outward dores vnder my boulſter? Go your way to bed then. I wonder who did at the firſt inuent Theſe beds, the breeders of diſeaſe and ſloth, A was no ſouldier ſure, nor no ſcholler, And yet a might be vety well a Courtier; For no good husband would haue bin ſo idle, No Vſurer neither; yet here the bed affords Store of ſweet golden ſlumbers vnto him; Here ſleepes commaund in warre, Caeſar by this Obtain'd his triumphs, this will fight mans cauſe, When fathers, brethren, and the neer'ſt of friends Leaues to aſsiſt him, all content to this Is meerely vaine, the louers whoſe affections Do ſimpathize together in full pleaſure, Debarr'd of this their ſummer ſodaine ends, And care the winter to their former ioyes, Breath's ſuch a cold blaſt on their Turtles bils; Hauing not this, to ſhrow'd him forth his ſtormes, They ſtraight are forc't to make a ſeparation, And ſo liue vnder thoſe that rule ore this. The Gallant, whoſe illuſtrious out-ſide drawes The eyes of wantons to behold with wonder Hir rare ſhap't parts, for ſo he thinks they be, Deck't in the roabes of gliſtering gallantry: Hauing not this, attendant on his perſon, Walkes with a clowdy brow, and ſeemes to all A great contemner of ſociety; Not for the hate he beares to company, But for the want of this ability: O ſiluer! thou that art the baſeſt captiue Kept in this priſon: how many pale offendors For thee haue ſuffered ruine; but ô my gold Thy ſight's more pleaſing, then the ſeemely locks Of yallow hair'd Apollo, and thy touch More ſmooth and dainty, then the downe-ſoft white Of Ladies tempting breaſt, thy bright aſpect Dimm's the great'ſt luſter of heauens Waggoner. But why goe I about to extoll thy worth, Knowing that Poets cannot compaſſe it; But now giue place my gold for heres a power Of greater glory and ſupremacy Obſcures thy being, here ſits enthroniz'd The ſparkling diamond, whoſe bright reflection Caſts ſuch a ſplendor on theſe other Iemmes, Mongſt which he ſo maieſticall appeares, As if— now my good angels guard me. Indeed I doe, and Hogge ſhall finde it ſo. Then youle not haue her ſir? Was it you ſonne that cride ſo loud for iuſtice? O my moſt honored lord I am vndone, rob'd this black night of all the wealth and treaſure, which theſe many yeeres I haue howerly labored for. Oh knew I that, I then my lord were happy. Plot summary

Plot — The Hog Hath Lost His Pearl
Haddit has spent all his money and mortgaged his lands to Hog the usurer, and now ekes out a living selling jigs to players. He aims to solve his financial problems by marrying Rebecca, Hog’s daughter, whose affections he had previously refused in the belief that she was too low-born for him. Hog intends Rebecca to marry young Lord Wealthy, but she refuses, and helps Haddit trick her father. Haddit’s uncle, Lightfoot, helps to get the servant Peter out of the way by picking a quarrel, to give him a pretext for making peace with a drinking contest; Peter imbibes himself insensible in the cellar. Rebecca steals Hog’s keys, giving Haddit and his accomplices access to his bedroom, where he keeps his gold. Lightfoot appears to Hog as the ghost of Croesus, come to make the usurer rich: a spirit will ===== PAGE 291 ===== 1613 take his gold and transform it into pearl, which is even more valuable; in reality, Haddit steals the money and retrieves his mortgage, then absconds with Rebecca and marries her. Hog goes to complain to old Lord Wealthy, but his supernatural experiences induce a conversion: he renounces avarice and gives the couple his blessing. In the sub-plot, Carracus plans to elope with young Lord Wealthy’s sister Maria, and entrusts his friend Albert with the arrangements. Albert visits Maria early, posing as Carracus, and has sex with her; she then leaves with Carracus as planned, and marries him: Albert suffers remorse and decides to leave human society. Some time later, Carracus notices that Maria is wearing Albert’s ring, which he gave her on the night of the elopement. Both realize what Albert did; Maria faints and Carracus is told that she has died of shame. In fact, she disguises herself as a page and flees to the woods, where Albert has carved penitential verses on every tree. Posing as a hermit, Albert finds her almost dead from starvation; he takes her to his cave; and feeds her. Carracus also goes to the woods, where an echo tells him that Maria is alive and innocent. They meet up, and revelations ensue: the page is chaste Maria and the hermit is penitent Albert. Carracus and Maria forgive him, and make their peace with Maria’s family.
