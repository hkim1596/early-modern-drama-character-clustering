From our long desiring Father. No Ladie, but the perfect counterfeit. The Painters Art could yeeld. Why fairest Princes, if your eye dislike that deader peece, behold me his true forme and liuelier image, such my Father hath beene. Tis withered Ladie, the flowers scent is gone. Aurora yet keepes chast old Titheus bed. More, more, by many a one. More. The curtesie of Art hath giuen more life to that part, than the sad cares of state would graunt my father. Then doth it somewhat flatter, for our father hath seene more yeares, and is a little shrunke from the full strength of time. What meanes my Lord? Your grace doth much amaze mee. I deepely vow my Lord. My gratious Lord if I vnknowingly haue er'de, Nay but my Lord— Proue you but iustlie, louing and conceiue me, Iustice shall leaue the gods before I leaue thee: Imagination proue as true, as thou art sweete And tho the Duke seeme wise, heele finde this straine When two harts yeelde consent, all thwartinges vaine. O quick deuisefull strong braind Dulcimel Thou art too full of witte to be a wife, Why dost thou loue? or what strong heat gaue life To such faint hopes? O woman thou art made Most onelie of, and for deceit, thy forme Is nothing but delusion of our eyes, Our eares, our heartes, and sometimes of our hands, Hipocrisie and vanitie brought forth, Without male heat, thy most most monstrous being Shall I abuse my royall fathers trust, And make my selfe a scorne, the very foode Of rumor infamous? shall I that euer loathede, A thought of woman, now begin to loue, My worthy fathers right, breake faith to him that got me To get a faithlesse woman? To take from my good father the pleasure of his eyes, And of his hands, imaginary solace of his fading life. And my selfe good, his lifes most onely end. Yes Fawne in time, we must not prescribe to nature, euery thing: ther's some end in euery thing. Shal I, I say?— Vncompassionate vilde man, shall I not pittie, if I cannot loue? Or rather shall I not for pittie loue, So wondrous wit in so most wondrous beautie, That with such rarest arte and cunning meanes Entreates? what (I thing valules) am not, Worthie but to graunt, my admiration, Are fathers to be thought on in our loues? O Fawne what man of so colde earth But must loue such a wit in such a body, Thou last and onelie rarenes of heauens workes, From best of man made modell of the Gods: Diuinest woman, thou perfection Of all proportions Beutie, made when, Ioue was blith, VVell filde with Nectar, and full friendes with man, Thou deare as aire, necessarie as sleepe, To carefull man: woman, O who can sin so deepely, As to be curst from knowing of the pleasures, Thy soft society, modest amorousnes, Yeeldes to our tedious life. Fawne, the Duke shal not know this Tush Fawne, to violence of womens loue and wit Nothing but not obtayning is impossible, Notumque furens quid foemina possit. To him that onely can giue dues, she rests most due. Exit What means the Duke? Good your grace some reason? What should I thinke, what hope, what but imagine of thes Enigmas ? This I know, With too much torture, since meanes are all vnknowne, To come vnto these endes, wheres this her chamber? Then what meanes shall without suspition Conuey me to her chamber? O these doubtes, End in despaire— Enter Gonzago hastily. Why now I haue it Fawne, the way, the meanes, & mea ning, good Duke, and t'were not for pitty, I could laugh at thee, Dulcimel I am thine most miraculously, I will now be ginne to sigh, reade Poets, looke pale, go neately, and be most apparantly in loue, as for— Alas he and all know, this an old saw hath bin, Faiths-breach for loue, and kingdoms is no sin. Exit. I am sure good Father wee are both bound together as fast as the Priest can make vs already, I thanke you for it kind father, I thanke you onely for't.

Plot summary

Plot — Parasitaster, or The Fawn
After failing to persuade his son Tiberio to marry, Hercules, the widowed Duke of Ferrara, plans to marry Princess Dulcimel of Urbino, and sends Tiberio to negotiate the treaty. Hercules goes to Urbino himself, assumes the alias Faunus, meets Nymphadoro and Herod, and hears some unflattering remarks about himself in his other persona, Hercules. He gets them to introduce him at court, and becomes a favourite with the Duke of Urbino. Dulcimel would rather marry Tiberio than Hercules, and contrives to woo him using her father, Duke Gonzago, as an intermediary. She brings him a scarf which she has embroidered with a message, and tells him that it was sent by Tiberio; the outraged Gonzago gives it to the young man, thinking he is returning an unwanted would-be lover's gift. She then reports to ===== PAGE 176 ===== Gonzago that Tiberio intends to climb up the tree by her bedroom window and surreptitiously marry her. Gonzago repeats the plan back to Tiberio, revokes his diplomatic status, and orders him to leave Urbino. Instead, Tiberio. puts the plan into effect and marries Dulcimel in her bedroom. Faunus: observes the foolish behaviour of the courtiers at Urbino. Herod cuckolds his brother, the impotent Sir Amoroso, and gets his sister-in-law with child — in effect, dispossessing himself of the estate by producing an heir apparent. Donna Zoya, who bas not slept with her husband Zuccone for four years, feigns pregnancy, and the gossip spreads rapidly through the court, each person telling the next on condition that they keep it strictly to themselves. Zuccone repudiates her, then regrets it on learning she is not really an adulteress, but she will only take him back on new terms which give her the upper hand in the marriage. Faunus lays on a court entertainment: a parliament of Cupid to consider the many grievances lovers have sent to heaven. Cupid’s statutes are read, and several of the foolish courtiers are tried. Nymphadoro is convicted as a multiple philanderer and sent to the ship of fools, Herod for falsely boasting of love, and Granuffo for affecting silence to seem wiser than he is. Sir Amoroso (charged with wooing on false pretences) and Zuccone are spared. The last fool to be exposed is Gonzago, for procuring the seduction of his own daughter. Resuming his own identity, Hercules thanks Gonzago for securing Tiberio’s marriage.
