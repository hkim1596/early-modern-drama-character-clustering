Why, loue you any one ſo wel to giue away your heart I know ſhee's dearer to you? Why, thinke you ſhee'l proue falſe? Madame, it's true, we not alone in Princes See the bitter effects of ſuch forc'd Marriages; But euen in priuate Families, Murders and Adulteries, doe often wait vpon thoſe Couples Whoſe Bodies are compeld by Parents or Friends To ioyne for worldly reſpects, without the ſoules conſent. O deare Madame, what a ſad obiect's this? O Madame ſay not ſo, had you but eu'n now So great a care to ſaue his life, and are you now So cruell to ſay that he muſt periſh by the hand Of Iuſtice, though he ſhould ſcape theſe wounds? Would not the Duke haue kild him if he could? Ile pawn my life vpon 't, my Brother kild him fairly. I want words to expreſſe how much I loue And honour you. Brother, I know you ſee it your ſelfe, Though you will not take notice of it. Ile ſee who it is. Exit. Exceeding well Madame; Brother, here is the Princeſſe. Madame, I rather wiſh My Brother neuer had beene borne, Then that the King ſhould know you loue him, Nay, I hope you know it not your ſelfe: Shall I belieue that your great heart, that euer Yet contemn'd loue, can on a ſodaine in foure Or fiue daies knowledge, be ſtruck by my vnworthy Brothers ſlender merits, and one that muſt Be periur'd too, if he ſhould loue you. Brother, me thinkes now your wounds being well. It were good to quit this Countrey for a while: For it is impoſſible but by ſome meanes or other, If you ſtay heare, you will be diſcouered. The time hath beene, that you without Clarinda's leaue would haue done nothing. Madame, it were in me preſumption To cenſure any of your actions. O Madam yes, my poore Brother muſt dye to day. Madame, I could wiſh that I might not out-liue him. Madame, how ſtrangely doe you talke.
