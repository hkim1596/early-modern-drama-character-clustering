When ſage Calistines for no requeſt, With ſuperſtitious cuſtomes could comport: But with franke wordes all flatterie did deteſt, He was abuſde, and in a barbarous ſort. So plaging him (no doubt) the king did ill, Yet to proſperitie we muſt impute Thoſe fatall faults that follow Fortune ſtill, As being of all great mindes a baſtard fruit. We ſhould in kings, as loth their ſtates to tuch, Speake ſparingly of vice, praiſe vertue much. But I whoſe ſoule that wiſe man deerely lou'd, Whilſt ſpying his perfections thus iniur'd: To tender paſſions by compaſſion mou'd, Would his reliefe haue willingly procur'd. But when my credite faild, all hope being paſt, That I could purchaſe grace in any ſort: I gaue him deſp'rate phyſicke at the laſt, That if his life was euill, it might be ſhort. The king enrag'd that I had thus preſumde, To limite his reuenge by ſodaine death: That by a Lion I ſhould be conſumde, Did throw my doome out of the depths of wrath. But when with rolling eyes the Lion roard, He by my ſtrength as ſtrengthleſſe was orethrowne: Which to the king whoſe mind did then remord, My courage and my conſtancie made knowne. So that incontinent I was ſet free, By this rare proofeeſteem'd amongſt the ſtrong: And with a minde from inward rancor free; As he his wrath, ſo I forgot my wrong. For whilſt alone he through a forreſt rang'd; If it had bin but ſo, to purchaſe fame: Some by that meanes had former wrongs reueng'd, Bent like Erostratus t'acquire a name. Yet that which others did attempt in vaine, And tirde by trauell, of a ſurffet dide, I did performe him bringing backe againe, Whil t I did runne as ſwift as he could ride. And of that deede my ſprite reſts well apaide, For ſince that time my ſoueraigne held me deere, Which afterwards he to the world bewraid, Whilſt in this forme his fauour did appeare. When vnawares he chanc'd my brow to wound, To ſtay my blood that ſtriu'd to die his launce: My temples with his diademe he crownd, A happy ſigne, though comming but by chaunce. And O! who knowes but once before I die, That the ſucceſſe may ſecond the preſage?

Plot summary

Plot — The Alexandraean Tragedy
Alexander the Great is dead, poisoned by Cassander. He leaves behind a pregnant widow but no definite heir. Unable to get into the underworld because as yet unburied, his ghost returns to earth and recounts his life story, a tale of escalating atrocity as power progressively divorced him from ordinary humanity. He decides to inspire his captains with ambition and bloodlust. The captains are unable to agree on how best to secure the Macedonian Empire, and various proposals are advanced: to await the birth of Alexander's posthumous child, and appoint a regent in the interim; to crown either Alexander’s bastard brother or his elder son, who is half-barbarian and so unacceptable to the people; to split up the empire between themselves. The only thing they can agree on is that the common foot-soldiers should have no say in the decision. Lysimachus and Seleucus decide to bide their time, let their fellow captains wear themselves out with wrangling, and then step in to take the empire for themselves. Meleager tries to have Perdiccas, his rival, murdered, but the plot fails and Perdiccas responds in kind with more success. When Alexander’s child is born, it is a son. Perdiccas and others become his joint regents, but Perdiccas schemes for personal supremacy. He removes his two colleagues in the regency, gives the other captains their own kingdoms in an effort to secure their loyalty, and plans to marry into the royal family. Two of his new subject kings, Antigonus and Ptolemy, ally themselves against him. He accepts the advice of Eumenes to deal with the rebels first before engaging with dynastic matters, but is murdered by his own soldiers during the campaign. Olympias, Alexander’s mother, plots against Cassander, but Cassander besieges her and, starving, she has to give herself up. She refuses his mercy and is executed. Cassander then procures the murder of Alexander’s remaining family. Antigonus secures the support of Eumenes, but then has him treacherously killed. The four remaining captains (Cassander, Ptolemy, Lysimachus, and Seleucus) agree to unite against Antigonus. Philastrus prophesies continuing strife: Cassander will die in disgrace, Seleucus will kil Lysimachus and then die himself, and Ptolemy’s line will ultimately end with a lustful queen who will see his empire fall into the hands of the enemy.
