Full speech

Yes faith Father, with all my heart. So could not Kate. Why Captaine, though ye be a man of warre, you cannot subdue affection; you haue no alacritie in your eie, and you speake as if you were in a Dreame, you are of so melancholy and dull a disposition, that on my Conscience you would neuer get Children; Nay nor on my bodie nei ther: and what a sinne were it in me, and a most pregnant signe of Concupiscence, to marrie a man that wantes the mettall of Generation; since that is the blessing ordain'de for Marriage, procreation the onely end of it. Besides, if I could loue you; I shall be heer at home, and you in Cleeue land abroad; I among the bold Brittaines, and you among the hot shots. Ha, Godamercie old Hieronimo. The Hose are comely. On my life tis so. Thou art not so vnworthy to beleeue him, If I did thinke thou didst, I would not open My lips, to satisfie so base a thought, Sprung from the slander of so base a Slaue. Sir you shall not: What staine my Honor hath receiu'd by this Base Villaine, all the world takes notice of, Marke what I Vow, and if I keepe it not, May I be so giuen o're, to let this Rogue Performe his slander; Thou that wert ordain'd, And in thy Cradle markt to call me wife, And in that Title made as my defence, Yet suffered'st him to go away with life, Wounding my Honor dead before thy face. Redeeme it on his head, and his owne way, Euen by the sword his long profession, And bring it on thy necke out of the field: and set it cleere amidst the tongues of men, That all eyes may discerne it slandered, Or thou shalt neare enjoy me as a wife: By this bright Sun thou shalt not; Nay Ile thinke As a biectly of thee, as any Mongrill Bred in the Citty; Such a Cittizen as the Playes flout still, and is made the subject Of all the stages. Be this true or no, Tis thy best course to fight. Pray pardon me, none seeles the smart but I, Tis thy best course to fight, if thou be'st still, and like an honest Tradesman eat'st this wrong: Oh, may thy Spirit and thy state so fall, Thy first borne childe may come to the Hospitall. I must not eate nor sleepe, weepe till't be done. Sister, informe your selfe, I haue no Ladyship To guild my infamie, or keepe tongues in awe: If God loue Innocencie, I am sure he shall not Loose in this action. This month or two. This month or two, de'e loue me, not before, It may be I will liue so long Fames Whore. Exit Kath. Introth thy State is happier much then ours, Were neuer two (like vs) vnfortunate. Lost, lost introth, to morrow I shall heare, I make account hee's gone some fiue yeares voyage, Till this disgrace of ours be ouer-blowne, and for my Captaine Powts, by this time hee Is ten mile on the Riuer toward Graues end. Life, I am not married then in earnest. Oh Sister, heere's the Villaine slander'd me. Oh my Diuining Spirit, hee's gone to Sea. Of all men liuing I could marrie thee, VVere not my heart giuen to another man, Sir you did speake of Strange. To marrie me, no villaine I do hate him On this report, worse then I do thy selfe, And may the plagues and Tortures of a Land Ceize me, if this be not an Innocent hand. Oh my deere Strange. Discouers himselfe.

Plot summary

Plot — A Woman Is a Weathercock
Scudmore is in love with Bellafront, and his friend Nevill persuades him, with difficulty, to show the love- letter he has received from her. There is a slight complication: Nevill is on his way to .Bellafront’s wedding. She is to marry Count Frederick that morning. The whole family is mired in matrimonial competition. Bellafront’s sister Lucida also loves Frederick, and has sworn never to marry for his sake; but she is being courted by the foolish Sir Abraham Ninny, who has got himself knighted expressly to make himself a more eligible son-in-law. The youngest daughter, Katherine, is also to be married that morning, to Strange the merchant, but she too has another admirer, Captain Pouts. Unbeknown to Scudmore, Nevill impersonates the parson who is to officiate at the wedding. Scudmore tries to catch Bellafront at the church door, but she refuses him; this convinces him that all women are perfidious. After the wedding, Pouts publicly declares that Katherine has slept with him on many previous occasions. Strange seems reluctant to seek legal redress for this slander, but only because he doesn’t want anyone to stop him secretly taking a more immediate revenge. He tries to see Pouts at his lodging, but Pouts assumes he has come to serve a writ and will not let him in. Pouts then prepares to flee England and his creditors, but Strange intercepts him disguised as a soldier, claiming that Katherine engaged him to murder her new husband and that she loves only Pouts. Pouts admits that he slandered her; Strange wounds him in a fight and forces him to sign a document affirming her innocence. By a pretext, Scudmore gets in to see Bellafront, rebukes her, and returns her love-letters. She tells him that her father forced her to marry Frederick, and that she will kill herself rather than consummate the marriage. He mistrusts her, but Nevill arranges for him to participate in a wedding masque that evening. He and Bellafront sneak away during the dancing and are married, this time by the right parson. Suspicions of polyandry are dispelled when Nevill explains that the parson that morning was really him, so that the marriages are invalid. Strange brings Pouts to apologise to the gathering, but is almost arrested for his own murder until he reveals his identity. Pouts returns to the war, abjuring women.
