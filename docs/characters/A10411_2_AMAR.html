Ceres has heard my prayers, For all my morning orisons beg'd no more Then one kind word from Damon. The same;—why startle you? you have not met A poyson, Damon. What should this mean, my Damon? how have I Displeas'd you, sweet? heaven knowes it is my praier More then for heaven, to please you. Can it be A sin to pray for Damon? Ceres, these obscure passions move me. For that I love it, Because it once was thine. So it would have done Had it not first touch't yours. Laurinda gave me This Garland, but nere told me of this accident. Damon, thy harsh language is more then death Vnto me. No, I am resolv'd to write what I determine. Welcome death! From him all things are so. Damon, fly hence, Thou hast shed bloud here in the Sacred Valley, Make hast away or thou art lost for ever. Oh I faint. Vnto Laurinda, take it. Say I wish all health to her and Damon; And being not able for to beare my griefes, I sought a remedy from mine own speare and died. Fear not Dorylas. Farewell! How fearfull death is unto them; whose life Had any sweetnesse in it! my daies have all Been so oreworne with sorrow, that this wound Is unto me rather a salve then fore, More physick then disease: whither my journey Shall lead me now; through what dark hideous place; Among what monsters, hags and snake-hair'd Furies, Am I to goe, I know not: but my life Hath been so spotlesse, chast, and innocent, My death so undeserv'd, I have no reason (If there be Gods) but to expect the best; Yet what doth most torment mee, is the thought How long 'twill bee ere I again enjoy My Damon's presence: untill then, Elysium Will be no place of pleasure; and perchance When he comes thither too, he then may slight mee As much as now.—That very feare doth make thee Dye, wretched Amaryllis! O was ever love So cros'd as mine! was ever Nymph so wretched As Amaryllis? O sir, my bloud Calls none but fortune guilty. I by chance Stumbled on mine own dart, and hurt my selfe. ▪ It was my selfe, Or one as deare. What joy have I to live, That nere liv'd yet: the time that I have spent Since first I wept, then, when I first had entrance Into this world, this cold and sorrowfull world, Was but a scene of sorrow; wretched I! Fatall to both my parents! For my birth Ruin'd my mother, and my death my father. O Tragick life! I either should have been Nere borne, or nere have died. When I began To be, my sinne began, why should it then Out live mee? for, though now I cease to be, That still continues: Eyes, flow forth a pace, And be asham'd to see my wound run blood Faster then you drop teares— See, here he comes. His absence never untill now I wisht. By Ceres, no; I have no heart to doe it: does that face Look cruell? doe those eyes sparkle with hate, Or malice? Tell me, Father, lookes that brow As if it could but frowne? Say, can you thinke Tis possible Damon could have the heart To wound a Virgin? surely barbarous cruelty Dwels not in such a brest: mercy, and mildnesse, Courtesy, love, and sweetnesse breath in him, Not Anger, wrath, or murther; Damon was not Fed at a Thracian teat, Venus did send Her Doves to nurse him, and can he be cruell? Whence should he learne so much of barbarisme As thus to wrong a Virgin? if he wound mee Tis only from his eyes, where loves blind God Whets his pil'd arrowes; He besides, you know, Had never cause to wrong mee, for he knowes Alwaies I lov'd him: Father, doe not wrong An innocent; his soule is white, and pure, Tis sinne to thinke there lives a sinne in him; Impiety to accuse him. Tis, alas, His modest, bashfull nature, and pure innocence, That makes him silent: think you that bright rose That buds within his cheekes, was planted there By guilt or shame? no, he has alwaies been So unacquainted with all act of sinne, That but to be suspected strikes him dumb With wonder and amazement. For by Ceres (I think my oath be lawfull) I my selfe Was cause of this. Indeed it was not hee. You are no competent witnesse; You are his Rivall in Laurinda's love, And speak not truth but malice, 'tis a plot To ruin innocence. Plot summary

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
