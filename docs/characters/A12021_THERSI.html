Plot — Troilus and Cressida
In the besieged city of Troy, Prince Troilus is in love with Cressida, and secures the help of her uncle, Pandarus, as a go-between. Pandarus’ praise for Troilus’ virtues seems not to convince Cressida — because she is secretly interested in him already. Pandarus brings them together in the house of Cressida’s absent father, and they spend the night with one another. After seven years of war, the Greeks are weary and disunited. Achilles has retired to his tent with his friend Patroclus, and spends his time satirizing his fellow princes; Ajax emulates him with the coarse slave Thersites. The commanders offer the Trojans peace, without repayment of the war’s expenses, if they will return the abducted Helen. The Trojan princes consider the proposal: Hector wants to accept it, but Troilus and Paris win the debate with their arguments that this would be dishonourable; Cassandra prophesies the fall of Troy if Helen is not returned. Meanwhile, in an attempt to encourage Achilles into action, Hector sends the Greeks a challenge to single combat. Ulysses, who considers Achilles the key to victory, arranges for the combatant to be decided by lottery, and rigs it so that Ajax is chosen, then provokes Achilles to jealousy of that outcome. Cressida’s father, Calchas, has defected to the Greeks, and has long been trying to arrange for her to join him; but the Trojans have always refused. With the capture of the valued soldier Antenor, he sees an opportunity, and persuades the Greeks to negotiate an exchange. Diomedes is sent to Troy to fetch Cressida, and there behaves offensively to Troilus. The lovers exchange tokens before they part: Troilus gives Cressida a sleeve and she gives him a glove.
