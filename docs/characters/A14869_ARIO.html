Full speech

Yes, Taylors in France, they grow to great Abominable purchase, and become great officers. How many Duckets thinke you he has spent Within a tweluemonth, besides his fathers allowance? He learnt that of an English drunkard, And a Knight too, as I take it. This comes of your numerous Wardrobe. Your daintie embroydered stockings, With ouerblowne Roses, to hide your gowtie anckles. Your losses I confesse, are infinite, Yet sir, you must haue patience. Sir, I am a Lawyer. You shall find I haue. Make me Cuckold? All the sicke horses in Italy Would haue been glad of your losse them. Come, come, come, You gaue those ships most strange, most dreadfull, And vnfortunate names, I neuer lookt they'd prosper. Did you not call one, The Stormes Defiance; Another, The Scourge of the Sea; and the third, The great Leuiathan? Well, take it on my word then, Your place will not long be emptie. Doe you call this a Briefe? Here's as I weigh them, some fourescore sheets of paper. What would they weigh if there were cheese Wrapt in them, or Figdates. Me thinks you prate too much. I neuer could endure an honest cause With a long Prologue too't. Hadst thou been drunke so long, T'hadst done our Court better Seruice. Cry ye mercy, doe I so? And as I take it, you doe very little remember, Either womanhood, or Christianitie: why doe ye meddle With that seducing knaue, that's good for nought, Vnlesse 'the to fill the Office full of Fleas, Or a Winter itch, weares that spatious Inkehorne All a Vacation onely to cure Tetters, And his Penknife to weed Cornes from the splay toes, Of the right worshipfull of the Office. Woman, yare mad, Ile swear't, & haue more need Of a Physician then a Lawyer. The melancholly humour flowes in your face, Your painting cannot hide it: such vildsuits Disgrace our Courts, and these make honest Lawyers Stop their own eares, whilst they p'ead & thats the reason Your yonger men that haue good conscience, weare such large Night-caps; go old woman, go pray, For Lunacy, or else the Deuill himselfe Has tane possession of thee; may like cause In any Christian Court neuer find name: Bad Suits, and not the Law, bred the Lawes shame. I aduise you, take their proffer, Or else the Lunacy runnes in a blood, You are more mad then shee. An angry fellow that would doe thee good, For goodnesse sake it selfe, I doe protest, Neither for loue nor money. Very well, may it please this honourable Court, If he be a bastard, and must forfeit his land for't, She has prooued her selfe a strumpet, and must loose Her Dower, let them goe a begging together. You may see now what an old house You are like to pull ouer your head, Dame. Are you angry yet? Take heed you doe not cracke your voice sir. Now the mettall comes to the Touchstone. Roome for the bagge and baggage. Come closer to the businesse. For a Iulipe. No? This Law businesse Will leaue me so small leasure to serue God, I shall serue the King the worse. Stay, I doe here first make protestation, I nere tooke fee of this Romelio, For being of his Councell, which may free me, Being now his Iudge, for the imputation Of taking a Bribe. Now sir, speake your mind. How young? of fiue and fortie? He that should haue married your daughter? It was a plot belike then to conferre The land on her that should haue bin his wife. A guard vpon him for the death of Contarino. What say you to this Leonora? My opinion is, That this late slaunder vrged against her sonne, Takes from her all manner of credit: Shee that would not sticke to depriue him of his liuing, Will as little tender his life. Goe when you please: what should moue you Be thus forward in the accusation? Stay sir, be well aduised, There is no testimony but your owne, To approue you slew him, therefore no other way To decide it, but by Duell. Weele take order for her. Haue the Knights weighed, And measured their weapons? Proceed then to the battell, and may heauen Determine the right. How insolently has this high Court of Honor Beene abused! How now, who are these? Shee's a blacke one indeed. No more: attend the Sentence of the Court. Rarenesse and difficultie giue estimation To all things are i'th world: you haue met both In these seuerall passages: now it does remaine, That these so Comicall euents be blasted With no seueritie of Sentence: You Romelio, Shall first deliuer to that Gentleman, Who stood your Second, all those Obligations, Wherein he stands engaged to you, Receiuing onely the principall. Next, you shall marry that Nun. Contarino, and Romelio, and your selfe, Shall for seuen yeares maintaine against the Turke, Six Gallies. Leonora, Iolenta, And Angiolella there the beautious Nun, For their vowes breach vnto the Monastery, Shall build a Monastery. Lastly, the two Surgeons, For concealing Contarino s recouery, Shall exercise their Art at their owne charge, For a tweluemonth in the Gallies: so we leaue you, Wishing your future life may make good vse Of these euents, since that these passages, Which threatned ruine, built on rotten ground, Are with successe beyond our wishes crown'd.

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
