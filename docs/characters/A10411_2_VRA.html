FAther perswade me not! The power of heaven Can never force me from Amyntas love; 'Tis rooted here so deepe within my heart That he which pulls it out, pulls out at once, That and my soule together. Father, you know Divinitie is powerfull, Cupids will Must not be question'd: When love meanes to sport (I'have heard your selfe relate it) he can make The Wolfe and Lambe kisse friendly; force the Lyon T'forget his Majestie, and in amorous dalliance Sport with the frisking Kid. When Venus rides, Shee'le linke the ravenous Kite, and milder Swan To the same chariot, and will yoak together The necks of Doves and Eagles; when as shee Commands, all things loose their Antipathie, Even contrarieties: can I alone Resist her will? I cannot, my Amynt as Shall witnesse that! I love him now the rather; he hath lost Himselfe for me, and should he loose me too? It were a sinne he should! Only that, His wildnesse; 'tis the comfort I have left To make my teares keepe time to his distractions; To think as wildl … as he talks; to marry Our griefs together, since our selves we cannot. The Oracle doth aske so strange a Dowry, That now his company is the only blisse My love can aime at: but I stay too long I'le in to comfort him. Doe not? I must and will; Nature commands me no, But Love more powerfull sayes it shall be so. Sweet Amyntas! Why to hell, My deere Amyntas? O Ceres, now behold him! canthy eyes Looke on so sad an object, and not melt Them and thy heart to pitty? And mine too. Come Amaryllis let's weepe both together, Contending in our sorrowes! And I had nere been borne! Then Amyntas Might have been happy. Yet still his love to me Lives constant. Amyntas. Amyntas, deere Amyntas — How shall I Apply my selfe to his wild passions! Tis my praier, And shall be ever, I will promise thee Shee shall have none but him. Come sweet Amyntas, rest thy troubled head Here in my lap:—Now here I hold at once My sorrow and my comfort: Nay ly still. Nay, good Amyntas. — Pray ly still! Good Amyntas, Rest here a while!— And so doe you. If Amyntas, Her deere Amyntas would but take his rest, Vrania could not want it. How miserable? Hee that I love is not!— I have undone Amyntas! More wretched I, for Damon has his reason, And he may love. True, Amaryllis, But he has lost his reason; mine has love, No reason. My Amaryllis, how thy griefes Meete full with mine to make the truest story Of perfect sorrow that ere eye bedew'd With teares of Pitty! Fy Brother, can you be of the same stock, Issue, and bloud with mee, and yet so cruell? Good Amyntas hold, This is the Sacred Vally: here 'tis death, For to shed human blood. Come my sad partner, Poore rivall of my sorrowes: Goe with mee Into the Temple; I'le intreat my Brother To use thee kindly: if in mee it lye, I'le helpe thee. Sweet Amaryllis! Amaryllis. Live happy sheapheardesse and weare it still! My Bellamore y'are under good protection; The Temple gates will close unlesse I hast. The like to her that ipitties the distressed Ama ryllis. Grant Ceres now My hopes be faithfull to me: my Amyntas, How come your thoughts so setled? Would we knew How to deserve it. Command it. Sir, I will Presently about it, I 'le only first Get some unknown disguise. Happinesse attend you! Yes, Amyntas. This habit well befits a Virgins life. For since my Dowry never can be paid Thus for thy sake I'le live and dye a maid.

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
