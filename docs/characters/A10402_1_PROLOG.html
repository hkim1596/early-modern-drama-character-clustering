'Tis thy release I seeke, I come to file Those heauy shackels from thy wearied limbes, And giue thee leaue to wa e the Stage againe, As free as vertue: Burne thy withered Bayes, And with fresh Laurell crowne thy sacred Temples, Cast off thy maske of darknesse, and appeare As glorious as thy sister Comedy. But first with teares, wash off that guilty sinne, Purge out those ill-digested dregges of wit, That vse their inke to blot a spotlesse fame, Let's haue no one particular man traduc'd, But like a Noble Eagle seaze on vice, As she flies bold and open! spare the persons: Let vs haue simple mirth, and innocent laughter; Sweet-smiling lips, and such as hide no fangs, No venemous biting teeth, or forked tongues, Then shall thy freedome be restor'd againe, And full applause be wages of thy paine. What should this Scotus meane by his possibilities & incompossibilities? my Cooper, Rider, Thomas, and Minshew are as farre to seeke as my selfe: not a word of compossibilitas or incompossibilitas is there. Well, I know what I'll doe. I haue heard of a great Philosopher: I'll try what he can doe; They call him Aristippus, Aristippus, A ristippus: sure a Philosophers name. But they say he lies at the Dolphin, and that me thinks is an ill signe: yet they say too, the best Philosophers of the towne neuer lye from thence: they say 'tis a Tauerne too; for my part I cannot tell, I know no part of the Towne but the Schooles and Aristotles Well: but since I am come thus farre, I will enquire: for this same compossibilitas and incompossibilitas sticks in my stomake.

Plot summary

Plot — Aristippus, or The Jovial Philosopher
Simplicius asks after the philosopher Aristippus in the Dolphin tavern, but is served the wine of the same name. On finding the right Aristippus, he enrols as a pupil, and is sworn in with an oath taken in drink. Aristippus encourages him to renounce beer and drink sack instead, to stay up at night, and to sleep in the morning. The Wild Man, who favours beer, beats Aristippus out of the tavern with the help of two brewers. However, he is quickly persuaded of the error of his ways, and seeks to make amends by fetching a surgeon to mend the philosopher’s head. The boastful Medico de Campo - who claims that he has treated many a world leader, and could even cure decapitation by making an artificial head - restores Aristippus to health with a draught of sack. For his reward, he asks that the Wild Man be forgiven, and the Wild Man promises that in future he will be Aristippus’ faithful disciple.
