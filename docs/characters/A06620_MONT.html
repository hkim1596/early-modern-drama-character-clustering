As good yeeld indeed submissiuely, and satisfie part of our affections; as bee stubburne without abilitie to resist, and enioy none of them: I am in worst plight, since I loue a Nymph that mockes loue. I this: Fructus abest facies cum bona teste caret. Faire faces lose their fauours, if they admit no louers. Come, let vs euerie one to our walkes, it may be we shall meete them walking. Exeunt. Though thou hast ouer-taken me in loue, yet haue I ouer-taken thee in running: faire Celia, yeelde to loue, to sweete loue. The fairest Wolfe chuseth the foulest, if he bee faithfullest, and he that indureth most griefe, nor hee that hath most beautie. Faire faces should haue smoothe hearts. Womens beauties will waine, and then no art can make them faire? To be amiable and not to loue, is like a painted Lady, to haue colours, and no life. What call you deserts, what louely ▪ Haue not I an excellent wit? Foole? No, Celia, thou shalt find me as wise, as I doe thee proud, and as little to disgest thy taunts, as thou to brooke my loue. Sweete Celia, I will be patient and forget this. A hard choyse, if I take all well, to be a foole, if find fault, then to want patience. Farewell, nay I will follow, and I know not how it commeth to passe; disdaine increaseth desire, and the farther possibilitie standeth, the neerer approacheth hope. Coy Celia, bred vp in skoftes! Mine in pride intolerable, who biddeth me looke for no other comfort then contempt. Here is the tree. O mischiefe scarce to be belee ued, in possible to be pardoned! And mine. You see their posies is as their hearts; and their hearts as their speeches, cruell, proud and wauering: let vs all to the Temple of Cupid, and intreate his fauour, if not to obtaine their loues, yet to reuenge their hates, Cupid is a kinde god, who knowing our vnsported thoughts, will punish them, or release vs. Wee will studie what re uenge to haue, that our paines proceeding of our owne minds, then plagues may also proceed from theirs. Plot summary

Plot — Love's Metamorphosis
Three nymphs are loved by three foresters but reject them. The foresters offend Erisychthon, ruler of the forest, by cutting down a tree sacred to Ceres and blaspheming the goddess. The tree is revealed to contain Fidellia, a nymph transformed after being raped by a satyr, and she calls for revenge. Ceres punishes Erisychthon with perpetual hunger, driving him to attempt to sell his daughter Protea for food. Neptune grants Protea the power of metamorphosis, allowing her to escape. Meanwhile, the foresters seek help from Cupid, who punishes the disdainful nymphs through transformations: Nisa becomes a rock, Celia a rose, and Niobe a bird. Protea later saves her lover from a Siren’s temptation. Eventually Cupid and Ceres reverse the punishments, freeing Erisychthon from hunger and restoring the nymphs to their original forms. The nymphs finally accept the love of the foresters.
