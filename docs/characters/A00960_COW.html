Full speech

And will report your bounty in the Court. Nay to the King. Nay certainties. All courtly, courtly. They are the sneaking'st things, and the contemptiblest; Such small beere braines, but aske'em any thing Out of the Element of their understanding, And they stand gaping like a roasted Pig; Doe they know what a Court is or a Councell, Or how th' affaires of Christendome are manag'd? Doe they know any thing but a tyr'd hackney? And they cry absurd as the Horse understood'em. They have made a faire youth of your elder brother, A pretty peece of flesh. It will doe well, love those that love good fashions, Good clothes and rich, they invite men to admire'm, That speake the lispe of Court, Oh 'tis great learning! To ride well, daunce well, sing well, or whistle Courtly, Th'are rare endowments; that have seene farre Countries And can speake strange things, though they speake no truths, For then they make things common. When are you marryed? That's true, wee'll bee Knights then, Some wandring Knights, that light here on a sudden. You must not say so. You are a man of peace, Therefore we must give way. O blest travaile! How much we owe thee for our power to suffer? We have no quarrell to you, that we know on, Sir. Yet beare it not like you With such dejection. Pish, that's nothing, Tis Damnum reparabile, and soone recover'd. Flatt'ry rubbes out, But since great men learne to admire themselves, Tis something crest-falne. It is a faith, That we will die in, since from the blacke guard To the grimme Sir in office, there are few Hold other Tenets. Y'are the wiser. A meere bubble, For what's growne common is no more regarded. Yes, and a good one, A Millan hilt, and a Damasco blade, For ornament, no use the Court allowes it. I nere tri'd this, Yet I have worne as faire as any man, I'me sure I've made my Cutler rich, and paid For severall weapons, Turkish and Toledo's, Two thousand Crownes, and yet could never light Upon a fighting one. Tis at your service Sir, A lath in a velvet scabberd will serve my turne. Sure hee'll beat us, I see it in his eyes. We honour the Court As a most sacred place. Yes, we are all these, and more, If you will have it so. Here's a cold breakfast After a sharpe nights walking.

Plot summary

Plot — The Elder Brother
Louis wishes his lazy daughter Angelina would take more exercise, but her woman Sylvia has a better idea: she should get married. Unfortunately Angelina has high standards: when Louis proposes one or other of Brisac’s sons, Charles the scholar and Eustace the courtier, she objects that neither will have the money to keep her as she would wish. Louis pursues the idea anyway, but the bookish Charles, who has only ever seen one woman, his mother, has been put off marriage by the bad examples of antiquity. Louis is willing to accept Eustace, the younger brother, so long as Brisac agrees to disinherit Charles. Much to the disgust of Charles’s uncle, Miramont, a legal instrument is drawn up for the purpose. Charles seems unconcerned by his father’s plans, until he sees Angelina, whereupon he changes his mind about women, refuses to sign away his birthright, and woos the girl himself. She finds him more attractive than Eustace, and the wedding is aborted. Brisac throws him out; Angelina goes with him, and they live together in unmarried chastity. Spurred on by his father, Eustace visits them at night and tries to seize Angelina, but Charles puts up a fight and takes Eustace’s sword from him. Brisac has arranged for Charles’s servant Andrew to marry Lily, and given the couple a farm; but in reality Lily is his mistress and, on the same night as the brothers fight, he visits her for sex. Andrew brings Miramont to the farm to see the liaison for himself, and intervenes as Brisac is undressing for bed. Brisac tries to buy Andrew’s silence with a larger grant of land before realizing that there is a second witness. Eustace borrows a sword and confronts Charles, but Charles will not give up Angelina, and they fight. The bout is interrupted by the arrival of news that Brisac has been arrested by Louis, who insists on the return of Angelina (in the same condition as before). The brothers join forces to rescue their father. Miramont points out that, if he pursues the case, Louis will both ruin Angelina’s reputation and put himself in the control of lawyers; so Louis drops the matter. Miramont pays off the officers and insists that, as a penance, Brisac should abide by his offer to enlarge Andrew’s farm.
