O my Lord? Y'are wrong: i'th open streete? you see shees dead. You, goody Punck, subandi Cockatrice, O yare a sweet whore of your promise, are you not think you? how wel you came to supper to vs last night: mew, a whore & breake her word! nay you may blush, & hold downe your head at it wel ynough: Sfoot, aske these gallants if we staid not till we were as hungry as Seriants. Pox on her, let her alone. Sfoot, she guls em the best: this is alwaies her fashion, when she would be rid of any com pany that she cares not for, to inioy mine alone. Fluello, spurne your hounds when they fyste, you shall not spurne my Punk, I can tell you my bloud is vext. Y'are a Slaue, will that serue turne? Spurne my sweet Varlet! I, I, any where, sauing at Church: pray take heed we meet not there. Ha, ha, thou doest gull em so rarely, so naturally: if I did not think thou hadst bin in earnest: thou art a sweet Rogue for't ifayth. How's this? Ist possible, to be impossible, an honest whore! I haue heard many honest wenches turne Stru npets with a wet finger; but for a Harlot to turne honest, is one of Her cules labours: It was more easie for him in one night to make fifty queanes, then to make one of them honest a gen in fifty yeeres: come, I hope thou doost but iest. God buy thee. How, marry with a Punck, a Cockatrice, a Har lot? mary foh, Ile be burnt thorow the nose first. Ile heare no more of this: this ground vpon, Th'art damn'd for altring thy Religion. Exit. I well said locke vs into some little roome by our selues that we may be mad for an houre or two. How! no noise! do you know where you are: sfoot amonst all the mad-caps in Millan : so that to throw the house out at window will be the better, & no man will suspect that we lurke here to steale mutton: the more sober we are, the more scuruy tis. And tho the Frier tell vs, that heere we are safest, 'me not of his minde, for if those lay here that had lost there mony, none would euer looke after them, but heare are none but those that haue lost their wits, o that if hue and cry be made, hether theile come, and my reason is, because none goes to be married till he be starke mad. Why whats the matter. Pox on em, & there be no faith in men, if a man shall not beleeue oathes: he tooke bread and salt by this light, that he would neuer open his lips. No words I pray Fluello, for it stands vs vpon. My puncke turnd mad whore, as all her fellowes are? God send me good lucke. O your wit drops. O rare tricke: You ha learnt one mad point of Arithmaticke. Sonne, by yonder Sunne. Yes lamb. Out you mad Asse away. I thinke I rifled her of some such paltry Iewell. How, marry her my Lord? sfoot marry a mad-wo man: let a man get the tamest wife he can come by, sheele be mad enough afterward, doe what he can. I cannot tell, I may choose. Well then, when her wits stand in their right place, Cony-catcht, guld, must I saile in your flie-boate, Because I helpt to reare your maine-mast first: Plague found you fort, - tis well. Plot — 1 The Honest Whore (1 The Converted Courtesan) (The Patient Man and the Honest Whore)
Count Hippolito attempts to make a scene at the funeral of the Duke’s daughter, Infelice, but is restrained. In fact, Infelice is not dead: the Duke administered a sleeping draught to prevent her from marrying Hippolito. When she wakes, he tells her Hippolito is dead and sends her away to Bergamo, then accepts Dr Benedict’s offer to poison Hippolito. Bellafront the courtesan falls in love with Hippolito when his friend and her client Matheo brings him to meet her. He mistrusts her protestation that, for him, she will become honest, and speaks harshly against whores. But she fulfils her promise: dismisses her pander Roger (who quickly finds alternative employment) and sends away her former clients, including Matheo. On a Monday, she goes to visit Hippolito, disguised as a page; she finds him in solemn meditation (since Infelice supposedly died on a Monday), and he rejects her again. Dr Benedict tells the Duke that he has been poisoned, and is banished for it; but in fact he has done no such thing, and tells Hippolito that Infelice is not only alive, but waiting at the madhouse to marry him. Meanwhile, Fustigo returns to Milan to find that his sister has married Candido, a man apparently incapable of anger. This is demonstrated in a wager between three gentlemen who try to make him lose his temper: first Castruccio poses as a customer and makes unreasonable demands, which Candido meets even though it means spoiling a piece of cloth; then Fluello steals a beaker from the household, for which Candido has them arrested, but then genially invites them to dinner. Viola also wants to make her husband angry, and has Fustigo pose as a swaggerer and attempt to seduce her. When he tries, however, Candido’s apprentices take it upon themselves to beat him while their master is away, and Candido’s equanimity remains intact. Viola continues to behave provocatively: she refuses Candido the key to fetch his gown for a senate meeting, so he goes wearing a carpet instead; she has George dress in his clothes and 1604 pose as him on his return, but Candido’s good-natured response is to dress himself as an apprentice; and he even looks on the bright side of a beating administered by ruffians hired by Fustigo to avenge his injury. Finally Viola has him committed to the madhouse, but soon changes her mind and petitions the Duke for his release.
