What, vp already Scudmore, neare a Wench with thee? Not thy Laundresse? What's this? A Letter; Sure it is not so, A Letter written to Hieronimo ! Not for the world my friend, Farewell, Good-morrow. Exiturus. Worthy friend, I leaue you not in anger: What de'e meane? Nor am I of that inquisitiue Nature fram'd, To thirst to know your priuate businesses: Why, they concerne not me; If they be ill And dangerous, t'would greeue me much to know em. If good; they be so, though I know em not: Nor would I do your loue so grosse a wrong, To Couet, to participate affaires Of that neere touch, which your assured loue Doth rhinke not fit, or dares not trust me with. I do beleeue you, farewell worthy friend. Tis your Iealousie That makes you thinke it so, for by my soule You have giuen me no distast, in keeping from me All things that might be burthenous, and oppresse me. In troth I am inuited to a wedding, And the Morne faster goes away from me, Then I go toward it: and so Good-morrow. Now by my life I not desire it Sir, Nor euer lou'd these prying listening men, That aske of other states and passages, Not one among a hundered but proues false, Enuious and slanderous, and wil cut that throat He twines his armes about, I loue that Poet That gaue vs reading, not to seeke our selues Beyond our selues, Farewell. Now if you loue me, do not wrong me so, I see you labour with some serious thing, And thinke (like Fayries Treasure) to reueale it, Will cause it vanish; and yet to conceale it Will burst your breast, tis so delicious, And so much greater then the Continent. Yet thinke on't, keepe thy secret, and thy friend Sure and entire; Oh! giue not me the meanes To become false hereafter; or thy selfe A probable reason to distrust thy friend, Though he be neare so true, I will not see't. I read it fearelesse of the forfeiture, Yet warne you, be as Cautelous, not to wound My integritie, with doubt, on likelyhoods, From misreport, but first exquire the Truth. Yours through the world, & to the end of time. Bellafront. Which, Bellafront Rich, Sir Iohn Wordlies Daughter? Oh heauen! we speake like Goddes, and do like Dogges. This day, this Bellafront the Rich Heire, Is married vnto Count Fredericke, And that's the wedding I was going to. It is no matter to be plaid withall, But euen as true as women all are false. For shame returne thy blood into thy face, Know'st not how flight a thing a woman is? Oh! prethee run not thus into the streets, Come dresse you better, so: Ah! as thy cloaths Are like thy mind, too much disorder'd. How strangely is this Tide turn'd? For a world I would not but haue cal'd heere, as I went. Collect thy Spirits, we will vse all meanes To checke this blacke fate, flying toward thee; Come, If thou miscarriest, tis my day of doome. Yes Sir, the eldest marries Count Erederick. But looke ye, you shall see I'me a Deuine, Of Conscience quite opposite to a Lawyer, Ile giue you Counsell Sir without a fee: This way they are to come, if you dare doo't, Challenge her as your owne, at the Church doore, I will not hinder you. Musicke playes. Plot summary

Plot — A Woman Is a Weathercock
Scudmore is in love with Bellafront, and his friend Nevill persuades him, with difficulty, to show the love- letter he has received from her. There is a slight complication: Nevill is on his way to .Bellafront’s wedding. She is to marry Count Frederick that morning. The whole family is mired in matrimonial competition. Bellafront’s sister Lucida also loves Frederick, and has sworn never to marry for his sake; but she is being courted by the foolish Sir Abraham Ninny, who has got himself knighted expressly to make himself a more eligible son-in-law. The youngest daughter, Katherine, is also to be married that morning, to Strange the merchant, but she too has another admirer, Captain Pouts. Unbeknown to Scudmore, Nevill impersonates the parson who is to officiate at the wedding. Scudmore tries to catch Bellafront at the church door, but she refuses him; this convinces him that all women are perfidious. After the wedding, Pouts publicly declares that Katherine has slept with him on many previous occasions. Strange seems reluctant to seek legal redress for this slander, but only because he doesn’t want anyone to stop him secretly taking a more immediate revenge. He tries to see Pouts at his lodging, but Pouts assumes he has come to serve a writ and will not let him in. Pouts then prepares to flee England and his creditors, but Strange intercepts him disguised as a soldier, claiming that Katherine engaged him to murder her new husband and that she loves only Pouts. Pouts admits that he slandered her; Strange wounds him in a fight and forces him to sign a document affirming her innocence. By a pretext, Scudmore gets in to see Bellafront, rebukes her, and returns her love-letters. She tells him that her father forced her to marry Frederick, and that she will kill herself rather than consummate the marriage. He mistrusts her, but Nevill arranges for him to participate in a wedding masque that evening. He and Bellafront sneak away during the dancing and are married, this time by the right parson. Suspicions of polyandry are dispelled when Nevill explains that the parson that morning was really him, so that the marriages are invalid. Strange brings Pouts to apologise to the gathering, but is almost arrested for his own murder until he reveals his identity. Pouts returns to the war, abjuring women.
