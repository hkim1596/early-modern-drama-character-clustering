Two Planets raind at once, Venus thats you, And Mars thats I, were in coniunction. Ha, ha, to see the world, we swaggerers That liue by oathes and big-mouth'd menaces, Are now reputed for the tallest men: He that hath now a black muchato Reaching from eare to eare, or turning vp Puncto reuerso, bristling towards the eye: He that can hang two hansom tooles at his side, Go in disguisde attire, weare Iron enough, Is held a tall man and a souldier. He that with greatest grace can sweare gogs zounds, Or in a Tauerne make a drunken fray, Can cheat at Dice, swagger in bawdie houses, Weare veluet on his face, and with a grace Can face it out with as I am a souldier. He that can clap his sword vpon the boord Hee's a braue man, and such a man am I. Is an old Bawd as you are Mistris Splay. And will and must: if you bid me be gone, I will withdraw, and draw on any he That in the worlds wide round dare cope with me. Mistris farewell, to none I neuer speake So kind a word. My saluations are, Farewell and be hangd, or in the diuels name. What they haue bene my many fraies can tell, You cannot fight therefore to you farewell. Wheres mistris Mary, neuer a post here, A bar of Iron gainst which to trie my sword? Now by my beard a daintie peece of steele. Come hither Mall, is none here but we two? When didst thou see the starueling Schoole-maister? That Rat, that shrimp, that spindleshanck, that Wren, that sheep-biter, that leane chittiface, that famine, that leane Enuy, that all bones, that bare Anatomy, that Iack a Lent, that ghost, that shadow, that Moone in the waine. When next I finde him here Ile hang him vp Like a dried Sawsedge, in the Chimnies top: That Stock-fish, that poore Iohn, that gut of men. When he comes next turne him into the streets, Now come lets dance the shaking of the sheets. O M. Iustice, M. Arthur, M. Mistris I long haue seru'd you, euen since These brisled hayres vpon my graue like chin Were all vnborne: when I first came to you These Infant feathers of these rauens wings, Were not once begunne. Now in my two Muchatoes for a need, Wanting a rope, I well could hang my selfe: I prithee Mistris, for all my long seruice, For all the loue that I haue borne thee long, Do me this fauour now to marry me. Not haue her will, sir she shall haue her will, She saies she will, and sir I say she shall. Not haue her will? that were a Ieast indeed. Who saies she shall not, if I be disposde To man her forth, who shall finde fault with it? What's he that dare say black's her eie? Though you be married sir, yet you must know That she was euer borne to haue her will. Shall he? he shall not: breathes there any liuing, Dares say he shall, when Brabo saies he shall not? What stands the villain prating, hence you slaue. It is our pleasure sir to stand aside. Why whats the matter Mistris? Mistris I will, he shall not passe this land But I will bring him bound with this strong hand. Here is the murtherer, and Reasons man You haue the warrant: Sirs laie hands on him, Attach the slaue, and lead him bound to death. Constables forward, forward Officers, I will not thrust my finger in the fire. Laie hands on him I say, why step you backe? I meane to be the hindmost, least that any Should runne away and leaue the rest in perill: Stand forward, are you not asham'd to feare? Plot summary

Plot — How a Man May Choose a Good Wife from a Bad
Arthur has fallen out with his wife: he is bored with her, convinced she is always trying to put him down, and also just wants a change. When the couple’s fathers visit in an effort to achieve marital harmony, they enter without knocking and find Arthur beating his wife. Her father, Old Lusam, wants to take her home, but she won’t go; she also resists the efforts of Anselm to seduce her and rejects his criticisms of her husband. The fathers take her to Justice Reason in an attempt to have him intervene, but she will not co-operate and Reason will not get involved. Arthur becomes attracted to Mary, a courtesan whom the schoolmaster Aminadab also loves. Seeing her attentions to Arthur makes Aminadab suicidal, and he tries to obtain poison from Fuller, pretending he needs it for an infestation of rats; Fuller gives him a sleeping potion instead. Seeking to kill his wife, Arthur takes the ‘poison’ from Aminadab under cover of preventing his suicide, and holds a dinner party. One of the guests is Mary, but Mistress Arthur puts up even with this, only to be given ‘poisoned’ wine by her husband. Mistress Arthur is interred in her family vault. Anselm visits her tomb and she revives when he kisses her. He hides her in his mother’s house, but despite everything she remains faithful to her husband 1601 and resists her rescuer’s advances. Meanwhile Arthur marries Mary, but finds her an unloving, disobedient, spendthrift wife. In an attempt to win her trust, he tells her the guilty secret of his first wife’s death; she immediately puts the information in the hands of the authorities, and Arthur is forced to go on the run. Rejected by his family, Arthur is starving when he calls at the house of Anselm’s mother and meets a young woman who reminds him of (and, of course, is) his ‘dead’ wife. Stricken with conscience, he gives himself up and is brought before Justice Reason. The use of poison, which it is felony to sell, brings first Aminadab, then Fuller into trouble, until Mistress Arthur arrives and the truth comes out. After his experiences, Arthur now knows the difference between a good wife and a bad.
