I thanke you Sir, Nephew to the old Lady, his name is Wildbraine, And wild his best condition. Had Hartlove no fast friends? I am sorry fort, Here comes poore Franke, nay we are friends, start not Sir, We see your willow and are sorry for, And though it be a wedding we are halfe mourners. Come good Franke, Forget now, since there is no remedy, And shew a merry face, as wise men would doe. And so doe I too. But the Plague is, heele burne out instantly; Give him another cup. Come have a good heart, And win him like a bowle to lye close to you, Make your best use. This is some tricke. Is it possible? Hold, hold, he's desperate too. What booke has he given thee? Dost thou beleeve it? I have John Taylors booke of Hempeseede too, Which for two lines I hapned on by chance, I reverence. They are so put upon the time, as if He studied to answer the late Histriomastix, Talking of change and transformations, That wittily, and learnedly he bangs him, So may a Puritans ruffe, though starched in Print, Be turn'd to Paper, and a Play writ in't: A Play in the Puritans ruffe? Ile buy his Workes for't, And confute Horace with a Water Poet: What hast there, a ballad too? Some cry ith' streets; prethee sing on. Tis a strange noise? and has a tang o'the Iustice. Vnty his feete, pull out his gagge, he will choake else; What desperate rogues were these. He gave us bookes sir, dainty bookes to busie us; And we were reading in that which was the Brewhouse; A great way off, we were finging ballads too, And could not heare. Shall we pursue 'em? Your little Prayer booke? But the keyes in 'em, and if they be gone, They could not want wit to locke us in sir. The likest face — She gabbles innocently. Lets interrupt him, hee'le be mad our right else. Tis not Maria. We are helhounds helhounds, that have commission From the Prince of darkenesse, To fetch thy blacke soule to him. Thou art, but we have brought thee instruments Will quickly rid thy miserable life, Stabbe, Hang thy selfe, this choyse is offer'd And all the Fiends expect thee. Come with us to that pit of endlesse horrour, Or we will force thee. Grones are too late, sooner the ravisher, Whose soule is hurl'd into eternall frost, Stung with the force of twenty thousand Winters To punish the distempers of his blood, Shall hope to get from thence, then thou avoid The certaintie of meeting hell where he is. Shall murderers be there for ever dying, Their soules shot through with adders, torne on Engines, Dying as many deaths for killing one, Could any imagination number them, As there be moments in eternity: And shall that Justice spare thee, that hast slaine, Murdered by thy extortion so many? Plot summary

Plot — The Night Walker, or The Little Thief
The usurer Justice Algripe has seized the wastrel Lurcher’s lands to settle a mortgage; he was also amorously interested in Lurcher’s sister, who has since disappeared. The destitute Lurcher is left to steal for a living; invited to join him, his friend Wildbrain prefers womanizing, on the grounds that it will not end on the gallows. Lurcher is approached by a boy, Snap, and, after ascertaining his skills at deception, takes him into his service. Algripe marries Wildbrain’s cousin Maria, who consented out of filial obedience rather than love: she loves Hartlove. Her mother’s household opposes the match, suspecting that old Algripe is impotent, and Wildbrain arranges for Hartlove to meet her on her way to bed and cuckold Algripe on his wedding night. She refuses his advances and faints when they are found together; she is taken for dead and prepared for burial. Her mother throws Wildbrain out for his part in the incident. Lurcher burgles the house that night, frightening the servants by pretending to be a ghost; but what he supposes to be a chest of jewels is actually Maria’s coffin. Upon discovering what their swag really is, he and his accomplices attempt to bury her in secret, but have a chance encounter with Algripe in the churchyard. Snap frightens him away by impersonating Maria’s ghost, but the thieves are scared off themselves when Maria wakes up in her coffin. Wildbrain meets Hartlove in the dark; Hartlove bears him a grudge and fights him. Maria hears the commotion and pretends to be her own ghost, enjoining them not to fight. Wildbrain is already wounded, and takes refuge in the next house he finds, which belongs to Lurcher’s mistress and partner in crime. They fall for one another and he shows off her gifts to Lurcher, who recognizes them as stolen 1615 property — his own booty. Maria’s mother accuses Algripe of stealing the body; when he refuses to repay the marriage portion, she begins litigation. Lurcher cons his way into Algripe’s house posing as a bookseller, ties him up, and steals his valuables and papers, including his own mortgage. Maria returns to her mother’s household posing as her nurse’s Welsh niece. Struck by the resemblance to dead Maria, Hartlove falls in love with her. Lurcher impersonates a constable and takes Algripe to a church where, he says, the villains who robbed him are hiding out; he then drugs Algripe and stows him in the vault.
