"If what to some séemes honest, good and iust, "Could séeme euen so in euery doubtfull mind, "No darke debate nor quarell could arise: "But looke, how many men so many minds, "And that, that one man iudgeth good and iust, "Some other déemes as déepely to be wrong. To say the truth (mother) this minde of mine Doth fléete full farre from that farfetch of his, Ne will I longer couer my conceit: If I could rule or reigne in heauen aboue, And eke commaund in depth of derksome hell, No toile ne trauell should my sprites abashe, To make the way vnto my restlesse will, To climbe aloft, nor downe for to descend. Then thinke you not, that I can yeld consent To yeld a parte of my possession, Wherein I liue and lead the monarchie. "A witlesse foole may euery man him gesse, "That leaues the more and takes him to the lesse. With this, reproch might to my name redound, If he, that hath with forren power spoilde Our pleasaunt fields, might reaue from me perforce, What so he list by force of armes demand. No lesse reproofe the citizens ensewes, If I, for dread of Gréekish hosts, should graunt That he might climbe to height of his desire. In fine, he ought not thus of me to craue Accord or peace, with bloudy sword in hand, But with humilitie and prayer both. For often is it séen , and proofe doth teach, "Swete words preuaile, where sword and fire faile. Yet this, if here within these stately walles He list to liue, the sonne of Oedipus, And not as king of Thebes, I stand content. But let him thinke, since now I can commaunde, This necke of mine shall neuer yeld to yoke Of seruitude: let bring his banners splaide, Let speare and shielde, sharpe sworde, and cyndring flame Procure the parte that he so vainely claimes: As long as life within this brest doth last, I nill consent that he should reigne with me. When thou ariuest in Greece, Chuse out thy dwelling in some mustie Moores. Away I say out of these walls. And canst thou yet With shamelesse tong once name thy noble race That art become a common foe to Thebes? Be sure thou shalt them neuer sée againe, Nor other friend that in these walls remaine. Ah wretch, my thirstis all as drie as thine. And that right soone the proofe shall playnely shewe. O slow & sluggish heart of mine, Why do I stay t' embrew these slouthfull hands? But for his greater griefe I will departe, And at returne if here I finde my foe, This hastie hande shall ende our hote debate.

Plot summary

Plot — Jocasta
Oedipus unknowingly murdered his father and married his mother Jocasta; on learning the truth, he blinded himself. His sons imprisoned him and agreed to alternate annually as kings of Thebes: Polynices went into exile while Eteocles took the first turn on the throne; but Eteocles refused to give it up at the end of his year in office, and Polynices has besieged Thebes in association with the King of Argos. Jocasta intervenes as their mother, and persuades Polynices to enter Thebes and meet Eteocles under truce. Eteocles will not hand over power, but offers to accept his brother back as a private citizen. Jocasta is unable to reconcile her sons, and Polynices’ forces prepare for battle. Eteocles names Creon as his successor, and asks him to marry Antigone to his son Haemon. Creon consults Tiresias, and learns that Thebes is doomed to destruction in divine revenge for Jocasta’s incest; only the death of his other son, Menoeceus, can save the city. Keen to die for his country, Menoeceus refuses to go into exile as his father wishes, reports the prophecy to Eteocles, and kills himself. Meanwhile, the battle is indecisive, and Eteocles and Polynices agree to settle their dispute in single combat. Jocasta and Antigone are too late to stop them killing each other: arriving to find them both dying, Jocasta stabs herself with Polynices’ dagger. The besieging army is massacred by the Thebans. Antigone brings the three bodies back to Thebes and tells her father what has happened. Creon declares himself King of Thebes and gives order for the funerals of Jocasta and Eteocles, but demands that Polynices, as an enemy of the city, should remain unburied. Antigone protests, pleads, and refuses to marry Haemon, threatening to kill him and then herself should Creon try to force her. Creon banishes both her and Oedipus. She helps her blind father to touch the dead bodies, and he asks her to see that Polynices is buried.
