Full speech

Yes trust me: I wondred who should be so bolde to send for me, you are welcome to Millan brother. Very well; you ha travelled enough now, I trowe, to sowe your wilde oates. These are your olde oaths. Well, well, you shall have them: put your selfe into better fashion, because I must imploy you in a serious matter. You ha cast off all your olde swaggering humours. I am the more sory, for I must imploy a true swagge rer. Then lend me your eares. I am married to a man that haz wealth enough, and wit enough. Very true, a grave Cittizen; I want nothing that a wife can wish from a husband: but heeres the spite, hee haz not all things belonging to a man. No losse of goods can increase in him a wrinckle, no crabbed language make his countenance sowre, the stubburn nes of no servant shake him, he haz no more gall in him than a Dove, no more sting than an Ant: Musitian will he never bee, (yet I finde much musicke in him,) but he loves no frets, and is so free from anger, that many times I am ready to bite off my tongue, because it wants that vertue which all womens tongues have (to anger their husbands:) Brother, mine can by no thun der: turne him into a sharpenes. I protest to thee Fustigo, I love him most affecti onately, but I know not — I ha such a tickling with in mee — such a strange longing; nay, verily I doo long. Y'are wide ath bow hand still brother: my longings are not wanton, but wayward: I long to have my patient hus band eate vp a whole Porcupine, to the intent, the bristling quills may sticke about his lippes like a flemmish, mustacho, and be shot at me: I shall be leaner than the new Moone, vn lesse I can make him borne mad. Puh, he would count such a cut no vnkindenes. Fie, fie, idle, idle, hee's no French-man, to fret at the losse of a little scalde haire. No brother, thus it shall be, you must be secret. Repaire to the Tortoys heere in S. Christophers streete, I will send you mony, turne your selfe into a brave man: insteed of the armes of your mistris, let your sword and your militarie scarfe hang about your necke. O, by any meanes, to shew your light head, else your hat will sit like a coxcombe: to be briefe, you must bee in all points a most terrible wide-mouth'd swaggerer. Resort then to our shop, and (in my husbands presence) kisse me, snatch rings, jewells, or any things so you give it backe agen brother in secret. Sweare as if you came but new from knight ing. Swagger worse then a Lievetenant among fresh-wa ter souldiers, call me your love, your yngle, your coosen, or so; but sister at no hand. Haz heard I have a brother, but never saw him, there fore put on a good face. Take vp wares, but pay nothing, rifle my bosome, my pocket, my purse, the boxes for mony to dice with all; but bro ther, you must give all backe agen in secret. Be circumspect, and do so then, farewell. Thither Ile send: this law can none deny, Women must have their longings, or they die. Exit.

Plot — 1 The Honest Whore (1 The Converted Courtesan) (The Patient Man and the Honest Whore)
Count Hippolito attempts to make a scene at the funeral of the Duke’s daughter, Infelice, but is restrained. In fact, Infelice is not dead: the Duke administered a sleeping draught to prevent her from marrying Hippolito. When she wakes, he tells her Hippolito is dead and sends her away to Bergamo, then accepts Dr Benedict’s offer to poison Hippolito. Bellafront the courtesan falls in love with Hippolito when his friend and her client Matheo brings him to meet her. He mistrusts her protestation that, for him, she will become honest, and speaks harshly against whores. But she fulfils her promise: dismisses her pander Roger (who quickly finds alternative employment) and sends away her former clients, including Matheo. On a Monday, she goes to visit Hippolito, disguised as a page; she finds him in solemn meditation (since Infelice supposedly died on a Monday), and he rejects her again. Dr Benedict tells the Duke that he has been poisoned, and is banished for it; but in fact he has done no such thing, and tells Hippolito that Infelice is not only alive, but waiting at the madhouse to marry him. Meanwhile, Fustigo returns to Milan to find that his sister has married Candido, a man apparently incapable of anger. This is demonstrated in a wager between three gentlemen who try to make him lose his temper: first Castruccio poses as a customer and makes unreasonable demands, which Candido meets even though it means spoiling a piece of cloth; then Fluello steals a beaker from the household, for which Candido has them arrested, but then genially invites them to dinner. Viola also wants to make her husband angry, and has Fustigo pose as a swaggerer and attempt to seduce her. When he tries, however, Candido’s apprentices take it upon themselves to beat him while their master is away, and Candido’s equanimity remains intact. Viola continues to behave provocatively: she refuses Candido the key to fetch his gown for a senate meeting, so he goes wearing a carpet instead; she has George dress in his clothes and 1604 pose as him on his return, but Candido’s good-natured response is to dress himself as an apprentice; and he even looks on the bright side of a beating administered by ruffians hired by Fustigo to avenge his injury. Finally Viola has him committed to the madhouse, but soon changes her mind and petitions the Duke for his release.
