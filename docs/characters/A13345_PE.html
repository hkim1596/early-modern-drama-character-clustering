No in very deed ſir, is a towardly young gentleman, ſhall a haue my young Miſtris, your daughter, I pray you ſir? As ere came in parchment, but ſee, here comes my young lord. Truſt me a ſayes wel for the moſt part. Yes indeed ſhee's an early ſtirrer, and I doubt not here after, but that your lordſhip may ſay ſhee's abroad before you can riſe. No and it like your lordſhip, nor is fit ſhe ſhould. Indeed your lordſhips conqueſt would haue ſeem'd the greater. For any thing I know, your lordſhip may goe without her. God giue you ioy. Afore god is a moſt vnanſwerable lord, and holds her toot ifaith. Yes indeed an't like your lordſhip. Ile warrant you my lord, ſhee's your owne, for ile giue out to all that comes neere her, that ſhees betrothed to you, and if the worſt come to the worſt, Ile ſweare it. Anon ſir. Why ſir being vndone himſelfe; a liues by the vndoing or by-lady, it may be by the doing of others, or peraduenture both a decayed gallant may liue by any thing, if a keepe one thing ſafe. Wilt pleaſe you to taſt a cup of September beare gentiem. The gentleman here fals out with me, vpon nothing in the world but mere courteſie, Is his name Lighfoote, a plague on him, a has a heauie hande. Three at the leaſt ſir. Yes indeed ſir. I thinke a doſen howers my Lord, and tis nothing, Ile vndertake to ſleepe ſixteene, vpon the receipt of two cups of muskadine. Hang'em flinchers they ſluncke away as ſoone as they had druncke as much as they were able to carry, which no ge nerous ſpirit would a done in deed. Come ſoftly by any meanes, for we are now vpon the threſhold of my maſters chamber, through which ile bring you to Miſtris Rebeckaes lodging, giue me your hand and come very nicely. O my Lord aboue, ſtand ſtill, I am falne downe at leaſt 30. fathome deepe, if you ſtand not ſtill till I recouer and haue lighten a candle, y'are but a dead man. Where are you my Lord? Breaking my necke in your ſeruice a plague ont. Shees not in the houſe ſir? the ſtreete doores are wide open. Good my Lord promiſe him to marry his daughter, or a will be mad preſently, though you neuer intend to haue her. Till which be had, my fare will be but cold. Yet if you pleaſe, ſir Iohn will backe ſurrender The ouerplus of what you now did tender. O pardon ſir, for by our worſhips leaue, We ought to giue from whence we doe receaue. A good night to all. NOW expectation hath at full receiued What we late promiſed, if in ought we haue pleaſed, Tis all vve ſought to accompliſh, and much more Then our vveake merrit dares to attribute Vnto it ſelfe, till you vouchſafe to dayne In your kinde cenſure, ſo to gratifie Our triuiall labours: — If it hath pleaſed the iudiciall eare, Wee haue our Authors wiſh, and void of feare Dare ignorant men, to ſhew their worſt of hate. It not detracts, but adds vnto that ſtate Where deſert floriſheth. Plot summary

Plot — The Hog Hath Lost His Pearl
Haddit has spent all his money and mortgaged his lands to Hog the usurer, and now ekes out a living selling jigs to players. He aims to solve his financial problems by marrying Rebecca, Hog’s daughter, whose affections he had previously refused in the belief that she was too low-born for him. Hog intends Rebecca to marry young Lord Wealthy, but she refuses, and helps Haddit trick her father. Haddit’s uncle, Lightfoot, helps to get the servant Peter out of the way by picking a quarrel, to give him a pretext for making peace with a drinking contest; Peter imbibes himself insensible in the cellar. Rebecca steals Hog’s keys, giving Haddit and his accomplices access to his bedroom, where he keeps his gold. Lightfoot appears to Hog as the ghost of Croesus, come to make the usurer rich: a spirit will ===== PAGE 291 ===== 1613 take his gold and transform it into pearl, which is even more valuable; in reality, Haddit steals the money and retrieves his mortgage, then absconds with Rebecca and marries her. Hog goes to complain to old Lord Wealthy, but his supernatural experiences induce a conversion: he renounces avarice and gives the couple his blessing. In the sub-plot, Carracus plans to elope with young Lord Wealthy’s sister Maria, and entrusts his friend Albert with the arrangements. Albert visits Maria early, posing as Carracus, and has sex with her; she then leaves with Carracus as planned, and marries him: Albert suffers remorse and decides to leave human society. Some time later, Carracus notices that Maria is wearing Albert’s ring, which he gave her on the night of the elopement. Both realize what Albert did; Maria faints and Carracus is told that she has died of shame. In fact, she disguises herself as a page and flees to the woods, where Albert has carved penitential verses on every tree. Posing as a hermit, Albert finds her almost dead from starvation; he takes her to his cave; and feeds her. Carracus also goes to the woods, where an echo tells him that Maria is alive and innocent. They meet up, and revelations ensue: the page is chaste Maria and the hermit is penitent Albert. Carracus and Maria forgive him, and make their peace with Maria’s family.
