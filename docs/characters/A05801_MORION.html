Full speech

Oh my Lord, my father is comming to your Grace, with such a many of Damsons and shee Shittle cockes: They smell of nothing in the world but Rozin and Coblers waxe; such a many lights in their heeles, & lungs in their hands, aboue all cry, yfaith. By my troth, my stomacke rumbleth at the ve ry conceit of this Iamall loue, euen from the sole of my head, to the crowne of the foote. Surely, I will haue more acquaintance of that Gentlewoman; me thinks she daunceth like a Hobby-horse. Come, Ratsbane: Oh the intolerable paine that I suffer for the loue of the Fayry Queene! my heeles are all kybde in the very heate of my affection, that runnes down into my legs: me thinkes I could eate vp a whole Brokers shoppe at a meale, to be eased of this loue. I assure thee, thou fayest true, tis but grosse meate. But Ratsbane, thou toldst mee of a rare fellovv, that can tell misfortunes, and can coniure: prethee bring me to him. Ile giue him somewhat, to helpe mee to speake with the Fayry Queene. Whose face like to a Butchers doublet lookes, Varnisht with tallow of some beautious Oxe; Or like the aprons of some Pie-corner Cookes, Whose breath smels sweeter then a hunted Foxe: Whose eyes, like two great foot-balls made of lether, Were made to heate the gods in frosty weather. Sir, and't shall please you, I come to know some of that excellent skill, the world hath blisterde mine eares with. Oh helpe me quickly; Come, Ratsbane, vncase, my loue is come. Great Queene, thou soueraigne of Pheanders heart, Vouchsafe a word vnto thy Mayden Knight, That bowes his guts vnto thy mighty face. Helpe, Ratsbane, helpe, helpe. Oh father, father, ffoot, I sweate, as if I had been buried in a Tunne of hote graynes. My Father knowes all. Indeed, father, the naked truth is, that the Fayry Queene robd me of all my clothes: you might haue seen me as poore as an Open-arse. But I can tell you newes; the King is poysoned; Lord Codigune crowned; The Lady Guiniuer, & the young Gentlewoman imprisoned. Why, I pray you, father, when did you heare a Gentleman of Wales tell lyes?

Plot summary

Plot — The Valiant Welshman (Caradoc the Great)
The Earl of. Monmouth seeks to usurp old King Octavian’s throne, and has won two significant victories over him. Cadallan and his sons come to support Octavian. Cadallan is killed in the ensuing battle, but his son Caradoc avenges him by killing Monmouth. Re-established on the throne, Octavian rewards Caradoc with marriage to his daughter and heir, Guinevere. This displeases his bastard son, Codigune, who had regarded Guinevere as his only obstacle to the succession. King Guiderius has failed to pay tribute to the Romans, so the Emperor Claudius invades Britain. Guiderius appeals to Octavian, and Caradoc is sent, fresh from his wedding, to offer military aid. In his absence, Codigune conspires with Gloucester and Cornwall: they poison Octavian and imprison Guinevere, and Codigune seizes power. Gloucester writes warning Guiderius not to trust Caradoc, so Guiderius, about to go into battle, orders the Welsh troops to observe from a hilltop and intervene only ifa British defeat seems likely. Caradoc disregards this, joins the battle in disguise, and bests Claudius in hand- to-hand combat; he refuses Claudius’ offer to buy his freedom, but accepts a token of the Emperor’s friendship, a golden lion. Prince Gald is impressed by Caradoc’s single-handed contribution to the British victory, accompanies him back to Wales, and marries his sister Voada. Finding Codigune installed as King of Wales, Caradoc offers to settle matters in single combat. Codigune is beaten, refuses an offer to make him Viceroy of North Wales, and flees to the Romans. Cornwall submits to Caradoc, who is crowned King. Gloucester flees to make more mischief: he persuades a witch to create a ravaging monster which Caradoc will be expected to fight, and which will kill him. The Romans send Caradoc an ultimatum, requiring him to resign the throne to Codigune; at the same time, he is summoned to deal with the monster, a giant serpent. He gives priority to the serpent, and defeats it with the help of a magic herb, then disposes of the witch and forces her son Bluso to swear that in future he will use his magic powers only for good. Gloucester hangs himself. While Caradoc is away, Cornwall hands over the town of Gloucester to the Romans. Gallicus, the Roman legate who brought the ultimatum, falls in love with Voada and abducts her. Bluso enables Gald to visit her bedchamber, magically unseen.
