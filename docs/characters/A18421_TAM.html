Full speech

Those vvith faire warnings might haue beene reform'd, Not these vnmanly rages. You haue heard The fiction of the North winde and the Sunne, Both vvorking on a Traueller, and contending Which had most power to take his cloake from him: Which when the Winde attempted, hee roar'd out Outragious blasts at him to force it off, That vvrapt it closer on. When the calme Sunne (The Winde once leauing) charg'd him vvith still beames, Quiet, and feruent, and therein was constant, Which made him cast off both his cloake and coate: Like vvhom should men doe. If yee vvish your Wiues Should leaue dislik'd things, seeke it not vvith rage; For that enrages: vvhat yee giue, yee haue: But vse calme warnings, and kinde manly meanes, And that in Wiues most prostitute will winne Not onely sure amends; but make vs Wiues Better then those that ne'er led faultie liues. I see y'are Seruant, sir, to my deare sister, The Lady of her lou'd Baligny. She writes to mee as much, and much desires, That you may be the man, whose spirit shee knowes Will cut short off these long and dull delayes, Hitherto to bribing the eternall Iustice: Which I beleeue, since her vnmatched spirit Can iudge of spirits, that haue her sulphure in them; But I must tell you, that I make no doubt, Her liuing brother will reuenge her dead, On whom the dead impos'd the taske ▪ and hee, I know, will come t'effect it instantly. See; this is the vault, where he must enter: Where now I thinke hee is. One that your selfe will say, I well may trust. See her Letter sir. Good Madame, I rue your fate, more then mine, And know not how to order these affaires, They stand on such occurrents. O my seruant! let vs embrace. I, none. That you may doe well, It is the bloud you lately shed, you smell. What vault? hold your sword. Trust not his othe. Hee will lie like a Lapwing, when shee flyes Farre from her sought nest, still here tis shee cryes. Out on my fortune to wed such an abiect. Now is the peoples voyce, the voyce of God; Hee that to wound a vvoman vants so much, (As hee did mee) a man dares neuer touch. Come sir, good sir hold him. I sir, shee sent him, With this kinde Letter, to performe the vvreake Of my deare Seruant. O it breakes my heart. Misery on misery! O me wretched Dame Of all that breath, all heauen turne all his eyes, In harty enuie, thus on one poore dame. Hide, hide thy snakie head, to Cloisters flie, In pennance pine, too easie tis to die.

Plot — The Revenge of Bussy D'Ambois
Before the start of the action, the Duke of Guise had Bussy D’Amboise and his brother Clermont brought ===== PAGE 63 ===== 1610 to court. Montsoreau had Bussy killed in reprisal for his adultery with Tamyra, and he died prophesying the deaths of Monsieur and Guise; his ghost then appeared to Clermont demanding revenge. Their sister Charlotte also seeks to avenge Bussy, and made her marriage to Baligny conditional on his swearing to participate in the plot; but Clermont insists on doing it alone, as the ghost commanded. Baligny’s role is, therefore, to deliver Clermont’s challenge to Montsoreau; but he cannot get access because Montsoreau has fearfully barricaded himself into his house. Two allies help him. Renel, who is selling his lands to Montsoreau, enables him to get in. When Montsoreau refuses to accept the challenge, Baligny leaves it behind anyway and Tamyra, who has reluctantly gone back to her husband, promises to make him read it. Unknown to the others, Baligny is reporting to the King on potential troublemakers like Clermont and Renel. The King is concerned at the growing power of Guise’s faction, so he and Baligny arrange for Clermont to be arrested: Clermont is to travel with Baligny to inspect the troops at Cambrai, and there he will be taken into custody by Baligny’s lieutenant, Maillard; thus Baligny will not have to participate directly in his brother-in-law’s downfall. Clermont has to leave Paris before concluding his business with Montsoreau. In Cambrai, he receives an anonymous letter warning him of Baligny’s treachery, but refuses to credit it. Maillard arrests him, with difficulty, and he is taken back to the Bastille; but he is allowed to send a message to his lover, the Countess of Cambrai, who was expecting him to visit her that evening. ~ Guise pressurizes the King into signing an order for .
