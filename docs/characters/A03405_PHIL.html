Full speech

I Geometry. All this wee needes must know assuredly. Although I am not satisfied in this, It doth me good to heare him thus discourse. In nothing am I better pleas'd, let's goe. The nut-brwne ale, the nut-browne ale, Puts downe all drinke when it is stale, The toast, the Nut-meg, and the ginger, Will make a sighing man a singer. Ale giues a buffet in the head, But ginger vnder-proppes the brayne: When ale would strike a strong man dead, Then nut-megge tempers it againe, The nut-browne ale, the nut-browne ale, Puts downe all drinke when it is stale. Giue mee a season that will sturre the blood I like not Nigardice to hungar-starue, Tis good when pooremen frolicke in the hall, The whil'st our fathers in the Chambers feast, And none repines at any stranger-guest. Your bookes are Adamants and you the Iron That cleaues to them till you confound your selfe Let them doe soe that list, so will not!. What Maister Pedant, pray forbeare, forbeare. 'Tis still safe erring with the multitude: By'r Lady sir, I like not of this pride, Giue me the ancient hospitallity, They say 'tis merry in hall, when beards wag all. The Italian Lord is an Asse, the song is a good song, 'Tis true my Lord, they carelesly deuoure. In faith good fellowes get some other trade, Yee liue but idle in the common-wealth. Cease Ruffians, with your swords and bucklers, hence. Playes, well remembred, we will haue a play, Steward lets haue Sir Olliuer Owlets men, Faith Ile fly intoo't with a sweeping wing, Me thinkes your honours hose sit very well, And yet this fashion is growne so stale; Is on a better block, your Lordship meanes; Your Lord-ships doublet-skirt is short and neate, A Play, a Maske, a Banquet, weele haue all. How soone they can remember to forget, Their vndeserued Fortunes and esteeme; Blush not the peasants at their pedigree? Suckt pale with lust; what, bladders swolne with pride, To strout in shreds of nitty brogerie? Well let them blase, ther's none so blind but sees, Prydes fall is still frost-bit with miseries. What, come they in so blunt without deuise? The first is pledg d, and heere begins a fresh. The grape begins to fume. The thirst of Honour call'd me to the warres, Where I haue drunke a health (too deepe a draught) My full-mouthd bags may now be fild with ayre, The Diuell and Ambition taught it me. See how Mauortius turnes away his face, To seeke to friends 'tis holden for disgrace. Are wounds rewards for Souldiers in the field? What? sell our lands, are these the fruits of Warre? Then dye Philarchus, let not shame suruiue, Thy fainting honour, dead and yet aliue. Behold my wife like Winters parramour, Rob'd and bereau'd of nuptiall Ornaments. "Hide thee Philarchus lower then the graue, "The Earth will couer though it cannot saue. Why, thou art farre more wretcheder then wee, How canst thou teach vs then tranquillity?

Plot summary

Plot — Histriomastix, or The Player Whipped
Under the benevolent rule of Peace, various orders of society turn to learning in their different ways: Mavortius and his fellow noblemen and gentlemen become students of the liberal arts, professional people appoint the scholar Chrisoganus as their lecturer, and some artisans organize themselves as a company of players, Sir Oliver Owlet’s Men. However, the new reign of Plenty tempts many of them away from their studies, leaving only Chrisoganus loyal. While the lawyers and merchants take advantage of the leisure of the vacation to go hunting, Mavortius entertains his guests with a play given by Owlet’s Men. Plenty is succeeded by Pride, who encourages a dedication to fashion and a neglect of proper decorum: the lawyers and merchants enrich themselves unjustly, Mavortius dismisses many of his servants to release more money to spend on pleasure, and both Chrisoganus (who has turned playwright) and Owlet's Men price themselves out of the market. Next, Envy comes to power, causing class resentment in the realm: the citizenry’s adoption of the latest fashions causes resentment among the nobility, envious behaviour disrupts the players’ rehearsal, and Mavortius becomes belligerent. The next ruler is War, under whom the players are conscripted as soldiers, Mavortius fights with his friends, the common people rise in rebellion against their social superiors, and the Spanish invade the realm. Out of this chaos comes Poverty, leading aristocrats, merchants. and peasants alike to debt, bankruptcy, and famine. Owlet’s men, unable to pay their taxes, are banished from the realm. Finally Poverty and her associates are expelled by the return of Peace and Plenty. Queen Elizabeth also appears, in the guise of Astraea, and the play ends with the five lesser rulers resigning their sceptres to Peace.
