Like a prison: What is it else? you giue me golden fetters, As if their value could my bondage lessen. That which alone is better then all these, My liberty. Why am I cloyster'd thus, And kept a prisoner from the sight of man? What hath my innocence and infancy Deseru'd to be immur'd in brazen walls? Can you accuse my faith, or modesty? Hath any loose demeanour in my carriage Bred this distrust? hath my eye plaid the rioter? Or hath my tongue beene lauish? haue my fauours Vn-virginlike to any beene profuse, That it should breed in you such ielousie, Or bring me to this durance? Did hee bestow these freely? Dana's guard Are much indebted to King Iupiter. If he haue store wee'l buy some for our vse, And wearing. They are wondrous beautifull, Where's the man that brought them? Much haue I heard of his renowne in armes, His generousnesse, his vertues, and his fulnesse Of all that Nature can bequeath to man. His bounty I now tast, and I could wish, Your eare were his, that I might let him know What interest he hath in me to command, We are amaz'd, and the large difference Betwixt your name and habite, breeds in vs Feare and distrust. Yet if I censure freely I needes must thinke that face and personage Was ne're deriu'd from basenesse. And the spirit To venture and to dare to court a Queene I cannot stile lesse then to be a Kings. Say that we grant you to be Iupiter, What thence inferre you? So far as Iupiter loues Dana's honour, So farre will Danae loue Iupiter. Beware your ruine, if you Beldams heare. All this is true. You couet that, which saue the Prince of Creet None dares. Both I do. But all things hinder, yet were Danae free, She could affect the Cretan. Which Danae must repeale. Oh Iupiter. I must hence: For if I stay, I yeeld: Il'e hence, no more. Yon is my doore, Dare not to enter there. I will to rest. Attendance. You had not best. Command our Eunuch's with their pleasing'st tunes To charme our eyes to rest. Leaue vs all, leaue vs. The God of dreames hath with his downy fanne Swept or'e our eye-lids, and sits heauy on them. Then to your chambers, & let wakelesse slu ̄ bers Charme you in depth of silence and repose. Let musick through this brazen fortresse sound Till all our hearts in depth of sleepe be drown'd. Who's that? What meane you Prince? how dare you enter here? Knowing if I but call, your life is doom'd, And all Creetes treasure cannot guard your person. Il'e loue you too, Would you but leaue me. Beshrow you sir, that for your amorous pleasure Could thus sort all things, person, place and leasure. Exclaime I could, and a loude vproare keepe, But that you say the Crones are all a sleepe: And to what purpose should I raise such feare, My voyce being soft, they fast, and cannot heare? Kisses, tush. I'le sinke into my sheetes, for I shall blush. I'le diue into my bed. Good my Lord forbeare What do you meane? (oh heauen) is no man neere, If you will needs, for modesties chast law, Before you come to bed, the curtaines draw, But do not come, you shall not by this light, If you but offer't, I shall cry out right. Oh God, how hoarse am I, and cannot? fie Danae thus naked and a man so nye. Pray leaue me sir: he makes vnready still, Well I'le euen winke, and then do what you will. All men proue false, if Ioue be found vntrue.

Plot summary

Plot — The Golden Age, or The Lives of Jupiter and Saturn
King Uranus of Crete is dead. Titan is the eldest son, but his younger brother Saturn is the popular choice to ascend the throne. Titan agrees to make way for him provided that he agrees to settle the succession on Titan’s issue and kill his own sons as soon as they are born. Saturn is declared a god, and under his benevolent rule, the people learn agriculture, ship- building, and architecture, and begin to make weapons for hunting. His wife, Sibylla, becomes pregnant, but Saturn keeps his promise to Titan and kills the baby. During a second pregnancy, an oracle foretells that the child will depose Saturn. This time Sibylla demands the privilege of killing her own child; but neither she nor her women can bring themselves to do it, so the baby is sent to a foster mother, the Princess of Epirus, and Saturn is deceived with the blood of a goat. Jupiter grows up in the court of King Melisseus of Epirus, and is sent as an envoy to conclude a peace with King Lycaon of Pelasgia; but Lycaon has murdered the Epirote hostage and offers his limbs to Jupiter as a cannibalistic banquet. Jupiter deposes Lycaon and the people elect him their King. During the conflict, he encounters Callisto, Lycaon’s daughter, and is attracted to her. She asks him to grant her a boon to prove his love; he promises, but her request is to live a chaste life as a nymph of Diana. She joins Diana, but the nymphs are already paired off, so she must await the next recruit to have a bedfellow. This turns out to be Jupiter in female disguise. During a stag hunt, he diverts her, reveals his identity and rapes her. Months later, the nymphs undress to bathe together; Callisto is reluctant, and is forcibly stripped.
