Full speech

Bethinke you good Sir William, I reckon thereabout my selfe, so by that account Theres full three Winters yet he must attend, Vnder our awe, before he sue his Liuery: Ist not so? Sir William you are his Vnckle, and I must speake That am his Guardian, would I had a son Might merit commendations euen with him. Ile tetl you what he is, he is a youth, A Noble branch, increasing blessed fruit. Where Caterpiller vice dare not to touch, He is himselfe with so much grauity, Praise cannot praise him with Hypperbole: He is one whom older looke vpon, as one a booke, Wherein are Printed Noble sentences For them to rule their liues by. Indeed he is one All Emulate his vertues, hate him none. And yet Sir William being as he is, Young, and vnsetled, tho of virtuous thoughts, By Genuine disposition, yet our eyes See daily presidents, hopefull Gentlemen, Being trusted in the world with their owne will, Diuert the good is lookt from them to Ill, Make their old names forgot, or not worth note With company they keepe, such Reuelling With Panders, Parasites, Podigies of Knaues, That they sell all, euen their old fathers graues. VVhich to preuent, weele match him to a wife, Marriage Restraines the scope of single life. And I haue found him one of Noble parentage, A Neece of mine, nay I haue broke with her, Know thus much of her mind, what for my pleasure As also for the good appeares in him, She is pleas'd of all thats hers to make him King. Also I haue apointed Doctor Baxter, Chansellor of Oxford to attend me heere And see he is come. Good maister Doctor. Tis so, and I did looke for him by this. So, so. Then he will be heere forthwith: you Maister Baxter Go Vsher hether straight young Katherine, Sir William, here and I will keepe this roome til you returne. Tis well done Scarborrow. You haue bin welcome in your Country Yorkshire. Twas well, twas very well, and in your absence, your Vnckle heere and I, haue bin bethinking what gift betwixt vs we might bestow on you, That to your house large dignity might bring, With faire increase, as from a Christall spring. We accept thy promise, now returne thee this, A vertuous wife, accept her with a kisse. Feare not to take her man, she will feare neither, Do what thou canst being both abed together. But me a Dog of wax, come kisse, and agree, Your friends haue thought it fit, and it must be. How Sawce-box. Sir, sir, I and sir knaue, then here is a mellowed experience knowes how to teach you, O Iacke. How both our cares, your Vnckle and my selfe, Sought, studied, found out, and for your good, A maid, a Neece of mine, both faire and chast, And must we stand at your discretion. Ha, ha, whats that, let me heare that againe? And haue you knit that knot Sir. Ha, ist euen so, My secretary there, Write me a Letter straight to Sir Iohn Harcop, Ile see Sir Iacke and if that Harcop dare, Being my Ward, contract you to his daughter. My steward too, post you to Yorkeshire, Where lyes my youngsters Land, and sirrah, Fell me his wood, make havocke, spoyle and wast. Sir you shall know that you are Ward to me, Ile make you poore inough: then mend your selfe. Contract your selfe and where you list, Ile make you know me Sir to be your guard. And where I meant my selfe to haue disburst Foure thousand pound, vpon this mariage Surrendred vp your land to your owne vse, And compast other portions to your hands, Sir Ile now yoke you still. Now death of me, shall I be crost by such a Iacke, he wed himselfe, and where he list: Sirrha Malapart, Ile hamper you, You that will haue your will, come get you in: Ile make thee shape thy thoughts to marry her, Or wish thy birth had bin thy murtherer. Whose this, young Scarborrow? Is this the reuerence that you owe to me? That vice should thus transforme man to a beast. I pitty thee euen with my very soule. What will you do Sir? Your sober blood wil teach you otherwise. Ill met to see your Nephew in this case, More like a brute Beast, then a Gentleman.

Plot summary

Plot — The Miseries of Enforced Marriage
The ward William Scarborow betroths himself to Clare Harcop, but on his return to London he is forced to marry Katherine, the niece of his guardian, Lord Falconbridge. He is rewarded with control of his own’ and his younger brothers’ property. He writes to tell Clare the news of his unwelcome marriage; upon receiving the letter, she dies (possibly by suicide). ===== PAGE 337 ===== 1606 Scarborow takes her death badly: he blames his wife, and becomes prodigal. Ilford fakes his own arrest for debt in order to induce Scarborow to guarantee the supposed loan, thereby defrauding him of his money. This still leaves him with the patrimony of his younger brothers, Thomas and John; but he spends it, and drunkenly assaults his friends and kinsmen. The brothers and the honest family servant Butler are forced into theft to keep themselves from starvation. They steal £300 from Harcop, Clare’s father, which they use to make their sister appear to be a rich woman. Butler tricks Ilford into marrying her, but on learning that she is penniless her new husband throws her out; Butler offers to support her out of his meagre wages. Thomas confronts and fights with his elder brother, but saves his life when they are attacked by Ilford. Now penitent, Thomas declares his intention to confess to Harcop that he and John stole the money, even though it will mean hanging for them both. John falls out with him over this, but they are reconciled by Butler. Dr Baxter, who married Scarborow to Katherine, talks him into repentance. On his deathbed, Lord Falconbridge also repents and leaves the Scarborow brothers double their original wealth in restitution, along with a dowry for their sister.
