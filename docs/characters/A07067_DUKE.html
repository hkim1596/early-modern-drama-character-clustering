IVstice that makes Princes like the Gods, drawes vs vnto the Se nate, That with vnpartiall ballance wee may poyse The crimes and innocence of all offenders, Our presence can chase bribery from Lawes: He best can iudge, that heares himselfe the cause. The Land and people's mine, the crimes being knowne, I must redresse my subiects wrong's, mine owne. Call for the two suspected for the murder Of Mendosa, our endered kinsman. These voluntary murderers That confesse the Murder of him that is yet aliue. Wee'll sport vvith serious Iustice for a vvhile, In shew wee'll frowne on them that make vs smile. Stand forth you Vipers, that haue suck'd bloud, And lopt a branch sprung from a royall tree: What can you answere to escape tortures? On what ground sprung your hate to him we lou'd? Our Iustice dwels with mercy; be not desperate. Take them asunder, graue Iustice makes vs mirth, That man is soulelesse that ne'er sinnes on earth. Signior Mizaldus, relate the weapon you kill'd him with, and the manner. Take him aside. Signior Claridiana, what weapon had you for this bloudy act? vvhat dart vs'd Death? With seuerall Instruments. Bring them face to face. With what kill'd you our Nephew? Part them, part'em. You doe confesse the murther done by both. You hang together, that shall make you friends, An euerlasting hatred death soone ends: To prison with them till the day of death; Kings words like Fate, must neuer change their breath. Now to our kinsman, shame to royall blood, Bring him before vs. Theft in a Prince is sacrilege to honour 'Tis vertues scandall, death of Royalty, I blush to see my shame; Nephew sit downe Iustice that smiles on those on him must frowne, Speake freely Captaine, where found you him wounded? Thou know'st too much, would I were past all know ledge, I might forget my griefe springs from my shame, Thou monster of my blood, answere in briefe To these Assertions made against thy life. Is thy soule guilty of so base a fact? Thy head's thy forfeit for thy harts offence, Thy bloods prerogatiue may claime that fauour, Thy person then to death doomb'd by iust lawes ▪ Thy death is infamous, but worse the cause. I am amazed at this maze of wonder, Wherein no thred or clue presents it selfe, To winde vs from the obscure passages, What saies my Nephew? Wee'll vse him like a fellon, cut him off: For feare he doe pollute our sounder parts. Yet why should he steale, That is a loaden Vine? riches to him, Were adding sands into the Libian shore, Or farre lesse charitie: what say the other prisoners? And he is yet aliue, keepe them asunder We may sent out the wile. Signior, thinke, and dally not with heauen, But freely tell vs, did you doe the murther? In Gods name then on to the death with them, For the poore widdowes that you leaue behinde, Though by the law, their goods are all confiscate, Yet wee'll be their good Lord, and giue 'em them. Now by my life I doe, faire Ladies rise, I nee'r did purpose any other end, To them and these designes. I was inform'd, Of some notorious errour, as I sate in iudgement. And doe you heare? these night workes require a Cats eyes, To impierce deiected darknesse: call back the prisoners. Since you are to die, wee'll giue you winding sheetes, Wherein you shall be shrouded aliue, By which we winde out all these miseries. Segnior Rogero, bestow a while your eye, And reade here of your true wiues chastity. Giues him a Letter. The like Sir is apparant on your side. To tother. Then we impose a strict command vpon you: On your Allegeance, reade what there is writ. You'le finde it so I feare. Keepe them asunder, list to vs, we command. You haue saued vs a labour in your loue. But Gentlemen, why stood you so prepostrously? Would you haue headlong runne to Infamy, In so defam'd a death? Night, like a Masque, is entred heauens great hall, With thousand Torches vshering the way: To Risus will wee consecrate this Euening, Like Missermis cheating of the brack. Plot summary

Plot — The Insatiate Countess
Suitors visit Isabella, who is in open mourning for her dead husband, but secretly glad to be free of his jealousy and restrictions. She accepts Count Roberto’s suit and they are married the following day; but when she dances with Count Rogero in the wedding masque, her affections turn. She woos Rogero by letter and elopes with him to Pavia; Roberto enters holy orders. At Pavia, Rogero is visited by his friend Gniaca; Isabella falls in love with the newcomer, and feigns illness to give herself an opportunity to seduce him. After bedding Gniaca, she then feigns penitence to free herself from Rogero. The spurned lover takes his revenge by circulating bitter satires on Isabella’s promiscuity, so she sets on Gniaca to kill him. In the attempt, Gniaca is reconciled with his former friend, so Isabella seduces Don Sago, colonel in the Spanish power occupying Pavia, to murder both Rogero and Gniaca. Sago kills Rogero, and is arrested, but pardoned by the Duke of Medina. Isabella’s attempt to bribe the Duke fails to secure her freedom, and she is beheaded after a scaffold reconciliation with Roberto. In the sub-plot, the enemies Claridiana and Mizaldus are both married on the same day. They quarrel when they meet, and Mizaldus’s wife Abigail and Lady Lentulus, who are close friends, compare notes and swap places: each receives her own husband in the other’s bedroom. Meanwhile their friends Mendosa and Lady Lentulus, attracted to one another, try to circumvent the latter’s vow not to admit a man through her front door: he climbs up to her window by rope ladder, but falls into the street, where the watch find him injured and send him to a surgeon. Claridiana and Mizaldus are arrested in each other’s house; appalled at the thought of being cuckolded, they both confess to murdering Mendosa. Mendosa in turn tries to save Lady Lentulus’s honour with a false confession that he was attempting to burgle her house. In court, the Duke establishes that Claridiana and Mizaldus are telling incompatible stories about the ‘murder’, and they are sentenced to death in jest; in Mendosa’s case, however, the sentence is genuine. Claridiana and Mizaldus are determined to die until confronted with evidence of their wives’ chastity; with reconciliation comes reprieve.
