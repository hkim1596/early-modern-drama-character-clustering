I might very well be here, Sir, at a wooing match; but, I goe: yet I will not be farre off. Exit. I will, I will. Faith you had beſt turne Paper-man, & ſell Maps; and yet that trade is almoſt downe the wind now: or you may get a pretty young—one—and ſet vp a Tabacco-ſhop. Oh your fatteſt ſoiles are moſt full of dirt; and I haue knowne a fellow, that was not worth a haire of his head, nay, that had not an haire of an honeſt man, gather more gold out of this dung-hill, then euer Maro did out of his Ennius; that now he cares not for any man in the Pariſh: Oh! this is the trade that yeelds è fumo fulgorem; Gold out of ſmoke. Sir, not many yeeres ſince, before I vndertooke with you our iourney about the wide world, I was my ſelfe driuen to the like ſtreights; I meane, Sir, in that Cod-piece-ago, when the innocency of men did not bluſh to ſhew all that Nature gaue them, indeed, becauſe they did no more, then, that taught them: then, when they wore doublets with crawes, and ſleeues with pockets, then (I ſay) the faſhion was ſo long at a ſtand, that I had like to haue beene at a fall: then your Philoſopher in the Vniuerſitie, ſcorn'd nothing but (the vniuſt cauſe of ſcorne) fine apparell, ſhewing the ſeuerity of his profeſſion, by the ruggedneſſe of his gowne: but ſince, I thinke, I haue faſhion'd them all; though, of late, ſome of your gor-belli'd country-chuffes, haue caſt themſelues into their frieze jerkins, with great tinn'd buttons ſiluer'd or'e, ra ther out of a proud niggardlineſſe then an honeſt thrift. O, Sir, I perfectly remember it, 'twas thus: The in habitants haue, at the foot of the out-ſide of the Hill, dig'd forth an entrance, and vnderneath haue made a Vault which reaches iuſt to the banke of the Riuer, all along the ſide of which, they haue made a many trap-doores, and ſo when a man has heard enough, they vnbolt the trap-doores within, and let him ſlide downe gently. Well, Sir, by my Mothers ſoule (that oath I learn't in Spaine) 'tis a truth; and the reaſon it cannot be heard lower, is, becauſe the ſound does not deſcend below the water. Nay, by Ioue, Sir, I ſcorne to lie; I ſcorne to ſpeake any thing without reaſon, by Ioue; by Ioue, Ile giue as good a reaſon of thoſe things I know, as any man vnder the cope of Heauen; I will, by Ioue. Plot — Technogamia, or The Marriages of the Arts
Astronomia has several suitors: Geographus (whom her mother dislikes), Geometres (whom her friends favour), and Poeta (who has recently jilted Historia to pursue her). Against her kinsman Polites’ advice, Historia still wants to marry Poeta, consummating the long-standing association between history and the poetic imagination. Poeta quarrels with Grammaticus, and Logicus becomes involved in the ensuing affray. Unsure whether to fight a duel with Poeta or sue him for assault, Logicus sets both courses in train. Hearing that Poeta has been hurt in the fight, Historia sends to Medicus for help, but Magus, who is helping Geometres, sees an opportunity to dispose of one of his client’s rivals, and persuades Medicus to send poison instead. Medicus’ man, Sanguis, delivers the poison, but spots that it isn’t medicine. Poeta infers Historia's treachery, but persuades himself out of absolute misogyny by thinking of Astronomia. To reconcile Poeta with Logicus and Grammaticus, Ethicus arranges a banquet. Several of the wooers use magical help in their suits. Poeta meets two gipsies, who pick his- pocket and foretell that he will marry Astronomia. Using sieve- divination, Magus predicts that Geometres will marry Astronomia, and will achieve this mainly through hypnotism, with a little pharmaceutical help. (Geometres finds the third option, conjuration, too frightening to try.) However, when Astronomia drinks the supposed love philtre at Ethicus’ feast, it poisons her. Meanwhile, Poeta seems reconciled to his former adversaries, but gets drunk and tries to pick fights with Geographus and Grammaticus; Magus charms him to sleep. Causidicus accepts fees from both Logicus and Poeta, so when the case comes to trial, Polites dismisses him and forces the parties to speak for themselves. The trial is adjourned when news arrives that Astronomia, burning up with the poison, has fallen into a coma. Medicus attends her, and cures her by administering an enema. Meanwhile the gipsies and servants indulge themselves in country pastimes: morris dancing, barley-break, and riddles. Polites takes up Geographus’ and Historia’s love- suits: he persuades Astronomia’s mother that Geographus will be a worthy son-in-law, so long as he gets rid of his servant Phantastes, and the couple are married.
