Tis ſo, Ianthus, Aemon is in loue With faire Antigone ; no other paſſion Could make ſo freſh a youth and ſpirit as his To ſeeke ſuch ſad retreats; from that dark groue, Which cloaths Cythaerens rough and craggy top, Where farre from ſight and company of men The wofull Oedipus laments alone His happleſſs;e errours fault, vnſeene by any But good Antigone his pious daughter, How oft of late braue Aemon has beene mett! What but her loue had power to draw him thither? As much true wortha nd manly vertue liues In noble Aemons breaſt, hee's the true brother Of braue Menaeceus, whoſe deuoted head Sau'd Thebes from ruine. But I haue heard newes from the enemies campe, Alls quiet there, and tis ſuppoſ'd they meane To fight no more, but ſecretly diſlodge, And fly away by night. Oh horrid monſtrous challenge? Vnnaturall! Prince Aemon is abroad, and woe is me Gone with too great a confidence I feare Vpon my plot, which is defeated quite. The watch is doubled, and more ſtrictly kept Then heretofore, no poſſiblity To lay them all aſleepe, what he entended To worke vpon it, is quite fruſtrate now. Oh could I meete him but to let him know What has befall'n. Ile range theſe fields to find him. Good my Lord, Take fairer hopes, and liue; caſt not away The Kingdomes ioy; what cruelty can touch So ſweet a vertue as Antigone? Retire with me into yon little houſe; Ile there binde vp your wounds; you bleede too faſt, And needes muſt faint before you reach the walls. The wounds I tooke are ſcratches. Delay not Sir. Their goodneſſe will protect them: what other Lady Was that with her? Wandering about the fields to finde out you, I met with witches, impious haggs, that came As I ſuppoſe, for execrable ends There to abuſe the bodies of the dead. Oh partiall fates, oh too iniurious night, Can theſe eſcape, when piety muſt ſuffer? You ſhall my Lord. When I haue dreſt you, Ile to court, and thence Bring you a true and ſwift intelligence. Oh black accurſed doome; oh my ſad fate, That muſt report this newes to noble Aemon, And with that breath deſtroy the beſt of men. Not dead, my Lord, But liues among the dead. In a tombe, Where neuer more ſhe ſhall behold the day Nor Phoebus ſplendour, by the Kings command, Is faire Antigone encloſ'd aliue— To famiſh there and dy. Feare not my care, my Lord, but let me craue (By your owne worth I begge it, and that fauour Which you were euer pleaſed to reflect On my poore ſeruices) till I returne, Attempt no other courſe, I will be ſpeedy, And if perſwaſions of the queene doe faile; Wee'll finde a way to ſaue the princeſſe life, But tis a deſperate way, and muſt be vſ'd The laſt of all. I will out-fly the winde. Muſt I returne the Prince this killing newes? Your highneſſe pardon, Not for the world would I delay the time— Vpon vncertainties; I feare I haue Already ſtay'd too long. My quicke returne Is th' only meanes to keepe the prince aliue. Pleaſe it your highneſſe then when I am gone To mooue the King, and feare not, gratious madam, The Princes life a while how ere. Yonder's the tombe, my Lord, which though it ſeeme Too hard, and ſolid for our ſtrength to force, I know a place will open preſently. Ile ope the tombe Immediatly, my Lord. What end of tragedyes Can wofull Thebes for euer hope to ſee After this ſorrow. Oh I more then feare The Princes fury; Moſt noble prince, Collect that ſtrength of man, which all the world Expects from you, and arme your ſelfe to beare With fitting patience this calamity. The paſſiue fortitude is great and noble As is the actiue. My Lord, I will obey; And thus I take my leaue.

Plot summary

Plot — Antigone, the Theban Princess
Self-blinded and banished from Thebes, Oedipus wishes only for death. His daughter Antigone temporarily persuades him to give up such thoughts, but it is not long before he is dead. Thebes itself is at war: Eteocles has dishonoured a power-sharing agreement with his brother Polynices, and seven Greek champions have besieged the city in support of the latter’s claim. After sustaining heavy casualties, Polynices proposes settling the case in single combat with Eteocles. Both are killed. The new King, Creon, decrees that no Theban shall bury any enemy soldier, including Polynices. He also places Antigone under house arrest and refuses his son Haemon permission to marry her. In defiance of Creon, Antigone goes to the battlefield in search of Polynices’ body. There she meets his widow, Argia, and together they convey the corpse past the watchmen and give it a funeral. Creon too comes to the battlefield, to consult the witches who are gathering Parts of the dead bodies for magical use. They make a dead body tell his fortune: he will die soon, and disorder will come to Thebes. Antigone and Argia are arrested. Creon holds Argia, a foreign princess, for ransom, but sentences Antigone to be entombed alive and left to starve to death. By threatening to take his own life, Haemon persuades his father to allow her release, but it is too late: she has taken poison to hasten her death. Haemon kills himself. Thebes is attacked by Theseus, who has been summoned by another widow to intervene for the unburied. Argia is released and Creon killed, but Theseus allows him the burial he denied his enemies.
