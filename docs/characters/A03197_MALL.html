NOw for my true-loues hand-kercher; these flowers Are pretie toyes, are very pretie toyes: O but me thinkes the Peascod would doe better, The Peascod and the Blossome, wonderfull! Now as I liue, ile surely haue it so. Some maides will chuse the Gilliflower, some the Rose, Because their sweet cents do delight the nose, But very fooles they are in my opinion, The very worst being drawen by cunning art, Seemes in the eye as pleasant to the heart. But heer's the question, whether my loue or no Will seeme content? I, there the game doth goe: And yet ile pawne my head he will applaude The Pescod and the flower, my pretie choice. For what is he louing a thing in heart, Loues not the counterfeit, though made by art? I cannot tell how others fancie stand, But I reioyce sometime to take in hand, The simile of that I loue; and I protest, That pretie pescod likes my humor best. But ile vnto the Drawers, heele counsell me, Heere is his shop: alas, what shall I doe? Hee's not within, now all my labor's lost, See, see, how forward loue is euer crost, But stay, what Gallant's this? Vpon what acquaintance? Hands off, sir Knaue, and weare it for a fauor. No foole, the knaue, O grosse; A gentleman and of so shallow wit! How then? Yes, to make an asse on. No, ile lackey by his side, and whip the asse. With that face! away you want-wit. By the asse-head you shall not. Come, come, ye are a man. I haue heard as wise. As wise as yong Bowdler. Hoida; come vp. No good asse, bate an Ase of that. Lord, how eloquence flowes in this Gentleman! No sir, I will hold out as long as I may, Though in the end you beare the foole away. No by this night, not I. Yea, I dare sware you'le goe a foole to bed. And so I doe indeed, himselfe can tell: But this it is, speake Maidens what they will, Men are so captions the 'ile euer conster ill. I neuer made you promise, did I, I pray? No for-sooth not I. Then mine shall be the field: Wisedome, adue, once more faint heart farewell; Yet if thou seest the Drawer, I prethee tell him, Mall Berry hath more worke for him to doe; And for your selfe, learne this when you doe woe, Arme you with courage, and with good take heede, For he that spares to speake must spare to speede, And so farewell. Exit. Whats that to you sir? Yes forsooth. Alls one to me, whom he please. And well remembred husband. He hath worke of mine, I pray forget it not. On him Cripple! for what? was it for marriage? Cripple, you lose your time, with your faire teares To circumuent my heart: Bowdler I loue thee, Barnard I hate, and thou shalt neuer mooue me. That I loue Barnard ! by heauens I abhorre him. What wilt thou make me mad? I say, I hate him. For Barnard ! twas for Bowdler. Will you be sworne I did. Cripple, If thou canst prooue that euer I Did fancie Barnard, I will loue him still. Yes, I forsooth father, ile be his baile, Body for body; thinke you ile stay at home, And see my husband carried to the Iaile? My husband I assure you: Father, these Serieants both can witnes it. He is my husband. Faith but I will, Barnard, speake for thy selfe. Yes sir, and heere protest, Of all in London I loue Barnard best. You and I sir, alas, we are not play-fellowes, though we be turtles: I am prouided. I sir, but this is my Paris, I am resolu'd, And what I doe is by authoritie.

Plot summary

Plot — The Fair Maid of the Exchange
Phyllis and Ursula are assaulted by two ruffians as they go to deliver wares to a customer at night. A cripple rescues them, and a second assault is foiled by the intervention of Frank Golding; the cripple promises to help Frank if ever he can. Frank's two brothers are both in love with Phyllis, and, though he scorns amorous passions, Frank has fallen for her too. Phyllis, however, loves the cripple, and woos him by giving him a handkerchief to embroider “for a friend’. Meanwhile, Bobbington poses as a sailor going on a voyage, and borrows money from Phyllis’s father, Flower, leaving a diamond ring with him as security. Ferdinand and Anthony overhear one another's plans to woo Phyllis, but neither knows that Frank is also a rival. Ferdinand writes to her; Anthony plans to intercept the letter and substitute his own, but Ferdinand disguises Frank as a porter and gets him hired as Anthony's messenger. Planning to double-cross them both, Frank enlists the cripple's help. The cripple supplies him with two rejection letters to take back to his brothers, as from Phyllis. They each enlist the help of one of Phyllis’s parents, ===== PAGE 378 ===== who then quarrel over which brother shall marry their daughter. _ Knowing Phyllis loves him but thinking himself unworthy, the cripple lends Frank his clothes. Frank courts Phyllis in disguise, and she gives him her ring; then. he brings the parents forged letters from his brothers withdrawing their suits. Each parent decides to:spite the other by offering Phyllis to Frank instead of the other’s favoured son-in-law. The thwarted brothers realize what has happened, but Phyllis asks to be allowed a say in her.own marriage — and chooses Frank. As the play ends, Flower is arrested for receiving stolen property — Bobbington’s ring — and sent for trial. In the sub-plot, Mall Berry is in love with Barnard, but suffers the unwanted attentions of Bowdler. Barnard is surety for a £100 debt to Mall’s father; the original borrower is dead and the debt about to fall due, but Berry refuses to allow Barnard any more time to find the money. Bowdler persists in his pursuit of Mall, using lines he remembers from Venus and Adonis. Berry engages sergeants to arrest Barnard. Mall agrees to marry Bowdler, but the cripple points out that, through a party wall, he has overheard her talking about Barnard in her sleep: he is the man she truly loves.
