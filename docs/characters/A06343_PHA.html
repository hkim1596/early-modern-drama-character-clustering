Hee's mad, beyond cure mad. What you haue seene in me to stirre offence, I cannot finde, vnlesse it be this Lady offered into my armes, with the succession which I must keepe: though is hath pleasde your fure to muteny within you, without disputing, your Geneallegies or taking knowledge whose branch you are, the King will leaue it to me, and I dare make it mine: you haue your answere. To what? what would he haue answer? Sira, I forbare you before the King. But now the time is fitter, doe but offer to make mention of right to any kingdome, though it lie scarce habitable. And by the gods What now? Doe you sleight my greatnesse so much, and in the chamber ofthe Princesse? 'Tis an odd fellow Madame, wee must stop his mouth with some office when we are married. I thinke he would discharge it well. Madame, I hope our hearts are knit, but yet so slow, the cerimonies ofstate are, that twill bee long before our hearts bee so, then if you please being agreed in heart, let vs not waite for dreaming forme, but take a little stolne delights, and so preuent our ioyes to come. The constetution ofmy body will neuer hold out till the wedding, I must seeke else where. Why should these Ladies stay so long, they must Come this way, I know the Queene imployes vm not, For the reuerend mother sent me word, They would all be for the garden: if they should all Proue honest now, I were in a faire taking: I was neuer so long without sport before in my life, And in my conscience tis not my fault. Oh for our countrey Ladies, here's one boulted, I'le hound at her. Madame. Shall I not be a trouble? Nay, nay, y'are too quicke by this sweete hand. Deare Lady, can you loue? You much mistake me Lady. Y'are very dangerous bitter, like a potion. Do Ladies of this Countrey vse to giue no more respect to men of my full being. Lady you talke of nothing all this time. This is a crafty wench, I like her wit well, 'Twill be rare to stir vp a leaden appetite, Shee's daintie, and must be courted with a shewer of gold, Madame looke here, all these and more, then — Lady, Lady. If there be but two such in this Kingdome more, and neere the Court, we may ene hang vp our harpes, ten such Campher Con stitutions as this would call the golden age againe in question, and teach the old way for euery ill fast husband, to get his owne chil dren, and what a mischiefe that would breed, let all consider. Heres's another, ifshe be of the same last, the diuell shall pluck her on: Many faire mornings Lady. She giues good words yet, sure this wench is free. Ifyour more cerious businesse doe not call you Lady, Let me hold quarter with you, wee'le talke an houre Ont quickly. Ofsome such pretie subiect as your selfe, I'le go no further then your eye, your lip, theres time enough For one man for an Age. O they are two twend Cherries dyde in blush, Which those faire sunnes aboue with their deepe beams Reflect vpon, and ripen, sweetest beauty; Bow downe those branches, that the longing taste Of the sweete looker on, may meete these blessings, And taste and liue. Doe it in prose; you cannot misse it Madame. By my life but you shall not, I'l prompt you first, Can you doe it now? Sticke till to morrow, ile neuer part you sweetest, but we lose time. Can you loue me? I'le teach you in a short sentence, Cause I will not load your memory, This is all: Loue me and lie with me. Not to a willing minde, that wil endeauour, If I doe not teach you to doe it as easily in one night, As you'le go to bed: I'le lose my royall bloud for't. I'le sooner teach a mare the old measures, Then teach her any thing belonging to the function, Shee's affraid to lie with her selfe, If she haue but my mascaline imagination about her, I know when we are married, I must rauish her. And for my other I see excepting your deere selfe, deerest Lady I had rather be Sir Timen a schoolemaister, and keepe a darie maid.
