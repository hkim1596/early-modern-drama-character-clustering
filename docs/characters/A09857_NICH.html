O 〈…〉 you must not leape ouer the stile before you come at it, haste makes waste, softe fire makes sweere mast, no too fast for falling, there's no hast to hang true men. I promise yee maistor Philip you haue spoken as true as steele. And it seemeth vnto me, I it seemes to me, that you maister Phillip mocke me, do you not know qui mocat mo bitur, mocke age and see how it will prosper? What is it sir? Beare withesse he threatens me. I may and I will, I need not except I list, you shall not commaund me, you glue me neither meate, drinke, nor wages, I am your fathers man, and a man's a man, and a haue but a hose on his head, do not misuse me so, do not, for though he that is bound must obay, yet he that will not tarrie, may runne away so he may. To whom is it? Forsooth though none of the best yet meanly: Forsooth as vse makes perfectnes, so seldome seene is soone forgotten. What need you to care? who speakes to you? you may speake when you are spoken to, and keep your winde to coole your pottage: well, well, you are my maisters sonne & you looke for his lande, but they that hope for dead mens shooes, may hap to go barefoote: take heed, as soone goes the yong sheep to the pot'as the olde. I pray God saue my May sters life, for sildome comes the better. Farewell frost, No, you should say God send faire weather after me, A good lucke onye. I wil not thrust my hand into the flame and need not, tis not good to haue an oare in another mans boate, little said is soone amended, & in litle medling commeth great rest, tis good sleeping in a whole skin, so a man might come home by weeping crosse: no by lady, a friend is not so soone got ten as lost, blessed are the peace-makers, they that strike with the sword, shall be beaten with the scabberd. I, brag's a good dog, threatned folkes liue long. Well, I see the foole wil not leaue his bable for the Tower of London. And make no 〈◊〉 And mistresse Mary, when yee go to bed, God send you good rest, and a peck of Fleas in your nest, euery one as big as Francis. And you more money. Twill be a good while, ere you wish your skinfull of let holes.

Plot summary

Plot — 1 The Two Angry Women of Abingdon
The Gourseys share a meal with their neighbours, the Barnes family, and the two wives fall out over a game of chance. When Barnes admonishes his wife, she suspects him of adultery with Mistress Goursey. With a view to patching up the wives’ quarrel, Barnes plans to marry their daughter Mall to Goursey’s son, Frank, but Mistress Goursey finds out about it and goes to pick a fight at the Barnes house. ; Frank successfully woos Mall, but Mistress Barnes’s intervention sends the lovers fleeing, intending to rendezvous in Oxford. They get lost in a nearby rabbit warren, where Sir Ralph Smith is also out hunting, determined to bag some venison if it takes him all night. The other characters search for the lovers in the dark; there is an extended sequence of mistaken identities, during which Will, Sir Ralph’s woodman, gets the mistaken idea that his master is looking for a wench. Sir Ralph meets one — Mall. She tells him her story and he offers to intervene to reconcile the two mothers. The husbands find the wives brawling over possession of a torch. Mistress Barnes’s tendency to call Mistress Goursey a strumpet leads Goursey to suspect that there is no smoke without fire, and that his wife is having an affair with Barnes. Philip Barnes sees things are getting out of hand, and persuades the two angry women to retract their harsh words and make up their quarrel and agree to the marriage. Sir Ralph arrives to find there is no work for him as peacemaker; instead, he asks the two families to dinner.
