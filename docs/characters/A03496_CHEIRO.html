Gentle Ruler of this place, if ſo you be, vouchſafe to fauour vs in the way of truth for the gods cauſe. Mary, Sir, a booke, and that I thinke is a ſigne of a Scholer; but I haue a purſe too, and that, I thinke, is not a ſigne of a Scholer. Nay, Ile ſweare, wee both ran ſince I Nimb'd it, that wee durſt not be ſo bold yet, as to take leiſure to looke in't, but now Ile ſee. A fire burne it; here's nothing but a ſcuruy paper. I, 'faith, I ha' the tricke on't: for (a rapture of loue ſeizing on him, and caſting him into an extaſie) hee fell a tal king to himſelfe of a dreame he had: I ſeeing he was falne in to a Dreame, perſwaded my ſelfe he was faſt aſleepe; and ſo preſumptuouſly diu'd into his pockets, whence I brought theſe ſpoiles. What a Rogue's this? hee laughes at vs in his Dreame. He will ſweare by the Great. You ſhall not neede to make a ſhort Verſe to that Sir: weele be very ſhort with you. He is about to picke his pocket. Beſhrow his drunken fingers; Magus you ha' not charm'd him well. A murren on't, here's nothing but a Purſe with a paper in't. Nay, by this Hand, I hold them to be euerlaſting villaines. I, and I know the raſcall to haue a ſoft and moiſt Hand, by which I alſo infallibly know hee loues: for take a Poet without his wine & his wench; and if he make not drie, pitifull drie Verſes, Ile forſweare Fortune-telling as long as I liue. HEre comes ſome body, Phyſiognomus; ſet a good Face on't and Affront him; and Ile ſet my Fingers a worke, preſently. I'me ſure thou look'ſt as red as fire; I thinke all the Bloud in thy body is in thy face. Of me? what? I defie mine accuſers, and I call honeſtie it ſelfe to witneſſe, that I get my liuing by my fingers ends. We three will ſet vpon the next man we meet. My Hand ſhal be alwaies readie to help my friend. O my hand! my hand! O you rogue, you bow it quite double almoſt. Worke. (Choler drinks) Phyſiognomus, will you taſte this liquor? Trie me. Come on, let me ſee your hand, ſweet Muſica: you ſhall be belou'd of two, a Courtier and a Scholer; you ſhall loue the Courtier more; but the Scholer ſhall haue you; and it ſhall ſo come to paſſe, that the Courtier ſhall afterward be your ſeruant: your husband ſhall be exceeding melancholy: you ſhall haue three ſonnes; the firſt ſhall be call'd by his fa thers name (but I know not what that ſhall be) and hee ſhall be extreme diſcontent and ſolitarie; and if he preuent a con ſumption, he may liue till fortie; for longer he cannot, being of a cold and drie conſtitution: the ſecond ſhall be called Ti mido, and hee'll be indanger of being bit with a mad dogge; which if he ſcape, he may liue till fiftie: the third ſhall be cal led Iucundo; the other two tooke after their father; but hee'll take after his mother; hee will be exceedingly giuen to good cheere, muſike, and women: he will be in danger of a Surfet; and of Fire; and if he ſcape theſe two, eſpecially burning, he may liue to be an old man. You, Phlegmatico; 'twill be long ere you can get you a wife; yet you'll haue one, and one daughter; the child will die very young, of the blacke Iaundice, and your wife of the dropſie. I Doe finde my ſelfe at this preſent affected with that which ſhould not touch a good Magiſtrate, an vnwillingneſſe to doe Iuſtice: yet I profeſſe it proceeds not from a deſire to bee iniurious, but mercifull; not for an ill-will to either, but a loue to both. Whileſt heretofore, I vnderſtood of this diſſen tion, as I was ſomewhat caſt downe with ſorrow, ſo I was rai ſed with an hope of happie reconcilement, but now that hope alſo which before was the cauſe of an vncertaine ioy, is become the ground of my moſt certaine griefe; and the rather to ſee the ſtate of our moſt bleſſed Commonwealth (which the gods haue decreed ſhall be eternall, if our ſelues hinder not) to be thus torne with our ciuill Diſcords. You are not igno rant of the miraculous meanes which the gods haue vs'd in rayſing vs to this greatneſſe: not by riches, but pouertie; not by plentie, but want; that what to others has beene the occa ſion of diſgrace, has to vs beene the meanes of our preſent honour: It is the obſeruation of the Grecians, Tacitus, and trueſt Oracle of Greece, Thucidides, that the Athenians Com mon-wealth was not rays'd to that glorie (like the reſt of Greece ) by the fruitfulneſſe but barrenneſſe of the ſoyle: for which cauſe whileſt the Inhabitants liu'd ſecure from the in uaſion of Borderers, others growing rich, were at laſt con ſum'd by their owne diſſentions: ſo that for the auoyding of publike diſturbance, when any were afflicted, they retyred to the Athenians, with what they had left, before all were loſt; who as they did partake of the Athenians ſecuritie, ſo mutu ally offer'd to the Athenians the participation of their wealth: the like I may ſay of our preſent eſtate; we haue not ſought vnto others; yet who haue not ſought to vs? we had nothing, yet what want we, vnleſſe it be a moderation of our felicitie? All other Mechanicke faculties, of whatſoeuer Corporati ons, haue they not forſooke themſelues to retyre to vs? and yeelded vp their eſtates, which they thought vnhappie, to re ceiue them as an happineſſe from our bountie? I ſpeake not theſe things vnto you as an inſtructer, but a remembrancer: Not to impoſe on you a new beliefe beyond your experience, but to imprint in your mindes a iuſt conſideration of your dangerous contention. I haue yet but begunne to ſpeake; but ſorrow is a bad Oratour, and I muſt continue my ſpeech with a ſilent Rhetorike. He ſpeakes this aſide to Cauſid. In good faith, I had the prettieſt dreame that e're you heard, mee thought as I was about to picke a fellowes pocket, hee ſtrooke mee quite thorow the hand with a knife, and leauing the knife in my hand, thruſt his hand into my pocket and pickt it, and ſo puniſht me, as I haue puniſht o thers many a time. Yes, Sir. I proteſt vnto your Honour, there was nothing but a few idle papers in't, but not a peny of mony. Sure then, Sir, you put it forth againe before you came forth. And Ile quickly bite it out of my hand againe. Wee ſcorne to ſcape this puniſhment. Exeunt Choler, Phyſiog. Cheiro.

Plot — Technogamia, or The Marriages of the Arts
Astronomia has several suitors: Geographus (whom her mother dislikes), Geometres (whom her friends favour), and Poeta (who has recently jilted Historia to pursue her). Against her kinsman Polites’ advice, Historia still wants to marry Poeta, consummating the long-standing association between history and the poetic imagination. Poeta quarrels with Grammaticus, and Logicus becomes involved in the ensuing affray. Unsure whether to fight a duel with Poeta or sue him for assault, Logicus sets both courses in train. Hearing that Poeta has been hurt in the fight, Historia sends to Medicus for help, but Magus, who is helping Geometres, sees an opportunity to dispose of one of his client’s rivals, and persuades Medicus to send poison instead. Medicus’ man, Sanguis, delivers the poison, but spots that it isn’t medicine. Poeta infers Historia's treachery, but persuades himself out of absolute misogyny by thinking of Astronomia. To reconcile Poeta with Logicus and Grammaticus, Ethicus arranges a banquet. Several of the wooers use magical help in their suits. Poeta meets two gipsies, who pick his- pocket and foretell that he will marry Astronomia. Using sieve- divination, Magus predicts that Geometres will marry Astronomia, and will achieve this mainly through hypnotism, with a little pharmaceutical help. (Geometres finds the third option, conjuration, too frightening to try.) However, when Astronomia drinks the supposed love philtre at Ethicus’ feast, it poisons her. Meanwhile, Poeta seems reconciled to his former adversaries, but gets drunk and tries to pick fights with Geographus and Grammaticus; Magus charms him to sleep. Causidicus accepts fees from both Logicus and Poeta, so when the case comes to trial, Polites dismisses him and forces the parties to speak for themselves. The trial is adjourned when news arrives that Astronomia, burning up with the poison, has fallen into a coma. Medicus attends her, and cures her by administering an enema. Meanwhile the gipsies and servants indulge themselves in country pastimes: morris dancing, barley-break, and riddles. Polites takes up Geographus’ and Historia’s love- suits: he persuades Astronomia’s mother that Geographus will be a worthy son-in-law, so long as he gets rid of his servant Phantastes, and the couple are married.
