Well, he shall not be, My Oedipus, Ile rather dwell in darkenesse. But my good Lord Tiberio, This Francisco, Is, on the suddaine, stran ely rays'd. But how is his absence borne? It becomes her Vertue, And does confirme, what was reported of her. 'Tis strange. But see, her fauorite: & accompani'd, To your report. There is no contending. How coldly she receiues it. How the Duke stands? And he repents it Madame. He hath payed the forfeit Of his offence, I'me sure, with such a sorrow, As, if it had been greater, would deserue A full remission. That a wise man, And what is more, a Prince, that may command, Should sue thus poorely, and treat with his wife, As she were a victorious enemie, At whose proud feet, himselfe, his State, and Countrey, Basely beg'd mercie. You shall, you are too fond, And feed a pride that's swolne too bigge alreadie, And surfeits with obseruance. Let my head answere it If I offend. Plot summary

Plot — The Duke of Milan
Sforza, Duke of Milan, dotes on his haughty Duchess Marcelia, and fears the prospect of losing her. Her birthday banquet is interrupted by bad news: Sforza rebuffed Charles the Emperor’s overtures and chose to ally Milan with France instead; but now the Empire has routed France at the Battle of Pavia. Sforza expects imperial reprisals, and goes to submit himself to Charles. Before he leaves, he appoints Francisco his deputy, with instructions that, should he not return, Marcelia is to be murdered so that nobody else shall enjoy her. He submits himself to Charles, offers an explanation but no excuse for his supporting France, and expresses his willingness to pay with his life in order to prevent the imperial army from sacking Milan, thus sparing his subjects further bloodshed. His dignity impresses Charles, who confirms his ducal title. In Sforza’s absence, the tensions between Marcelia and his sister Mariana break out into open violence. Though Mariana is his own wife, Francisco takes Marcelia’s side in the quarrel and imprisons Mariana. Believing that Sforza will not return, he propositions Marcelia, shows her the warrant for her execution, and suggests that they should seize power together. She refuses him, so he explains the excessive love which led Sforza to order her death. This annoys her. When Sforza returns to Milan, she greets him coldly, which gives offence. Mariana learns of Francisco’s efforts to seduce Marcelia, and informs Sforza, who will not believe her. Francisco finds out her plans in advance and sets up the situation to favour his revenge: three years before, his sister Eugenia was seduced by Sforza with a promise of marriage on which he then reneged. First he presents himself to Marcelia as a potential victim of Sforza’s jealousy, inducing her to promise to protect him. Then he tells Sforza that Marcelia has tried to seduce him. News arrives that Francisco has fled Milan; Marcelia tells her husband the truth, and dies. Sforza is distracted with grief.
