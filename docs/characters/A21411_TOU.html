Full speech

You'r the gloue, which stil I seeke to weare Make me happy, match it to a paire. Tis rare to be a tinker boy, worke inough, wench inough, and drinke inough, is't not boy? I Maister. Docters came her pulse to feele, And Surgions with their tooles of steele, To dig, to delue, to find her paine, But all they did it was in vaine, Still on her back this maiden lies, And with an open throat she cries. O stone, stone na ne ra, stone ne na ne ra stone. Old wiues they made answere thus, Greene sicknes was most dangerous. And oate-meale eating is afood, That neuer yet did maiden good. Tut, tut, tut, tis nothing so, Still she cri'd out with paine and wo. O stone, stone ne na ne ra, stone ne na ne ra, stone Till she was deliuered of a chopping boy, and all was as I am, Omne bene. Knight, feast, knight, a good celler keeper knight. I'le cusse thy daughter knight. Beare w'e me maister, he that pines in griefe, Liues as you sailors doe, thinking at sea, Euery storme ends, when flattery flouts ye, So to our loue-sick sorrow comes a calme, By ease of fancies, when tis furthest, And many times the weapon that doth wound, Is salue, and Surgeon both, to make all sound. All sitted, let me take my last farewel, I am all gelly in my teares and sighes, Wasted by waiting her vntimely losse, So long I did consume in drops of woe, That contrary I laugh to thinke it so. He that weepes much, hauing no teares to spend, Smiles out the rest, but inwardly does rend, O God that I ventring so hard a chaunce, Should loose my dice, before my hand be out, Tis euen so, in all things man intends The losse is ours, the winnings not our friends. Then thus, and this the last, Moll, I take leaue yet on thy hersed selfe, Dead selfe, and selfe slaine. Moll d … cest I am thy morning musike, call thee vp, To wedded rights, I leade thee to the Church, And there receiue thee, dine with thee at noone, Daunce all the after day, bring thee at night Into the wedding chamber, this is it. And here I leaue thee to thy virgen slumber, Neuer attempted, as thy birth bestowd, Madam beginning, take it in thy end, So liue thus die, onely my married friend, And nothing else, gone art thou to a power, Which will with welcomes take thee, left I am To the worlds crosse, thy father who inrag'd, Will bitterly reuenge thy death on me, But I will proue his martyr, se-thee Moll, Foole that I am to say so, here is all, Gownes, tire, all abilliments of thine, No rag shall rest remembrant in my viewe. To stirre the imbers of thy dying fire, I kisse thy key cold corse, and with this key, Lock thee for euer vp, farewell, farewell, My mouth the Church, my voice thy Parting knell. All cares that liue and heare This bell to toule, Christian-like bid peace vnto her soule.

Plot summary

Plot — The Two Maids of More-Clacke
Before the action, James Humil wrote to his wife from what he took to be his deathbed. Believing herself widowed, she made swift arrangements for her remarriage to Sir William Vergir, with the support of her son, who hoped to marry one of Sir William’s daughters, Now, on her way back from the wedding, she sees James in the street. She loses her glove, and offers a reward for its discovery: the person who finds . it will have the honour of deciding the festivities in celebration of her second marriage. This is a ruse to enable her to see James, who has found the glove. She tells him that, though she genuinely thought him dead, she contracted her second marriage as a means of paying his debts and securing a desirable match for ===== PAGE 471 ===== 1608 their son. She takes on James as a servant, and tells her second husband that she will only consummate the marriage once Young Humil is married to his elder daughter, Mary. Since both daughters are already engaged, Sir William tries to put off the fiancés by setting impossible conditions: Toures will only be allowed to marry Mary after she has died and come back to life; Young Filbon will get Tabitha only if he becomes a woman. Filbon sends a letter to Tabitha via Touch, Sir William’s servant, but the letter is found and Touch dismissed; Tabitha becomes estranged from her father. Toures visits Mary disguised as a tinker, and they go off to Putney to get married. Tabitha has two other suitors ~ Auditor, Toures’s father, and the Welsh knight Sir Robert, who is really Touch in disguise — but she tells them she is only interested in Filbon. The creation of Sir Robert is a ruse to allow Filbon access to her, despite her father’s opposition: when he releases her to be married to the Welsh knight, Touch hands over the role to Filbon. Toures and Mary are married, and escape by ship, but Mary falls ill during a storm at sea. She appears to die and is buried with all her possessions in a trunk on the beach in the Scilly Isles. The Governor of the Scillies digs up the trunk, thinking it booty, and finds her inside, still alive; he happens to be her uncle. Humil finds his mother in bed with James, and tells Vergir. He is unable to substantiate the charge of adultery because, by the time Vergir arrives to see for himself, James has left the bed (but is still in the room).
