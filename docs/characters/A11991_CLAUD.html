Thus answer I in name of Benedicke, But heare these ill newes with the eares of Claudio: Tis certaine so, the Prince wooes for himselfe, Friendship is constant in all other things, Saue in the office and affaires of loue: Therefore all hearts in loue vse their owne tongues. Let euery eie negotiate for it selfe, And trust no Agent: for Beauty is a witch, Against whose charmes, faith melteth into blood: This is an accident of hourely proofe, Which I mistrusted not: farewel therefore Hero. And I my Lord. Yea my good lord: how stil the euening is, As husht on purpose to grace harmonie! O very wel my lord: the musique ended, Weele fit the kid-foxe with a penny worth. Faith like enough. Baite the hooke wel, this fish will bite. She did indeede. He hath tane th' infection, hold it vp. Ile bring you thither my lord, if youle vouchsafe me. Thats as much as to say, the sweete youthe's in loue. And when was he woont to wash his face? Nay but his iesting spirit, which is now crept into a lute-string, and now gouernd by stops. Nay but I know who loues him. Yes, and his ill conditions, and in dispight of al, dies for him. Tis euen so, Hero and Margaret haue by this play ed their parts with Beatrice, and then the two beares will not bite one another when they meete. If there be any impediment, I pray you discouer it. May this be so? O mischiefe strangely thwarting! Leonato, stand I here? Is this the prince? is this the princes brother? Is this face Heroes? are our eies our owne? Let me but moue one question to your daughter, And by that fatherly and kindly power, That you haue in her, bid her answer truly. To make you answer truly to your name. Mary that can Hero, Hero itselfe can blot out Heroes vertue. What man was he talkt with you yesternight, Out at your window betwixt twelue and one? Now if you are a maide, answer to this. O Hero! what a Hero hadst thou bin, If halfe thy outward graces had bin placed, About thy thoughts and counsailes of thy heart? But fare thee well, most foule, most faire, farewell Thou pure impietie, and impious puritie, For thee ile locke vp all the gates of Loue. And on my eie-liddes shall Coniecture hang, To turne all beautie into thoughts of harme, And neuer shall it more be gracious. Now signior, what newes? Wee had likt to haue had our two noses snapt off with two old men without teeth. We haue beene vp and downe to seeke thee, for we are high proofe melancholie, and would faine haue it beaten away, wilt thou vse thy wit? Neuer any did so, though very many haue been be side their wit, I will bid thee drawe, as wee doe the minstrels, w to pleasure vs. What, courage man: what though care kild a catte, thou hast mettle enough in thee to kill care. Nay then giue him another staffe, this last was broke crosse. If he be, he knowes how to turne his girdle. God blesse me from a challenge. Well I wil meet you, so I may haue good cheare. I faith I thanke him he hath bid me to a calues head & a capon, the which if I doe not carue most curiously, say my kniffe's naught, shall I not find a woodcocke too? For the which shee wept heartily and saide she ca red not. All all, and moreouer, God sawe him when he was hid in the garden. Rightly reasoned, and in his owne diuision, and by my troth theres one meaning wel suted. I haue dronke poison whiles he vtterd it. To night ile mourne with Hero. Plot summary

Plot — Much Ado About Nothing
Returning to Messina after a war, Claudio falls in love with Hero. At a masked revel, Don Pedro woos her for him by proxy; Don John maliciously tells Claudio that Pedro has wooed her for himself, but the truth soon purges Claudio’s jealousy and he and Hero are engaged to be married in a week’s time. In the interim, Don Pedro undertakes to make Beatrice and Benedick, who have a humour of mutual hatred, fall in love with each other. This is done by letting each of them overhear a conversation about how much in love the other is. Don John attempts to prevent Claudio’s marriage by showing him what he takes to be a nocturnal assignation between Hero and another man; in fact it is Margaret in Hero’s clothes, wooed by Borachio whom Don John has suborned. Claudio rejects Hero at the wedding, and the Friar proposes that she feign death to inculcate remorse in him. Borachio and his associate Conrad are arrested and examined, and confess the plot. As penance, Claudio undertakes to marry another ===== PAGE 61 ===== 1598 lady, said to be Leonato’s niece, without first seeing her; she is actually Hero. Benedick and Beatrice join Hero and Claudio to make it a double wedding. Don John flees Messina, but is apprehended and brought back to face punishment.
