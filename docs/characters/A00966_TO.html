He is very merry Madam M. Wildbraine, has him in hand, ith' bottome o'th Sellar He sighes and tipples. We may thank your dry chaps for't, the Canaries gone too No substance for a sorrowfull minde to worke upon, I cannot mourne in beere, if she should walke now As discontented spirits are wont to doe. A fortification of March Beere will doe well, I must confesse tis a most mighty Armour, For I presume I cannot pray. We Coachmen have such tumbling faiths, no pray'rs Can goe an even pace. Verily Nurse, I have cry'd so much For my young Mistresse, that is mortified, That if I have not more sacke to support me, I shall even sleepe, heigho, for another flagon; These burialls and Christnings are the mournefulst matters, And they aske more drinke. Mine's ever sad, for I am ever dry Nurse. There may be one neere it. Would I had a ladder to behold it, Mercy upon me, the Ghost of one oth' Guard sure, Tis the Devill by his clawes, he smells of Brimstone, Sure he farts fire, what an earth-quake I have in me; Out with thy prayer booke Nurse. Who's that. I feele his talents through me Tis an old haggard devill, what will he doe with me? A lecherous Devill. If I should have a young Satan by him, for I dare not de ny him, In what case were I? who durst deliver me? Sweete Devill be good to me. Some Jew, some Justice, A thousand heathen smells to say truth Madam, And would you mellow my young pretty Mistresse In such a mis-ken? So had I Madam, simply though I stand here, I had beene ravish'd too: I had twenty Spirits In every corner of the house a Fiend met me. And shall we part with dry lips; Shall we that have beene fellow devills together Flench for an old womans fart? Shall we never ring againe? ne're tosse the tenor, And roule the changes in a Cup of Claret? You shall not want what ere I lay my hands on, As I am sure Automedon the Coachman, Shall be distributed; beare up, I say, hang sorrow, Give me that bird abroad that lives at pleasure, Sam the Butlers true, the Cooke a reverend Trojan, The Faulkner shall sell his Hawkes, and sweare they were rotten, There be some wandring spoones, that may be met with, Ile pawne a Coach horse, peace, utter no sentences. The harnesse shall be us'd in our warres also; Or shall I drive her (tell me but your will now, Say but the word) over some rotten bridge, Or by a Marle pit side, she may slip in daintily, Let me alone for my selfe. Sweet Master Wildbraine. Take a lodging with me to night in the Stable, And ride away to morrow with one of the horses Next your heart, pray doe. He is not up yet Madam, what mean't you To come forth so earely? They said they would follow us. Let him be hang'd fort, You have my voyce. Doe, and Ile helpe him; And if he were here this whip should conjure him, Here's a capias, and it catch hold on's breech, Ide make him soone beleeve the devill were there. He married the mony, thats all he lookt for, For your daughter, let her sinke or swimme. They'le come, Feare not Madam, and bring Clappers with 'em, Or some have lost their old wont, I have heard No disparagement to your Ladyship, some o'their tongues Like Tom a Lincolne three miles off. What and we lost no time, You and I shall make a shift to beginne with him, And tune our Instruments, till the confort come To make up the full noise, Ile knocke. Tis I, Toby, come downe, or else we'le fetch you downe, Alas, this is but the Sauncebell, here's a Gentlewoman Will ring you another peale, come downe, I say. Iew's too good for him. Oh Reprobate! A worshipfull ropes end is to good for him. Come and be whipt first. I am glad y'are come, wele blow the house downe. A charitable motion, fire is comfortable. Bury him alive by all meanes for a testimony. That might ha beene my case last night, Ile ha't what ere it cost me. I will have taht too; There wasan Aunt of mine, I thinke amongst e'm, I would be glad to heare her Testament. Nor I, they walk'd me the fooles morris. With a small print, that no body can reade it. Plot summary

Plot — The Night Walker, or The Little Thief
The usurer Justice Algripe has seized the wastrel Lurcher’s lands to settle a mortgage; he was also amorously interested in Lurcher’s sister, who has since disappeared. The destitute Lurcher is left to steal for a living; invited to join him, his friend Wildbrain prefers womanizing, on the grounds that it will not end on the gallows. Lurcher is approached by a boy, Snap, and, after ascertaining his skills at deception, takes him into his service. Algripe marries Wildbrain’s cousin Maria, who consented out of filial obedience rather than love: she loves Hartlove. Her mother’s household opposes the match, suspecting that old Algripe is impotent, and Wildbrain arranges for Hartlove to meet her on her way to bed and cuckold Algripe on his wedding night. She refuses his advances and faints when they are found together; she is taken for dead and prepared for burial. Her mother throws Wildbrain out for his part in the incident. Lurcher burgles the house that night, frightening the servants by pretending to be a ghost; but what he supposes to be a chest of jewels is actually Maria’s coffin. Upon discovering what their swag really is, he and his accomplices attempt to bury her in secret, but have a chance encounter with Algripe in the churchyard. Snap frightens him away by impersonating Maria’s ghost, but the thieves are scared off themselves when Maria wakes up in her coffin. Wildbrain meets Hartlove in the dark; Hartlove bears him a grudge and fights him. Maria hears the commotion and pretends to be her own ghost, enjoining them not to fight. Wildbrain is already wounded, and takes refuge in the next house he finds, which belongs to Lurcher’s mistress and partner in crime. They fall for one another and he shows off her gifts to Lurcher, who recognizes them as stolen 1615 property — his own booty. Maria’s mother accuses Algripe of stealing the body; when he refuses to repay the marriage portion, she begins litigation. Lurcher cons his way into Algripe’s house posing as a bookseller, ties him up, and steals his valuables and papers, including his own mortgage. Maria returns to her mother’s household posing as her nurse’s Welsh niece. Struck by the resemblance to dead Maria, Hartlove falls in love with her. Lurcher impersonates a constable and takes Algripe to a church where, he says, the villains who robbed him are hiding out; he then drugs Algripe and stows him in the vault.
