Good. These letters from my wife bring certainty Of our obtained pardons, on condition We hence forth for the state of France imploy Our liues and seruice. My valiant friends this 4 years Dansiker Hath led you proudly through a sea of terrour, Through deeds so full of prowesse they might haue grac'd The brow of worthinesse, had iustice to our cause Giuen life and action. But since the breach of lawes Of Nations, ciuill society, iustly intitles vs With the hatefull stile of robbers, let's redeeme our honour And not returne into our country, with the names Of pardoned theeues, but by some worthy deed, Daring attempt, make good vnto the world Want of imployment, not of vertue forct Our former act of spoyle and rapine. Then thus, that with the same weapon, we may Our country cure, with which we wounded her, My purpose is to ruine all the Pyrats Lie in the harbour here. We must not trust to such incertainties. Thus I haue plotted it: we first will set a fire Some house ith' towne, to which when each man makes, As they will be inforced from the hauen To yeeld their helps, with much facility We may performe our purpose. That charge be mine, the Renegado Iew, You know giues free and open entertaine To all of our profession. In some out-house of his I will conuey a pot of wild-fire to it, I'le make a traine of match, that at three howres Shall giue it fire. To morrow night: meane time make ready For our departure, but with such secresie Suspitions selfe may not discry it: provide the balles We must bestow vpon the ships. The rest leaue vnto me, Wee'l returne nobly, or else nobly dye. Ruben, what newes? More yet? do they come on so fast? Your maister would in grosse his prize. Il'e vse my Art sir to his benefit, And for the Crownes, no longer Ile delay him, Heere is my hand to morrow night Ile pay him. Forbeare, giue the poore fellow leaue to prate, he payes for't. The happinesse of the day besall you I adyes. Next night, time of my proiect, if I proue not as hot a shot as came in your quarters since the losse of your virginity, let me suffer the paines of S. Anthonies purgatory. What moues this passion? By no meanes: we will compound the businesse. This is no slaue, he payes 600. duckets at their mee ting, true, we are witnesse on't. I hate this villaine, hee's all bloud. I willingly would stake my life to thine at that iust game, there wants but an occasion. But you are not likely to surfet on it, I'le haue a finger in the platter with you, were you the great Turks selfe. What thou dar'st not. Ward turn'd Turke? it is not possible. I see the hand of heaven prevented mine, Death was too faire a guerdon for him. But to the present, Deseruing sir, I now am to coniure you By all the offices of friendship past, By what my future loue, and meanes may stead you, To vow performance of one small request. I am most confident, best sir adew, If Dansiker do liue, he liues to you. Hast laid the traine to my directions? Danvils makes with vs, all the rest giue fire to, Sares ship except, to him we are ingaged, Nor will we proue vngratefull, are all things ready, Aboard then instantly, Tunis fare-well, Dansiker bids all pyrats now adue. Hee'l shew you, what you might do, were you true Benwash murdered? he hath saued me a labour. What meanes the slaue to eye me so? On me? Villaine, thou buy'st my bloud At a deere rate. O thou immortall God Who know'st my innocence! that for his former sins Hast guiven vp, Dansiker into the hands Of these damn'd miscreants. I Dansiker, that would with all your deaths Haue cancelled his former infamy, Left to the world a president of valour, Writ in your sad confusions: but heauen is iust, Christians did fall by me, by slaues I must. I will confesse it willingly: It was to haue conueid This Iew from hence, haue made a massacre Of the whole Towne, dasht out the miscreant braines ▪ Of your yong Infidels. Yes Dogge, I am sorry, and confesse my crimes Preuented such a merit: I was not worthy To do heaven so good a seruice. Yes Pagan, villaine, I will. Forgiuenesse heauen, Let my example moue all Pyrates, Robbers To thinke how heavy thy revenging hand Will sit vpon them. I feele thy iustice now, Receiue my soule, accept my intended vow.

Plot summary

Plot — A Christian Turned Turk (The Two Famous Pirates)
By a ruse, the pirate Ward takes prisoner two French merchants, Ferdinand and Albert. He attacks the ship taking Alizia to her bridegroom, Young Raymond, and kills her brother; she escapes by disguising herself as a ship-boy, Fidelio. Another pirate, Francisco, challenges him for the spoils; they engage in single combat, and ultimately agree to share the booty. During the fight, Ward’s men Gismund and Gallop mutiny and steal a ship, and make for Tunis, taking the disguised Alizia with them. Ward swears vengeance, seizes further prisoners, among them Alizia’s fiancé, and sells them into slavery in Tunis. Gallop makes his peace with Ward by pretending he was abducted, like ‘Fidelio’. The Turkish authorities in Tunis attempt to convert Ward to Islam. He is initially reluctant, but agrees when Voada offers herself as his wife; even so, he evades circumcision by putting an ape’s tail under the knife instead of his penis. He marries Voada as planned and gives her ‘Fidelio’ as her page; but in fact ‘Fidelio’ has been the object of her affections all along, and she has no sexual interest in Ward. ‘Fidelio’ agrees to sleep with her if she arranges for a particular slave to escape, claiming him to be a brother (though of course it is really Young Raymond). To evade the sexual part of the bargain, ‘Fidelio’ then makes an alternative arrangement with Ward, who agrees to help her, but then double-crosses her by telling his wife. Voada kills Raymond, thinking he is trying to rob her of ‘Fidelio’, then Alizia kills herself. Seeking revenge, Voada tries to attack Ward; she is slightly wounded when he defends himself, but exaggerates the injury in order to get him arrested. (The Tunisian authorities are uninterested in the murder of the two Christians.) Ward stabs himself, and dies cursing Turkish treachery. In the other plot, Gallop makes a date for sex with Agar. Her jealous husband Benwash allows the tryst to go ahead, since he needs definite proof of adultery if he is to end the marriage. Meanwhile, the reformed pirate Dansiker has been offered a pardon by the French King, but wishes to do something positive to redeem his past crimes, rather than passively accept forgiveness; so he plans to rid Tunis of its pirates by blowing up the town. Gallop keeps his date with Agar, but thieves steal his clothes and rope ladder while he is busy enjoying himself.
