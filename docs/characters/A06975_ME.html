A word good Mr. Orator. Tis not mans fortune, enuy or neglect, Which makes him miserable, but tis meane fate, Euen sole predestination, a firme gift, Fixt to his birth, before the world was made, For were it otherwise, then within our liues, We should finde some distractions, errours change. And other toies of much vncertaintie: But my mishaps are fixt so to my blood, They haue no sire but my creation: The Queene out of suspition that my loue, First set an edge vpon the Kings desires, And made him wooe her with a victors sword, Casts me from fauour, seazes all my lands, And turnes my naked fortunes to the cold. The King made proud with purchase of his wish Neglects my sufferance for him, and ore lookes, The low-tide of my fortunes; lest my woes Should speake my wrongs to his ingratitude; The whil'st those lords whose supple hams haue bow'd To doe me formal reuerence, now despise And sleight me in their meanest compliments: O tis a torment more then hell yet knowes To be an honest flatterer, or to liue A saint in Limbo, which that I may preuent, Ile be nor best nor worst, but all indifferent. But here comes a noble man, I must turne petitioner. My Lord, may I not see the king? O but he doth neglect, And sleights me like his weake orations, And by your Lordships leaue, I do not thinke His wisedome worthy of the conference. Why, and fare you well, S'foot this is more then strange, That being grieu'd I may not say i'me paind. But heere comes another: Mine honorable Lord May I not haue some conference with the king? Yet methinkes twere meet Its strange. Why this is court grace to men in misery, And thus these taile-lesse Lions with their roare, Affright the simple heard: O I could now Turne rebell gainst their pride. But heere comes the Duke: My gratious Lord, vouchsafe to heare my griefes. Words are no heauy burthen. Your grace doth do me comfort, and I will Study with seruice to deserue your fauours, And so I take my leaue. Saue you Gentlemen; sir is your master at any leasure? VVe must attend, vm, euen snailes keep state VVhen with slow thrust their hornes peep forth the gate. VVe must attend, tis customs fault not mine To make men proud, on whom great fauours shine, Its somewhat gainst my nature to attend. But when we must, we must be patient, A man may haue admittance to the king As soone as to these long roabes, and as cheap. Come gentlemen shall we walke? Thus are the pauement stones before the doores Of these great tongue guilt Orators, worne smoth With clients dauncing for them. O onely; they of all men in request. Your physitian is the lawier for your health: And moderate vnruly humours best. Others are no body compard with him: For all men neglect their health in regard of their profit. Yea with golden fees, And golden titles too, they can worke miracles, And like creators, euen of emptie nothing Erect a world of goodly liuings, faire demeanes, And gallant mannors, heapt one on another. Why heres a fellow now, this Orator Euen Prat, you would little thinke it, his father was An honest proiner of our cuntry vines; Yet hee's shot to his foot-cloath. Faith reasonable learning, a smattring in the Latine tongue, A little Rhetoricke, with wrangling sophistrie, Were his preparatiues vnto his art. Nay for his pride, of weaker soules tearmd state, It hurts none but himselfe. Srange; it's not strange a whit, Dunghils and marrish bogs, dart store of vapours, And viscous exhalations, against heauen, Which borrowing luster there (though basly bred) Seeme yet like glorious planets fairest starres: To the weake eies of wondring ignorance, When wise men know they are but Meteors. It is, and with such strictnes and aduice, For speedy execution of the same, That by this time I know they are in the way Vnto their execution, for the hower Of death doth runne vpon his latest minutes. Of all which my Lord, I being (in a strict conceit) a baudy wit nesse: and hauing both from the Orators scornes and delaies receiued many indignities; though by this discouery to cry quittance with my proud enemy.

Plot summary

Plot — The Dumb Knight
The King of Cyprus, in love with the Queen of Sicily; lays siege to her city, but accepts the Queen’s proposal that they settle the affair with a trial by combat: the King and his champion Philocles versus the Duke of Epire and Lord Alfonso. Epire defeats the King but is bested in a play-off with Philocles; the Queen accepts the outcome and submits to the King’s love, but Epire swears to avenge his dishonour. Philocles woos Epire’s sister, Mariana, but gets the brush-off. Asked to set him a task to prove his love, she demands that he remain silent for a year; when he swears to do so, she is pleased not to have to listen to any more love-talk from him. His dumbness is taken to be a malady, which medicine fails to remedy. The King offers a reward for a successful cure ~ but those who try unsuccessfully will be executed. Mariana offers to achieve the cure, but Philocles will not speak at her command and she is duly sentenced to death. Philocles waits until the headsman is about to strike before speaking out. She is spared, but Philocles refuses to marry her until she demonstrates that she cares as much for him as he for her. Epire’s vindictive vexation grows. Epire suggests to the King that the Queen is having an affair with Philocles: he takes his opportunities to put the Queen into Philocles’ company, and arranges for the King to see them dancing and playing cards together. They are arrested and imprisoned, and parliament condemns them to death. Epire’s plan is to proclaim the Queen’s innocence after she has been executed, and then see the King deposed by an outraged populace. Mariana bribes Philocles’ jailer to allow her a visit. They swap garments: Philocles escapes posing as her, and she joins the Queen on the scaffold on the day of execution. Epire agrees to defend his accusation as the King’s champion in a trial by combat. Philocles comes forward to fight for the Queen, and defeats Epire, who admits malicious slander. The Queen forgives him, but only for Mariana’s sake. In the sub-plot, Lollia is unhappily married to the choleric orator Prate. Alfonso attempts to seduce her, using her neighbour as a go-between; Lollia agrees to receive him when her husband is next away. Lord Méchant has had his lands confiscated for abetting the Cypriot invasion; the King has sent him and others to Prate, but Prate neglects these cases in favour of the King’s own business. Méchant plans to avenge himself by cuckolding Prate, but finds that Alfonso has got there first.…
