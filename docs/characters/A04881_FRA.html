Sir, thanke your selfe. You know sir, I did inioy a quiet country life, Spotlesse and free, till you corrupted mee, And brought me to the Court, I neuer knew, What sleeking, glazing, or what pressing meant, Till you preferd me to your Aunt the Lady, I knew no Iuotie teeth, no caps of heire, No Mercury water, fucas, or perfumes, To helpe a Ladies breath, vntill your Aunt, Learnt me the common trick. Sir, you are his friend (I loue him to) Propound a course which may aduantage him, And you shall finde such reall worth in me, That rather then Ile liue his hindrance, I will assume the most penurious state The Citty yeelds, to giue me meanes of life ▪ Beleeue me loue, how ere some stricter wits, Condemne all women which are prone to loue, And thinke that if their fauour fall on any, By consequence they must be naught with many, And hold a false position, that a woman False to her selfe, can trusty be to no man, Yet know I say, how ere my life hath lost The fame which my Virginity aspyr'd, I will be true to thee, my deed shall mooue, To win from all men pitty, if not loue. How now you slaue? my rampant trickes you rogue, Nay feare not me my onely feare is still, Thy filthy face betrayes vs, for all men know, Thy nose stands compas like a bow, Which is three quarters drawne, thy head. Which is with greasy haire ore-spred, And being vncurld and blacke as cole, Doth show some Scullion in a hole Begot thee on a Gipsie, or Thy mother was some Colliers whore My rampant tricks you rogue, thou't be descride Before our plor be ended. Sir I haue giuen A longer venture then true modesty Will well allow, or your more grauer witte Commend. I must beleeue it now. I am glad he is gon, a put me to the blush When a did aske me of ritch Somerfields death. Thankes gentle brother. But will you do it truly. This Gentleman whom I so much affect, I scarsly yet doe know, so blind is loue, In things which most concernes it, as y'are honest Tell me his birth, his state, and farthest hopes. O mee accurst, Has a no credit, Lands and Mannors. I am abus'd, coosned, and deceiu'd. Is there no trust, No honesty in men. Good sir, may I not by law forsake him And wed another, though my word be past To be his wife. I am resolu'd, I will not wed him, though I beg my bread. I thanke you sir, I will rather wed a most persidious Redshanke, A noted Iewe, or some Mechanick slaue, Then let him ioy my sheets. He has beene nibling. Seruant. Ex. We are vndone. This fellow has no land; and which is worse, Hee has no credit. That were too great a scandall. 'Tis impossible, this night he meanes to wed mee. Lets heere the meanes. I giue my liking. That must not be, Furnish me with Iewels, and then my selfe, Attended by you man and honest Beard, Will thether first, and with my Lady mother Craue a peace for you. I when shee knowes Before by me, from what an eminent plague Your wisdome has preseru'd me. I can hold no longer, impudent man. O thou perfidious man darst thou presume To call her wife, whom thou so much hast wrong'd, What conquest hast thou got, to wrong a maide, A silly harmelesse maide? what glory ist That thou hast thus deceiued a simple Virgin, And brought her from her friends? what honor wast For thee to make the Butler loose his office And runne away with thee. Your tricks are knowne, Didst thou not sweare thou shouldst be Baroniz'd? And hadst both lands and fortunes? both which thou wantst. I know your trick:, And know I now am wife vnto this man. Therefore subscribe, Take some-what of him for a full release, And pray to God to make you an honest man, If not, I doe protest by earth and Heauen, Although I starue, thou neuer shalt inioy me. I hope a will compound. Sir you may choose, What's the morgage worth? Doe it, doe it, Come you shall do it, we will be rid of him At any rate. Let it come sweet Rogue. This health is pledgd And honest Sargeant how does maister Gripe The Drawer stands aside. The keeper of the Counter, I do protest I found him alwayes fauorable to mee, A is an honest man, has often stood to me, And beene my friend and let me goe a trust For victuall when a denied it knights: but come, Lets pay and then begon, th'arrest you know Was but a trick to get from nimble Dash My husbands man: I doe protest, By all the honesty twixt thee and mee, I got her in that gowne in sixe weekes space Foure pound and foureteene pence giuen by a Clarke Of an Inne of Chancerie, that night I came Out of her house, and does the filthy Iade Send to me for money? but honest Sergeant, Let me go, and say thou didst not see me, Ile doe thee as great a pleasure shortly. Plot summary

Plot — Ram Alley, or Merry Tricks
The rich heiress Constantia Somerfield disguises herself as a page and enters the employ of her beloved, Boucher, intending to keep an eye on his fidelity. The rich widow Taffeta drops a handkerchief to get Boucher’s attention, and invites him into her house. Despite a prophecy that a widow will endanger his life, soul, lands, and reputation, Boucher is attracted to her. He has several rivals, including the old knight Sir Oliver Smallshank and Captain Puff, who claims marital rights in Taffeta and threatens to attack anyone who shows any sexual interest in her. Sir Oliver comes to woo Taffeta, but hides under her farthingale when Puff arrives. Puff assaults her, and Sir Oliver is forced out of his hiding place. Seeing the advantages of being a courtier’s wife, Taffeta agrees to marry him. Sir Oliver’s younger son, Will, is in debt, and his lands are mortgaged to Throat the lawyer. He pretends to have eloped with Constantia, but the woman he introduces to his family is actually his whore, Frances. He leaves her with Throat while he goes to arrange the wedding, asking Throat to confirm his cover story: that he has expectations of becoming a lord. Throat tells her the truth, that Will is a penniless fortune-hunter, and woos the lady for himself. When Will takes her, ostensibly to a nocturnal marriage ceremony, Throat has them attacked in the darkened streets. They are separated from Frances, whom Throat then marries himself. Will arranges to have himself arrested for debt when he comes looking for his putative fiancée; Throat is initially unwilling to help him, but, encouraged by Frances, agrees to pay the debt and return Will’s mortgage if he will sign a legal document renouncing all prior claims to Constantia Somerfield. Will agrees, and gets his lands back and some of his debts paid. Constantia’s mother, Lady Somerfield, comes to town, and is told that Will has abducted her daughter. Throat introduces himself as her son-in-law, and is arrested. Meanwhile Frances steals away with her new husband’s jewels. She is arrested at a tavern, and thinks it a ruse to get her away from Throat’s man; when the debt turns out to be real, she tries to sweet-talk the sergeant into releasing her. She is redeemed by her accomplice, Beard, only to be ‘rescued’ from him by Will's elder brother, who still thinks her Constantia and intends to marry her himself.
