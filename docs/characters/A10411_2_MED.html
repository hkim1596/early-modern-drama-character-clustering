You'l in! Pilumnus having lost this hopefull Sonne, Though he had two more children, fair Vrania And noble Damon; yet the death of Lalage Suffic'd not his revenge, but he a new implores His goddesse wrath 'gainst Claius: —Doth Ceres prize me thus? Shall Claius tread upon the flowry Plaine, And walke upon the Ashes of my boy? Will I be Archyflamen where the Gods Are so remisse? let wolves approach their shrines; Their howlings are as powerfull as the Praiers Of sad Pilumnus! —Such disgusts at last Awaken'd Ceres: with hollow murmuring noise Her Ompha like a thunder'gins to roare. (The Ompha if it menace speakes at large In copious language, but perplexed termes.) And laid this curse on all Trinacria. Sicilian swaines, ill luck shall long betide To every bridegroome, and to every bride: No sacrifice, no vow shall still mine Ire, Till Claius blood both quench and kindle fire. The wise shall misconceive me, and the wit Scornd, and neglected shall my meaning hit. Doe so then: For now you see with what unfortunate choice Pilumnus daughter, delicate Vrania loves The mad Amyntas: for the angry Goddesse, Though she repaid the wrong done to Philaebus, Yet not approving the reuengefull mind Of great Pilumnus, scourg'd him with his own asking, By threatning an unhappy marriage To his Vrania, unlesse he that wooes her Pay an impossible Dowry; for as others Give Portions with their daughters, Ceres Priests Vse to receive for theirs. The words are these, That which thou hast not, mayst not, canst not have Amyntas, is the Dowry that I crave ▪ Rest hopelesse in thy love, or else divine To give Vrania this, and she is thine: Which while the poore Amyntas would Interpret, He lost his wits. Take heed of love, Laurinda, You see th'unhappinesse of it in others; Let not experience in thy selfe instruct thee. Be wise my Girle: so come and follow me. How sweet a folly is this love? But rash youth, Alexis, (As youth is rash) runnes indiscreetly on While mature judgment ripened by experience Stayes for loves season. Yes, Alexis, Nothing's borne ripe, all things at first are greene, You are to hasty reapers that doe call For Sickles in the spring: Woemen indeed too soone begin their fall. Yet till curst Claius dye, as now he must, Alexis, and Laurinda, let my counsell Asswage the heat of youth; pray be perswaded A while for to deferre your nuptiall blisse; 'Tis but a while. Temper loves fire. Yet, loving paire, stay 'till a fayrer gale; He deserves shipwrack, ('tis the Marriners flout) And justly too, that in a storme sets out. Tis well; now let's goe take our place, to see For our sad griefes a sadder remedy.

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
