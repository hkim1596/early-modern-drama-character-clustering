Thrise noble king, be patient, this I reade, The Gods haue feet of wooll, but hands of lead: And therefore in reuenge as sure, as slow. What though two Royall Armies we haue lost? He that beares man about him, must be crost: And that base Monmouth, that with his golde ̄ head Salutes the Sunne, may with the Sunne fal dead. For base Rebellion drawes so short a breath, That in the day she moues, she moues to death: And like the Marigold opens with the Sunne, But at the night her pride is shut and done. Go you vnto the Church, and with your holy fires Perfume the Altars of your country gods, Whilst I in curses, swifter in pursute, Then winged lightning, execrate your soules, And all your Hymeneall iollity, Now swels the wombe of my inuention, With some prodigious proiect, and my brayne Italianates my barren faculties To Machiuilian blacknesse. Welshman, stand fast; Or by these holy raptures that inspire The soule of Polititians with reuenge, Blacke proiects, deepe conceits, quaynt villanies, By her that excommunicates my right Of my creation, with a bastards name, And makes me stand nonsuted to a crowne; Ile fall my selfe, or plucke this Welshman down. Cornwall, he kild thy brother. There's the base, Whereon my enuy shall erect the frame Of his confusion. Gloster, I know, Is Natures master-piece of enuious plots, The Cabinet of all adulterate ill Enuy can hatch; with these I will beginne, To make blacke enuy Primate of each sin. Now, in the heate of all their reuelling, Hypocrisie, Times best complexion, Smooth all my rugged thoughts, let them appeare As brothell sinnes benighted, darkely cleare. Lend me thy face, good Ianus, let mee looke Iust on Times fashion, with a double face, And clad my purpose in a Foxes case. Exit. Now friends and fellow Souldiours in iust Arms, Prepare your selues against the haughty foe, Who, as wee heare, marches not farre from hence. What we haue done, by force weele make it good, Or seale our bold attempts, with death and bloud. What tidings brings this sweating Messenger? Now, Caradoc, what ist thou canst demaund? Ile heare no mad men speake. Great Generall, that which he holds, is mine; And though infor'st by violence and wrong, From that which Nature left my heritage: Yet, since I see such hopes, so fayrely sprung From such an honourable head, as Rome, Whose fame for honour, cheualry and armes, Out-shines all Nations with her glorious rayes: This Caradoc, whom men doe causlesse feare, Is of condition insolent and proud, Ambitious, tyrannous, speckled with euery vice The infectious time can harbour. Say, we confesse him bold, And of a courage that grim visag'd death, The obiect of true valour, cannot daunt; Though Proteus -like, he came in thousand shapes, What's he, comparde to numbers infinite? Or that Imperiall Rome, whose Eagle eyes Haue gaz'd against the sunne of matchlesse tryumphs, Should basely feare a weake and silly Fly? This Welshman is all superficiall, Without dimensions, and like a mountaine swels, In labour onely with great ayry words, Whose birth is nothing, but a silly Mouse; Actions without their measure or their weight. Then, Romanes, derogate not from the worth, That time in ancient Chronicles records Of your eternall honours got in warre. But if you prize your honours more than life, Or humane happinesse, here's a noble cause Of wrong and vsurpation, to erect A statue to your dying memory. Then on, great Generall, waue the Romane Eagle, Euen to the Tents of haughty Caradoc, And with my bloud Ile second this braue fight, Or hide my shame by death in endlesse night. Stay, Prince, and let me speake. Weake Prince, first keepe a dyet for a time, To adde fresh vigour to thy feeble limmes, And then, perhaps, weele teach thee how to fight.

Plot summary

Plot — The Valiant Welshman (Caradoc the Great)
The Earl of. Monmouth seeks to usurp old King Octavian’s throne, and has won two significant victories over him. Cadallan and his sons come to support Octavian. Cadallan is killed in the ensuing battle, but his son Caradoc avenges him by killing Monmouth. Re-established on the throne, Octavian rewards Caradoc with marriage to his daughter and heir, Guinevere. This displeases his bastard son, Codigune, who had regarded Guinevere as his only obstacle to the succession. King Guiderius has failed to pay tribute to the Romans, so the Emperor Claudius invades Britain. Guiderius appeals to Octavian, and Caradoc is sent, fresh from his wedding, to offer military aid. In his absence, Codigune conspires with Gloucester and Cornwall: they poison Octavian and imprison Guinevere, and Codigune seizes power. Gloucester writes warning Guiderius not to trust Caradoc, so Guiderius, about to go into battle, orders the Welsh troops to observe from a hilltop and intervene only ifa British defeat seems likely. Caradoc disregards this, joins the battle in disguise, and bests Claudius in hand- to-hand combat; he refuses Claudius’ offer to buy his freedom, but accepts a token of the Emperor’s friendship, a golden lion. Prince Gald is impressed by Caradoc’s single-handed contribution to the British victory, accompanies him back to Wales, and marries his sister Voada. Finding Codigune installed as King of Wales, Caradoc offers to settle matters in single combat. Codigune is beaten, refuses an offer to make him Viceroy of North Wales, and flees to the Romans. Cornwall submits to Caradoc, who is crowned King. Gloucester flees to make more mischief: he persuades a witch to create a ravaging monster which Caradoc will be expected to fight, and which will kill him. The Romans send Caradoc an ultimatum, requiring him to resign the throne to Codigune; at the same time, he is summoned to deal with the monster, a giant serpent. He gives priority to the serpent, and defeats it with the help of a magic herb, then disposes of the witch and forces her son Bluso to swear that in future he will use his magic powers only for good. Gloucester hangs himself. While Caradoc is away, Cornwall hands over the town of Gloucester to the Romans. Gallicus, the Roman legate who brought the ultimatum, falls in love with Voada and abducts her. Bluso enables Gald to visit her bedchamber, magically unseen.
