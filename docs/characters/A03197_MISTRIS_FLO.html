Mary and shall, kind Gentleman, my furthe rance saist thou? Yes Anthony, assure thy selfe, for by the mo therly care that I beare to my daughter, it hath been a desire that long hath lodgde within my carefull breast, to match hir with thy well-deseruing selfe; and to this end haue I sent for my daughter, and chargde my seruants, that presently vp on her repaire hither from her Mistrisses, that shee enter this priuate walke, where, and with whome, I will so worke, that doubt it not, deere sonne, but she shall be thine. A thousand crowns for you to make the match, pretty heart, how loue can worke! by Gods blest mother, I vow she shall be thine, if I haue any interest in my daughter; Flower smiles reading the Letter, they snatch the Letter from each other. but stay, whom haue I espied? my husband likewise reading of a Letter, and in so good an humour, ile lay my life, good Gentleman hee hath also wrought with him for his good wil; and for I long to know the truth thereof, my sodaine purpose shall experience it. Whats heere husband? She reades priuately, and frownes. a Letter from maister Ferdinand, to intreate a marriage with your daughter. If shee respect her mothers fauour, Tis Anthony shall be her loue. Euen so husband. In this you shall not bridle me I sweare. You teach me husband, what your wife should say, I thinke her life is deerest vnto me, Though you forget the long extreamitie And paine which I indurd, when foorth this wombe With much adoe she did inioy the life she now doth breathe, And shall I now suffer her destruction? A figge for your conceits, in this I know there can be none that: Say he be his fathers eldest sonne, and a merchaunt of good wealth, yet my deere Anthonies as rich as he: What though his portion was but small at first, His industrie hath now increasd his talent; And he that knoweth the getting of a penny, Will feare to spend, she shall haue him, if any. Tis your owne impatience, you may chuse. By this. And may I neuer liue (so God me help) If euer she be wed to Ferdinand. Fret on, good husband, I will haue my will. All's one for that, I know my daughters mind if I but say the word. Why now old Flower speaketh like himselfe. No daughter, tis thine Anthony. Anthony is rich, yet is not so old. Anthony's more gratious, if more may be. Anthony more wise, then Gerle desire to proue him. He is deceiu'd, tis in thine Anthony. If but rulde by me. If she tend to thee. Fiddle, I wonder that he staies so long, Thou toldst me Anthony would follow thee. Fiddle, are all things ordred well within? Heere, take my keyes, bid Susan take enough. Maister Golding, a word I pray sir, You know my daughter Phillis, doe you not? She is a starre I tell you. I tell you sir vpon the sodaine now, There came an odde conceit into my head, Are you a batcheller? And are you not promisde? Well, do you heare sir? if you will be pleasde To wed my daughter Phillis, you shall haue her. Alls one for that, she will be rulde by me: Disdaine her not because I proffer her, I tell you sir, Merchants of great account Haue sought her loue, and Gentlemen of worth, Haue humbly sude to me in that behalfe: To say the trueth, I promisde her to one, But I am crossd and thwarted by my husband, Who meanes to marry hir vnto another: Now sir, to cry but quittance for his guile, I offer her to you; if you accept her, Ile make her dowrie richer by a paire Of hundred pounds than else it should haue beene. Nay, nay, deferre no time if you wil haue her, Ile search my coffers for another hundred. Now Husband. Yfaith she shall not. No. His will! A fig for your conceit; yet for because, I know I shall preuent him of the match, That he intends, henceforth I will dissemble. Well maister Flower, because it shalbe saide, And for kind maister Berry may report The humble loyalty I beare to you, Such as a wife should doe vnto her husband. I am content to yeeld to your desires, Protesting, whiles I liue, I neuer more Will speake that Anthony may marry her. Husband, I doe. Indeede forsooth I doe.

Plot summary

Plot — The Fair Maid of the Exchange
Phyllis and Ursula are assaulted by two ruffians as they go to deliver wares to a customer at night. A cripple rescues them, and a second assault is foiled by the intervention of Frank Golding; the cripple promises to help Frank if ever he can. Frank's two brothers are both in love with Phyllis, and, though he scorns amorous passions, Frank has fallen for her too. Phyllis, however, loves the cripple, and woos him by giving him a handkerchief to embroider “for a friend’. Meanwhile, Bobbington poses as a sailor going on a voyage, and borrows money from Phyllis’s father, Flower, leaving a diamond ring with him as security. Ferdinand and Anthony overhear one another's plans to woo Phyllis, but neither knows that Frank is also a rival. Ferdinand writes to her; Anthony plans to intercept the letter and substitute his own, but Ferdinand disguises Frank as a porter and gets him hired as Anthony's messenger. Planning to double-cross them both, Frank enlists the cripple's help. The cripple supplies him with two rejection letters to take back to his brothers, as from Phyllis. They each enlist the help of one of Phyllis’s parents, ===== PAGE 378 ===== who then quarrel over which brother shall marry their daughter. _ Knowing Phyllis loves him but thinking himself unworthy, the cripple lends Frank his clothes. Frank courts Phyllis in disguise, and she gives him her ring; then. he brings the parents forged letters from his brothers withdrawing their suits. Each parent decides to:spite the other by offering Phyllis to Frank instead of the other’s favoured son-in-law. The thwarted brothers realize what has happened, but Phyllis asks to be allowed a say in her.own marriage — and chooses Frank. As the play ends, Flower is arrested for receiving stolen property — Bobbington’s ring — and sent for trial. In the sub-plot, Mall Berry is in love with Barnard, but suffers the unwanted attentions of Bowdler. Barnard is surety for a £100 debt to Mall’s father; the original borrower is dead and the debt about to fall due, but Berry refuses to allow Barnard any more time to find the money. Bowdler persists in his pursuit of Mall, using lines he remembers from Venus and Adonis. Berry engages sergeants to arrest Barnard. Mall agrees to marry Bowdler, but the cripple points out that, through a party wall, he has overheard her talking about Barnard in her sleep: he is the man she truly loves.
