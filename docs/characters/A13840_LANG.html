I salute you both with the spirit of copulation, 〈◊〉 already informed of your matrimoniall purposes, and will 〈◊〉 testimonie to the integritie. Fie, fie, fie, these carnall kisses doe stirre vp the Concupisences of the flesh. Sir, I want words and protestation to insinuate into your credit; but in plainnesse and truth, I wil quallifie her griefe with the spirit of consolation. If your Lordship will be pleased to salute me without ceremonie, I shall be willing to exchange my seruice for your fauour; but this worshipping kinde of entertainment, is a super stitious vanitie; in plainnesse and truth I loue it not. That Gentlewoman is most sweetly modest, faire, ho nest, handsome, wise, well-borne, and rich. Shee's like your Dyamond; a temptation in euery mans eye, yet not yeelding to any light impression her selfe. All men are mortall. The houre of death is vncer taine. Age makes sicknesse the more dangerous. And griefe is subiect to distraction. You know not how soone you may be depriu'd of the benefit of sense. In my vnderstanding (there fore) you shall doe well if you be sicke to set your state in pre sent order. Make your Will. Our opposition will but trouble him. No. T'is prophane. Spirits are inuisible. T'is the fiend i'the likenesse of Charlemont. I will haue no conuersation with Sathan. Puritie be in this House. My duty Madame.—Falles and Tyres? I begin to suspect what Falles and Tyres you meane. My Lady and Sebastian the Fall and the Tyre, and I the Shadow. I perceiue the puritie of my conuersation is vs'd but for a propertie to co uer the vncleanenesse of their purposes. The very contempla tion o' the thing, makes the spirit of the flesh begin to wriggle in my bloud. And heere my desire has met with an obiect al readie. In the way of holinesse; Mistresse Cataplasma. Your hand Gentlewomen.— The flesh is humble till the Spirit moue it; But when t'is rais'd it will command aboue it. Murder, murder, murder. Murder, murder, murder. Here you shali finde the murder'd body. No my good Lord. Wee come to apprehend the murderer. The Ghoast (great Pluto ) was a foole; vnfit to be imployed in any serious businesse for the state of hell. Why? could not he ha' suffer'd me to raise the mountaine o' my sinnes with one as damnable as all the rest; and then ha' tumbled me to ruine? But apprehend me e'en betweene the purpose and the act? before it was committed? No verily. This is my Lord D'amville. And his di straction (I thinke) growes out of his griefe for the losse of a faithfull seruant. For surely I take him to be Borachio that is slaine. Yes in sincerity my Lord. A Ghoast? where my Lord?—I smell a Foxe. Tush, tush; their walking spirits are meere imagina rie fables. There's no such thing in rerum natura. Heere is a man slaine. And with the Spirit of confideration, I rather think him to be the murderer got into that disguise, then any such phantastique toy.

Plot summary

Plot — The Atheist's Tragedy, or The Honest Man's Revenge
Montferrers has forbidden his son Charlemont from going to war and refused him the money necessary to equip himself. Charlemont gets a subvention from his uncle, D’Amville, and this persuades Montferrers to change his mind. But D’Amville is an atheist who believes that he must make his happiness in this world rather than wait for reward or punishment in the next, and he has an ulterior motive for wanting his nephew out of the way. Castabella loves Charlemont, and has privately agreed to marry him when he returns. They entrust their secret only to the Puritan Languebeau Snuffe. D’Amville bribes Snuffe to betray them and persuade Belleforest, her father, to make her marry Rousard, his son. Castabella is pressured into the match, despite the protestations of Sebastian, Rousard’s younger brother. As soon as he is married, Rousard falls ill with symptoms which include impotence. On the wedding day, D’Amville’s accomplice Borachio brings false news that Charlemont has been killed in battle. Montferrers makes a new will in D’Amville’s favour. D’Amville gets Montferrers’s torchbearers drunk at the wedding celebrations, and tricks them into quarrelling with one another when lighting their master home; the torches go out in the fight, allowing Borachio to murder Montferrers under cover of darkness. Montferrers’s ghost appears to Charlemont, tells him of the murder, and enjoins him not to take revenge. Charlemont returns to France to find himself presumed dead and his fiancée unhappily married. He confronts D’Amville and has an altercation with Sebastian, but another appearance by the ghost prevents him from getting carried away. D’Amville fabricates an unpaid debt in order to have Charlemont imprisoned, but Sebastian, who has been slightly injured, asks for money to pay a surgeon and uses it to pay the supposed debt and get Charlemont released. D’Amville takes steps to secure the future of his estate. Reasoning that Rousard will beget no son and that Sebastian, a libertine, will never marry, he decides that he will have to produce a bastard to be his heir, and selects Castabella as the mother. He commissions Borachio to murder Charlemont in the churchyard, but Charlemont kills Borachio instead. Snuffe uses the same churchyard for an assignation, intending to scare off any unwanted attention by disguising himself as the ghost of Montferrers, but he is himself frightened by Charlemont and runs away leaving the disguise behind.
