Full speech

I thanke you sir, Indeed I thanke you sir, and ere I stir It shall bee knowne, how euer you do deeme, I am of gentle bloud and gentle seeme. Sir my friend, Although, as Writers say, all things haue end, And that we call a pudding, hath his two O let it not seeme strange I pray to you, If in this bloudy simile, I put My loue, more endlesse, then fraile things or gut. I take your gentle offer and withall Yeeld loue againe for loue reciprocall. Faire Mistresse Luce, how do you, are you well? Giue me your hand and then I pray you tell, How doth your little sister, and your brother? And whether you loue me or any other. So they are. Where women are not cruel: but how farre Is it now distant from this place we are in, Vnto that blessed place your fathers warren. Euen that face For stealing Rabbets whilome in that place, God Cupid, or the Keeper, I know not whether Vnto my cost and charges brought you thither, And there began. Let no game, Or any thing that tendeth to the same. Bee euermore remembred, thou faire killer For whom I sate me downe and brake my Tiller. So would I. Vnlesse you had more maw to do me good. O no, my valiant loue will batter downe Millions of Constables, and put to flight, Euen that great watch of Mid-summer day at night. Yeeld then, I am full Of pitty, though I say it, and can pull Out of my pocket, thus, a paire of gloues, Looke Lucy, looke, the dogs tooth, nor the Doues Are not so white as these; and sweete they bee, And whipt about with silke, as you may see. If you desire the price, sute from your eie, A beame to this place, and you shall espie F. S. which is to say, my sweetest hony, They cost me three and two pence, or no mony. Nor so, nor so, for Lady I must tell, Before we part, for what we met together, God grant me time, and patience, and faire weather. I shall, then first and formost for reliefe I call to you, I if that you can affoord it, I care not at what price, for on my word, it Shall be repaid againe, although it cost me More then I'le speake of now, for loue hath tost me, In furious blanket like a Tennis ball, And now I rise aloft, and now I fall. I thanke you hartely, and as I say, Thus do I still continue without rest, I'th' morning like a man, at night a beast, Roaring and bellowing myne owne disquiet, That much I feare, forsaking of my diet, Will bring me presently to that quandary, I shall bid all adeiw: The Worshipfull your sire will not deny me. For I haue askt him, and he hath repli'd, Sweete Maister Humfrey, Luce shall be thy Bride. And so am I intruth. Stay Nimph, staie, I haue a double Gelding culored bay, Sprung by his father from Barbarian kind, Another for my selfe, though somewhat blind, Yet true as trusty tree. Though I die, I am resolu'd to venter life and lim, For one so yong, so faire, so kind, so trim. Father, it's true, in armes I nere shall claspe her, For shee is stolne away by your man Iasper.

Plot summary

Plot — The Knight of the Burning Pestle
At the theatre, a citizen and his wife object to the satirical subject matter of the play, The London Merchant: they insist on a more romantic offering starring their apprentice, Rafe, as a heroic grocer. The boy actors do their best to provide this within the play they have rehearsed. In the play, Luce prefers her father Venturewell’s apprentice Jasper to his chosen son-in-law, Humphrey; so Venturewell discharges Jasper, and he goes home to a family impoverished by the irresponsible merriment of his father, Old Merrythought. His mother, who 1607 favours his younger brother Michael, insists that he be paid his inheritance and sent out into the world. Several of the characters go to Waltham Forest. Luce elopes with Humphrey, intending to meet Jasper there. Rafe the grocer, a voluminous reader of chivalric romance, becomes the Knight of the Burning Pestle and leaves in search of heroic deeds. He scares Mistress Merrythought, who leaves behind her jewels. These are found and taken away by Jasper, who then meets the eloping couple, beats Humphrey, and takes Luce. In the audience, the citizen and his wife take Jasper to be the villain and Humphrey the hero; they demand that Rafe return to fight Jasper, but Jasper beats him. Notified by Humphrey, Venturewell confronts Jasper’s father, but is offended by his refusal to take the matter seriously. Jasper tests Luce’s love by threatening her with violence. She accepts her fate, but Humphrey and her father seize her before he can admit he was only pretending. Rafe stays at the Bell inn, but cannot understand that he must pay for its hospitality. (The citizen settles the bill for him.) The host sends him to fight the giant Barbaroso, better known as Nick the barber, who has imprisoned many knights and ladies — actually clients and venereal disease patients. Rafe defeats the ‘giant’ and releases the ‘knights’. The citizens then have a scene inserted in which he meets a foreign king’s daughter, but refuses her offer oflove. Later interpolated scenes show Rafe as the city’s May Lord, and mustering troops at Mile End. Venturewell incarcerates Luce in her room, but Jasper gains access by pretending to be dead: Venturewell accedes to a request that his body should be brought to Luce, and then has the coffin sent on to Old Merrythought, unaware that Luce has taken Jasper’s place inside.
