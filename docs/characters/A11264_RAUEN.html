Full speech

Troth Ile take part with thee then, Sariant, not for the sake of the mony so much, as for the hate I beare to a Schol ler: why Seriant tis Naturall in vs you know to hate Scholers: naturall 〈◊〉 , they will publish our imperfections, Knaueryes, and Conuay 〈◊〉 vpon Scaffolds and Stages. I and so close without yeelding; oh their parlous fel lows, they will search more with their wits then a Cunstable with all his officers. The best is Sariant if he be a true Scholler he weares no weapon I thinke. Masse, I am right glad of that: 'tas put me in better heart; nay if I clutch him once, let me alone to dra e him if he be stiff-necked; I haue beene one of the sixe my selfe, that has dragd as tall men of their hands, when their weapons haue bin gone as euer bastinadoed a Sariant—I haue done I can tel you. I and caught many a foole Seriant. A rablement, nay you shall haue a bloudy rablement; vpon you I warrant you. Troth prisoner, an exceeding fine house. Masse it seemes the Gentleman makes great account of him. I warrant thee that stands for the Counter, for you know theres a company of bare fellowes there. I marry sir, Ide buye one then my selfe. But this talke is by the way, where shall's sup to night, Fiue pound receiu'd, 〈◊〉 talke of that, I haue a trick worth all, you two shall beare him to 'th Tauerne, whilst I goe close with his Hostisse, and worke out of her, I know shee would bee glad of the summe to finger money; be cause shee knowes tis but a desperat debt, and full of hazard, what will you say if I bring it to passe that the Hostisse shall bee contented with one halfe for all; and wee to share tother; fift-shillings bullies.

Lady Plus has just been widowed, but her son Edmond. does not grieve appropriately at the funeral. She mourns a good, liberal husband, who strove to make his family rich by fair means and foul; she swears not to remarry, and her elder daughter Frances renounces marriage too. Not so her younger daughter, Moll, who entertains a suitor. The other two Plus women are also being pestered by unwanted suitors, one of whom has bribed Lady Plus’s brother-in-law, Sir Godfrey, to recommend him. Captain Idle is taken to prison for highway robbery; there he is visited by his puritan kinsman Nicholas, one of. the Plus household’s servants, and also by Skirmish and Pieboard, two tricksters who plan to get him out. To that end, they persuade Nicholas to steal Sir Godfrey’s chain. Pieboard visits Lady Plus posing as a fortune teller, and tells her that her husband is in purgatory, from which he can be released only if both she and her daughters reverse their marital intentions (which he has overheard): she and Frances must marry, and Moll must not. As a puritan, Lady Plus doubts the theology of this, so he establishes his authenticity by predicting that, before the day is out, there will be bloodshed outside her door and Sir Godfrey will suffer a loss, with worse to follow for the women of the household. Pieboard gives Oath a medicine which is actually a sleeping potion, so that he appears to have been killed. Nicholas tells Sir Godfrey that Idle is a conjurer who can get his chain back for him; so Sir Godfrey arranges for Idle to be released and the charges dropped.
