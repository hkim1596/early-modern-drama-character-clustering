In a fine Iland, say you? I con … e you. I would haue had your Ile brought … oting in, now In a braue broth, and of a sprightly greene, Iust to the colour of the Sea; and then, Some twentie Syrens, singing in the kettel, With an Arion, mounted on the backe Of a growne Conger, but in such a posture, As, all the world should take him for a Dolphin: O, 'twould ha' made such musick! Ha' you nothing, But a bare Island? Your prime Masquers? But where's your Antimasque now, all this while? I hearken after them. None? O, you are all the heauen awrie! Sir. For blood of Poetry, running in your veines, Make not your selfe so ignorantly simple. Bycause Sir, you shall see I am a Poet, No lesse then Cooke, and that I find you want A speciall service here, an Antimasque, Ile fit you with a dish out of the Kitchin, Such, as I thinke, will take the present palates, A metaphoricall dish! And, do but mark, How a good wit may iump with you. Are you rea dy, Child? (Had there bin Maske, or no Maske, I had made it.) Child of the boyling house. Bring forth the pot. Plot — Neptune's Triumph for the Return of Albion
A masque is to be performed in the Banqueting House. The poetis interrupted by the master-cook, who claims te be in charge of the venue, and, after a discussion of their jobs, the coek claims professional brotherhood of the poet. At his request, the poet describes the argument of the masque. Having sent his son Albion with Hippius and Proteus out into foreign countries, Neptune subsequently dispatched a floating island to bring them home. Monsters attempted to detain the travellers, but they eventually returned, to general public rejoicing. Now Neptune himself is to welcome their return — somewhat belatedly, in order to avoid commparisan with the people’s vulgar celebrations. ‘The cook is perturbed to learn that there is to be no antimasque, and provides one from his stew-pot. The main masque follows: the masquers arrive on the floating island. Dancing ensues, after which the cook provides a second antimasque of sailors.
