Hey, now the Fayre's a filling! O, for a Tune to startle The Birds o'the Booths here billing ▪ Yeerely with old Saint Barthle! The Drunkards they are wading, The Punques, and Chapmen trading; Who'ld see the Fayre without his lading? Buy any ballads; new ballads? How now Vrsla ? in a heate, in a heat? Alas, good Vr's ; was Zekiel heere this morning? Zekiel Edgeworth, the ciuill cut-purse, you know him well enough; hee that talkes bawdy to you still: I call him my Se cretary. When he comes, bid him stay: I'll be backe againe pre sently. Nay, Vrsla, thou'lt gall betweene the tongue and the teeth, with fretting, now. Ballads, Ballads! fine new ballads: Heare for your loue, and buy for your money. A delicate ballad o' the Ferret and the Coney. A preseruatiue again' the Punques euill. Another of Goose-greene-starch, and the Deuill. A dozen of diuine points, and the Godly garters. The Fairing of good councell, of an ell and three quarters. What is't you buy? The Wind-mill blowne downe by the witches fart! Or Saint George, that O! did breake the Dragons heart! O my Secretary! what sayes my Secretarie? We shall haue a quarrel presently. I faith, when the panne came, they had made you runne else. (this had beene a fine time for purchase, if you had ven tur'd.) My masters and friends, and good people, draw neere, &c. Sir, this is a spell against 'hem, spicke and span new; and 'tis made as 'twere in mine owne person, and I sing it in mine owne defence. But 'twill cost a penny alone, if you buy it. It was intended Sir, as if a purse should chance to be cut in my presence, now, I may be blamelesse, though: as by the se quell, will more plainely appeare. To the tune of Paggingtons Pound, Sir. It is a gentle admonition, you must know, Sir, both to the purse-cutter, and the purse-bearer. My masters and friends, and good people draw neere, And looke to your purses, for that I doe say; And though little money, in them you doe beare. It cost more to get, then to lose in a day. It hath bin vpbrayded to men of my trade, That ofte ̄ times we are the cause of this crime. At Worc'ter 'tis knowne well, and euen i'the Iayle, A Knight of good worship did there shew his face, Against the foule sinners, in zeale for to rayle, And lost (ipso facto) his purse in the place. At Playes and at Sermons, and at the Sessions, 'Tis daily their practice such booty to make: Yea, vnder the Gallowes, at Executions, They sticke not the Stare-abouts purses to take. Nay one without-grace, at a better place, But O, you vile nation of cutpurses all, Relent and repent, and amend and be sound, Edgworth gets vp to him, and tickles him in the care with a straw twice to draw his hand out of his pocket. And know that you ought not, by honest mens fall, Aduance your owne fortunes, to die aboue ground, And though you goe gay, In silkes as you may, It is not the high way to heauen, (as they say) Repent then, repent you, for better, for worse: And kisse not the Gallowes for cutting a purse. Youth, youth, thou hadst better bin steru'd by thy Nurse, Then liue to be hanged for cutting a purse. I hope you suspect not me, Sir. A mad-man that haunts the Fayre, doe you not know him? it's maruell hee has not more followers, after his ragged heeles. Yes, and agreed for his basket of peares; hee is at the corner here, ready. And your Prise, he comes downe, sailing, that way, all alone; without his Protector: hee is rid of him, it seemes. Wh, wh, wh, wh, &c. A new tune, I am practising, Sir. Cokes falls a scrambling whilest they runne away with his things. Let me hold your sword, Sir, it troubles you. Would he had another purse to cut, Zekiel. Plot summary

Plot — Bartholomew Fair
Bartholomew Cokes has two objectives in life: to visit the fair which shares his name, and to marry Grace Wellborn, who, as a ward, faces a financial penalty should she refuse. He sends his servant Wasp to Littlewit the proctor to obtain a marriage licence, but then, impatiently, comes himself. He brings Grace with him, and she attracts the interest of Winwife, present with his friend Quarlous as a suitor to Littlewit’s mother-in-law, Dame Purecraft. Littlewit is more interested in Cokes’s talk of the fair, having secretly written one of the puppet plays to be performed there. The only snag is that the puritanical Dame Purecraft will disapprove of their visit, thinking fairs profane. Busy, an elder of Purecraft’s church, advises the family that fairs may lawfully be visited for good reason, and comes along with them to partake of the pork. After some unfortunate experiences involving mistaken identity, Justice Overdo no longer trusts informers and has decided to do his spying personally. He visits the fair in disguise to seek out ‘enormities’ perpetrated by the fairground folk. He is impressed by one young man, Edgeworth, and decides to rescue him from the clutches of the cheaters; but he does not know that Edgeworth is a cutpurse. While Overdo is busy telling him about the wickedness of tobacco, Edgeworth steals Cokes’s purse; Overdo gets the blame. When it happens a second time, Overdo is arrested and put in the stocks. There he encounters the madman Trouble-All, who persistently demands Overdo’s warrant for any action; it transpires that he was a court official driven out of his mind by Overdo’s own actions. The watchmen, concerned to get better authority, release Overdo from the stocks for the time being. Meanwhile, the Littlewits arrive at the fair, but Busy becomes over-excited in his condemnation, attacks the fairground booths, and is arrested; Dame Purecraft goes after him, leaving Littlewit and Win free to enjoy themselves. Edgeworth tricks Wasp into making affidavit so that, at Quarlous’s instigation, he can steal Cokes’s marriage licence. Wasp too is arrested and put in the stocks, but escapes by a ruse when Overdo and Busy are put in beside him. The women visitors to the fair are separated from the men. Littlewit leaves Win in the care of two fairground bawds while he goes off to check the arrangements for his puppet show.
