Either I am John a Noakes, or I am not John a Noakes. Must this dayes pride so blow you up, that a Country-mans tale may not be heard? Or what ist make you Gamboll so? Thou lyest, and I am none of thy Country-man, I was borne out of the sound of your Pancake bell, I cannot abide to see a proud fellow: and it were not for us in the Country, you would have but a leane City, wee maintaine your Charter, and your Chamber too, you would ha but ill markets, and we should forsweare to furnish e'm, where were your hides, hornes and plenty of other provision? your, wives could not doe as they doe, with your short yard and your false light, and the Country should not come in upon them. Come, you can not live without us, you may be cald a body Po liticke, but the Country is the soule, and therefore subscribe and give way to me. Ile have as good Law for my money, as the best on you, I know what belongs to't, I have al most broke the Parson of the Parish already, about his Tithe-egges. Sirra Cit, I doe challenge thee. The next 〈◊〉 shall furnish us both if thou hast any morall, let us try before we part who is the better man. I will flash hy skin like a Summer dou blet, come thy wayes. A Pitch-forke? Thou shalt know thy destiny by this, though it have but one point I know where thy heart lies, I desire no more, and lesse would sa tisfie me, unlesse thou wilt eate thy words, and con fesse thou hast wrong'd me, out it shall, I have a sto macke to cut thee up, and my sword has a pretty edge of it selfe, and my greatest griefe is, that I owe thee nothing, to discharge all together, but tis no matter, I can but kill thee. But here's water Dost not see? I shall be un done. Why, tis Foule-Weather-in-Harvest, all spoil'd, I wo'not have thy heart now, and thou wouldst gee'tme. Kill me: Ile forgive thee, I shall have no Har vest to yeere. Long-Vacation? And I but little, and this weather hold. Enter Riches. Here she comes. By my Cart, and by my Plough, My dun Mare, and best red Cow, By my Barne, and fattest Weather, My grounds, and all my state together, In thy love I over-take thee, Else my whistling quite forsake me, And let me ever lye, which worse is, At racke and manger with the horses. Ha, ha, with all my heart, am I the man? There's no Lady That shal out-shine my Darling: tis no matter, though I be in Russet all the weeke, Riches shal live like a La dy, have perfum'd linnen, costly Gownes, and Pe ticoats worth taking up, and as the fashion is; I will put thee into a bagge. Hang Country Cuts! Doe but marry me.— Why, is not your name Riches? If you were borne in America, wee are but kindred a farre off. I would be loth to marry an Infidell borne, and yet I like your complexion so well, that— After this rate you are my wife in Law. Well: give you joy. I know not Honor if I see her, I have heard of such a Lady: ten to one, but Riches can direct you to her.

Plot — The Contention for Honor and Riches
Honour wants to see Riches, but Riches.is too proud to visit her: even though Honour has injured her foot and has limited mobility, Riches insists that she must be the one who travels. Riches is further annoyed by Honour’s choice of messenger, Ingenuity, simply because she hates scholars. She prefers her rival suitors, the countryman Clod and the citizen Gettings, who are both anti-intellectual. They quarrel over Riches, and the oneupmanship culminates with Clod challenging Gettings toa duel. Honour has two suitors of her own, a courtier and a soldier, who both urge their merits and mock each other’s deficiencies. Honour proves hard to get for both of them, and the argument ends when each is scared by an-apparition: Honesty terrifies. the courtier and No-Pay perturbs the soldier.
