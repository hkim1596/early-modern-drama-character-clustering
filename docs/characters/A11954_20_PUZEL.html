Reignier, is't thou that thinkeſt to beguile me? Where is the Dolphin? Come, come from behinde, I know thee well, though neuer ſeene before. Be not amaz'd, there's nothing hid from me; In priuate will I talke with thee apart: Stand back you Lords, and giue vs leaue a while. Dolphin, I am by birth a Shepheards Daughter, My wit vntrayn'd in any kind of Art: Heauen and our Lady gracious hath it pleas'd To ſhine on my contemptible eſtate. Loe, whileſt I wayted on my tender Lambes, And to Sunnes parching heat diſplay'd my cheekes, Gods Mother deigned to appeare to me, And in a Viſion full of Maieſtie, Will'd me to leaue my baſe Vocation, And free my Countrey from Calamitie: Her ayde ſhe promis'd, and aſſur'd ſucceſſe. In compleat Glory ſhee reueal'd her ſelfe: And whereas I was black and ſwart before, With thoſe cleare Rayes, which ſhee infus'd on me, That beautie am I bleſt with, which you may ſee. Aske me what queſtion thou canſt poſſible, And I will anſwer vnpremeditated: My Courage trie by Combat, if thou dar'ſt, And thou ſhalt finde that I exceed my Sex. Reſolue on this, thou ſhalt be fortunate, If thou receiue me for thy Warlike Mate. I am prepar'd: here is my keene-edg'd Sword, Deckt with fine Flower-de-Luces on each ſide, The which at Touraine, in S. Katherines Church-yard, Out of a great deale of old Iron, I choſe forth. And while I liue, Ile ne're flye from a man. Chriſts Mother helpes me, elſe I were too weake. I muſt not yeeld to any rights of Loue, For my Profeſſion's ſacred from aboue: When I haue chaſed all thy Foes from hence, Then will I thinke vpon a recompence. Why no, I ſay: diſtruſtfull Recreants, Fight till the laſt gaſpe: Ile be your guard. Aſſign'd am I to be the Engliſh Scourge. This night the Siege aſſuredly Ile rayſe: Expect Saint Martins Summer, Halcyons dayes, Since I haue entred into theſe Warres. Glory is like a Circle in the Water, Which neuer ceaſeth to enlarge it ſelfe, Till by broad ſpreading, it diſperſe to naught. With Henries death, the Engliſh Circle ends, Diſperſed are the glories it included: Now am I like that prowd inſulting Ship, Which Caeſar and his fortune bare at once. Come, come, 'tis onely I that muſt diſgrace thee. Talbot farwell, thy houre is not yet come, I muſt goe Victuall Orleance forthwith: O're-take me if thou canſt, I ſcorne thy ſtrength. Goe, goe, cheare vp thy hungry-ſtarued men, Helpe Salisbury to make his Teſtament, This Day is ours, as many more ſhall be. Aduance our wauing Colours on the Walls, Reſcu'd is Orleance from the Engliſh. Thus Ioane de Puzel hath perform'd her word.

Plot summary

Plot — 1 Henry the Sixth
King Henry Vis dead, and England's French lands are lost. After a military setback, the French accept the offered aid of Joan Pucelle , who claims supernatural powers from the Virgin Mary; but they also release Talbot in an exchange of prisoners. With Joan 's help the French recapture the occupied town of Orleans, but the English take it back in a night attack led by Talbot. The Countess of Auvergne invites Talbot to visit her. intending treachery, but he second-guesses her and brings soldiers with him. In England, feuds brew between Gloucester and Winchester, and between Richard Plantagenet and the Duke of Somerset, whose supporters show their allegiance by picking different coloured roses. Richard , who has been deprived of his titles through the attainder of his father, learns from the imprisoned and dying Mortimer that he has a claim to the throne . King Henry restores him to his inheritance, creating him Duke of York. Joan takes Rouen by a ruse , but Talbot recaptures it the same day; she then persuades England's ally Burgundy to join the French. After King Henry's coronation in Paris, York is left in command of Prance, but personal tension between him and Somerset means that troop reinforcements arrive late; in consequence, Talbot is defeated, surviving only long enough to learn that his son has perished in battle. Joan conjures devils, but they will not help her: she is captured by York and burned at the stake. The French and English agree a peace which will entail King Henry making a diplomatic marriage, but this is pre-empted when Suffolk captures Margaret and (having fallen in love with her but being alr eady married) plans to install her as Queen of England . Henry agrees to marry her, and Suffolk goes to France to mili the arrangements.
