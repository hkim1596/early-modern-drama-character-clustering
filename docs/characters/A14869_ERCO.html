Mistake not excellent Mistres, these commends Expresse, his Maiestie of Spaine has giuen me Both addition of honour, as you may perceiue By my habit, and a place heere to command Ore thirtie Gallies; this your brother shewes, As wishing that you would be partner In my good Fortune. Sir, you haue done me The maynest wrong that ere was offred To a Gentleman of my breeding. You haue led me With a vaine confidence, that I should marry Your sister, haue proclaim'd it to my friends, Employd the greatest Lawyers of our State To settle her a ioynture, and the issue Is, that I must become ridiculous Both to my friends and enemies: I will leaue you, Till I call to you for a strict account Of your vnmanly dealing. Lady, I will doe a manly Office for you, I will leaue you, to the freedome of your owne soule, May it moue whither heauen and you please. Alas, shee mistakes, Tis Contarino she wishes liuing; but I must fasten On her last words, for my owne safetie. Here in the vowed comfort of your daughter. Collect your selfe, good Lady, Would you behold braue Contarino liuing? There cannot be a nobler Chronicle Of his good then my selfe: if you would view him dead, I will present him to you bleeding fresh, In my penitency. Here begin a I my compassion: oh poore soule! Shee is with child by Contarino, and he dead, By whom should she preerue her fame to'th world, But by my selfe that loued her boue the world? There neuer was a way more honourable, To exercise my vertue, then to father it, And preserue her credit, and to marry her. Ile suppose her Contarino's widdow, bequeath'd to me Vpon his Death: for sure shee was his wife, But that the Ceremony a'th Church was wanting. Report this to her, Madam, and withall, That neuer father did conceaue more ioy For the birth of an heyre, then I to vnderstand, Shee had such confidence in me. I will not now Presse a Visit vpon her, till you haue prepar'd her: For I doe reade in your distraction, Should I be brought a'th sudden to her presence, Either the hastie fright, or else the shame May blast the fruit within her. I will leaue you, To commend as loyall faith and seruice to her, As ere heart harbour'd; by my hope of blisse, I neuer liu'd to doe good act but this. Stay my Lord here's a window To let in more light to the Court. I am Ercole. I obey the arrest o'th Court. Away, thou art the Traytor: I onely liue to challenge this former suite, Toucht but thy fame, this accusation Reaches to thy fame and life: the braue Contarino Is generally supposed slaine by this hand. The report of his mother deliuered to me, In distraction for Contarino's death. My loue to Contarino. Tis true: but I begun to loue him, When I had most cause to hate him, when our bloods Embrac'd each other, then I pitied, That so much valour should be hazarded On the fortune of a single Rapier, And not spent against the Turke. Sir, I doe not know you. Cry you me , I haue knowne of your Nation Braue Souldiers. You haue iudged to day A most confused practise, that takes end In as bloody a tryall, and we may obserue By these great persons, and their indirect Proceedings, shaddowed in a vaile of State. Mountaines are deformed heaps, sweld vp alofts Vales wholsomer, though lower, and trod on oft. Leaue the Roome, Gentlemen. Sir, Ile tell you, My loue, as I haue formerly protested To Contarino, whose vnfortunate end, The traytor wrought: and here is one thing more, Deads all good thoughts of him, which I now receiu'd From Iolenta. Yes, in this Letter: For hauing sent to her to be resolued Most truely, who was father of the child, Shee writes backe, that the shame she goes withall, Was begot by her brother. Yes sir. Of whom? Liuing! You were but now my second, now I make you My selfe for euer. Oh tis the faire Iolenta; to what purpose Are you thus ecclipst?

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
