O false ambitious pride in yong and old: Harke how the heauens our follies hath contrould. If not religious feare, what may represse These wicked passions, wretched Citizens. O Rome, poore Rome, vnmeet for these misdeedes, I see contempt of heauens will breed a crosse: Sweete Cynna gouerne rage with reuerence. O fellow Citizens, be more aduisde. Octauius these are scourges for our sinnes, These are but ministers to heape our plagues: These mutinies are gentle meanes and waies, VVhereby the heavns our heauie errors charmes, Then with content and humbled eyes, behold The christall shining globe of glorious Ioue: And since we perish through our owne misdeedes, Go let vs flourish in our frutefull praiers. My Lord, may courage wait on conquered men? Then to the downfall of my happines, Then to the ruine of this Citie Rome. But if mine inward ruth were laid in sight, My streames of teares should drowne my foes despight. VVhere state's opprest by cruell tyrants bee, Old Anthony, there is no place for thee. Harke, by this thundring noyse of threatning drums, Marius with all his faction hether comes. I wonder why my peasant staies so long, And with my wonder hasteth on my woe, And with my woe I am assaild with feare, And by my feare await with faintful breath The final period of my paines by death. Betraid at last by witles ouersight, Now Anthony, prepare thy selfe to die: Loe where the monstrous ministers of wrath Menace thy murther with their naked swords. Immortall powers that know the painefull cares, That waight vpon my poore distressed hart, O bend your browes and leuill all your lookes Of dreadfull awe vpon these daring men. And thou sweet neece of Atlas on whose lips And tender tongue, the pliant Muses sit, Let gentle course of sweet aspiring speech, Let honnie flowing tearmes of wearie woe, Let frutefull figures and delightfull lines Enforce a spring of pitie from their eyes, Amase the murthrous passions of their mindes, That they may fauour wofull Anthonie. Oh countrimen what shal become of Rome, VVhen reuerend dutie droopeth through disgrace? Oh Countrimen, what shal become of Rome, VVhen woful nature widdow of her ioyes, VVeepes on our wals to see her lawes deprest? Oh Romaines hath not Anthonies discourse, Seald vp the Mouthes of false seditious men, Assoild the doubts and queint controlls of powre; Releeud the mournfull matrone with his pleas? And will you seeke to murder Anthonie? The Lions brooke with kindnes their releefe, The sheep reward the shepheard with their fleece: Yet Romanes seeke to murder Anthony. VVhat enuious eies reflecting nought but rage, VVhat barbarous hart refresht with nought but blood, That rents not to behold the sensles trees In doaly season drooping without leaues? The shepheard sighs vpon the barrain hills To see his bleating lambs with faintfull lookes. Behold the vallies robd of springing flowres, That whilom wont to yeeld them yerely food. Euen meanest things exchangd from former state, The vertuous minde with some remorse doth mate. Can then your eyes with thundering threats of rage, Cast furious gleames of anger vpon age? Can then your harts with furies mount so hie, As they should harme the Romane Anthonie? Plot summary

Plot — The Wounds of Civil War, or Marius and Scilla
King Mithridates has defied Roman power in Asia Minor, and the consul Sulla has been selected by lot to take reprisals. But Sulla lingers with the army in Capua, and the Senate votes to transfer the command to Marius, a more experienced soldier. Sulla invades the Senate with his troops and threatens to uphold his rights with violence. The senators take sides, and a civil war follows. Sulla is victorious; Marius takes refuge at Minturnum. Sulla proscribes Marius and his allies as traitors, and leads the army to Asia Minor. Marius is warned that his hosts plot his murder, but accepts his fate and goes to sleep. The assassin, Pedro, is unable to go through with the killing, so the magistrates simply ask Marius to leave town. He flees to Africa, and lives in the mountains on a diet of roots. Marius’ son comes to recall him to Rome: the new consul, Cinna, is sympathetic to him. But Sulla returns victorious from the east, and attempts to prevent his rival from entering Italy. Nevertheless Marius reaches Rome, and begins to settle old scores with violence. Cinna deposes and murders his fellow consul Octavius, Marius’ enemy, and Marius is appointed to fill the vacancy. Sulla is proscribed and his property confiscated, and Marius sends his son to engage Sulla’s army in battle. Sulla’s wife and daughter are arrested as they attempt to leave Rome; Marius threatens them with death, but then sends them honourably back to Sulla. Marius dies in office. After a bloody battle, Sulla enters Rome victorious and compiles an extensive death-list of his enemies. Young Marius holes up in Praeneste, the last stronghold of resistance, but after a siege he concedes the inevitable and commits suicide; Sulla’s forces conquer the city and put the inhabitants to the sword. Sulla is proclaimed dictator, and insists that the appointment be made perpetual; but he soon finds that supreme power brings with it a troubled mind, and resigns the office. He plans to retire to the country, but a spirit appears with a prophecy of his death. He takes his leave of his family, and is buried with great pomp.
