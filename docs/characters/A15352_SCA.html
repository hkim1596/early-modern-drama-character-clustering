But change of women bawld Knaues, Sir Knight. Prepar'd to aske how you like that we haue done, your daughters made my wife, and I your sonne. Ha. Would thou wert not so neere me: yet farewell. Be not a bar betwixt vs, or by my sword Ile mete thy graue out. Thou lyest, I ha not a wife. None can be cald, True man and wife, but those whom heauen instald. Say, You are very welcome, peace: wele ha complement. Who are you Gentlewoman. Mine? Where, how begot: Proue me by certaine instance thats deuine, That I should call them lawfull, or the mine. No, tho we heard the words of Ceremonie, But had hands knit as fellons that weare fetters Forsty vpon them. For tell me woman, Did ere my Loue with sighs intreat thee mine, Did euer I in willing conference, Speake words, made halfe with teares that I did loue thee. Or was I euer But glad to see thee as al Louers are. No, no, thou knowst I was not. Thou lyest? stumpet thou lyest? Pretty, pretty Impudence infaith, Then haue subornd that slaue to lift his hands against me. Ile teach you tricks for this, ha you a companion. You are most kindly welcome, sooth ye are. And I haue leysure to attend your hearing. I know you did sir. Heere? Ile read a Letter to the Doct. too, hees a Deuine? I hees a Deuine. Ile be with you straight sir, Saluation to afflicted consciences, And not giue torment to contented minds, Who should be lamps to comfort out our way, And not like Firedrakes to lead men astray, I, Ile be with you straight sir. These for thy act should die, she for my Clare, Whose wounds stare thus vpon me for reuenge. These to be rid from misery, this from sinne, And thou thy selfe shalt haue a push amongst em, That made heauens word a pack-horse to thy tongue. Cotest scripture to make euils shine like good, And as I send you thus with wormes to dwell, Angels applaud it as a deed done well. Iniurious villen that preuentst me still. All this is heere, Is heauen so gracious to sinners then? Children and seruants pay their duty thus. And all are pleas'd.

Plot summary

Plot — The Miseries of Enforced Marriage
The ward William Scarborow betroths himself to Clare Harcop, but on his return to London he is forced to marry Katherine, the niece of his guardian, Lord Falconbridge. He is rewarded with control of his own’ and his younger brothers’ property. He writes to tell Clare the news of his unwelcome marriage; upon receiving the letter, she dies (possibly by suicide). ===== PAGE 337 ===== 1606 Scarborow takes her death badly: he blames his wife, and becomes prodigal. Ilford fakes his own arrest for debt in order to induce Scarborow to guarantee the supposed loan, thereby defrauding him of his money. This still leaves him with the patrimony of his younger brothers, Thomas and John; but he spends it, and drunkenly assaults his friends and kinsmen. The brothers and the honest family servant Butler are forced into theft to keep themselves from starvation. They steal £300 from Harcop, Clare’s father, which they use to make their sister appear to be a rich woman. Butler tricks Ilford into marrying her, but on learning that she is penniless her new husband throws her out; Butler offers to support her out of his meagre wages. Thomas confronts and fights with his elder brother, but saves his life when they are attacked by Ilford. Now penitent, Thomas declares his intention to confess to Harcop that he and John stole the money, even though it will mean hanging for them both. John falls out with him over this, but they are reconciled by Butler. Dr Baxter, who married Scarborow to Katherine, talks him into repentance. On his deathbed, Lord Falconbridge also repents and leaves the Scarborow brothers double their original wealth in restitution, along with a dowry for their sister.
