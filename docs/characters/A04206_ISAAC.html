WHere art thou my boy Mido, when I doe thée lacke? To myfe Rebecca. Sauing that what ſo euer God doth is all right, No ſmall griefe it were for a man to lacke his ſight. But what the Lord doth ſende or worke by his high will, All bodily puniſhement or infirmitie, With all mannes of nature, what euer they be, Yea and all other aff ictions temporall: As loſſe, perſecution, or troubles mortall, Are nothing but a triall or probation. And what is he that firmely truſteth in the Lorde, Or ſtedfaſtly beleueth his promiſe and worde, And knoweth him to be the God omnipotent, That féedeth and gouerneth all that he hath ſent: Protecting his faithfull in euery degrée, And them to relieue in all their neceſſitie? What creature (I ſay) that doth this vnderſtande, Will not take all thing in good parte at Gods hande? Shall we at Gods hand receyue proſperitie, And not be content likewiſe with aduerſitie? We ought to be thankefull what euer God doth ſende, And our ſelues wholy to his will to commende. Why then ſhould not I thanke the Lorde, if it pleaſe him That I ſhall nowe be blynde, and my ſight waxe all dim. For who ſo to olde age will here liue and endure, Muſt of force abide all ſuche defautes of nature. If the Lorde haue pointed thée ſuch olde dayes to ſée, He wil alſo prouide that ſhall be méete for thée. Nowe wife, touching the purpoſe that I ſought for you. Ye haue ofte in couerte wordes ben right earneſt To haue me graunt vnto you a boune and requeſt: But ye neuer tolde me yet plainly what it was, Therfore I haue euer yet lette the matter paſſe. And now of late by ofte being from me abſent, I haue halfe ſuſpected you to be ſcarce content. But wife Rebecca, I woulde not haue you to mourne, As though I did your honeſt petition wourne. For I neuer ment to denie in all my life Any lawfull or honeſt requeſt to my wyfe. But in caſe it be a thing vnreaſonable, Then muſt I néedes be to you vntractable. Now therfore ſay on, and tell me what is your caſe. To ſpeake do not refraine, And if it be reaſonable ye ſhall obtaine: Otherwiſe, ye muſt pardon me gentle ſwéete wife. A prerogatiue he hath in euery thing. Of all the goodes his porcion is greater. Emong his bretherne, he hath the preeminence. Ouer his bretherne he is ſoueraigne and lorde. Plot summary

Plot — Jacob and Esau
Esau is obsessed with hunting: Ragau is forced to get up early to go out with him, and in consequence 1553 doesn’t get enough sleep or food. The neighbours, too, are inconvenienced by noise during the night, and they grumble about disorderly youth. Esau is nevertheless Isaac’s favourite son, while Rebecca favours his brother Jacob. She remembers God’s prediction that her elder son shall be subject to her younger, but Isaac is steadfast in his belief in seniority. On returning from hunting, Esau and Ragau are extremely hungry, so Jacob sells his brother a dish of rice pottage, at the price of his birthright. Esau eats it all himself, leaving none for Ragau, and then tries to renege on the agreement. Isaac still favours him over Jacob, and sends him out to hunt for food. Rebecca sends Jacob out to kill a kid: if he serves a meal to Isaac first, his blind father will mistake him for Esau and bless him instead. The problem is that Esau is hairy and Jacob smooth: Isaac will be able to feel the difference. Rebecca cooks the kid and makes sleeves and a collar from its hairy skin. Isaac recognizes Jacob’s voice, but the artificial hair persuades him: he administers the blessing and grants Jacob his inheritance. Esau’s venison arrives too late. Esau plans to avenge himself by killing Jacob. trusting that God is not truly omniscient and that he will therefore get away with it. Rebecca and Isaac send Jacob away to safety with his uncle in Mesopotamia, but their admonition persuades Esau to give up his hate for Jacob.
