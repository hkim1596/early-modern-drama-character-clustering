Full speech

How she winds her selfe Into his Soule! This is some allay To his hot passion. Thou dost mistake; they durst not Vse the least word of scorne, although prouok'd, To any thing of mine. Goe, get you home, And 〈…〉 , seruants, friends, and flatterers, number How many discents you are noble; Look to your wiues too, The smooth-chin'd Courtiers are abroad. I can speake sometimes. If to vexe her, May be interpreted to doe her Honor, She shall haue many of 'em? Ile make vse Of my short Raigne: my Lord, now gouernes all: And she shall know, that her Idolater, My Brother, being not by, now to protect her, I am her equall. On her Birth-day, We were forc'd to be merrie: & now she's musty We must be sad, on paine of her displeasure; We will, we will. This is her priuate Chamber, Where like an Hypocrite, not a true Turtle, She seemes to mourne her absent Mate, her Seruants Attending her like Mutes: But Ile speake to her And in a high Key too, play any thing That's light and loud enough but to torment her, And we will haue rare sport. Song. Marcelia aboue in blacke. May it please your greatnesse, We heard that your late Physicke hath not work'd, And that breed, Melancholy, as your Doctor tells vs: To purge which, we that are born your Highnesse Vassals, And are to play the fooles to doe you seruice, Present you with a sit of mirth: what thinke you Of a new Anticke? Being inteaded for so sweet a creature, Were she but pleas'd to grace it. The Mistresse of all hearts; one smile I pray you On your poore seruants, or a Fidlers fee: Comming from those faire hands, though but a Ducat, We will inshrine it as a holy relique. Pen'd by her Chaplaine. Note her now. What, of me? Pine-tree. To you? I had rather be a Slaue vnto a Moore, Than know thee for my equall. Let her but remember The Issue in her Legge: And, howsoe're, She seemes, when she's made vp: As she's her selfe, She stinkes aboue ground. O that I could reach you, The little one you scorne so, with her nayles, Would teare your painted Face, & scratch those Eyes out. Doe but come downe. That it were lawfull To me te her with a Ponyard, and a Pistoll; But these weake hands shall shew my spleene Here, Giantesse, here. Let her come on. I am out of breath; But we shall meete, we shall. And doe you heare, Sir, Or right me on this Monster (she's three foote Too high for a Woman) or ne're looke to haue, A quiet houre with me. This is fine, yfaith. O base. Thou art a base Fellow, To take her part. I marrie was I, And will be one againe. I'le rather liue in any loathsome Dungeon, Than in a Paradice, at her intreatie: And, for you vpstart. No more, I marke it.

Plot — The Duke of Milan
Sforza, Duke of Milan, dotes on his haughty Duchess Marcelia, and fears the prospect of losing her. Her birthday banquet is interrupted by bad news: Sforza rebuffed Charles the Emperor’s overtures and chose to ally Milan with France instead; but now the Empire has routed France at the Battle of Pavia. Sforza expects imperial reprisals, and goes to submit himself to Charles. Before he leaves, he appoints Francisco his deputy, with instructions that, should he not return, Marcelia is to be murdered so that nobody else shall enjoy her. He submits himself to Charles, offers an explanation but no excuse for his supporting France, and expresses his willingness to pay with his life in order to prevent the imperial army from sacking Milan, thus sparing his subjects further bloodshed. His dignity impresses Charles, who confirms his ducal title. In Sforza’s absence, the tensions between Marcelia and his sister Mariana break out into open violence. Though Mariana is his own wife, Francisco takes Marcelia’s side in the quarrel and imprisons Mariana. Believing that Sforza will not return, he propositions Marcelia, shows her the warrant for her execution, and suggests that they should seize power together. She refuses him, so he explains the excessive love which led Sforza to order her death. This annoys her. When Sforza returns to Milan, she greets him coldly, which gives offence. Mariana learns of Francisco’s efforts to seduce Marcelia, and informs Sforza, who will not believe her. Francisco finds out her plans in advance and sets up the situation to favour his revenge: three years before, his sister Eugenia was seduced by Sforza with a promise of marriage on which he then reneged. First he presents himself to Marcelia as a potential victim of Sforza’s jealousy, inducing her to promise to protect him. Then he tells Sforza that Marcelia has tried to seduce him.
