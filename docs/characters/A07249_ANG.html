What way? the diuels way, the way of sinne, The way of hot damnation, way of lust: And you to, wash away the poore mans bread In bowles of drunkennesse. Your names euen brand you, you are Spurgius cald And like a Spunge you sucke vp liquorous wines Till your soule reeles to hell. For blood of grapes you sold the widowes food. And staruing them, tis murder, whats this but hell. Hircius your name, and Goatish is your nature: You snatch the meate out of the prisoners mouth, To fatten harlots, is not this hell to, No Angell, but the diuell waites on you. Play no more Villaines with so good a Lady, For if you doe — — —. Away, and once more mend. Heere most holy Mistresse. No my deare Lady, I could weary slarres, And force the wakefull Moone to lose her eyes By my late watching, but to waite on you, When at your prayers you kneele before the Altar, Me thinkes I'm singing with some Quire in Heauen, So blest I hold me in your company: Therefore my most-lou'd mistresse do not bid Your boy so seruiceable to get heuce, For then you breake his heart. Proud am I that my Ladies modest eye, So likes so poore a seruant. I am not, I did neuer Know who any mother was, but by yon Pallace Fill'd with bright heauenly Courtiers, I dare assure you, And pawne these eyes vpon it, and this hand, My father is in Heauen, and pretty Mistresse, If your illustrious houre Glasse spend his sand No worse then yet it does, vpon my life You and I both shall meete my father there, And he shall bid you welcome. Yes, my sweetest Mistresse. O my admired mistresse; quench not out The holy fires within you, though temptations Showre downe vpon you: claspe thine armour on, Fight well and thou shalt see, after these warres Thy head weare Sun beames, and thy feet touch starres. Like slaues you sold your soules for golden drosse, Bewitching her to death, who stept betweene You, and the gallowes. The misery of that mistresse is mine owne, She beggerd, I left wretched. Go, go, y'are beggers both, you are not worth That leather on your feete. I'am glad I tryde your loue, which see I want not, So long as this is full. No blowes, no bitter language, all your gold gone. To you: I had a mistresse late senthence by you Vpon a bloudy errand, you intreated That when she came into that blessed Garden Whither she knew she went, and where (now happy) Shee feedes vpon all ioy, she would send to you Some of that Garden fruit and flowers, which heere To haue her promise sau'd, are brought by me. Yes, if the Master Will giue you entrance. Angelo vanisheth. Plot summary

Plot — The Virgin Martyr
An imperial edict orders the extirpation of Christianity from the Roman Empire, and the re-establishment of the worship of the pagan gods. In Caesarea, Theophilus works to that end, abetted by Harpax; his many successes so far include the forced apostasy of his own daughters, once Christians but now votaresses of Jupiter. Diocletian gives his daughter Artemia the right to choose her own husband, and she selects the unwilling Antoninus, who loves the Christian Dorothea and asks for time to consider. Artemia remains in Caesarea as her father’s deputy. Harpax tells Theophilus that Antoninus is having an illicit relationship with Dorothea, and Theophilus suborns her servants Spungius and Hircius to smuggle him into her home so that he can witness a meeting between them. Dorothea and Antoninus are arrested. Theophilus sets his daughters, Dorothea’s childhood friends, to convert her to paganism; but instead Dorothea persuades them to revert to Christianity. When they demonstrate their new faith by spitting at an idol of Jupiter, their outraged father kills them. Antoninus is taken ill. His father brings Dorothea to his sick-bed in the hope that he will rape her and so be cured. He refuses, and Dorothea is taken for torture and execution. Destitute after losing the gold Theophilus gave them, Spungius and Hircius are engaged by Harpax to be the torturers. They flog her, but miraculously she feels nothing; they are wrongly suspected of botching the job out of regard for their former mistress, and hanged. After praying that Antoninus’ love for her be transformed into love for God, Dorothea is beheaded. Antoninus drops dead at the sight. Artemia gets her second choice of husband: the co-Emperor Maximinus. Dorothea’s faithful page Angelo, whom she took in as a beggar, turns out to be an angel, and brings Theophilus a gift from her: a basket of fruit and flowers to persuade him of the joys of the Christian heaven. Harpax comes in his true form, a devil, to claim Theophilus, but is frightened off by a floral cross in the basket. Theophilus is converted, orders the release of all Christian prisoners, and gives them ships in which to flee persecution. He is arrested by the authorities and asks to die on the worst of his extensive collection of torture instruments. He is racked but will not recant; Harpax is balked of his aim, and Dorothea and the other martyrs receive him into heaven. Diocletian is unmoved: the state will continue the persecution of Christians. =====…
