Why, what do you meane Sir? ha'you your reason? Are you not enough The talke, of feasts, and meetingy, but you'll still Make argument for fresh? Yes, I haue share in this The scorne will fall As bittterly on me, where both are laught at. I must obey. Why S r . and you might see't. What thought, they haue of you, may be soone collected By the young Genlemans speache. My cage, yo' were best To call it? Looke at the back-doore, One knocks, see who it is. I cannot get this venter of the cloake, Out of my fancie; nor the Gentlemans way, He tooke, which though 'twere strange, yet 'twas handsome, And had a grace withall, beyond the newnesse. Sure he will thinke mee that dull stupid cre ture, Hee said, and may conclude it; if I finde not Some thought to thanke th'attemp. He did presume, By all the carriage of it, on my braine, For answer; and will sweare 'tis very barren, Diuell re turnes. If it can yeeld him no returne Who is it? What's this? I pray you come to your selfe and thinke What your part is: to make an answer. Tell, Who is it at the doore? O! you'll anon proue his hyr'd man, I feare, What has he giu'n you, for this message? Sir, Bid him put off his hopes of straw, and leaue To spread his nets, in view, thus. Though they take Master Fitz-dottrel, I am no such foule, Nor faire one, tell him, will be had with stalking. And wish him to for-beare his acting to mee, At the Gentlemans chamber-window in Lincolnes-Inne there, That opens to my gallery: else, I sweare T'acquaint my husband with his folly, and leaue him To the iust rage of his offended iealousie. Or if your Masters sense be not so quicke To right mee, tell him, I shall finde a friend That will repaire mee. Say, I will be quiet. In mine owne house? Pray you, in those words giue it him. If he be the Master, Now, of that state and wit, which I allow him; Sure, hee will vnderstand mee: I durst not Be more direct. For this officious fellow, My husbands new groome, is a spie vpon me, I finde already. Yet, if he but tell him This in my words, hee cannot but conceiue Himselfe both apprehended, and requited. I would not haue him thinke hee met a statue : Or spoke to one, not there, though I were silent. How now? ha' you told him? And what saies he? This can be None but my husbands wit. How now! Good! You are a stranger to the plot! you set not Your saucy Diuell, here, to tempt your wife, With all the insolent vnciuill language, Or action, he could vent? You'll ha'too much, I feare, in these false spirits, You ha' strange phantasies! WHere are you, Sir? You make me paint, S r . Who is there with you, S r ? Who was it sung? Sir, if you iudge me by this simple action, And by the outward habite, and complexion Of easinesse, it hath, to your designe; You may with Iustice, say, I am a woman: And a strange woman But when you shall please, To bring but that concurrence of my fortune, To memory, which to day your selfe did vrge: It may beget some fauour like excuse, Though none like reason. What you please, Ladies. Plot summary

Plot — The Devil Is an Ass
. _ Pug the junior devil is keen to visit earth. Satan svarns him that his ideas of effective devilment are half a century out of date, but grants him leave to spend the day in London, made flesh and subject to all the frailties and limitations of the human body. A ready-made body is available, in the shape of a recently hanged cutpurse, but Pug will have to make his own arrangements for clothes. Another proviso is that he must serve the first man he meets. Unfortunately for Pug, he turns out to be a twit: Fabian Fitzdottrel. Fitzdottrel longs to see the devil, and agrees to accept Pug as an unpaid servant because he gives his mame as Devil. The same obsession makes Fitzdottrel eager to see a new play, The Devil is an Ass, but he needs some finery to show off as he sits on the stage. Wittipol is eager to sleep with Fitzdottrel’s wife, and offers him a suitable cloak in exchange for fifteen minutes’ uninterrupted conversation with the lady. He uses the time to proposition her, and engages her interest even though she has been forbidden to reply. Fitzdottrel is introduced to Merecraft, a purveyor of imaginative financial schemes, who offers to make him rich and a duke through land reclamation in the eastern fens. Fitzdottrel agrees to meet with him, leaving Pug to ensure that nobody attempts to visit his wife. Pug instead brings her a follow-up message from Wittipol, then makes advances to her on his own account, but is caught by Fitzdottrel and gets a beating. Wittipol’s friend Manly has a room which abuts Mistress Fitzdottrel’s gallery, which he uses to have a second meeting with her. This is much more intimate than the first, despite being conducted through the windows of adjacent buildings. Pug gets back into favour by tipping off Fitzdottrel, who puts a stop to the assignation and threatens to take up another woman to share his expectations and be his Duchess. Merecraft suggests that what Fitzdottrel’s wife needs is to be taught proper manners, and recommends a widow who knows the ways of the Spanish court, who might be persuaded to undertake the task if sent an expensive ring as a gift, and who is entirely fictitious. His accomplice Engine proposes hiring an actor to impersonate her, but Merecraft worries that his business methods might be leaked to a playwright and end up on stage; in the event, Engine finds a gentleman to take on the role instead, who happens to be Wittipol.
