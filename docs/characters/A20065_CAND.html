O Gentlemen, so late, y'are very welcome, pray sit downe. What's that you laugh at, Signiors? Indeed you are good obseruers, it shewes strange. But Gentlemen, I pray neither contemne, Nor yet deride a ciuill ornament; I could build so much in the round Caps praise, That loue this hye roofe, I this flat would raise. So all these guests will pardon me, Ile doo't. Thus then in the Caps honor, To euery Sex and state, both Nature, Time, The Countries lawes, yea and the very Clime Doe allot distinct habits, the spruce Courtier Iets vp and downe in silke: the Warrier Marches in buffe, the Clowne plods on in gray: But for these vpper garments thus I say, The Sea-man has his Cap, par'd without brim, The Gallants head is featherd, that fits him; The Soldier has his Murren, women ha Tires; Beasts haue their head-peeces, and men ha theirs. Each degree has his fashion, it's fit then, One should be laid by for the Citizen, And that's the Cap which you see swels not hye, For Caps are Emblems of humility; It is a Citizens badge, and first was worne By'th Romanes; for when any Bondmans turne Came to be made a Freeman: thus 'twas said, He to the Cap was call'd; that is, was made Of Rome a Freeman, but was first close shorne, And so a Citizens haire is still short worne. Of Geometricke figures the most rare, And perfect'st are the Circle and the square, The Citty and the Schoole much build vpon These figures, for both loue proportion. The City Cap is round, the Schollers square. To shew that Gouernment and learning are The perfect'st limbes i'th body of a State: For without them, all's disproportionate. If the Cap had no honor, this might reare it, The Reuerend Fathers of the Law doe weare it. It's light for Summer, and in cold it sits Close to the scull, a warme house for the wits; It shewes the whole face boldly, 'tis not made As if a man to looke on't were afraide, Nor like a Drapers shop with broad darke shed, For hee's no Citizen that hides his head. Flat Caps as proper are to Citty Gownes, As to Armors Helmets, or to Kings their Crownes. Let then the City Cap by none be scornd, Since with it Princes heads haue beene adornd. If more the round Caps honor you would know, How would this long Gowne with this steeple show? Pray Signior pardon me, 'twas done in iest. Oh, peace I pray thee, thus far off I stand, I spied the error of my seruants, she call'd for Claret, and you fill'd out Sacke; that cup giue me, 'tis for an old mans backe, and not for hers. Indeed 'twas but mistaken, aske all these else. Good Luke reach her that glasse of Claret. Here, Mistris Bride, pledge me there. How now? I pray you hold your tongue, my seruant there tells me she is not well. How, hang her in her garters? What would you haue me doe, sir? In any thing that's ciuill, honest, and iust. I haue the very same which my selfe wore. It will be but some iest, sir. Pray conceite not ill of my wiues sodaine rising. This young Knight, Sir Lodouico, is deepe seene in Phisicke, and he tells me, the disease call'd the Mother, hangs on my wife, it is a vehement heauing and beating of the Stomacke, and that swelling did with the paine thereof crampe vp her arme, that hit his lips, and brake the glasse: no harme, it was no harme. The straightest arrow may flye wide by chance. But come, we'll cloze this brawle vp in some dance. Neuer, beleeue me, Signior. Well: for she takes you for a very simple fellow. I shall not act it, chide you say, and fret, And grow impatient: I shall neuer doo't. Were I a Painter, that should liue by drawing no thing but Pictures of an angry man, I should not earne my colours; I cannot doo't. Say I should turne to Ice, and nip her loue now 'tis but in the blood. It will so ouerchange her heart with griefe, That like a Cannon, when her sighes goe off, She in her duty either will recoyle, Or breake in pieces and so dye: her death, By my vnkindnesse might be counted murther. Well, I will try your pills, doe you your faithfull seruice, and bee ready still at a pinch to helpe me in this part, or else I shall be out cleane. Ile call her forth now, shall I? Luke, I pray bid your Mistris to come hither. Sirra, bid my wife come to me: why, when? Mary wife, I haue intent, and (you see) this stripling here, He beares good will and liking to my trade, And meanes to deale in Linnen. I hope to finde him honest, pray good wife looke that his bed and chamber be made ready. Euen so I sweare to you a great oath. I will not, goe to wife, I will not, Swallow these gudgeons. Then fast, then you may choose. You know at Table What trickes you played, swaggerd, broke glasses! Fie, Fie, fie, fie: and now before my Prentice here You make an asse of me; thou, (what shall I call thee?) Oh fie, by no meanes, then she'll call me Cuckold, sirrah, goe looke to'th shop: how does this show? A curst Cowes milke I ha drunke once before, And 'twas so ranke in taste, Ile drinke no more. Wife, Ile tame you. And so you'll throw me. Reach me (Knaues) a yard. Ile teach you fencing trickes. Yet I ha the longer arme, come fall too't roundly, And spare not me (wife) for Ile lay't on soundly. If o're husbands their wiues will needes be Masters, We men will haue a law to win't at wasters For the breeches. Nor I. What's that? The first blow, wife, shall I? Prompt? A bargaine. Strike. Thou winst the day indeed, giue me thy hand, Ile challenge thee no more: my patient brest Plaid thus the Rebell, onely for a iest: Here's the rancke rider that breakes Colts, 'tis he Can tame the mad folkes, and curst wiues. My man? my Master, tho his head be bare, But he's so courteous, he'll put off his haire. Beare witnes else. For the good seruice you to me haue done, I giue you all your yeeres. What is't you lacke, Gentlewoman? Cambricke or Lawnes, or fine Hollands? Pray draw neere, I can sell you a penny-worth. Cambricke? you shall, the purest thred in Millan. My Worshipfull good Seruant, view it well, for 'tis both fine and euen. Then this shall fit her, this is for your Ladiship. You'll giue no more you say? I cannot take it. It must not fetch it. What wud you haue, sweet Gentlemen? I will but lay these parcels by—My men are all at Custome-house vnloding Wares, if Cambricke you wud deale in, there's the best, all Millan cannot sample it. Ha, ha, ha: you Gentlemen are full of Iest. If she be vp, she's gone some wares to show, I haue aboue as good wares as below. Now Gentlemen, is't Cambricks? What's that he saies, pray 'Gentlemen? The best wares? all are bad, yet wares doe good, And like to Surgeons, let sicke Kingdomes blood. Indeed I vnderstand no word he speakes. It may be so, that's likely, true indeed, In euery garden, sir, does grow that weed. 'Tis no matter. So doest not me. My Customers doe oft for remnants call, These are two remnants now, no losse at all. But let me tell you, were my Seruants here, It would ha cost more.—Thanke you Gentlemen, I vse you well, pray know my shop agen. These Lawnes, sir? A dangerous Voyage, sailing in such Ships. It may be so, good Madam. I pray no more forsooth, 'tis very well, indeed I loue no sweet meats:—Sh'as a breath stinkes worse then fifty Polecats. Sir, a word, is she a Lady? A Bawde? Sir, Ile steale hence, and see your Lawnes some other time. How, Bots? now blesse me, what doe I with Bots? no wine in sooth, no wine, good Master Bots. Here's Ratsbane vpon Ratsbane: Master Bots, I pray, sir, pardon me: you are a Soldier, presse me not to this seruice, I am old, and shoot not in such pot-gunnes. To drinke healths, is to drinke sicknesse: Gentle men, pray rescue me. I ha reckonings to cast vp, good Master Bots. The palsie, Signiors, danceth in my blood. Hold, hold, good Master Bots, I drinke. To the old Countesse there. With much adoe the poison is got downe, Tho I can scarce get vp; neuer before Dranke I a whores health, nor will neuer more. Your price? No: too deare: thus. Be it so, good Signior, send me going. Ile rather stay, then goe so: stop your Bowle. Why, sir? what house I pray? Is't so? thankes, sir: I'm gone. Lawnes which I bought, sir, of the Gentleman that keepes the house. Indeed! Me, sir, for what? Must I so? Ile send for Bayle. To Bridewell too? Yes, patience: being not mad, they had mee once to Bedlam, Now I'm drawne to Bridewell, louing no Whores. Yes, my good Lord. My Lord, what make you here? And I to beare wrong here with patience. So they doe say, my Lord, Yet bought I them vpon a Gentlemans word, And I magine now, as I thought then, That there be Theeues, but no Theeues Gentlemen. No more then Gold Being crack'd which does his estimation hold. I was in Bedlam once, but was I mad? They made me pledge Whores healths, but am I bad, Because I'm with bad people?

Plot summary

Plot — 2 The Honest Whore (2 The Converted Courtesan)
On his way to court, Hippolito receives petitioners, including the reformed courtesan Bellafront: she wants his help in petitioning the Duke to pardon her husband Matheo, who has been condemned to death for homicide. Hippolito solicits the help of Bellafront’s father Orlando, but he is still morally outraged at her former sexual misconduct. Nevertheless he secretly arranges for Matheo’s release, and enters his household disguised as a servingman, Pacheco. Matheo proves dissolute, and when ‘Pacheco’ gives him money for safe keeping, he gambles it all away. Bellafront is trying not to let poverty and Matheo’s prodigality force her back into prostitution. Hippolito, who converted her from whoredom, now tries to seduce her back again. She sends ‘Pacheco’ to return his love-tokens, but ‘Pacheco’ instead takes them to Infelice, who identifies them as her own gifts to Hippolito. She interrogates Hippolito’s go-between, Brian the footman, then tells her husband that Brian gave her the love-tokens and had an affair with her. Brian is dismissed and goes home to Ireland. Desperate for money, Matheo strips the gown from his wife’s back and sells it. Lodovico promises to furnish him as a gentleman, so he is properly dressed when Orlando visits in his own person. The old man feigns lack of sympathy, then returns as Pacheco in time to prevent Matheo assaulting Bellafront. Prompted by Orlando, the Duke takes a hand: he issues a warrant to arrest Matheo for robbing two pedlars (who were actually Orlando’s men); and, hearing that Hippolito is in love with a whore, but not knowing her identity, passes new anti-prostitution laws. Meanwhile, Candido is married a second time, but his bride tests his patience with unruly behaviour on their wedding day. He determines to tame her, and Lodovico helps in the guise of an apprentice; she submits herself after a mock duel with measuring sticks. Some gallants attempt to seduce her into a brothel, but fail, and Candido still keeps his temper when, drawn to an alehouse for a business deal, he is forced to drink to a bawd’s health. Matheo is arrested at the alehouse on the Duke’s warrant, and Candido too for receiving the cloth stolen from the pedlars. At Bridewell, Matheo admits the robbery, but attempts to incriminate Bellafront out of jealousy for her supposed relationship with Hippolito.
