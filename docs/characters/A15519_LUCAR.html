But why camest thou into England, seeing Uenice is a Citie where Usurie by Lucar may liue in great glorie? Gramercie Usury, & doubt not but to liue here as pleasantly, and pleasanter too: but whence came you Symonie, tell me. Wel Symonie, I thank thee, but as for Fraud & Dissimulation I know their long continuance, and after what fashion. Therefore Dissimulation, you shall be my steward, An office that euerie mans case by you must be preferd. And you Fraud shall be my rent-gatherer, my letter of leases & my put chaser of land, so that manie olde bribes will come to thy hand. And Usurie because I know you be trustie, you shall be my secretarie, to eale amongst merchants, to bargen and exchange money. And Symonie, because you are a slie fellow, & haue you tongue liberall, we will place you ouer such matters as are Ecclesiastica And though we appoint sundrie offices where now ye are in, yet ioyntly we meane to vse you together ofttimes in one thing. Then master Dauie to my pallaice hast thee away, 〈◊〉 will Craftie Conueyance my butler to make readie The best fare in the house, to welcome thee and thy companie: but stay Dissimulation, my selfe will go with thee. Gentlemen ile goe before, but see in anie case, so soone as ye please resort to my place. Maister Dauy Dissimulation, what new acquaintance haue ye gotten there? Gentleman, you are hartily welcome, how are you called, I pray you tell vs? But I pray you tell me what Countryman. Yet let me trouble ye, I beseech ye whence came ye? Gramercie, but senior Mercadore dare you not vndertake, Secretly to conuey good commodities out of this country for my sake? Hast thou a letter for me? Sir, I haue read the tenure of your letter, wherein I find, that at the request of Loue and Conscience I should shew my selfe kinde, And bestow some spirituall liuing on you, parsonage, or benefice, For you stand greatly in need, as appeares by this. And trust me would do for you, but it lies not in me, For all such matters are referred to my seruant Symonie. You must speake to him, and if you can get his good will, then be sure of mine, their minds to fulfill. Pray thee what is it? for thou knowest while for their house I am bargaining, And it be neuer so little, I must seeme to do something. Thou saiest true indeed: draw neare Sinceritie, Loe for their sakes I will bestow frankly on thee, The parsonage of saint Nihil ile giue thee to pleasure them withall, And such another to it, if thou watch till it fall. What are they so hastie? belike they spent it merrsly. I perceiue you haue beene mindfull of me, for which I thanke yee: But Usurie tell me, how haue you sped in that you went about? Good Conscience if thou loue me say nothing. O Conscience thou hast kild me, by thee I am ouerthrowne.

Plot — The Three Ladies of London
Lady Love and Lady Conscience are concerned that Lady Lucre now rules in London, but Fame assures them that she and her adherents will meet their due punishment in the end. Four knaves and Simplicity, a penurious miller, go to London in the hope of employment by one of the ladies. Conscience rejects their applications, but Love agrees to take on Simplicity; the knaves find positions in Lucre’s household. Out of work because foreigners have taken all the jobs, Artifex also seeks a place with Lucre. Simplicity procures a testimonial from Conscience for his cousin Sincerity, who seeks a benefice; but he is unable to get anything from Simony because he has no bribe to offer. Dissimulation fobs him off with the parsonage of St Nihil — nothing - and an invitation to dine with Nemo — nobody — is likewise worthless (though Simplicity has _ difficulty understanding this). Simony employs Sir Peter Pleaseman, who can be either Protestant or Catholic as it suits his circumstances, as Lucre’s chaplain. Conscience is forced to sell her cottage to Lucre, but hopes to stay on as a sitting tenant. Usury threatens eviction; there is a proposal to pack the house with foreigners who will require less living space than English tenants. Eventually he relents and quadruples the rent instead. Love and Conscience are reduced to sending Usury their clothes in lieu of payment, but Dissimulation gives Simplicity money to redeem one of the gowns. He intended it to be Love’s, since he cares nothing for Conscience, but Simplicity returns Conscience’s clothes and delivers Love’s to Usury. Lucre enters an agreement with the Italian merchant Mercadore, whereby he will export essential materials and foodstuffs from England, and import foreign trinkets for gentlewomen. Mercadore goes to Turkey to procure knick-knacks, and encounters the Jew Gerontus. Two years ago, he borrowed a lot of money from Gerontus and avoided paying it back by skipping the country. Fearing that he might be blamed for a Christian’s apostasy, Gerontus cancels the debt, whereupon Mercadore’s Muslim convictions evaporate.
