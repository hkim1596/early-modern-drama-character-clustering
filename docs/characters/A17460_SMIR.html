This fellow talkes as he thinkes, and thinkes as he talkes, and talkes what he knowes. I wonnot repeat it ouer againe, for the disparagement of my Wit: but what I say, I will maintaine, what I maintaine, I will say; and the very Bells themselues shall ring it out Probatum. The dumbe Lord's frighted, his haire stands an end. Oh, Oh. Oh, Oh. First take my tongue, and afterward my heart. Kickt with disgrace, and turn'd out of the Court Both to the guard and blacke guard made a sport. The Carters, Colliers, and the Carriers curst me The Porters puld me, and the Pages pur … … me. O that I had bin so happy to haue liu'd and clefe wood i'the countrey, preacht at the Buttery barre vnto the Ploughmen, and there haue vsde my authoritie in Folio, when all the seruants of the house shud be drunke at midnight, Cum Priuilegio. Learne to winde whipcord, and go hang my selfe. I, and the place were not priuiledg'd. Let 'hem alone, and laugh at 'hem. Yet he he gaue me a bob i'the proiect. Why Sir, A cook wil vndertake to bake in a pasty Foure oxen, without Buttor corner wise, Lambe and Mutton in the middle, All kinde of Fowle on each side, With their bils lying out to discouer their condition, With which he wilbe bound to find the fair seuen daies, And giue the ouer plus to the poore. Then I haue another, for the crying of small Beare, from six … i'the morning, till twelue at noone, for all that are Barnab … . Nay, it wud ha gone forward, for all your high bloods wud a countenanc'd it, yet it was thought there was a rich Milke-woman wud a crost it, and haue brought it to posset drinke i'the winter, and whay i'the Summer, and the Apothecaries wud haue ioyn'd with her, to haue clarified it. I donnnot looke for it, because it comes with a backe blow, and there is no ward for it but Patience. I, but when affliction comes like a Fury, with a whip in her hand, 'tis a fore matter. This is the foure winds driuing of fiue Diuels—This same Ring wud faine giue Me the slip, I must e'ne pocket him, for Feare of the worst. Why, do you see me? And you know not me, you know no body. But keepe off my Lord. You see me you say, Duke I speake to thee. And you all see me. Shall I demonstrate matter of Art. And haue nothing for my paines? Proclamations may say what they list, Something in hand doth well. I, but no body heares not on that Eare, yet be cause I—wonnot doe Royaltie wrong, in suspecting your bountie—You see me you say. You do. Who sees me now? I shoud be sorry else; for, and my inuisible Ring shoud not keepe his olde vertue, I wud hang my selfe directly. I will haue Maiestie call me first. Let me heare him vuia voce, Smirke is my name A well beloued subiect, once a Painter, But now Esquire of the inuisible Ring. See here I am, what wudst thou mighty monarch I do command thee not to command me that, For from my inuisible Ring I will not part. Assist me my deare Ring, no hands vpon me, For being inuisible, I am a Prince, And being a Prince no hands is to be laid on me; Treason doth neuer prosper. O, O, O. The cramps in my finger. My Ring is gone now, the diuell go with it, for a my conscience he fetcht it. I, I, and my middle finger, which serued me for more vse then all the rest. Illustrious, bright shining, well spoken, and blood stirring Lady. If the Rope of my Capacitie, could reach to the Belfrie of your Beautie, these words of mine like siluer Bels, might be worthy to hang in the eares of your fauor but the Ladder of my Inuention is to low to clime vp to the Steeple of your Vnderstanding. If it were not, I shoud ring out my minde to you in a sweet Peale of most sauory conceits. For your face it is like the Sun, no man is able to indure it. Why then Ile take your little women. Sed non feci, Stay a little, here's a couple of lines, a halter on 'hem, they wonnot twist handsomely, go forward I haue ended. Hum Drum, hee has lightn'd within an Inch of a conceit of mine. I warrant you. Giue me audience. In praise of little women I begin. And will maintaine what I haue enter'd in. Is not your Parochit or Marmoset In more request then your Baboone or Parret. Giue but your little wench freely her licour, And to bed send her you will find her quicker; Pearter, nimbler, both to kisse and cogge, Then your great wench that'll lie like a logge. And he that all day at the Drum doth labor, Wud at night gladly play vpon a Taber. I hope ther's no man, but of this beliefe, That Veale's more sweete and nourishing then Beefe: Small meats is still preferd; for aske your glutton, Heele alwayes say, Lamb's sweeter then your Mutton. Your Smelt then Whiting firmer is and sounder, Nor must your Place compare with your neat Flounder. In fish or flesh Ile proue it to each wight, A Larkes leg, then the body of a Kite Is better farre: our Bakers alwayes make The finest flowre in the lesser Cake. And Ile be iudge by those that Roots do eate, That your small Turnep's better then your great. Who list to be resolu'd, let'hem both trie; In that boleefe I liue, in that Ile die.
