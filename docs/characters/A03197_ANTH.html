That these my wandring eyes had kept their stay; That I my selfe had still beene like my selfe; That my poore heart had neuer felt the wound, Whose anguish keepes me in a deadly sound: Oh how deluding dreames this night ore-past, Drench'd my sad soule in pleasures floting sea! Me thought I clasp'd my loue within my armes, And circling her, sau'd her from threatning harmes; Me thought there came an hundred in an houre That sought to rob me of my sweetest flower: But like a champion I did keepe her still Within this circle, free from euery ill: But when I wak'd and missd my Phillis there, All my sweete ioyes conuerted into feare. How, blind? I would it were as blinde as Ebon night, That loue had neuer hit my heart so right; But what is loue in your opinion? Thus may the free-man iest at mannacles, The furre-clad citizen laugh at a storme, The swartie Moore diuing to gather pearle, Challenge the scalding ardor of the sunne; And aged Nestor sitting in his tent, May tearme wounds sport, & warre but merriment. That thou wilt pittie me, Yonder she comes that holds me prisoner. Oh, for to heare that comfort I doe long, Ile turne it to a straine to right my wrong. I am that brother riuall in his loue, I am that brother hates him for his loue: Not his but mine, and I will haue that loue, Or neuer liue to see him kisse my loue; What thou erst sayd, I am that man alone, That will depose you brother from loues throne; I am that man, though you my elder be, That will aspire beyond you one degree. So, hee is gone, I scarsely trust him neither; For tis his custome, like a sneaking foole, To fetch a compasse of a mile about, And creepe where he would be; well, let him passe, I heard him say, that since by word of mouth He could not purchase his sweet mistresse fauour, He would endeuour what his wit might doe By writing, and by tokens; O tis good Writing with inke; O no, but with his blood. Well, so much for that, now I know his minde I doe intend not to be farre behinde: Heele send a letter, I will write another, Doe what you can, ile be before you brother; Ile entercept his letter by the way, And as time serues, the same I will bewray: Mine being made, a Porter Ile procure, That shall conuey that heart-inticing lure; About it then, my letter shall be writ, Though not with blood, yet with a reaching wit. Exit. Canst thou be trusty then, and secret too, Being imployd in weighty businesse? Then marke me well, in Cornhill by th'Exchange, Dwells an old Merchant, Flower they call his name, He hath one onely daughter, to whose hands, If thou conueniently canst giue this letter, Ile pay thee well, make thee the happiest Porter That euer vndertooke such businesse. Anthony Golding is my name, my friend, About it then, thy message being done, Make haste to me againe, till when, I leaue thee. Exit. Porter, what newes? spake you with Phillis ? Plot summary

Plot — The Fair Maid of the Exchange
Phyllis and Ursula are assaulted by two ruffians as they go to deliver wares to a customer at night. A cripple rescues them, and a second assault is foiled by the intervention of Frank Golding; the cripple promises to help Frank if ever he can. Frank's two brothers are both in love with Phyllis, and, though he scorns amorous passions, Frank has fallen for her too. Phyllis, however, loves the cripple, and woos him by giving him a handkerchief to embroider “for a friend’. Meanwhile, Bobbington poses as a sailor going on a voyage, and borrows money from Phyllis’s father, Flower, leaving a diamond ring with him as security. Ferdinand and Anthony overhear one another's plans to woo Phyllis, but neither knows that Frank is also a rival. Ferdinand writes to her; Anthony plans to intercept the letter and substitute his own, but Ferdinand disguises Frank as a porter and gets him hired as Anthony's messenger. Planning to double-cross them both, Frank enlists the cripple's help. The cripple supplies him with two rejection letters to take back to his brothers, as from Phyllis. They each enlist the help of one of Phyllis’s parents, ===== PAGE 378 ===== who then quarrel over which brother shall marry their daughter. _ Knowing Phyllis loves him but thinking himself unworthy, the cripple lends Frank his clothes. Frank courts Phyllis in disguise, and she gives him her ring; then. he brings the parents forged letters from his brothers withdrawing their suits. Each parent decides to:spite the other by offering Phyllis to Frank instead of the other’s favoured son-in-law. The thwarted brothers realize what has happened, but Phyllis asks to be allowed a say in her.own marriage — and chooses Frank. As the play ends, Flower is arrested for receiving stolen property — Bobbington’s ring — and sent for trial. In the sub-plot, Mall Berry is in love with Barnard, but suffers the unwanted attentions of Bowdler. Barnard is surety for a £100 debt to Mall’s father; the original borrower is dead and the debt about to fall due, but Berry refuses to allow Barnard any more time to find the money. Bowdler persists in his pursuit of Mall, using lines he remembers from Venus and Adonis. Berry engages sergeants to arrest Barnard. Mall agrees to marry Bowdler, but the cripple points out that, through a party wall, he has overheard her talking about Barnard in her sleep: he is the man she truly loves.
