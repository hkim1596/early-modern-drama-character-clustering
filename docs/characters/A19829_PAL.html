But Thirsis, you know how her father meant To match her with Alexis, and a day To celebrate the nuptials was prefixt. Womens affections doe like flashes proue, They oft shew passion when they feele small loue. Alas with what poore Coyne are louers paid, And taken with the smallest bayte is laid? Fie Thirsis, with what fond remembrances Doest thou these idle passions entertaine? For shame leaue off to waste your youth in vaine, And feede on shadowes: make your choice anew. You other Nimphes shall find, no doubt will be As louely, and as faire: and sweete as she. Thou maiest be such a fond I dolater To die for loue; though that were very strange. Loue hath few Saints, but many confessors. And time no doubt will raze out all these notes, And leaue a roome at length for other thoughts. There is no dealing with this man, I see, This humour must be let to spend it selfe Vnto a lesser substance, ere that we Can any way apply a remedy. But I lament his case, and so I know Do all that see him in this wofull plight: And therefore will I leaue him to himselfe, For sorrow that is full, hates others sight. In troth, Charinus, I haue oftentimes, As one that suffred for his grieuances, Assayd to finde a way into the cause Of his so strange dismay; and by all meanes Aduis'd him make redemption of himselfe, And come to life againe, and be a man With men: but all serues not, I finde him lockt Fast to his will, alleadge I what I can. The cause is loue; but it is such a loue, As is not to be had. Shee is not in Arcadia whom he loues, Nor in the world, and yet he deerely loues. Thus plainly; he's in loue with a dead woman, And that so farre, as with the thought of her Which hath shut out all other, he alone Liues, and abhorres to be, or seene, or knowne. Faire Siluia, old Medorus daughter, who Was two yeares past reported to be slaine By sauage beasts vpon our countrey shore. Plot summary

Plot — Hymen's Triumph
Hymen has come to Arcadia incognito to promote married love; but Envy, Avarice, and Jealousy have also come to try and cross his objectives. Thyrsis mourns for his dead love Silvia, while his friend Palaemon tries to persuade him to forget her, but he has been told by the oracle that he wil] be happy again on the day he dies. Cloris sends her boy Clarindo to Thyrsis as a messenger of her unrequited love, remarking that he is rumoured to be in love with another nymph. This distresses Clarindo, who is actually Silvia: though deemed dead, she was in fact abducted by pirates, and has now returned in disguise in order to avoid a forced marriage to her father's preferred suitor (who is now to marry another woman). Meanwhile Phyllis, who once scorned love, has fallen in love with ‘Clarindo’. Her encounter with the ‘boy’ is witnessed by Montanus, who loves her, but when he jealously confronts her, he is persuaded that his eyes deceived him; as penance, he is asked to frighten the ‘boy’. Having witnessed Thyrsis’ grief, Silvia plans to reveal her true identity to him. First she leaves a message carved on a tree, then, at his request, tells him a story based on her own life: how one Sulia, beloved by Syrthis, was taken by pirates, entered the service of the pirate captain’s wife in Egypt dressed as a boy, and after two years was rescued and brought home by Corinthian merchants. Montanus stabs her, and is arrested; Thyrsis, tending to the body, realizes her identity and determines to die with her, but both lovers are restored to life by the medical skill of Lamia.
