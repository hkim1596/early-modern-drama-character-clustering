Nay, we muſt longer kneele; I am a Suitor. Thanke your Maieſty That you would loue your ſelfe, and in that loue Not vnconſidered leaue your Honour, nor The dignity of your Office; is the poynt Of my Petition. I am ſolicited not by a few, And thoſe of true condition; That your Subiects Are in great grieuance: There haue beene Commiſſions Sent downe among 'em, which hath flaw'd the heart Of all their Loyalties; wherein, although My good Lord Cardinall, they vent reproches Moſt bitterly on you, as putter on Of theſe exactions: yet the King, our Maiſter Whoſe Honor Heauen ſhield from ſoile; euen he eſcapes not Language vnmannerly; yea, ſuch which breakes The ſides of loyalty, and almoſt appeares In lowd Rebellion. No, my Lord? You know no more then others? But you frame Things that are knowne alike, which are not wholſome To thoſe which would not know them, and yet muſt Perforce be their acquaintance. Theſe exactions (Whereof my Soueraigne would haue note) they are Moſt peſtilent to th' hearing, and to beare 'em, The Backe is Sacrifice to th' load; They ſay They are deuis'd by you, er elſe you ſuffer Too hard an exclamation. I am much too venturous In tempting of your patience; but am boldned Vnder your promis'd pardon. The Subiects griefe Comes through Commiſſions, which compels from each The ſixt part of his Subſtance, to be leuied Without delay; and the pretence for this Is nam'd, your warres in France: this makes bold mouths, Tongues ſpit their duties out, and cold hearts freeze Allegeance in them; their curſes now Liue where their prayers did: and it's come to paſſe, This tractable obedience is a Slaue To each incenſed Will: I would your Highneſſe Would giue it quicke conſideration; for There is no primer baſeneſſe. I am ſorry, that the Duke of Buckingham Is run in your diſpleaſure. My learn'd Lord Cardinall, Deliuer all with Charity. If I know you well, You were the Dukes Surueyor, and loſt your Office On the complaint o' th' Tenants; take good heed You charge not in your ſpleene a Noble perſon, And ſpoyle your nobler Soule; I ſay, take heed; Yes, heartily beſeech you. God mend all. My Lord, my Lord, I am a ſimple woman, much too weake T' oppoſe your cunning. Y' are meek, & humble-mouth'd You ſigne your Place, and Calling, in full ſeeming, With Meekeneſſe and Humilitie: but your Heart Is cramm'd with Arrogancie, Spleene, and Pride. You haue by Fortune, and his Highneſſe fauors, Gone ſlightly o're lowe ſteppes, and now are mounted Where Powres are your Retainers, and your words (Domeſtickes to you) ſerue your will, as't pleaſe Your ſelfe pronounce their Office. I muſt tell you, You tender more your perſons Honor, then Your high profeſſion Spirituall. That agen I do refuſe you for my Iudge, and heere Before you all, Appeale vnto the Pope, To bring my whole Cauſe 'fore his Holineſſe, And to be iudg'd by him. Take thy Lute wench, My Soule growes ſad with troubles, Sing, and diſperſe 'em if thou canſt: leaue working: ORpheus with his Lute made Trees, And the Mountaine tops that freeze, Bow themſelues when he did ſing. To his Muſicke, Plants and Flowers Euer ſprung; as Sunne and Showers, There had made a laſting Spring. Euery thing that heard him play, Euen the Billowes of the Sea, Hung their heads, & then lay by. In ſweet Muſicke is ſuch Art, Killing care, & griefe of heart, Fall aſleepe, or hearing dye. How now? Would they ſpeake with me? Pray their Graces To come neere: what can be their buſines With me, a poore weake woman, falne from fauour? I doe not like their comming; now I thinke on't, They ſhould bee good men, their affaires as righteous: But all Hoods, make not Monkes. Your Graces find me heere part of a Houſwife, (I would be all) againſt the worſt may happen: What are your pleaſures with me, reuerent Lords? Speake it heere. There's nothing I haue done yet o' my Conſcience Deſerues a Corner: would all other Women Could ſpeake this with as free a Soule as I doe. My Lords, I care not (ſo much I am happy Aboue a number) if my actions Were tri'de by eu'ry tongue, eu'ry eye ſaw 'em, Enuy and baſe opinion ſet againſt 'em, I know my life ſo euen. If your buſines Seeke me out, and that way I am Wife in; Out with it boldly: Truth loues open dealing. O good my Lord, no Latin; I am not ſuch a Truant ſince my comming, As not to know the Language I haue liu'd in: A ſtrange Tongue makes my cauſe more ſtrange, ſuſpiti ous: Pray ſpeake in Engliſh; heere are ſome will thanke you, If you ſpeake truth, for their poore Miſtris ſake; Beleeue me ſhe ha's had much wrong. Lord Cardinall, The willing'ſt ſinne I euer yet committed, May be abſolu'd in Engliſh. To betray me. My Lords, I thanke you both for your good wills, Ye ſpeake like honeſt men, (pray God ye proue ſo) But how to make ye ſodainly an Anſwere In ſuch a poynt of weight, ſo neere mine Honour, (More neere my Life I feare) with my weake wit; And to ſuch men of grauity and learning; In truth I know not. I was ſet at worke, Among my Maids, full little (God knowes) looking Either for ſuch men, or ſuch buſineſſe; For her ſake that I haue beene, for I feele The laſt fit of my Greatneſſe; good your Graces Let me haue time and Councell for my Cauſe: Alas, I am a Woman frendleſſe, hopeleſſe. In England, But little for my profit can you thinke Lords, That any Engliſh man dare giue me Councell? Or be a knowne friend 'gainſt his Highnes pleaſure, (Though he be growne ſo deſperate to be honeſt) And liue a Subiect? Nay forſooth, my Friends, They that muſt weigh out my affllictions, They that my truſt muſt grow to, liue not heere, They are (as all my other comforts) far hence In mine owne Countrey Lords. How Sir? Ye tell me what ye wiſh for both, my ruine: Is this your Chriſtian Councell? Out vpon ye. Heauen is aboue all yet; there ſits a Iudge. That no King can corrupt. The more ſhame for ye; holy men I thought ye, Vpon my Soule two reuerend Cardinall Vertues: But Cardinall Sins, and hollow hearts I feare ye: Mend 'em for ſhame my Lords: Is this your comfort? The Cordiall that ye bring a wretched Lady? A woman loſt among ye, laugh't at, ſcornd? I will not wiſh ye halfe my miſeries, I haue more Charity. But ſay I warn'd ye; Take heed, for heauens ſake take heed, leaſt at once The burthen of my ſorrowes, fall vpon ye.

Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
