I will not court the madnesse of the times, Nor fawne vpon the Riots that embalme Our wanton Gentry, to preserue the dust Of their affected vanities in coffins Of memorable hame ▪ when Common-wealths Totter and reele from that nobilitie And ancient vertue, which renownes the great, Who steere the Helme of gouernment, while Mush rooms Grow vp, & make new lawes to licence folly: Why should not I, a May-game, scorne the weight Of my sunke fortunes? snarle at the vices Which rot the Land, and without feare or wit Be mine owne Anticke? Tis a sport to liue When life is irkesome, if we will not hug Prosperity in others, and contemne Affliction in our selues. Plot — 420
A group of mounted knights arrive at a wedding, and one of their number explains their presence. They worship Diana for as long as they remain unmarried, but also sacrifice to Hymenaeus, god of marriage. When they were last doing so, the god spoke, telling them one of Diana’s nymphs had got married to an earl favoured by Minerva. Diana is particularly pleased to know that the couple have agreed to wait a year before consummating the marriage. The two goddesses have asked Hymenaeus to arrange for the marriage- feast to be honoured, so he sends the knights with gifts: Minerva sends the groom a garland, Diana sends the bride a veil, and Hymenaeus sends them respectively a picture of a dove and a coronet. The knights present their shields and dance with ladies from the audience.
