Nimphe I doe beleeue thee; For eu'ne vpon his death, I heard the wretch Call ſtill vpon thy name to his laſt breath. I was vpon the middes of yon high hill, Where I had ſpred abroad ſome netts of mine To drie them, when not far off from me, came Aminta by, with a ſad clowdy looke, And altred much from what he wont to bee Both in his face and faſhion; which I spying Ranne after him; and ſtaying him, quoth hee Ergaſtus thou muſt doe a curteſie For me of much importance and auaile; 'Tis to goe with me but a little hence, For witneſſe of a deede I haue to doo; But firſt I'le haue thee binde thy faith to me By a ſtrict oath to ſtand aloofe from mee And not approach to lett or hinder that That I ſhall do: I (that could nere haue dream't Of ſuch a furious madneſſe in him) yeelded To's will; and made deſperate inuocations Calling to witneſs Pan, and Priapus: Pales, Pomona', and nightly Hecate, Which done, he led me higher vp the hill: Where, clambring through wilde rocky paſſages, (By wayes nere found, and neuer trode before) VVee gayn'd the top, that ouer-hung a valley, 'Twixt which and vs was a ſteepe precipice, And there we ſtay'd; I caſting downe mine eye, Began for feare to tremble, and ſhrunke back. After a little pauſe, he ſmil'de me thought, And ſeem'd more cheerefull then he was before; And that made me miſdoubt him leſſe then euer: After that; (quoth he to me) ſee thou tell The Nimphes and Shepherds what thou ſhalt behold; Then looking downe, Ah that I had (ſayd he) So ready at my will, the throat and teeth Of thoſe ſame greedy VVolues, as theſe rocks be; I would not dye of other death, then ſhe VVho was my life; nor haue my carkaſs torne But by thoſe teeth that tore thoſe delicate And beautious limbs of hers; but ſince that heau'n Denies ſo great a bleſsing to me, I Muſt be content ſome other way to dye; And though a worſe way, yet a ſpeedier. Siluia I follow thee, Siluia I come To beare thee comapny, If thou diſdaine me not; O I ſhould dye Much more contentedly; were I but ſure My follow'ing thee would not diſquiet thee, And that thy hate had ending with thy life: Siluia I follow thee, I come. Which ſayd, Downe from the place he headlong threw himſelfe, And I turn'd yee to ſee't. Not ſo; for ſetting all ſuch oathes at nought, (Vaine doubtleſſe in ſuch caſes) when I ſaw VVhither his fond and headdy madneſſe tended, I reacht at him; and (as ill hap would haue it) Layd hold but of this thinne ſcarfe, wherewithall He girt himſelfe; which (all too weake to beare His bodies weight, that reſted all vpon't) Remayn'd broke in my hand. I know not; For I was ſo dead ſtrucken at the ſight, As my heart would not ſuffer me, to looke And ſee him daſht to peeces. There O'that hand 'tis, and not far from hence. Plot summary

Plot — Aminta
Resenting his mother’s efforts to control his activities and limit them to persons of high estate, Cupid absconds to live as a shepherd and make Silvia fall in love. Silvia was Aminta’s companion in boyhood. Three years ago he declared his love for her, and she has refused to see him ever since. Despite Daphne’s attempts to persuade her that she should take her amorous pleasures while she can, Silvia has no maternal desires: she loves only hunting, and hates Aminta because he loves her. Recognizing that Aminta needs to be more masterful, Daphne and Thyrsis arrange for him to encounter Silvia while she is bathing. But Silvia has another rejected admirer with similar ideas, a Satyr, and when Aminta arrives he finds Silvia stark naked, tied to a tree with her own hair, and about to be raped. He drives the Satyr off, but Silvia is haughtily ungracious and runs away. Daphne restrains Aminta’s suicidal impulses. Silvia borrows some clothes from Nerina and goes hunting. She encounters a wolf and chases it; Nerina follows, loses her, and then sees a wolf-pack licking blood off the ground, with Silvia’s bloodstained veil nearby. When Aminta hears of this, his suicidal impulses are renewed: with the help of Ergastus, he climbs a cliff and throws himself off. Silvia is not really dead — the wolves’ prey was only an animal — but the news of Aminta’s death prompts her to recognize her love for him, and seek to join him in the afterlife. It turns out that he is not dead: he fell into a bush, stunned himself, and recovers on feeling Silvia’s tears splashing onto his face.
