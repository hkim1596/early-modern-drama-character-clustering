Sir, the consent That made me yours, shall neuer teach me to Repent I am so, yet be you but pleasde To giue me leaue to say so much; the honor You offer me were better giuen to her, To whom you owe the power of giuing. 'Tis that which I shall euer owe you Madam, And must haue none from you, pray you pardon me. What moues my Lord to this strange sadnes? If any late discerned want in me, Giue cause to your repentance, care and duty Shall finde a painefull way to recompence. Speake nearer to my vnderstanding, like a husband. It is a phrase will part with the same ease From you with that you now deliuer. Striue not to win content from ignorance Which must be lost in knowledge: heauen can witnesse My farthest hope of good reacht at your pleasure, Which seeing alone may in your looke be read: Adde not a doubtfull comment to a text That in it selfe's direct and easie. I know no bar. 'Tis true, that to bring forth a second to your selfe, Was only worthy of my Virgin losle; And should I prize you lesse vnpatternde Sir, Then being, exemplified, ist not more honor To be possessor of vnequalde vertue, Then what is paralelde? giue me beleefe, The name of mother knowes no way of good, More then the end in me: who weds for lust Is oft a widdow; when I married you, I lost the name of maid to gaine a title Aboue the wish of change, which that part can Only maintaine, is still the same in man, His vertue and his calme society, Which no gray haires can threaten to dissolue, Nor wrinkles bury. We are but to praise the coolenes of their streames. As it made way vnto their enuied rest, With weary knots binding their harmeles eyes. Vnlesse it were to welcome his wisht sight, Whose absence only gaue her mourning voyce. Let proofe plead for me, let me be mewde vp Where neuer eye may reach me but your owne; And when I shall repent, but in my lookes, if sigh, But in your sadnesse. If any thing may make a though suspected, Of knowing any happines but you, Diuorse me by the title of most falshood. Brother. She is more then dull Sir, lesse and worse then woman, That may inherit such an infinite As you propound, a greatnesse so neare goodnesse; And brings a will to rob her. Many dead Sir, Liuing I thinke as many: A generall curse Light on her hart denies it. You strangely stir me Sir, and were my weaknes In any other flesh but modest womans, You should not aske more questions, may I do it? I ioy in't, Aboue a moderate gladnesse, Sir, you promise It shall be honest. Let it be what it may then, what it dare, I haue a mind will hazarde'it. Only her duty Sir. 'Tis so much the more noble. So is sleepe Sir, Or anything that's meerely ours and mortall, We were begotten gods else; but those feares Feeling but once the fires of nobler thoughts, Fly like the shapes of clouds we forme to nothing. I do. 'Tis of all sleepes the sweetest, Children begin it to vs, strong men seeke it, And kings from heigth of all their painted glories Fall, like spent exhalations, to this center; And those are fooles that feare it, or imagine A few vnhandsome pleasures, or lifes profits Can recompence this place; and mad that staies it, Till age blow out their lights, or rotten humors, Bring vm despers'd to the earth. Brunhild fears that Thierry's bride, Ordella, will eclipse her in preeminence, so at the wedding banquet she gives Thierry a potion that makes him impotent that night. Ordella is very understanding , but Thierry is concerned about the need to beget an heir, so Brunhild suggests he should consult a local hermit and astrologer. Her parasite, Lecure, impersonates the wise man, and tells Thierry that he will regain his potency ifhe kills the first female person he encounters the next day, whoever she may be. Lecure arranges for this to be Ordella herself. Not recognizing her in her veil, Thierry explains why she must die, and she agrees; but he cannot bring himself to go through with it when he realizes who she is. Martel dissuades Ordella from loyally committing suicide, and, suspecting a plot, has her pretend to be dead in order to smoke it out Meanwhile Brunhild spreads a rumour that Theodoret is illegitimate and has him secretly murdered by Protaldi at the continuing wedding revels. Believing that Theodoret was not his brother and that Ordella is dead, Thierry offers to make amends by marrying Memberge, Theodoret's daughter. Brunhild is forced to admit her lies in order to prevent an incestuous marriage. She gives Thierry a poisoned handkerchief, which kills him; Martel brings Ordella to his deathbed. Brunhild admits complicity in his murder, and is sentenced to remain awake for the rest of her life; disturbed by the sight of Protaldi being tortured to death, she commits suicide. Thierry and Ordella die together , and Martel becomes King of France.
