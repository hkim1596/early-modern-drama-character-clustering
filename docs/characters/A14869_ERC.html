Oh rise Lady, certainly heauen neuer intended Kneeling to this fearefull purpose. Giue me your hand. Oh my right worthy friends, you haue staid me long, one health, and then aboord; for all the Gallies are come about. Pray why sit? Pray leaue vs Gentlemen. Wilt please you sit? Vnfained. Compare her beauty, and my youth together, And you will find the faire effects of loue No myracle at all. Your Warrant must be mightie. You deale faire sir. Moue it. If I tell truth, you will not credit me. I will tell you truth, Yet shew some reason you haue not to beleeue me: Her Brother had no hand in't, ist not hard For you to credit this: for you may thinke, I count it basenesse to ingage another Into my quarrell; and for that take leaue To dissemble the truth. Sir, if you will fight With any but my selfe, fight with her Mother, Shee was the motiue. I will sir. I will haste before you, poynt whither. Yet me thinks, this looks not like a quarrell. You haue not apparelled your sury well, It goes too plaine like a Scholler. You promise well to your selfe. Shall's haue no Seconds? The length of our weapons. For that let me embrace you: No beleeue me, I take your heart to be sufficient proofe, Without a priuie coat; and for my part, A Taffaty is all the shirt of Mayle I am armed with. My sword shall answer that; come, are you ready? Ide as soone take Your counsell in Diuinitie at this present, As I would take a kind direction from you For the managing my weapon; and indeed, Both would shew much alike. Come are you ready? Oh, I cannot forget it. Did you come hither only to tell me so, Or to doe it? I meane well, but 'twill not thriue. Oh most foolishly demaunded, To bid me beg that which thou canst not giue. I do looke on my action with a thought of terror, To doe ill and dwell in't, is vnmanly. I fought for one, in whom I haue no more right, Then false executors haue in Orphans goods, They cozen them of; yet tho my cause were naught, I rather chose the hazard of my soule, Then foregoe the complement of a chollerick man. I pray continue the report of my death, and giue out, Cause the Church denyed me Christian buriall, The Vice admirall of my Gallies tooke my body, With purpose to commit it to the earth, Either in Cicil, or Malta. There is hope of life In Contarino; and he has my prayers, That he may liue to enioy what is his owne, The faire Iolenta; where, should it be thought That I were breathing, happily her friends Would oppose it still. That's preuented thus, There does belong a noble Priuiledge To all his Family, euer since his father, Bore from the worthy Emperour Charles the fift, An answere to the French Kings challenge, at such time The two noble Princes were ingag'd to fight. Vpon a frontier arme o'th sea in a flat-bottom'd Boat, That if any of his Family should chance To kill a man i'th Field, in a noble cause, He should haue his Pardon; now sir, for his cause, The world may iudge if it were not honest. Pray helpe me in speech, tis very painfull to me. The guilt of this lyes in Romelio, And as I heare, to second this good Contract, He has got a Nun with child. I haue much compassion on him, For sinne and shame are euer tyde together, With Gordion knots, of such a strong threed spun, They cannot without violence be vndone. Yes sir. But truth is, Hauing receiued from me some certaine wounds, Which were not mortall, this vild murderer, Being by Will deputed Ouerseer Of the Noblemans Estate, to his sisters vse, That he might make him sure from suruiuing, To reuoke that Will, stole to him in's bed, and kild him. I protest, before I thought 'twas Contarinos Issue, And for that would haue vail'd her dishonour.

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
