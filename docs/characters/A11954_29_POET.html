G Ood day Sir. I haue not ſeene you long, how goes the World? I that's well knowne: But what particular Rarity? What ſtrange, Which manifold record not matches: ſee Magicke of Bounty, all theſe ſpirits thy power Hath coniur'd to attend. I know the Merchant. When we for recompence haue prais'd the vild, It ſtaines the glory in that happy Verſe, Which aptly ſings the good. A thing ſlipt idlely from me. Our Poeſie is as a Gowne, which vſes From whence 'tis nouriſht: the fire i' th' Flint Shewes not, till it be ſtrooke: our gentle flame Prouokes it ſelfe, and like the currant flyes Each bound it chaſes. What haue you there? Vpon the heeles of my preſentment ſir. Let's ſee your peece. So 'tis, this comes off well, and excellent. Admirable: How this grace Speakes his owne ſtanding: what a mentall power This eye ſhootes forth? How bigge imagination Moues in this Lip, to th' dumbneſſe of the geſture, One might interpret. I will ſay of it, It Tutors Nature, Artificiall ſtrife Liues in theſe toutches, liuelier then life. The Senators of Athens, happy men. I will vnboult to you. You ſee how all Conditions, how all Mindes, As well of glib and ſlipp'ry Creatures, as Of Graue and auſtere qualitie, tender downe Their ſeruices to Lord Timon : his large Fortune, Vpon his good and gracious Nature hanging, Subdues and properties to his loue and tendance All ſorts of hearts; yea, from the glaſſe-fac'd Flatterer To Apemantus, that few things loues better Then to abhorre himſelfe; euen hee drops downe The knee before him, and returnes in peace Moſt rich in Timons nod. Sir, I haue vpon a high and pleaſant hill Feign'd Fortune to be thron'd. The Baſe o' th' Mount Is rank'd with all deſerts, all kinde of Natures That labour on the boſome of this Sphere, To propagate their ſtates; among'ſt them all, Whoſe eyes are on this Soueraigne Lady fixt, One do I perſonate of Lord Timons frame, Whom Fortune with her Iuory hand wafts to her, Whoſe preſent grace, to preſent ſlaues and ſeruants Tranſlates his Riuals. Nay Sir, but heare me on: All thoſe which were his Fellowes but of late, Some better then his valew; on the moment Follow his ſtrides, his Lobbies fill with tendance, Raine Sacrificiall whiſperings in his eare, Make Sacred euen his ſtyrrop, and through him Drinke the free Ayre. When Fortune in her ſhift and change of mood Spurnes downe her late beloued; all his Dependants Which labour'd after him to the Mountaines top, Euen on their knees and hand, let him ſit downe, Not one accompanying his declining foot. Vouchſafe my Labour, And long liue your Lordſhip. How now Philoſopher? Art not one? Then I lye not. Yes. That's not feign'd, he is ſo. What's to be thought of him? Does the Rumor hold for true, That hee's ſo full of Gold? Then this breaking of his, Ha's beene but a Try for his Friends? What haue you now To preſent vnto him? I muſt ſerue him ſo too; Tell him of an intent that's comming toward him. I am thinking What I ſhall ſay I haue prouided for him: It muſt be a perſonating of himſelfe: A Satyre againſt the ſoftneſſe of Proſperity, With a Diſcouerie of the infinite Flatteries That follow youth and opulencie. Nay let's ſeeke him. Then do we ſinne againſt our owne eſtate, When we may profit meete, and come too late. Haile worthy Timon. Sir: Hauing often of your open Bounty taſted, Hearing you were retyr'd, your Friends falne off, Whoſe thankeleſſe Natures (O abhorred Spirits) Not all the Whippes of Heauen, are large enough ▪ What, to you, Whoſe Starre-like Nobleneſſe gaue life and influence To their whole being? I am rapt, and cannot couer The monſtrous bulke of this Ingratitude With any ſize of words. Nor I.

Plot summary

Plot — Timon of Athens
; Timon gives freely of his riches to his acquaintances: every little gift will trigger disproportionate largesse by way of requital. He intervenes financially to secure Ventidius’ release from a debtors’ prison, and settles money on his servant Lucilius to enable him to marry the girl he loves, a wealthy man’s daughter. He entertains guests at a lavish feast at which a masque of Amazons is performed. But his creditors. are alarmed by his uncontrolled spending, and call in his debts, which amount to more than the value of his entire estate. He imagines that he can still call on the former recipients of his liberality, but they make their excuses and leave him in difficulties; he is failed even by Ventidius, now a rich man after his father’s death. Timon lays on a second feast, and invites his unreliable friends; they think normal service has been resumed, but are discomfited to find that the menu consists of water alone. Timon drives them off with abuse and leaves Athens in misanthropic fury. Alcibiades attempts to save a friend from execution for murder, but the Senate dismisses the appeal; when he persists, they banish him. In the forest, Timon digs for roots to eat, but finds only buried gold. He meets Alcibiades, marching against Athens with his soldiers, and gives him gold to ensure the city’s destruction; he also pays the army’s whores to spread venereal disease. He encourages a band of thieves to prey upon mankind, but his words persuade them to rethink their way of life. His faithful steward seeks him out and offers to share his own savings; Timon rewards him for his honesty but does not change his low opinion of the rest of humanity. News gets round that Timon still has gold, and hangers-on arrive, hoping this means his bankruptcy was only a bluff to test his friends; he drives them violently away. The Senate asks Timon to return to Athens as its tuler, to defend it against Alcibiades, but Timon, who is busy writing his own epitaph, wishes annihilation on them, and recommends suicide as their only means of escape. Alcibiades’ messenger to Timon finds only his tomb. Athens surrenders, and Timon’s epitaph is read.
