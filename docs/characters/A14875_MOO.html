Full speech

I would haue you look vp, Sir; these Court teares Claime not your tribute to them. Let those weepe That guiltily pertake in the sad cause. I knew last night by a sad dreame I had Some mischiefe would insue; yet to say truth My dreame most concern'd you. Mee thought sir, you came stealing to my bed. Fy sir! as I told you, Me thought you lay downe by me. Verily I did dreame, You were some what bold with me; but to come to't. There was a dreame indeed. Come, sir; good fortune tends you; I did tell you I would reueale a secret, Isabella The Duke of Florence sister was impoison'd, By a 'fum'd picture: and Camillo's necke Was broke by damn'd Flamineo; the mischance Laid on a vaulting horse. Most true. I sadly do confesse I had a hand In the blacke deed. Right, For which, vrg'd with contrition, I intend This night to rob Vittoria. To further our escape, I haue entreated Leaue to retire me, till the funerall, Vnto a friend i'th country. That excuse Will further our escape, In coine and iewels I shall, at least, make good vnto your vse An hundred thousand crowns. It is a dowry, Me thinkes, should make that sun-burnt prouerbe false, And wash the Ethiop white. Be ready for our flight. You'le waight about midnight In the Chappel. Looke you; who are yonder. Her Ladiships foolish. 'Tis Flamineo.

Vittoria tells Bracciano of a dream which seems to suggest the murder of Camillo and the Duchess Isabella; but the liaison is forestalled by the intervention of Vittoria’s mother, Cornelia. Flaminio inveighs against Cornelia for compromising his chances of preferment. Isabella comes to Rome to effect a reconciliation with her husband. Cardinal Monticelso and the Great Duke of Florence also put pressure on him to fulfil his responsibilities, both marital and political. In a private interview with Isabella, Bracciano vows never again to sleep with her, but she tries to limit the political damage by making the same vow herself in public. Florence and Monticelso plan to tempt Bracciano into adultery by getting Camillo out of Rome on public business, but Bracciano plots murder: Isabella is killed when she kisses a poisoned picture of Bracciano, and Flaminio breaks Camillo’s neck. Vittoria is arrested and arraigned before an ecclesiastical court; unable to prove her complicity in Camillo’s murder, it convicts her of adultery and commits her to a house of convertites. Flaminio feigns madness to evade interrogation. Lodovico witnesses Isabella’s murder. He returns to Rome illegally to bring Florence the news, but his pardon is signed after the event. He and Flaminio meet and quarrel. Florence borrows from Monticelso a list of villains, in order to select an agent of vengeance. By writing a letter feigning love for Vittoria, he induces Bracciano to contrive her escape from the house of convertites, disguised as a page. Monticelso is elected Pope; his first act is to excommunicate Bracciano and Vittoria. Florence sends Lodovico to Padua to assassinate Bracciano, and by trickery makes it appear that Monticelso supports the conspiracy. Bracciano marries Vittoria and accepts into his court Mulinassar, a Moorish soldier, who is actually Florence in disguise.
