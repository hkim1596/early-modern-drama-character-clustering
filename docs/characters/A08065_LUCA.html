But to throw downe the walls, and Gates of Rome, To make an entrance for an Hobby-horſe; To vaunt toth' people his rediculous ſpoyles; To come with Lawrell, and with Olyues crown'd, For hauing beene the worſt of all the Singers, Is beyond Patience; Pittie, though't doth a kind affection ſhow, (If it end there) our weakeneſſe makes vs know. Why doe we goe to fight in Brittanie? And end our liues vnder an other Sunne? Seeke cauſeleſſe dangers out? The German might Enioy his Woods, and his owne All is drinke, Yet we walke ſafely in the ſtreets of Rome: Bodinca hinders not, but we might liue, Whom, we doe hurt; Them we call enemies, And thoſe our Lords that ſpoyle, and murder vs. I ſee you feare vs Seruinus. I hate him that he is Romes enemie, An enemie to Vertue; fits on high To ſhame the ſeate; And in that hate, my life, And blood, le mingle on the earth with yours. Is he truſtie? I aske not that we meane to neede his truſt. Gaine hath great ſoueragintie ore ſeruile mindes. Piſo, his comming to your houſe at Baiae To bathe, and banquet, will fit meanes afford, amidſt his cups, to end his hated life, Let him die drunke, that nere liu'd ſoberly. What thinke you of Poppea, Tigellinus, And tho'ther odious Inſtruments of Court: Were it not beſt at once to rid them all? By this we may, our ſelues Heauens fauour promiſe, Becauſe all nobleneſſe, and worth on earth We ſee's on our ſide; Here the Faby's ſonne, Here the Coruini are, and take that part; There noble Fathers would, if now they liu'd; There's not a ſoule that claimes Nobilitie Either by his, or his fore-fathers merit, But is with vs; with vs the gallant youth Whom paſſed dangers, or hote bloud makes bould: Staid men ſuſpect their wiſedome, or their faith, To whom our counſels we haue not reueald. And while (our party ſeeking to diſgrace) They traitors call vs, Each man treaſon praiſeth, And hateth faith, when Piſo is a traitor. O may the earth lye lightly on his Courſe, Sprinckle his aſhes with your flowers and teares, The loue and dainties of Mankind is gone.

Plot summary

Plot — Nero (Piso's Conspiracy)
The Emperor Nero celebrates his latest triumph : not a military victory, but success in a singing and acting competition against the Greeks; he also has one of his critics executed. He gives a performance in the role of Orestes; the theatre is guarded so that the audience cannot escape, and those who do not respond with due enthusiasm are marked down for execution. Deciding that he has treated Rome too lenien:tly, he has the city burned down . The wind blows the fire back towards the palace, and his own possessions are destroyed. . Antonius loves _ the Empress Poppaea, despite Petronius' efforts to persuade him to settle for someone of lower rank. Poppaea refuses Antonius, preferring Nymphidius, who has cultivated her affections as a means to make himself Emperor. Scaevinus broaches --a conspiracy to assassinate Nero, thinking it demeaning to have _an Emperor so dedicated to artistic pursuits. Piso is recruited, and the plotters discuss the best way to assassinate Nero; but they are overheard by the freedman Milichus, who reports them to Nero. Piso commits suicide; other conspirators are arrested, and Nero institutes a purge of their sympathizers. Petronius is arrested and executed simply because Tigellinus has -a score to settle with him. A young man is executed merely for pitying Piso's death; Poppaea tries to save him because he resembles a former lover of hers, so Nero kicks her to death in a rage. News arrives from France that Vindex has revolted and chosen · Galba as Emperor. Nymphidius spreads rumours that Nero has resigned, and tries both to curry favour with Galba's agents and to buy military support for his own ambitions. Nero is condemned by the Senate and commits suicide . Nymphidius is killed by Galba's supporters.
