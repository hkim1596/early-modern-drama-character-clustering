Full speech

How may we glory aboue other kings Being (by our birth) descended from the Gods? Our wealth renowned through the world tripartite, Most in the riches of the golden fleece, And not the least of all our happinesse, Medea for her powerfull magicke skill, And Negromanticke exorcismes admir'd, And dreaded through the Colchian territories. And he that striues by purchase of this fleece, To weaken vs, or shake our Royalty, Must tast the fury of these fiery fiends, The nouell: speake. Whose their Generall? Vsher them in, that we may know their quest And what aduenture drew them to these shoares. Princes, you aime at dangers more in proffe Then in report, which if you should behold In their true figure, would amaze your spirits: Yea, terifye the Gods; let me aduise you, As one that knowes their terrour, to desist Ere you enwrap your seffe into these perils, Whence there is no euafion. Hercules. Princes, since you will needs attempt these dangers You shall; and if atchieue the Golden Fleece Transport it where you please, meane time, this day Repose your selues, w … l'e feast you in our Pallace. To morrow morning with the rising Sunne, Our golden prise shall be conseru'd or wonne. Vpon the safeguard of this golden Fleece Colchos depends, and he that beares it hence Beares with it all our fortunes; the Argonautes Haue it in quest, if Lason scape our monsters I'le rather at some banquet poyson him, And quaffe to him his death, or in the night Set fire vpon his Argoe, and in flames Consume the happy hope of his returne, This purpose we, as we are Colchos King, Absyrtus where's your sister? When you next see her giue to her this noate, The manner of our practise, her fell hand Cannot be mist in this, but it shall fall Heauy on these that Colchos seekes to thrall. The howre drawes nigh, the people throng on heapes, To this aduenture in the field of Mars, And noble Iason arm'd with his good … hield, Is vp already and demands the field. Yet valiant Greeke desist, I, though a stranger Pitty thy youth, or if thou wilt persist So dreadfull is the aduenture thou persuest, That thou wilt thinke I shall vnbowell hell, Vnmacle the fiends, and make a passage Free for the Infernals. Discouer them. That practise by your ruins; Ile preuent, And sooner then with that returne to Greece, Your slaughtered bodies leaue with this rich fleece. Enioy it Iason, I admire thy worth, Which as it hath exceeded admiration, So must we needs applaud it. Noble gentlemen. Depart not Colchos, ere your worths and valour We with some rich and worthy gifts present. The conquest of our Buls, and Dragons death, (Though we esteem'd them) yet they sad vs not, Since we behold the safety of this prince. Enter our palace, and your praise sownd hye, Where you shall feast, (or all by treafon dye.) Medea, we are rob'd, despoil'd, dishonored, Our Fleece rap't hence, we must not suffer it, Since all our ominous fortunes it includes, I am resolu'd Iason this night shall dye. A strong guard I will select, and in the dead of night, When they are sunke in Leth … , set vpon them, And kill them in their beds. Iason then dyes, When he most hopes for this rich Colchian prize.

Plot summary

Plot — The Brazen Age (The Age of Brass)
Hercules and the shape-changing Achelous fight for the hand of Dejanira; Achelous transforms himself into a series of monsters, but Hercules retains the upper hand, and Achelous concedes defeat after one of his bull’s horns is pulled off (later to become the cornucopia). Hercules takes Dejanira home, and en route they meet Nessus, the only survivor of Hercules’ massacre of the centaurs. Feigning friendship, the vindictive Nessus offers to carry Dejanira across the river on his back; but when he gets to the other side, he attempts to abduct her. Hercules shoots him and, dying, he tells Dejanira that his blood is a love-charm, which she should use on Hercules if he ever seems interested in another woman; in fact, it is poisonous. Hercules travels to Egypt, where King Busiris sacrifices foreigners, and kills him on his own altar, but refuses to accept his crown. During his absence, Diana, offended that King Oeneus has sacrificed to all the gods but her, sends a boar to ravage the Calydonian countryside. The lords of Greece gather to hunt the boar. One of them is Adonis, and Venus falls in love with him; he insists on hunting, the boar kills him, and she transforms him into a flower. Atalanta joins the hunt, and Meleager falls in love with her; when the boar is killed, he is awarded its head, but he offends his companions by presenting it to her. In the ensuing fight, Meleager kills his uncles. His mother Althea is torn between her duty to her brothers and to her son. Meleager’s life is in her power, for, when he was born, the Fates visited her, pointed out a brand in the fire, and foretold that the child would live until the brand was burnt up; she rescued it and has kept it safe ever since. Now she burns the brand, and he dies; in remorse, she kills herself with his sword. Hercules joins Jason and the Argonauts on their quest to retrieve the golden fleece from Colchis. On the way they stop off at Troy, where King Laomedon faces an unwelcome obligation to sacrifice his daughter Hesione: he accepted a loan from Neptune’s priests to finance building the city walls, but failed to repay it when the work was complete, and retribution came in the form of a sea monster who must be appeased with the monthly sacrifice of a young Trojan woman; this month the fatal lot has fallen on Hesione.
