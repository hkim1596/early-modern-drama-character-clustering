'Tis I, Sir, your Barber. The beſt, the happieſt that euer was, Sir. There has beene a mad Gentleman with your Vncle, this morning, (I thinke this be the Gentleman) that has almoſt talk'd him out of his wits, with threatning him from Marriage— And your Vncle, Sir, he thinkes 'twas done by your procurement; therefore he will ſee the partie, you wot of, preſently: and if hee like her, hee ſayes, and that ſhee be ſo inclining to dombe, as I haue told him, he ſweares, hee will marry her, to day, inſtantly, and not deferre it a minute longer. Paſt imagination, Sir, omnia ſecunda; you could not haue pray'd, to haue had it ſo well: Saltat ſenex, as it is i'the Prouerbe, hee do's triumph in his felicity; admires the party! hee has giuen mee the Leaſe of my Houſe too! and, I am now going for a ſilent Miniſter to marry 'hem, and a way. Cum priuilegio, Sir. And that ſhall be within this halfe houre, vpon my dexteritie, Gentlemen. Contriue what you can, in the meane time, bonis auibus. Hee ſayes, Praeſto, Sir, whenſoeuer your Worſhip needs him, he can be readie with the like. Hee got this cold with ſitting vp late, & ſinging Catches with Cloth-workers. He cannot change it, Sir Cough againe. He will cough out the reſt, Sir. Pleaſe you, good Maſter Parſon. It muſt giue place to poſitiue Diunitie, Sir. Sir, though vnworthy, and the weaker, I will preſume. Your queſtion is, for how many cauſes a man may haue diuortium legitimum, a lawfull Diuorce. Firſt, you muſt vnderſtand the nature of the word Diuorce, a diuertendo — I anſwere then, the Canon-law affords Diuorce but in few caſes, and the principall is in the common caſe, the adulterous caſe. But there are duodecim impedimenta, twelue impediments (as wee call 'hem) all which doe not dirimere contractum, but irritum reddere matrimonium, as wee ſay in the Canon-law, not take away the bond, but cauſe a nullitie therein. The firſt is impedimentum erroris. I, as error perſonae. Then, error fortunae. Then, error qualitatis. Maſter Parſon ſayes right. Nec poſt nuptiarum bene dictionem. It doth indeed but irrita reddere ſponſalia, annull the Contract: after Marriage it is of no obſtancy. The next is conditio: if you thought her free borne, and ſhee prooue a bond-woman, there is impediment of e ſtate and condition. By your fauour, Maſter Parſon— Well then, the third is votum. If either partie haue made a vow of Chaſtitie. But that practice, as Maſter Par ſon ſaid of the other, is taken away among vs, thankes bee to diſcipline. The fourth is cognatio: if the perſons bee of kinne, within the degrees. But, there is a branch of this impediment may, which is cognatio ſpiritualis. If you were her Godfather, Sir, then the Marriage is inceſtuous. The ſift is crimen adulterij: the knowne Caſe. The ſixt, cultus diſparitas, difference of Religion: haue you euer examin'd her what Religion ſhe is of? The ſeuenth is, vis: if it were vpon compulſion, or force. The eighth is, ordo: if euer ſhee haue taken holy Orders. The ninth is, ligamen: if you were bound, Sir, to a ny other before. The tenth is, publica honestas: which is inchoata quae dam affinitas. The eleuenth is, affinitas ex fornicatione. True, quae oritur ex legitimo matrimonio. I conceiue you, Maſter Parſon. Ita per fornicatio nem aeque est verus pater, qui ſic generat — The twelfth, and laſt is, ſi forte coire nequibis. I conceiue you, Sir. But then there will ariſe a doubt, Maſter Parſon, in our Caſe, post Matrimonium: that frigiditate praeditus (doe you conceiue me, Sir?) Who cannot vti vxore pro vxore, may habere cam pro ſorore. You ſhall pardon me, Maſter Parſon, I can proue it. I grant you, but how doe they retractare, Maſter Parſon? That's falſe in Diuinitie, by your fauour. Yes: how if he doe conualere? Or, if he doe ſimulare himſelfe frigidum, odio vxoris, or ſo? I, if he be manifeſte frigidus, I grant you— And mine too. Yes, cauſa frigiditatis — I, diuorty libellum ſhe will ſure haue. Which I would doe, Sir— Becauſe you wanc indeed— No, if the man be frigidus, it is de parte vxoris, that we grant Libellum diuorty, in the Law. But Gentlemen, you haue not knowne her, ſince ma trimonium? Why, then I ſay, for any act before, the matrimo nium is good and perfect: vnleſſe, the worſhipfull Bride groome did preciſely, before witneſſe demand, if ſhee were virgo ante nuptias. If he cannot proue that, it is ratum coniugium, not withſtanding the premiſes. And they doe no way impedire. And this is my Sentence, this I pronounce. In primo graou.

Plot summary

Plot — Epicene, or The Silent Woman
Before the start of the action, the Moorish general Othello visited Brabantio’s home, told of his exotic military life, and wooed Desdemona; now she has run away with him. Iago has been building up Roderigo as a hopeful suitor to Desdemona, whilst pocketing the gifts Roderigo asks him to deliver. He is angry after ===== PAGE 130 ===== Othello has passed him over for promotion in favour of Cassio, who has no practical battlefield experience; so he uses Roderigo to tell Brabantio about the elopement. There is no chance of scoring a direct hit, because Othello is indispensable: there is about to be a war in Cyprus, and he is the only competent commander. When Brabantio complains to the Senate that his daughter has been seduced by magical means, Othello defends himself, is exonerated, and is sent to the war. Since her father will not take her back into his house, Desdemona is allowed to go with him. The Turkish attack is halted by a storm which also delays Othello’s arrival on Cyprus. Believing that both Othello and Cassio have cuckolded him, Iago plans to make Othello believe that Desdemona is having an affair with Cassio. To that end, he gets Cassio drunk during the celebration of the Turkish repulse, and uses Roderigo to provoke a brawl] in which Montano is wounded. Othello cashiers Cassio. Iago advises Cassio that Desdemona may persuade Othello to reinstate him, and. he duly sues to her. Iago then persuades Othello that there is something improper behind Desdemona’s advocacy of Cassio. Desdemona loses her handkerchief, a love-token from Othello. Iago acquires it and puts it in Cassio’s room; upon finding it, Cassio gives it to his girlfriend Bianca to copy the distinctive embroidery before the owner asks for it back. Iago leads Othello to believe that Desdemona has given it to Cassio, and sets up an apparently incriminating conversation with Cassio for Othello to watch. By chance, Bianca angrily returns the handkerchief to Cassio as he is talking to Iago. Othello is convinced in his jealousy, and his thoughts turn to murder. Othello is recalled to Venice and Cassio appointed as his successor on Cyprus. He accuses Desdemona directly, and she denies adultery. Roderigo confronts Tago and demands the return of the misappropriated gifts. Iago advises him that the only way to keep Desdemona in Cyprus is to murder Cassio, so that Othello will have to stay.
