Mine all of Cypres leaues, which are broadest and beautifullest, yet beareth the least fruit; for beautie maketh the brightest shew, being the slightest substance; and I am content to wither before I bee worne, and de priue my selfe of that which so many desire. Alas poore soules, how ill loue founds in their lips, who telling a long tale of hunting, thinke they haue bewray'd a sad passion of loue! Niobe, your affection is but pinned to your tongue, which when you lift you can vnloose ▪ But let vs read what they haue written: Penelope ipsam perstes mode tempore vinces. That is for you Nisa, whome nothing will mooue, yet hope makes him houer. I hope it will be market day till my deathes day. Indeed better to tell stars then be idle, yet bet ter idle then ill employed; Mine this, Sat ihi sifacies, sit bene nota mihi. Some Glasses are true. Let vs sing, and so attend on Ceres ; for this day, although into her heart neuer entred any motion of loue, yet vsually to the Temple of Cupid, hee offereth vo white Doues, as entreating his fauour, and one Eagle, as commanding his power. Praecibus que minaes regaliter addet. Our alas, what hath he done? Ah cruel Erisicthon, that not onely defacest these holy trees, but murtherest also this chast nimph. Montanus, thou art mad, that hauing no breath almost in running so fast, thou wilt yet spend more in spea king so foolishly: yeeld to loue! cannot, or if I doe, to thy loue I will not. If my thoughts were woluish, thy hopes might be as thy comparison is, beastly. I would thy lookes were, as thy affection is, blind. Fresh flowres haue crooked rootes. Mens follies will euer waxe, and then what rea son can make them wise? To bee amorous, and not louely, is like a plea sant foole full of words, and no deserts. No louelier thing then wit, no greater desert then patience. If thou thinke sothy selfe, thou art an excellent foole. I thought, Montanus, that you could not de serue, when I told you what it was, Patience. Then want you wit, that you can be content to be patient. The fortune of loue, and the vertue is neither to haue successe, nor meane. Farewel. I consent, so as Montanus, when in the midst of his sweete delight, shall find some bitter ouerthwarts, im pute it to his folly, in that he suffered me to be a Rose, that hath prickles with her pleasantnes, as hee is like to haue with my loue shrewdnes. I doe.

Plot summary

Plot — Love's Metamorphosis
Three nymphs are loved by three foresters but reject them. The foresters offend Erisychthon, ruler of the forest, by cutting down a tree sacred to Ceres and blaspheming the goddess. The tree is revealed to contain Fidellia, a nymph transformed after being raped by a satyr, and she calls for revenge. Ceres punishes Erisychthon with perpetual hunger, driving him to attempt to sell his daughter Protea for food. Neptune grants Protea the power of metamorphosis, allowing her to escape. Meanwhile, the foresters seek help from Cupid, who punishes the disdainful nymphs through transformations: Nisa becomes a rock, Celia a rose, and Niobe a bird. Protea later saves her lover from a Siren’s temptation. Eventually Cupid and Ceres reverse the punishments, freeing Erisychthon from hunger and restoring the nymphs to their original forms. The nymphs finally accept the love of the foresters.
