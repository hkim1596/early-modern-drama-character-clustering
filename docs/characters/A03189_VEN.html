Leaue those, turne head, chuse those thou maist pur sue Y'are not mine eyes, for they to fee him dead Would from their soft beds drop vpon the earth: Or in their owne warme liquid moisture drowne Their natiue brightnesse: th'art not Venus heart, For wer't thou mine, at this sad spectacle Th'dst breake these ribs though they were made of brasse, And leap out of my bosome instantly. My sorrowes like a populous throng, all striuing At once to passe through some inforced breach, In stead of winning passage stop the way, And so the greatest hast, breeds the most stay. Oh mee! my multiplicity of sorrowes, Makes me almost forget to grieue at all. Speake, speake, my Adon, thou whom death hath fed on Ere thou wast yet full ripe; and this thy beautie's Deuour'd ere tasted. Eye, where's now thy brightnesse? Or hand thy warmth? Oh that such louely parts Should be by death thus made vnseruiceable. That (liuest then) had the power to intrance Ioue: Rauish, amaze, and surfet, all these pleasures Venus hath lost by thy vntimely fall. And therefore for thy death eternally Venus shall mourne; Earth shall thy trunke deuoure, But thy liues bloud I'le turne into a flower, And euery Month in sollemne rights deplore, This beauteous Greeke slaine by Dianaes Boare. Sweet vulcan. No foole, before I did offend with feare, My guilt was but suspected, but not prou'd: And therefore I selected priuacy, Closenesse of place, and bashfully transgrest; But since both Gods and men now know my sinne, Why should I dread to say I loue God Mars? What helpe hast thou in prouing thy wife false? Onely to make me doe with impudence, What I before with feare did, on thy selfe Brought a most certaine shame, where it before Was but suspected. Now fare well iealous foole, for my disgrace, Him whom I loue, I blushlesse thus imbrace, And may all such as would their wiues so take, (Although they might) be seru'd thus for thy sake.

Plot summary

Plot — The Brazen Age (The Age of Brass)
Hercules and the shape-changing Achelous fight for the hand of Dejanira; Achelous transforms himself into a series of monsters, but Hercules retains the upper hand, and Achelous concedes defeat after one of his bull’s horns is pulled off (later to become the cornucopia). Hercules takes Dejanira home, and en route they meet Nessus, the only survivor of Hercules’ massacre of the centaurs. Feigning friendship, the vindictive Nessus offers to carry Dejanira across the river on his back; but when he gets to the other side, he attempts to abduct her. Hercules shoots him and, dying, he tells Dejanira that his blood is a love-charm, which she should use on Hercules if he ever seems interested in another woman; in fact, it is poisonous. Hercules travels to Egypt, where King Busiris sacrifices foreigners, and kills him on his own altar, but refuses to accept his crown. During his absence, Diana, offended that King Oeneus has sacrificed to all the gods but her, sends a boar to ravage the Calydonian countryside. The lords of Greece gather to hunt the boar. One of them is Adonis, and Venus falls in love with him; he insists on hunting, the boar kills him, and she transforms him into a flower. Atalanta joins the hunt, and Meleager falls in love with her; when the boar is killed, he is awarded its head, but he offends his companions by presenting it to her. In the ensuing fight, Meleager kills his uncles. His mother Althea is torn between her duty to her brothers and to her son. Meleager’s life is in her power, for, when he was born, the Fates visited her, pointed out a brand in the fire, and foretold that the child would live until the brand was burnt up; she rescued it and has kept it safe ever since. Now she burns the brand, and he dies; in remorse, she kills herself with his sword. Hercules joins Jason and the Argonauts on their quest to retrieve the golden fleece from Colchis. On the way they stop off at Troy, where King Laomedon faces an unwelcome obligation to sacrifice his daughter Hesione: he accepted a loan from Neptune’s priests to finance building the city walls, but failed to repay it when the work was complete, and retribution came in the form of a sea monster who must be appeased with the monthly sacrifice of a young Trojan woman; this month the fatal lot has fallen on Hesione.
