Full speech

Telusa, Diana bid me hunt you out, & saith that you care not to hunt with her, but if you followe any other Game then she hath rowsd, your punishment shall be to bend all our bowes, and weaue al our strings. Why looke ye so pale, so sad, so wildly. I am no Oedipus to expound riddles, and I muse how thou canst be Sphinx to vtter them. But I pray thee Telusa tell mee what thou aylest, if thou be sicke, this ground hath leaues to heale: if melancholie, heere are pastimes to vse: if peeuish, wit must weane it, or time, or counsell. Yf thou be in loue (for I haue heard of such a beast called loue) it shall be cured, why blushest thou Telusa? I confesse that I am in loue, and yet sweare that I know not what it is. I feele my thoughts vnknit, mine eyes vnstaied, my hart I know not how affected, or infected, my sleepes broken and full of dreames, my wakenesse sad and full of sighes, my selfe in all thinges vnlike my selfe. If this be loue, I woulde it had neuer beene deuised. How did it take you first Telusa? By the eares, whose sweete words suncke so deepe into my head, that the remembrance of his wit, hath bereaued mee of my wisedome, ô eloquent Tyte rus, ô credulous Eurota. But soft heere commeth Ra mia, but let her not heare vs talke, wee will withdrawe our selues, and heare her talke. You shall see Ramia hath also bitten on a loue leafe. Indeede Ramia, if Louers were not vertu ous, then wert thou vicious. Tush Ramia, tis too late to recall it, to re pent it a shame: therfore I pray thee tell what is loue? Nor you Telusa. So doe I, and I will haue him. Talke no more Telusa, your words wound. Ah would I were no woman. We will plague yee for a little God. Make no excuse but to it. Let me see, nowe tis vnpossible to be vn doone. Why laugh you?
