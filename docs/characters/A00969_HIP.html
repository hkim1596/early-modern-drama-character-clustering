Poore Lady, say no more: I had as leife trace this good action with you As that whereto I am going, and never yet Went I so willing, way. My Lord is taken Hart deepe with your distresse: Let him consider: Ile speake anon. Though much unlike You should be so transported, as much sorry I should be such a Suitour; yet I thinke Did I not by th'abstayning of my joy Which breeds a deeper longing, cure their surfeit That craves a present medcine, I should plucke All Ladies scandall on me. Therefore Sir As I shall here make tryall of my prayres, Either presuming them to have some force, Or sentencing for ay their vigour dombe, Prorogue this busines, we are going about, and hang Your Sheild afore your Heart, about that necke Which is my 〈◊〉 , and which I freely lend To doe these poore Queenes service. Sir farewell; repeat my wishes To our great Lord, of whose succes I dare not Make any timerous question, yet I wish him Exces, and overflow of power, and't might be To dure ill-dealing fortune; speede to him, Store never hurtes good Gouernours. In's bosome: We have bin Soldiers, and wee cannot weepe When our Friends don their helmes, or put to sea, Or tell of Babe, broachd on the Launce, or wom … n That have sod their Infants in (and after eate them) Tho brine, they wept at killing 'em; Then if You stay to see of us such Spinesters, we Should hold you here for e ver. With much labour: And I did love him fort, they two have Cabind In many as dangerous, as poo … e a Corner, Perill and want contending, they have skift Torrents whose roring tyranny and power I'th least of these was dreadfull, and they have Fought out together, where Deaths-selfe was lodgd, Yet fate hath brought them off: Their knot of love Tide, weau'd, intangled, with so true, so long, And with a finger of so deepe a cunning May be out worne, never undone, I thinke Theseus cannot be umpire to himselfe Cleaving his conscience into twaine, and doing Each side like Iustice, which he loves best. Twas Flauia. Y'are ont of breath And this high speeded-pace, is but to say That you shall never (like the Maide Flavina ) Love any that's calld Man. Now alacke weake Sister, I must no more beleeve thee in this point (Though, in't I know thou dost beleeve thy selfe,) Then I will trust a sickely appetite, That loathés even as it longs, but sure my Sister If I were ripe for your perswasion, you Have saide enough to shake me from the Arme Of the all noble Theseus, for whose fortunes, I will now in, and kneele with great assurance, That we, more then his Pirotho … , possesse The high throne in his heart. I admire him, I have not seene so yong a man, so noble (If he say true,) of his sort. Never so pleasd Sir. By that faith, That faire hand, and that honest heart you gave me. By valour, By all the chaste nights I have ever pleasd you. By your owne eyes: By strength In which you swore I went beyond all women, Almost all men, and yet I yeelded Theseus. Next heare my prayers. Mercy. What will become of 'em? I wish it, But not the cause my Lord; They would show Bravely about the Titles of two Kingdomes; Tis pitty Love should be so tyrannous: O my soft harted Sister, what thinke you? Weepe not, till they weepe blood; Wench it must be. You must goe. Farewell Sister, I am like to know your husband fore your selfe By some small start of time, he whom the gods Doe of the two know best, I pray them he Be made your Lot. Plot summary

Plot — The Two Noble Kinsmen
Theseus’ wedding to Hippolyta is interrupted by three widowed queens, whose husbands have fallen in battle against Thebes. Creon, the victor, has denied them the right to bury their dead, so they request Theseus’ help in forcing his hand. At Hippolyta’s request, Theseus postpones the wedding feast and leads an army against Thebes. It is a bad moment for the Theban princes Palamon and Arcite: they are on the point of leaving Creon’s corrupt court, but now feel a patriotic duty to defend their city. Theseus wins the war, Palamon and Arcite are taken prisoner, and the dead kings are buried. Palamon and Arcite are imprisoned in Athens. Their cell overlooks a garden where Emilia walks; they both fall in love with her and quarrel jealously. Arcite is released, on condition that he never returns to Athens, but he stays in disguise and performs with distinction at a. sporting festival for Emilia’s birthday. Meanwhile Palamon is moved to a cell without a garden view. The jailer’s daughter falls in love with him and, aware that they are of markedly different rank, seeks to win his regard by engineering his escape. Palamon meets Arcite in the woods the following morning; Arcite agrees to bring him food, clothing, and weapons so that they can fight out their quarrel. In consequence, Palamon fails to turn up at his appointment with the jailer’s daughter, and she is driven mad by a combination of disappointed love and fear that her father will be blamed for the escape. The fight between Palamon and Arcite is interrupted by Theseus. Though one has broken banishment and the other broken prison, Hippolyta and Emilia beg that their lives be spared, but the kinsmen will not consent to Emilia’s proposal that they should be banished from Athens and_.swear never again to see either her or one another. Theseus proposes to execute only one of them if Emilia will agree to marry the other, but she cannot decide between them. Theseus resolves on a trial by combat, with each kinsman supported by three knights: the winner will get Emilia, and the loser will die. Palamon exonerates the jailer concerning his escape and gives his daughter a dowry. A doctor treats her by dressing her fiancé as Palamon and encouraging him to play up to her, even if it entails pre-marital sex. It does, and she is cured.
