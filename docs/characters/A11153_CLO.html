Then you doe wrong, Sir, for you take mony for 'm: What woman can have a husband, but you must Have custome for him? and often the ware proves Naught too not worth the Impost. Nay, at the second hand you'l have a fee too; You sell in the Church, and they bring 'm againe To your Church-yarde, you must have tollage: Me thinkes if a man dye whether you will or no, He should be buryed whether you would or no. Mistris make him your friend, for he knowes what rate Good husbands are at; if there hath bin a dearth Of women of late, you may chance picke Out a good prize; but take heede of a Clerke. Mistris, 'tis fasting day to day, there's nothing but fish. Marry there is Sammon, Pike, and fresh Cod, Soles, Maides, and Playce. Nay mistris, I'le helpe 'm too; the maides shall first Dresse the Pike, and the Cod, and then I'le dresse The maides in the place you wot on. You have had two husbands to my knowledge; And if you had not one joyfull houre betweene Both, I would you were hang'd i'faith. That was a tedious time indeede. I, curst widdowes are; but if they had all stiffe husbands To tame'm, they'd be quiet enough. I care not if I doe mistris, now my stomack's ready; Yet Ile stay a little and be but to vex you. I will not goe yet. Nay then Ile goe to vex you. Nay Sir, you doe wrong to give a woman so many lies, Shee had rather have had twice so many standings, than One lye. Little lesse I promise you, if you lye with him. You may talke with me Sir, in the meane time. I doe forbid the Banes: Alas poore Shagragge, My Mistris does but gull him; you may Imagine it to be T welfe-day at night, and the Beane found in the corner of your Cake, but Tis not worth a fetch I'l assure you. You love to be merry Mistris; Come, come, ve him foure Farthings, and let him goe. He'l pray for his good Dame, and be drunke; Why, if your blood does itch that way, we'l Stand together; how thinke you? I thinke here 〈◊〉 the sweeter bit, you see this Nap, and you See this Lowse, you may cracke o' your choyse, If you choose here. I will make blacke Munday of this: e'r I suffer This disgrace, the kennell shall run with blood and rags, I have nothing to say to you, Sir; I ayme at your Vncle That's all one, I'l take him as I finde him: I'l make you seeke your fingers among the Dogs, If you come to me; my Fellow? You lowsie Companion; I scorne thee. S'foote, is't come to this? Have I stood all this while to my Mistris, an honest, Hansome, plaine-dealing, serving-Creature; and she to Marry a Worson Tittere Tu Tattere with never a good rag About him? Stand thou to me, and be my friend; and since My Mistris has forsaken me: 'Twas well you came in good time. I was goingthe wrong way. Do'st not thou know the reason of that? why, 'twas To save my belly: dost thou thinke I am so mad to Cast my selfe away for e'r a woman of'm all, I'l see 'm hang'd first. Well, since there is no remedy, Oh teares bee you my friend! I cannot choose; nay I will steepe Mine eyes in crying teares, and crying weepe. The Good man of the house is within forsooth. 'Tis hard of digestion: Yes, my Master is within; Hee masters you therefore I must be Content: You have long'd for Crosses a good While, and now you are like to be Farther off them than e'r you were; For I'm affraid, your good husband will leave You ne'r a crosse i'th' house to blesse you with. Where he has mistaken the place a little, Being his wedding-day; he is in nomine, When he should be in re. In your Counting-house; If he were a kinde Husband, he would have bin in another Counting-house by this time: hee's tumbling Over all his money bags yonder; you shall Heare of him in the bowling Alley againe. Looke where he comes. Now you may see what hastie matching is; You had thought to have bin vext, and now You cannot: You have marryed a husband, That, Sir reverence of the title, now being my Master in law, I doe thinke hee'l proove the miserablest, covetous Rascall, that ever beate beggar from his gate. But 'Tis no matter; time was when you were fairely Offered, if you would have tooke it; you might have had Other matches y'faith, if it had pleas'd you; and those That would have crost you; I would have sold away All that ever you had had; have kept two or three Whores at liverie under your nose; have turn'd you out In your smocke, and have us'd you like a woman; where As now, if you'd hang your selfe, you can have none of These blessings: but 'tis well enough, now you must Take what followes. And they'd be loth to keepe their beds i'th'counter Mistris; they are affraid of Serjeants, Master Lambskin, Knowes that Mace is a binder. Plot — A New Wonder, A Woman Never Vexed
Old Forster has recently made his fortune by marrying a rich widow, but they are both dissatisfied with the financial irresponsibility of his brother and son, whom ===== PAGE 17 ===== 1624 they are on the point of disowning. Robert, the son, has purloined some of his stepmother’s money to buy his uncle, Stephen Forster, out of debtors’ prison; he also gives Stephen money to buy new clothes. Stephen uses the money for gambling, and is cheated. Robert is disappointed to find him in the gaming-house, and he himself likewise gets into trouble with his stepmother for going there, even though he only went in to look for Stephen. Mistress Forster has an uncommonly even- tempered friend, another rich widow, who enjoys miraculous good fortune. When she loses her wedding ring in the river, it turns up inside a salmon bought by her maid. She meets Stephen at the gaming-house and likes him. She suggests that he should marry for money, and offers herself. He accepts, and they perform a hand-fasting. Jane is being pursued by two undesirable suitors, an old knight and a young fool. Her father, Alderman Brune, suggests Robert Forster as a third option, if he can be reconciled with his father. Brune is Old Forster’s partner in a mercantile venture, but accepts Forster’s offer to buy him out; he takes payment in cloth rather than cash, which clears Forster’s warehouses for the new merchandise due in any day. However, Forster continues to look askance at Robert. News arrives that the ships have sunk in the Thames: Forster is bankrupt, and voluntarily commits himself to prison in order to avoid his creditors. Stephen takes charge of his new wife’s property, and proves, against expectations, to be provident rather than spendthrift. He asks Robert to help him manage the estate, and adopts him as his heir, insisting only that he does nothing to help his father. However, Stephen himself pays the prison-keeper to ensure that his brother remains comfortable. When Jane’s two other suitors complain of the favour shown to Robert, Stephen produces evidence that they are in debt to the widow’s first husband (and therefore now to him), and the threat of arrest gets rid of them. The widow generously accepts their payment of £200 to discharge a debt of ten times that amount. Unable to resist his sense of filial obligation, Robert purloins the money and gives it to his father. Stephen pretends to be angry, but secretly pays off his brother’s creditors and thereby secures his release.…
