Full speech

I long haue mist those honourable warres, Which warlike Rome against the Bryttaines hold: But since we heare, and that by true report, And credible intelligence from many, Who lately haue returned from the Campe, That Wales and Rome begin fresh bleeding war, I doe intend with speed to see the Army, And pay my loue, as tribute vnto Rome. But yet I grieue, that such intestine iarre Is falne betwixt such an heroike Prince, As is the King of Wales, and powerfull Rome. The Romanes doe in multitudes exceede, He, well instructed in true fortitude, A Graduate in Martiall discipline, And needs no Tutour: for in pupill age He was brought vp in honours rudiments, And learnde the elements of warlike Arts. Then much I muse, why Cesar should beginne, That scarce hath ended with the Bryttish warres; Or who's the Author of these firebrands Dissention thus hath kindled. Come, souldiour, prithee let me see: I long to heare from noble Caradoc. He reades it. Souldiour, come hither, where is Caradoc ? Souldiour, I wish, if wishes could preuayle, Thy princely Master were with vs awhile, Till all these cloudes of blacke contention Were eyther ouerblowne, or else dissolued. Fame hath not left a man, more fit for talke Or disputation in bright honours scholes, Then is thy noble Master. When I behold His noble portrayture but in conceit, Me thinks, I see the reall thing it selfe Of perfite Honour and Nobility, And not fantastically apprehend Onely the ayry fictions of the brayne. I now repent, that thus long I haue spent My honour and my time, in ayding Rome, And thus far haue digrest from Natures lawes, To ayde a forrayne Nation 'gainst mine owne. Were but thy Master here, he soone should see, He hath his wish, and Wales her liberty. Braue Prince, as welcome to Venusius, As sleepe to wearied Nature. But now the time Fits not for friuolous complements. Awhile Repose your selfe with me, where you shall be As secret, as men would keepe their sinnes From the worlds eye, whilest in the meane time, I Prepare my forces. Wife, view this noble Prince: This is that man, that, in despite of Rome, This nine yeares space hath brauely waged warre, And now by Treason's forst vnto his friends. Then, wife, as thou doest tender our regard, Respect this Prince, and keepe him priuately, Vntill I doe returne. Farewell, noble Prince. Exit. Thus, noble Lords, Venusius armed comes, In loue to Wales, and that much wronged Prince, Who now at Yorke, liues priuate from his foes, From whence we now will call him, and awake His ancient courage, that long time hath slept, Vpon the downy pillowes of repose. Good Angels, guide vs: this our latest strife Shall set a period to our death or life.

Plot summary

Plot — The Valiant Welshman (Caradoc the Great)
The Earl of. Monmouth seeks to usurp old King Octavian’s throne, and has won two significant victories over him. Cadallan and his sons come to support Octavian. Cadallan is killed in the ensuing battle, but his son Caradoc avenges him by killing Monmouth. Re-established on the throne, Octavian rewards Caradoc with marriage to his daughter and heir, Guinevere. This displeases his bastard son, Codigune, who had regarded Guinevere as his only obstacle to the succession. King Guiderius has failed to pay tribute to the Romans, so the Emperor Claudius invades Britain. Guiderius appeals to Octavian, and Caradoc is sent, fresh from his wedding, to offer military aid. In his absence, Codigune conspires with Gloucester and Cornwall: they poison Octavian and imprison Guinevere, and Codigune seizes power. Gloucester writes warning Guiderius not to trust Caradoc, so Guiderius, about to go into battle, orders the Welsh troops to observe from a hilltop and intervene only ifa British defeat seems likely. Caradoc disregards this, joins the battle in disguise, and bests Claudius in hand- to-hand combat; he refuses Claudius’ offer to buy his freedom, but accepts a token of the Emperor’s friendship, a golden lion. Prince Gald is impressed by Caradoc’s single-handed contribution to the British victory, accompanies him back to Wales, and marries his sister Voada. Finding Codigune installed as King of Wales, Caradoc offers to settle matters in single combat. Codigune is beaten, refuses an offer to make him Viceroy of North Wales, and flees to the Romans. Cornwall submits to Caradoc, who is crowned King. Gloucester flees to make more mischief: he persuades a witch to create a ravaging monster which Caradoc will be expected to fight, and which will kill him. The Romans send Caradoc an ultimatum, requiring him to resign the throne to Codigune; at the same time, he is summoned to deal with the monster, a giant serpent. He gives priority to the serpent, and defeats it with the help of a magic herb, then disposes of the witch and forces her son Bluso to swear that in future he will use his magic powers only for good. Gloucester hangs himself. While Caradoc is away, Cornwall hands over the town of Gloucester to the Romans. Gallicus, the Roman legate who brought the ultimatum, falls in love with Voada and abducts her. Bluso enables Gald to visit her bedchamber, magically unseen.
