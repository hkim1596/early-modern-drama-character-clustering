Nay, but Accutus, prethee what miſ-ſhapen vizard of Melancholly haſt thou mask't thy ſelfe in? thou lookſt as thou wer't changing thy religion: what? is there a breach in thy Faith? come, declare, and let me ſet thy wits on worke, to amend it. Then make me of thy counſell, and take my ad uice, for ile take no denyall, Ile not leaue thee til the next new Almanackes be out of date: let him threaten the ſhar peſt weather he can, in Saint Swithin week, or it ſnow on our Ladies face, ile not budge, ile be thy mid-wife til thou beeſt deliuered of this paſſion. I was ſure thou wert beyond the Antipodes: faith I am of that faith I was brought vp in, I haue heard my Fa ther ſay, and i'me ſure his Recordes came from his Father, that the Land and Sea are in nature thus much alike; the owne growes by the Sunne, the other by the Moone, both by Gods bleſſing; and the Sea rather the greater, and ſo thinke I. Nay, that's moſt certaine, I haue heard of Sea-hor ſes, Sea-calues, and Sea-monſters. Come, thou art Sea-ſicke, and will not be well at eaſe til thou haſt tane a vomit, vp with't. Prethee lets away. Sirra, ſtay, ile combat thee in his defence. They are good ſubiects for idle houres: but ſoft what ſecond courſe is entring heere? I marrie Accutus how likſt thou this Gentlewo man Gallant? I, and if my Ladie Argentile were dead, he wold rather liue vppon almes then fall to worke, In any caſe, but in ſome milde imbrace, for if we ſhould continue thus rough, we ſhould be ſhund like an Appoplex. Fye, without ceremony; lets yoake this triplicity as we did in the daies of olde, with mirth and melody. But the other walkes a looſe. The viſitation of your hand ſir. What kinde is your Dogge of Sir? What countrie? Verie tollerably. Follow it, away. Exeunt. Ile ſow it I warrant thee, thou talkſt of burſſe, I haue away worth ten on't, ile firſt giue it out in my Barbers ſhop, then at my ordinarie, and that's as good as a broad: and as I croſſe Tiber, my waterman ſhall attach it, heele ſend it away with the tide, then let it come out to an Oy ſter wenches eare, and ſheele crie it vp and downe the ſtreetes. Ile warrant thee ile manage it with as good iudgement as a Conſtable his charge. Better I hope: well about it. Exit. May I craue a word of you mine Hoſt? But prethee let me partake. Fortune my foe, why dooſt, &c. Drunk & his cloathes ſtoln, what theef wold do it? Wel Bos, to obtaine a ſuite at my handes, and to doe ſome pennance for your fault, you ſhal here maintaine an argument in the defence of drunkennes: mine Hoſt ſhall heare it ile be your oppoment, Acutus moderator: wilt thou doe it. Your proofe. Let him prooue that and Ile yeeld. Oh vrge not that I pray ye. Oh of neceſſitie, if it be that ſhow. I haue wrought the foole, Scilicet comes alone, & his Lady keeps the women company. Plot — Every Woman in Her Humor
Misogyny has made the normally jovial Acutus.- melancholy. After scaring some foolishly fashionable characters, he joins them at the Hobby tavern, where Gaetica has taken lodgings with her dog. Disguised as a © soldier, Acutus picks a fight with Scilicet and Servulus, © then exposes their hypocrisy by returning disguised as first a friend and then an enemy of the ‘soldier’, and eliciting inconsistent responses. Scilicet attempts to woo Gaetica; she is more concerned about the disappearance of her man Bos with her dog, but becomes betrothed to him. , Lentulus is in love, and asks Cicero to woo Terentia for him. They go to dine with Flaminius, Terentia’s father, and Cicero is left alone with the ladies of the house. The upshot is that Terentia falls in love — but -- with Cicero himself rather than Lentulus. Rumours circulate that she is to marry Cicero, but Flaminius has not given his consent and takes offence. Lentulus makes Cicero and Terentia admit wronging him, and. _ he sentences Cicero to death — but only if Terentia will kill him. He woos and wins Terentia’s sister Flavia, and plans a double wedding. Acutus gives Philautus a sleeping potion, and he appears to die. He is rumoured to have drunk himself to death, but the reported numbers of alcohol victims escalate as the news circulates. The Hostess allows him to hide in her chamber, but Acutus, pretending to have been robbed, sends a constable to search the tavern; Philautus is found and arrested. The drunken Bos has his clothes stolen, and is brought back to the tavern in a barrel. Gracchus agrees to give him a new suit if he can demonstrate that drunkenness is a virtue, which he proceeds to do in a debate. Gracchus promises to arrange for the Hostess and Dama to attend the wedding. At the wedding, Caesar declares a general amnesty; Acutus and Gracchus have Lentulus intercede to procure Philautus’ release.
