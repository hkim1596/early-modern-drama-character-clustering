Oh sir, that cannot make me iealous. From whom saiest thou? And whats your businesse? What would she haue me doe in this? Where lyes this Land? Is she then married? why stirres not her Husband in it? Let her in writing draw the cause at large: And I will moue the Duke. Old man, Ile pleade for her, but take no fees: Giue Lawyers them, I swim not in that flood, Ile touch no gold, till I haue done her good. My Husbands name, and hand and seale at armes to a Loue-letter? Where hadst thou this writing? My Lord turnd Ranger now? This very purse was wouen with mine owne hands, This Diamond on that very night, when he Vntyed my Virgin girdle, gaue I him: And must a common Harlot share in mine? O d man, to quit thy paines, take thou the gold. Cupid himselfe was sure his Secretary, These nes are euen the Arrowes Loue let flies, The very Incke dropt out of Uenus eyes. Here's honied poyson, to me he ne'r thus writ, But Lust can set a double edge on wit. Oathes, promises, preferments, Iewels, gold, What snares should breake, if all these cannot hold? What creature is thy Mistris? What manner of woman? They are deliuered better: Why should she send backe these things? Strumpets like cheating gamesters will not win At first: these are but baites to draw him in. How might I learne his hunting houres? Knowest thou what other gifts haue past betweene them? Him Ise examine presently. Be gon then, & what lyes in me command. Come hither sirra, how much cost those Satins, and cloth of Siluer, which my husband sent by you to a low Gentlewoman yonder? She there, to whom you carried letters. Did your Lord neuer send you with a Ring, sir, set with a Diamond? Are you so close, you Bawd, you pandring slaue? Out of my sight, base varlet, get thee gone. If you'll needs know, it was about the clocke: how workes the day, my Lord, (pray) by your watch? How, two? I am scarce at one. Then sure 'tis you, Mine goes by heauens Diall, (the Sunne) and it goes true. Set it to mine (at one) then. Faith then belike, Neither your clocke nor mine does truely strike, And since it is vncertaine which goes true, Better be false at one, then false at two. Yet not merry. Nothing my Lord, but my false watch, pray tell me, You see, my clocke, or yours is out of frame, Must we vpon the Workeman lay the blame, Or on your selues that keepe them? All is not well indeed, my dearest Lord, Locke vp thy gates of hearing, that no sound Of what I speake ma enter. Or if my owne tongue must my selfe betray, Count it a dreame, or turne thine eyes away, And thinke me not thy wife. Earth is sinnes cushion: when the sicke soule feeles her selfe growing poore, then she turnes begger, cryes and kneeles for helpe; Hipollito (for husband I dare not call thee) I haue slolne that Iewell of my chaste honour (which was onely thine) and giuen it to a slaue. On thy pillow adultery & lust haue sl pt, thy Groome Hath climbed the vnlawfull tree, and pluckt the sweets, A villaine hath vsurped a husbands sheetes. This Irish Footman. I beg but this, Set not my shame out to the worlds broad eye, Yet let thy vengeance (like my fault) soare hye, So it be in darkned clowdes. Hipollito? The slaue did woo me. 'Twas thus, he gaue me this battery first. Oh I Mistake, beleeue me, all this in beaten gold: Yet I held out, but at length this was charm'd. What? change your Diamond wench, the act is base, Common, but foule, so shall not your disgrace: Could not I feed your appetite? Oh Men, You were created Augels, pure and faire, But since the first fell, worse then Deuils you are. You should our shields be, but you proue our rods. Were there no Men, Women might liue like gods. Guilty my Lord? Nay, you may laugh, but henceforth shun my bed, With no whores leauings Ile be poysoned. Here, sir. 'Tis a good honest seruant, that old man. And it may be my husband, Because when once this woman was vnmaskt, He leueld all her thoughts, and made them fit: Now he'd marre all agen, to try his wit. Let me be gone, my Lord, or stand vnseene; 'Tis rare when a Iudge strikes, and that none dye, And 'tis vnfit then, women should be by. Pray doe so. Plot summary

Plot — 2 The Honest Whore (2 The Converted Courtesan)
On his way to court, Hippolito receives petitioners, including the reformed courtesan Bellafront: she wants his help in petitioning the Duke to pardon her husband Matheo, who has been condemned to death for homicide. Hippolito solicits the help of Bellafront’s father Orlando, but he is still morally outraged at her former sexual misconduct. Nevertheless he secretly arranges for Matheo’s release, and enters his household disguised as a servingman, Pacheco. Matheo proves dissolute, and when ‘Pacheco’ gives him money for safe keeping, he gambles it all away. Bellafront is trying not to let poverty and Matheo’s prodigality force her back into prostitution. Hippolito, who converted her from whoredom, now tries to seduce her back again. She sends ‘Pacheco’ to return his love-tokens, but ‘Pacheco’ instead takes them to Infelice, who identifies them as her own gifts to Hippolito. She interrogates Hippolito’s go-between, Brian the footman, then tells her husband that Brian gave her the love-tokens and had an affair with her. Brian is dismissed and goes home to Ireland. Desperate for money, Matheo strips the gown from his wife’s back and sells it. Lodovico promises to furnish him as a gentleman, so he is properly dressed when Orlando visits in his own person. The old man feigns lack of sympathy, then returns as Pacheco in time to prevent Matheo assaulting Bellafront. Prompted by Orlando, the Duke takes a hand: he issues a warrant to arrest Matheo for robbing two pedlars (who were actually Orlando’s men); and, hearing that Hippolito is in love with a whore, but not knowing her identity, passes new anti-prostitution laws. Meanwhile, Candido is married a second time, but his bride tests his patience with unruly behaviour on their wedding day. He determines to tame her, and Lodovico helps in the guise of an apprentice; she submits herself after a mock duel with measuring sticks. Some gallants attempt to seduce her into a brothel, but fail, and Candido still keeps his temper when, drawn to an alehouse for a business deal, he is forced to drink to a bawd’s health. Matheo is arrested at the alehouse on the Duke’s warrant, and Candido too for receiving the cloth stolen from the pedlars. At Bridewell, Matheo admits the robbery, but attempts to incriminate Bellafront out of jealousy for her supposed relationship with Hippolito.
