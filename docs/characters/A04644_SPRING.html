Full speech

It is already done, in flowers As fresh and new as are the howres, By warmth of yonder Sunne. But will be multipli'd on vs, If from the breath of ZEPHYRVS Like fauour we haue wonne. Beloued of the Spring! That wisest Nature cause can call Of quick'ning any thing. At which, Zephyrus passeth away through the ayre, and the Spring descendeth to the Earth: and is receiu'd by the Naiades, or Napeae; who are the Nymph's, Fountaynes, and Seruants of the season— Fresh Fountaynes, I am come to tell A tale in yond'soft eare, Whereof the murmure will do well: If you your parts will beare. Goe vp with me, then: helpe to sing The story to the King. Here the Spring goes vp, singing the argument to the King; and the Fountaynes follow with the close. Cupid hath ta'ne offence of late At all the Gods, that of the State, And in their Councell, he was so deserted, Not to be call'd into their Guild But slightly passd by, as a child. And though his Mother seeke to season, And rectifie his rage with reason, By shewing he liues yet vnder her command, Rebellious he, doth disobey, And she hath forc'd his armes away. Whereat the Boy, in fury fell, With all his speed, is gone to hell, There to excite, and stirre vp lealousy, To make a party 'gainst the Gods, And set Heauen, Earth, and Hell at odds. She that led, a Queene was at least, Or a Goddesse, 'boue the rest: And all their graces, in her selfe expres't! Run out, all the Flouds, in ioy with your siluer feete; And hast to meete, the enamour'd Spring; For whom the warbling Fountaynes sing: The story of the flowers; preserued by the Howers; At Iuno's soft command, and Iris showers; Sent to quench iealousie, and all those powers Of Loues rebellious warre: Whil'st Chloris sits a shining starre To crowne, and grace our iolly song, made long, To the notes, that we bring, to glad the Spring. Which ended, the Goddesse, and her Nymphs de scend the degrees, into the roome, and dance the entry of the grand-masque.

Plot summary

Plot — Chloridia: Rites to Chloris and Her Nymphs
The gods have decreed that heaven and earth shall love one another; in order to facilitate this, earth as well as heaven is to have stars, which will be spring flowers. Cupid, offended at being excluded from the gods’ council on account of his minority, travels to Hell to make trouble. There his arrival warrants a holiday: the damned rejoice, freed from their perpetual torments, while Cupid releases troublesome goblins and sends a messenger to attack Spring with bad weather. Tempest ensues. Juno calms the storm and brings the spring: Chloris is revealed in her bower with her nymphs, and dancing ensues. The disruptive forces flee back to Hell, and Cupid sues for pardon. Fame ascends to heaven with attendants representing the collaboration of the verbal and visual arts, leaving Chloris on earth as the guardian of spring. Further dancing ensues. 1631
