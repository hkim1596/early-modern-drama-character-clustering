Full speech

Yes Sir, tis for the great Marriage twixt I am appointed, Heere to attend the comming of the Brides, Old Sir Iohn Worldyes Daughters. The middlemost weares willow for his sake, The youngest marries the rich Merchant Strange. Now God foresend, the reason Sir I pray? Sir, Ile take no notice Of things, I do not know the iniur'd Gentleman, May bring em after inro the Spirituall Court, and haue a faire pull on't, a poore Gentleman, (For so I take him by his being deceiu'd) Gainst a great Count, and an old wealthy Knight. Alas my deere friend. Vpon what acquaintance Sir? They are great persons, and I meane to rise, I hope in time to haue three liuings man, and this were not the way I take it Sir. Oh by no meanes. By your leaue. Sir Iohn I thanke you, and my honor'd Lord: But I am sorrie for this other Newes Concerning Mistris Kate, and my good friend. You can play well my Lord. Is he good at the exercise of drinking Sir? I would he heard him. Oh sie no, none so good as my Lord. Introth you were too blame to venter so, Mischiefes finde vs, we need not mischiefes seeke; I am not ti'de to that opinion, They are like women, which do alwaies shun Their louers and pursuers, and do follow With most ranke appetites them that do flye: All mischiefe that I had is but one woman, and that one woman all mischance to me, Who speaks worst of them, then's the best of men, They are like shaddowes, mischiefes are like them. Death feares me, for introth I seeke him out; The Sun is stale to me, to morrow Morne as this, t'will rise, I see no difference; The night doth visit me, but in one roabe, She brings as many thoughts as she weares Starres When she is pleasant, but no rest at all, For what new strange thing should I couet life then? Is not she false, whom onely I thought true? Shall time to shew his strength make Scudmore liue. Till (perish the vicious thought) I loue not thee, Or thou deere friend, remoue thy heart from me. Time is as weake for that, as he is old, Take comfort, and attend this counsell friend, This match is neither Sacred nor sure, Close Fate annihilates what Opinion makes, and since she is resolu'd this night to die, If you do not redeeme her, giue the meanes, Or her bloud (credit me) will spring heauier greefes, Sorer and stranger in thy oppressed hart Then her false loue before. Besides, tis you My Scudmore that are false, if you will not Consent to let her make Vowes good, which were But in a possibility to be broke, This her Repentance casts her vice quite off: and if you leaue her now, you take it on, Nay you incurre a bloody mortall sinne, You do become an actuall murtherer. If you neglect her, she will kill her selfe This night, by poyson, knife, or other meanes, God giues you power to crosse her desperate will, and if you saue not where you may, you kill. How well does Scudmore speake, ill for himselfe, Wit's a disease, that fit employment wants, Therefore we see, those happiest in best parts, and vnder-borne fortunes vnder their merrits, Grow to a sullen enuie, hate, and scorne Of their Superiors; and at last, like winds Breake forth into rebellious ciuil warres, Or priuate Treasons; none so apt for these, as melancholy wits fetter'd with neede. How free's the Rusticke Swaine from these assaults, He neuer feeles a passion all his life, But when he cannot sleepe, or hunger gripes; And though he want Reason, Wit, Art, nay Sence, Is not so sencelesse to capitulate, and aske God why he made not him as great As that same foolish Lord, or that rich knaue: His braines with nothing does Negotiate, But his hard Husbandry, which makes him liue. But haue we worthy gifts, as Iudgement, Learning, Ingenious sharpnesse, which wise God indeed, Doth seldome giue out of his equall hand, But ioyn'd with pouertie to make it euen With Riches which he clogs with ignorance, We vent our blessing in prophane conceits, Or in strong Arguments against our selues, Foule Bawdry, and starke blindly hold it best, Rather to loose a soule, then loose a iest. Soule man, leaue eating now, looke, looke, you haue all dropt a yoursute. All readie, readie, onely we tarrie for our Vizards & our Caps, I put em to a knaue to doing, because I would haue em the better done. Your wit is most actiue, I cal'd him knaue in re gard of his long stay Sir, not his worke. A verie Diuels face, I feare nothing but that it wil fright the weomen. Fitted at all points. Right, tis agree'd, I (leading of the Maske,) Should dance with Bellafront. The rest That followes, is digested in my breast. I forbid any man to mend em Sir, 'good night vnto your worship. Yes. Who married her, or would haue caus'd hir marry To any man but this, no better was, Let circumstances be examined, Yet heere's one more, and now I hope you all, Perceiue my marrying not Cannon call. So Mistris Kate, I kept you for my self.

Plot summary

Plot — A Woman Is a Weathercock
Scudmore is in love with Bellafront, and his friend Nevill persuades him, with difficulty, to show the love- letter he has received from her. There is a slight complication: Nevill is on his way to .Bellafront’s wedding. She is to marry Count Frederick that morning. The whole family is mired in matrimonial competition. Bellafront’s sister Lucida also loves Frederick, and has sworn never to marry for his sake; but she is being courted by the foolish Sir Abraham Ninny, who has got himself knighted expressly to make himself a more eligible son-in-law. The youngest daughter, Katherine, is also to be married that morning, to Strange the merchant, but she too has another admirer, Captain Pouts. Unbeknown to Scudmore, Nevill impersonates the parson who is to officiate at the wedding. Scudmore tries to catch Bellafront at the church door, but she refuses him; this convinces him that all women are perfidious. After the wedding, Pouts publicly declares that Katherine has slept with him on many previous occasions. Strange seems reluctant to seek legal redress for this slander, but only because he doesn’t want anyone to stop him secretly taking a more immediate revenge. He tries to see Pouts at his lodging, but Pouts assumes he has come to serve a writ and will not let him in. Pouts then prepares to flee England and his creditors, but Strange intercepts him disguised as a soldier, claiming that Katherine engaged him to murder her new husband and that she loves only Pouts. Pouts admits that he slandered her; Strange wounds him in a fight and forces him to sign a document affirming her innocence. By a pretext, Scudmore gets in to see Bellafront, rebukes her, and returns her love-letters. She tells him that her father forced her to marry Frederick, and that she will kill herself rather than consummate the marriage. He mistrusts her, but Nevill arranges for him to participate in a wedding masque that evening. He and Bellafront sneak away during the dancing and are married, this time by the right parson. Suspicions of polyandry are dispelled when Nevill explains that the parson that morning was really him, so that the marriages are invalid. Strange brings Pouts to apologise to the gathering, but is almost arrested for his own murder until he reveals his identity. Pouts returns to the war, abjuring women.
