Full speech

Reuenge, that euer red itt'st in the eyes Of iniur'd Ladies, till we crowne thy browes With bloudy Lawrell; and receiue from thee Iustice for all our humors iniurie, Whose wings none flye, that Wrath or Tyrannie Haue ruthlesse made, and bloudy. Enter here, Enter, O enter: and, though length of time Neuer lets any scape thy constant iustice, Yet now preuent that length. Flye, flye, and here Fixe thy steele foot-steps: Here, O here, where still Earth (mou'd with pittie) yeelded and embrac'd My Loues faire figure, drawne in his deare bloud, And mark'd the place, to show thee where was done The cruell'st murther that ere fled the Sunne. O Earth! why keep'st thou not as well his spirit, To giue his forme life? No, that was not earthly: That (rarefying the thinne and yeelding ayre) Flew sparkling vp into the Sphaere of fire, Whence endlesse flames it sheds in my desire: Here be my daily pallet, here all nights That can be wrested from thy riuals armes; (O my deare Bussy) I will lye, and kisse Spirit into thy bloud, or breathe out mine In sighes, and kisses, and sad tunes to thine. That is so farre from all yee seeke of vs, That (though your selues be common as the ayre) We must not take the ayre, wee must not fit Our actions to our owne affectons: But as Geometricians (you still say) Teach that no lines, nor superficies, Doe moue themselues, but still accompanie The motions of their bodies; so poore wiues Must not pursue, nor haue their owne affections, But to their husbands earnests, and their iests, To their austerities of lookes, and laughters, (Though ne'er so foolish and iniurious) Like Parasites and slaues, fit their disposures. So said you, when you woo'd. So Souldiers tortur'd Withtedious sieges of some wel-wall'd Towne, Propound conditions of most large contents, Freedome of Lawes, all former gouernment; But hauing once set foote within the Wals, And got the reynes of power into their hands, Then doe they tyrannize at their owne rude swindges, Seaze all their goods, their liberties, and liues, And make aduantage, and their lusts, their lawes. Forgiuenesse! that grace you should seeke of mee: These tortur'd fingers, and these stab'd-through armes Keepe that law in their vvounds yet, vnobseru'd, And euer shall. You doe more then I.

Plot — The Revenge of Bussy D'Ambois
Before the start of the action, the Duke of Guise had Bussy D’Amboise and his brother Clermont brought ===== PAGE 63 ===== 1610 to court. Montsoreau had Bussy killed in reprisal for his adultery with Tamyra, and he died prophesying the deaths of Monsieur and Guise; his ghost then appeared to Clermont demanding revenge. Their sister Charlotte also seeks to avenge Bussy, and made her marriage to Baligny conditional on his swearing to participate in the plot; but Clermont insists on doing it alone, as the ghost commanded. Baligny’s role is, therefore, to deliver Clermont’s challenge to Montsoreau; but he cannot get access because Montsoreau has fearfully barricaded himself into his house. Two allies help him. Renel, who is selling his lands to Montsoreau, enables him to get in. When Montsoreau refuses to accept the challenge, Baligny leaves it behind anyway and Tamyra, who has reluctantly gone back to her husband, promises to make him read it. Unknown to the others, Baligny is reporting to the King on potential troublemakers like Clermont and Renel. The King is concerned at the growing power of Guise’s faction, so he and Baligny arrange for Clermont to be arrested: Clermont is to travel with Baligny to inspect the troops at Cambrai, and there he will be taken into custody by Baligny’s lieutenant, Maillard; thus Baligny will not have to participate directly in his brother-in-law’s downfall. Clermont has to leave Paris before concluding his business with Montsoreau. In Cambrai, he receives an anonymous letter warning him of Baligny’s treachery, but refuses to credit it. Maillard arrests him, with difficulty, and he is taken back to the Bastille; but he is allowed to send a message to his lover, the Countess of Cambrai, who was expecting him to visit her that evening. ~ Guise pressurizes the King into signing an order for .
