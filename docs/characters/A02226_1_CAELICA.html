Full speech

Though feare see nothing but extremity, Yet danger is no deep sea, but a Ford, Where they that yeeld can only drowned be. In wrongs, and wounds, Sir, you are to remisse. To Thrones a passiue nature fatall is. Yet liue: Liue for the State. When feare propounds, with losse men euer choose, And is it nothing then to lose the State? Yet Sir! if weaknesse be not such a sande, As neither wrong, nor counsell can manure; Choose, and resolue what death you will endure. Vnto that dutie if these hands be borne, I must thinke God, and Truth, but names of scorne. Againe, this iustice were, if life were lou'd; Now meerly grace; since death doth, but forgiue A life to you, which is a death to liue. Paine must displease that satisfies offence. Then sword! doe Iustice office thorough me; I offer more than that he hates to thee. No cliffe, or rocke is so precipitate, But downe it eyes can leade the blinde a way; Without me liue, or with me dye you may. Then be a King, no Tyrant of thy selfe: Be; and be what you will: what nature lent Is still in hers, and not our gouernment. Yet Sir! harke to the poore oppressed teares, The iust mens moane, that suffer by your fall; A Princes charge is to protect them all. And shall it nothing be that I am yours? The world without, my heart within doth know, I neuer had vnkinde, vnreuerent powers. If thus you yeeld to Alahams treacherie; He ruines you; 'tis you, Sir, ruine me. Will you doe that which Alaham can not? He hath no good: you haue no ill, but he: This Marre-right yeelding's honors Tyranny. " The lawes of power chain'd to mens humors be. " The good haue conscience; the ill (like instruments) " Are, in the hands of wise authority, " Moued, diuided, vsed, or layd downe; " Still, with desire, kept subiect to a Crowne. " Stirre vp all States, all spirits: hope, and feare; Wrong, and reuenge, are currant euery where. Let them feare rumor that doe worke amisse; Blood, torments, death, horrors of Cruelty, Haue time, and place. Looke through these skinnes of feare, Which still perswade the better side to beare. And since thy sonne thus trayterously conspires, Let him not prey on all thy race, and thee: Keepe ill example from posterity. My soule now ioyes. Doing breathes horrour out. Absence must be our first sleppe: Let vs fly: A pawse in rage makes Alaham to doubt; Which doubt may stirre in people hope, and feare, With loue, or hate, to seeke you euery where. For Princes liues are fortunes miserie; " As dainty sparks, which till men dead doe know, " To kindle for himselfe each man doth blow. But harke! What's this? Malice doth neuer sleepe: I heare the Spies of power drawing neere. Sir! follow me: Misfortunes worst is come; Her strength is change; and change yeelds better doome. Choice now is past. Hard by there is a pile Built, vnder colour of a sacrifice; If God doe grant, it is a place to saue; If God denies, it is a ready graue. Zophi. Celica. What see I here! More spectacles of woe? And are my kinred only made to be Agents, and Patients in iniquity? Ah forlorne wretch! ruines example right! Lost to thy selfe, not to thy enemie, Whose hand, euen while thou fliest, thou fal'st into; And with thy fall, thy father do'st vndoe. Saue one I may: Nature would saue them both; But Chance hath many wheeles, Rage many eyes. What shall I then abandon innocents? Not helpe a helplesse brother throwne on me? Is nature narrow to aduersity? No, No. Our God left duty for a law; Pittie, at large; Loue, in authority; Despaire, in bonds; feare, of it selfe in awe: That rage of time, and powers strange liberty, Oppressing good men might resistance finde: Nor can I to a brother be lesse kinde. Do'st thou, that canst not see, hope to escape? Disgrace, can haue no friend; contempt, no guide; Right, is thy guilt; thy Iudge, iniquity; Which desolation casts on them that see. Nay; God, the World, thy Parents it denie; A brothers iealous heart, vsurped might Growes friends with all the world, except thy right. Fly vnto God: For in humanity Hope there is none. Reach me thy fearfull hand: I am thy sister; neither fiend, nor spie Of Tyrants rage; but one that feeles despaire Of thy Estate, which thou do'st only feare. Kneele downe; embrace this holy mystery, A refuge to the worst for rape, and blood; And yet, I feare, not hallowed for the good, Eternall God! that seest thy selfe in vs! If vowes be more than sacrifice of lust, Rays'd from the smokes of hope, and feare in vs; Protect this innocent; calme Alahams rage; By miracles faith goes from age, to age. Affection trembles; reason is opprest; Nature, methinkes, doth her owne entrayles teare: In resolution ominous is feare. Alaham. Celica. Ah cruell wretch! guilty of parents blood! Might I, poore innocent, my father free, My murther yet were lesse impiety. But on; deuoure: feare only to be good: Let vs not scape: thy glory then doth rise, When thou at once thy house do'st sacrifice. O twofold scorne. God be reueng'd for me. Yet since my Father is destroy'd by thee, Adde still more scorne, it sorrow multiplies. This is reward which thou do'st threaten me: If terrour thou wilt threaten, promise ioyes. And if my Father I no more shall see, Helpe me vnto the place where he remaines; To hell below, or to the skie aboue: The way is easie, where the guide is loue. Racke not my woe. Thy glorious pride of this vnglorious deed Doth mischiefe, ripe; and therefore falling, show. He was (Vnhappy) cause that thou art now; Thou art, ah wicked! cause that he is not; And fear'st thou Parricide can be forgot? Beare witnesse, Thou Almighty God on high! And you blacke Powers inhabiting below! That for his life my selfe would yeeld to die. Seeke him amongst the dead, you plac'd him there: Yet lose no paines, good Soules! goe not to hell; And, but to heauen, you may goe euery where. Guilty, with you, of his blood let me be, If any more I of my Father know, Than that he is where you would haue him goe. If ought be quicke in me, moue it with scorne: Nothing can come amisse to thoughts forlorne. Reward, and paine; feare, and desire too, Are vaine, in things impossible to doe. Euen where he by his losse of eyes hath wonne, That he no more shall see his monstrous Sonne. First, in perpetuall night thou mad'st him goe; His flesh the graue, his life the stage, where sense Playes all the Tragedies of paine, and woe. And wouldst thou trayterosly thy selfe exceed, By seeking thus to make his Ghost to bleed? The flattering glasse of Power is others paine. Perfect thy worke; that heauen, and hell may know, To worse I cannot, going from thee, goe. " Eternall life, that euer liu'st aboue! " If sense there be with thee of hate, or loue; " Reuenge my King, and Fathers ouerthrow. " O Father! if that name reach vp so high, " And be more than a proper word of Art, " To teach respects in our humanity; " Accept these paines, wherof you feele no smart. King. Alaham.

Plot summary

Plot — Alaham
Before the start of the action, an oracle foretold that the kingdom’s security depended on the death of the King’s eldest son, Zophi; but the pashas, ruling during the King’s senility, merely banished him. Hala, wife of the younger son, Alaham, has had a series of affairs with pashas: first Muhammad, who was forced into exile as a result, then Cain. Seeking to secure his position, Alaham has recalled Muhammad, ===== PAGE 189 ===== 1600 but Muhammad has recalled Zophi in turn, cutting Alaham out of the succession. Hala seeks to make Cain king, and persuades him to murder Muhammad; but he changes his mind after Muhammad warns him of Alaham’s ambition: Cain will tell Alaham that Muhammad is dead, but in reality he will go into hiding. Meanwhile Alaham corrupts the guard, blinds his father and brother, and seizes power. When Cain tells him of Muhammad's death, he is sent for execution — if not for murder then for lying about it, as he is told when he tries to save himself. Alaham’s sister Caelica helps Zophi and their father to take sanctuary in the temple. Alaham has the city searched, finds Caelica, and arrests her. Her cries bring the old King out of hiding, and he too is arrested. The King, Caelica, and Zophi are all burned to death; as they die, their souls call out for vengeance. Seeking to avenge Cain’s death, Hala sends Alaham a poisoned robe, and plans to kill their son before his face, so that the other child, Cain’s bastard, will inherit the kingdom; but she mistakenly kills the wrong infant. The robe kills Alaham; Hala intends to kill herself too.
