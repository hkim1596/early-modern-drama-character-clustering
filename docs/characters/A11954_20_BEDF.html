What ſay'ſt thou man, before dead Henry's Coarſe? Speake ſoftly, or the loſſe of thoſe great Townes Will make him burſt his Lead, and riſe from death. Me they concerne, Regent I am of France: Giue me my ſteeled Coat, Ile fight for France. Away with theſe diſgracefull wayling Robes; Wounds will I lend the French, in ſtead of Eyes, To weepe their intermiſſiue Miſeries. Is Talbot ſlaine then? I will ſlay my ſelfe, For liuing idly here, in pompe and eaſe, Whil'ſt ſuch a worthy Leader, wanting ayd, Vnto his daſtard foe-men is betray'd. His Ranſome there is none but I ſhall pay. Ile hale the Dolphin headlong from his Throne, His Crowne ſhall be the Ranſome of my friend: Foure of their Lords Ile change for one of ours. Farwell my Maſters, to my Taske will I, Bonfires in France forthwith I am to make, To keepe our great Saint Georges Feaſt withall. Ten thouſand Souldiers with me I will take, Whoſe bloody deeds ſhall make all Europe quake. I doe remember it, and here take my leaue, To goe about my preparation. The Day begins to breake, and Night is fled, Whoſe pitchy Mantle ouer-vayl'd the Earth. Here ſound Retreat, and ceaſe our hot purſuit. 'Tis thought Lord Talbot, when the fight began, Rows'd on the ſudden from their drowſie Beds, They did amongſt the troupes of armed men, Leape o're the Walls for refuge in the field. No, truly, 'tis more then manners will: And I haue heard it ſayd, Vnbidden Gueſts Are often welcommeſt when they are gone. Oh let no words, but deedes, reuenge this Trea ſon. Lord Talbot, doe not ſo diſhonour me: Here will I ſit, before the Walls of Roan, And will be partner of your weale or woe. Not to be gone from hence: for once I read, That ſtout Pendragon, in his Litter ſick, Came to the field, and vanquiſhed his foes. Me thinkes I ſhould reuiue the Souldiors hearts, Becauſe I euer found them as my ſelfe. Now quiet Soule, depart when Heauen pleaſe, For I haue ſeene our Enemies ouerthrow. What is the truſt or ſtrength of fooliſh man? They that of late were daring with their ſcoffes, Are glad and faine by flight to ſaue themſelues.

Plot summary

Plot — 1 Henry the Sixth
King Henry Vis dead, and England's French lands are lost. After a military setback, the French accept the offered aid of Joan Pucelle , who claims supernatural powers from the Virgin Mary; but they also release Talbot in an exchange of prisoners. With Joan 's help the French recapture the occupied town of Orleans, but the English take it back in a night attack led by Talbot. The Countess of Auvergne invites Talbot to visit her. intending treachery, but he second-guesses her and brings soldiers with him. In England, feuds brew between Gloucester and Winchester, and between Richard Plantagenet and the Duke of Somerset, whose supporters show their allegiance by picking different coloured roses. Richard , who has been deprived of his titles through the attainder of his father, learns from the imprisoned and dying Mortimer that he has a claim to the throne . King Henry restores him to his inheritance, creating him Duke of York. Joan takes Rouen by a ruse , but Talbot recaptures it the same day; she then persuades England's ally Burgundy to join the French. After King Henry's coronation in Paris, York is left in command of Prance, but personal tension between him and Somerset means that troop reinforcements arrive late; in consequence, Talbot is defeated, surviving only long enough to learn that his son has perished in battle. Joan conjures devils, but they will not help her: she is captured by York and burned at the stake. The French and English agree a peace which will entail King Henry making a diplomatic marriage, but this is pre-empted when Suffolk captures Margaret and (having fallen in love with her but being alr eady married) plans to install her as Queen of England . Henry agrees to marry her, and Suffolk goes to France to mili the arrangements.
