Come on, I am euery day troubled with these néedie companions, what newes with you, what wind brings you hi ther. You yoong Gentleman is my mony readie. Is the winde in that doore, if thou hast my mony so it is, I will not defer a day, an houre, a minute, but take the forfeyt 〈…〉 sir consider that my losse was great by the 〈…〉 vp, you knowe sir I borrowed of you fortie 〈…〉 I had ten pounds in money, and thirty pounds 〈…〉 , which when I came to sell againe, I could get 〈…〉 for them, so had I sir but fiftéene poundes for 〈…〉 In consideration of this ill bargaine, I pray you sir 〈…〉 month longer. I answered thée afore not a minute, what haue I to do how thy bargain proued, I haue thy hand set to my booke that thou receiuedst fortie pounds of me in mony. Come to morrow to mée, and sée how I will vse thée. What a spight is this, hath sped of his Crownes, if he had mist but one halfe houre, what a goodly Farme had I got ten for fortie pounds, well tis my cursed fortune, Oh haue I no shift to make him forfeit his recognisance. Stay, what is this a clocke foure, let me sée, to be paid betwéen the houres of thrée and foure in the afternoone, this goes right for me, you sir, heare you not the clocke, and haue you not a counterpaine of your Obligation, the houre is past, it was to be paid betwéene thrée and foure, and now the clocke hath strooken foure, I will receiue none, Ile stand to the forfeyt of the recog nisance. Why didst thou not tender thy mony then? if I offer thée iniury take the law of me, complaine to the Iudge, I will re ceiue no mony. Hang beggarly knaue, commest to me for a Cow, did I not bind her bought and sold for a peny, and was not thy day to haue paid yesterday, thou getst no Cow at my hand. Thou getst no Cow of me knaue, I cannot stand pra ting with you, I must be gone. What pratest thou, haue I not answered thée thy day is broken? I haue other matters to talke on, farwell fellowes. Ile deliuer thée none, if I haue wronged thée, séeke thy mends at the law. Sir here is fortie angels for you, and if at any time you want a hundreth pound or two, tis readie at your command, or the féeding of thrée or foure fat bullocks: whereas these néedie slaues can reward with nothing but a cap and aknée, and therfore I pray you sir fauour my case. What maister Lawier what make you here, mine aduersary for these Clients? I know you know the old Prouerbe, He is not wise, that is not wise for himselfe. I would not be disgracst in this acti on, therefore here is twentie angels say nothing in the matter, and what you say, say to no purpose, for the Iudge is my friend. That first he had no Lute-strings of me, for looke you sir, I haue his owne hand to my booke for y e receit of fortie pound. This is manifest contrary sir, and on that I will de pose, for here is the obligation, to be paide betwéene thrée & foure in the after-noone, and the Clocke strooke foure before he offered it, and the words be betweene three and foure, therefore to be ten dred before foure. Friend let me sée it. How were they gotten? Honest men, toward men, good men, my friends, like to proue good members, vse me, command me, I will maintaine your credits, there's mony, now spend not your time in idlenesse, bring me commoditie I haue crownes for you, there is two shil lings for thée, and six shillings for thée. Good fellowes, propper fellowes, my companions, far well, I haue a pot for you. Groning in conscience, burdened with my crimes, The hell of sorrow hauntes me vp and downe. Tread where I list, mée-thinkes the bléeding ghostes Of those whom my corruption brought to noughts, Do serue for stumbling blocks before my steppes. The fatherlesse and widow wrongd by me. The poore oppressed by my vsurie, Mée-thinkes I sée their hands reard vp to heauen, To crie for vengeance of my couetousnesse. Where so I walke, Ile sigh and shunne my way. Thus am I made a monster of the world, Hell gapes for me, heauen will not hold my soule. You mountaines shroude me from the God of truth. Mee-thinkes I sée him sit to iudge the earth. Sée how he blots me out of the booke of life. Oh burthen more then Atna that I beare. Couer me hilles, and shroude me from the Lord. Swallow me Licas, shield me from the Lord. In life no peace: each murmuring that I heare, Mée-thinkes the sentence of damnation soundes, Die reprobate, and hie thée hence to hell. What fiend is this that temptes me to the death? What is my death the harbour of my rest? Then let me die: what second charge is this? Mée-things I heare a voice amidst mine eares, That bids me staie: and tels me that the Lord Is mercifull to those that do repent. May I repent? oh thou my doubtfull soule? Thou maist repent, the Iudge is mercifull. Hence tooles of wrath, stales of temptation, For I will pray and sigh vnto the Lord. In sackcloth will I sigh, and fasting pray: O Lord in rigor looke not on my sinnes. O Lord of light forgiue me my misdéeds. Come foorth my friends whom wittingly I wrongd, Before this man of God receiue your due, Before our King I meane to make my peace. Ionas behold in signe of my remorse, I heare restore into these poore mens hands, Their goods which I vniustly haue detaind, And may the heauens so pardon my misdéeds, As I am penitent for my offence.

Plot — A Looking Glass for London and England
Triumphant over the King of Jerusalem, the Assyrian king Rasni declares himself divine and marries his sister, Remilia; a viceroy who objects is banished and the flatterer Radagon given his place. Inordinately proud of her beauty, Remilia is struck by lightning. Rasni is unable to revive her, and buries her in state; but is encouraged by Radagon to think of the living rather than the dead, and to take Alvida, wife of the King of Paphlagonia. When her husband objects to the adulterous liaison, she poisons him. Alvida continues to be unfaithful: her ambition attracts her to Rasni, but her sexual appetite favours the Viceroy of Cilicia, so she takes both.
