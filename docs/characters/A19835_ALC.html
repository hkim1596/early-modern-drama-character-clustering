Ist possible that a societie Can with so little noyse, and sweat subsist? No man can wish a better place then this To practise in my arte, for here they will Be sicke for companie, they are so kinde. I haue now twenty patients at this time, That know not what they aile, no more doe I, And they haue phisicke all accordingly. First Phillis got running at Barly-breake A little cold, which I with certaine druggs I ministred, was thought to remedie, Doris saw that, how Phillis phisicke wrought (For Phillis had told her, she neuer tooke So delicate a thing in all her life That more reuiu'd her heart, and clear'd her bloud,) Doris would needes be sicke too, and take some. Melina seeing that, she would the like, And so she had the very same receipt, For to say tr th I haue no more but that, And one poore pill I vse for greate cures. But this is onely sweet and delicate, Fit for young women, and is like th'hearb Iohn, Doth neither good nor hurte, but that's all one, For if they but conceiue it doth, it doth, And it is that Phisitions hold the chiefe In all their cures, conceipt, and strong beleefe: Besids I am a straunger come from far Which doth adde much vnto opinion too. For who now but th' Arabian or the Iewe In forraine lands, are held the onely men, Although their knowledge be no more then mine. Then am I as abstruse and mysticall In Caracter, and giuing my receipt Obseruing still th'odd number in my pills, And certaine houres to gather and compound My simples, and make all t'attend the Moone. Then do I shew what rare ingredients I vse for some great cures, when need requires, The liuer of a Wolfe, the Lions gall, The leaft side of a Mole, the Foxes heart, The right foote of a Tortuse, Dragons bloud, And such strange sauage stuffe, as euen the names Are phisick of them selues, to moue a man. And all the drugs I vse, must come from farre, Beyond the Ocean, and the Sunne at least, Or else it hath no vertue Phisicall, These home-bred simples do no good at all. Tis true, and now to see with what a strange And gluttenous desire, th'exhaust the same How infinite, and how in atiably, They doe deuoure th'intoxicating fume, You would admire, as if their spirits thereby Were taken, and inchanted, or transformd, By some infused philter in the drug. For whereas heretofore they wonted were At all thei meetings, and their eastiualls, To passe the time in elling wittie tales, In questions, riddles, and in purposes, Now do they nothing else, but sit and suck, And spit and sla er, all the time they sit. That I go by, and laugh vnto my selfe, And thinke that this will one day make some worke For me or others, but I feare it will B'another age will finde the hurt of this. But sure the time's to come, when they looke back On this, will wonder with themselues to thinke That men of sense could euer be so mad, To suck so grosse a vapour, that consumes Their spirits, spends nature, dries vp memorie, Corrupts the bloud, and in a vanitie. Welcome, faire nimph, come let me try your pulse. I cannot blame you, hold your selfe not well. Plot — The Queen's Arcadia (Arcadia Reformed)
Arcadia is no longer the place of pastoral simplicity it once was: old shepherds have grown litigious, young ones are falling in love unwisely and unrequitedly, and the nymphs, who seem less beautiful than before, have abandoned the modest simplicity of traditional Arcadian dress in favour of quirky new fashions. The problems arise from foreign influences on this previously sequestered land. Colax has travelled abroad, and brought back with him the city woman Techne, and they are selfishly interfering in the love- lives of the nymphs and shepherds: they have made Silvia and Palaemon jealous of one another with mendacious tales of infidelity; and Colax has persuaded Dorinda to jilt Myrtillus in his favour, making her the latest in a string of sexual conquests. Meanwhile Lincus, a lawyer, and Doctor Alcon have both come from town to set up practices in Arcadia. Lincus has found the local amity bad for business: the Arcadians do not covet one another’s property and so never go to law. For Alcon, however, the Arcadians’ fellow-feeling is profitable, because when one falls ill, others follow suit, and they all want the same medicine from him — which is just as well, because he has only one to offer, and that is a placebo. Both are keen to drum up trade, so Lincus has set Montanus and Acrisius against one another, and Alcon has introduced the Arcadians to an unhealthy habit: tobacco-smoking. Carinus (who has recently jilted Amaryllis) and Amyntas are rivals for the love of Cloris, and argue about which of them has the greater command of her affections; but in fact Cloris loves neither of them, and has sworn chastity until she finds a worthy man. Colax now wants a change of girlfriend, and asks Techne to make Cloris love him. Techne offers to give Cloris a new outfit, and arranges for them to meet at a cave so she can try it on; but it is Colax who keeps the appointment, and attempts to rape her. Techne herself has fallen for Amyntas, and arranges for him to see Cloris meeting Colax. Amyntas becomes suicidal, so Techne sends Cloris to stop him; but she arrives too late, and he has already taken poison.
