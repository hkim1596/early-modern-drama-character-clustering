Full speech

Very good, I like your demonstration well; but doest thou thinke your Organ of Rixam can compare with ours for all that? There's none dwels here; you may speak with the Master of the house if you will. Mine Host of the house, see where he marches. Ah well done Master, tickle them noble Captaine. Yes if it please your Maidenship; my Master sends you word he is the old man, and his suite is the old suite still, and his cloaths the old cloaths: He scornes to be a change ling, or a shifter; he feares nothing but this, that hee shall fall into the Lord your fathers hands for want of repara tions. I dare sweare he is an honest man, but I dare not say he is a true man. I shall beare this with as good will as you would beare him, Vtcung volumus. Come to the Citty, the Habberdasher will sooner call us block heads, than blocke us; come to the Sempsters, unlesse we will give them money, we cannot enter into their bands: though we have the Law of our sides, yet wee may walke through Burchin-lane and be non-suited: come bare foot to a Shooe-maker, though he be a Constable, he will not put us into his Stocks; though the Girdler be my brother, yet he will not let his leather imbrace me; come to the Glover, his gloves are either so little that I cannot plucke them on, or so great that I cannot compasse. And for the Campe, there's honour cut out of the whole peece, but not a ragge of mo ney. Nay I warrant her, wenches can afford her that at all times. Marry I would after my Master. Ey that he is able to keepe a man, and himselfe too. Then shall I not goe in? No? what remedy? ha, ha; hee that rings at a doore with such a Bell, and cannot enter? Well, if there be no remedy, I'le even stay without. I cannot blame you to forget me, for I thinke this be the first time of ur meeting. Speake, shall you and condogge together? I'le pay you to a haire. No, no; I will not salute you after the Italian fa shion: I'le enter before. Goe you then with your paire of Whores, I'le goe with this old skuller that first ply'd me. I'le see what I shall have first for my money by your favour. See Sir, yonder are my old fellows, Match and Touch boxe; I doe not thinke but they come to offer their service to you. God-a-mercy horse, you shall not stand to my li very. Corporall, you see this Livery? if you had stay, d by it, we had beene both cut out of a peece: Match, if you had not left us, you had beene one of this guard: Goe, away, betake you to the end of the Towne; let me finde you be tweene Woods close-stile and Iflington, with will it please your Worship to bestow the price of two Cannes upon a poore souldier, that hath serv'd in the face of the Souldan, and so forth, Apage, away, I scorne to be fellow to any that wil leave their Masters in adversity: if he entertaine you, he shall turne away me, that's certaine. Loe, I have taught him his lesson already; I knew where I should have you? Here comes a Lord. So should all that keepe Ordinaries, bid their guests farewell, though their entertainment be never so ill. Well sir, I take you but for an ordinary fellow, and so I leave you. Master, who will not say that you are a brave fellow, and a most noble Captaine, that with a word or two can discom fit an Host.

Plot summary

Plot — The Royal King and the Loyal Subject
The King of England returns home from a crusade, where his life was saved in battle by the Marshal. He rewards the Marshal with eminence, which makes the other lords jealous. At a hunt, the King’s horse loses a shoe; the Marshal gives him the shoes off his own horse. He also magnanimously allows the Prince to beat him in a tournament. The envious lords try to persuade the King that the Marshal’s humility is a cloak for pride and ambition, and that he wishes to be greater than the King himself in his magnanimity. The King resolves to test the Marshal, and treats him badly at a public feast: he is dismissed from office and his job is handed over to his greatest enemy, Chester; then he is told to leave the court altogether. He retires to the country, but the King orders him to send his favourite daughter to court for some sexual purpose. The Marshal sends his elder daughter, Isabella, subject to the condition that the King should return her if he dislikes her; and gives her instructions that she should conceal any pregnancy and constantly tell the King that her sister is better looking. The King makes Isabella his Queen, and, unbidden, the Marshal sends a generous dowry. She becomes pregnant, but does as her father instructs; she also tells her husband of her sister Katherine’s superior beauty. Affronted, the King sends her home along with the dowry and orders the Marshal to send Katherine. The Marshal claims that Katherine is ill and unable to travel, but promises to send her when she is better. He does more than that: he sends back both daughters with the dowry doubled, continuing to outdo the King in magnanimity. The Prince is allowed to marry Katherine. The Marshal asks leave to return to court with a rich gift for the King, which turns out to be his new-born son by Isabella. The King tries to trump even this by giving the Marshal a new wife, the Princess. At the wedding, however, the Marshal seems discontented: he is worried that his fortunes depend on the pleasure of his new wife. The envious Chester encourages the King to take offence, and the Marshal is summoned from his bed, tried, and sentenced to death. He accepts his fate with dignity, but his and the King’s children all plead for his life. The King declares that the Marshal’s loyalty has been thoroughly tested and proven.
