You ſacred ſiſters of faire Hellion, On whom my fauours euermore haue ſhone, In this you muſt haue patience with my vow, I cannot graunt what you aſpire vnto. Nor was't my fault, ſhe was tranſformed ſo, But her owne fond deſire, as ye well know. We told her too, before her vow was paſt, That cold repentance would enſue at laſt. And ſith her ſelfe did wiſh the ſhape of man, She cauſde the abuſe, digeſt it how ſhe can. Ariſe deare Nourſes of diuineſt skill, You ſacred Muſes of Pernaſſus hill: Phoebus is conquerd by your deare reſpect, And will no longer clemency neglect. You haue not ſude nor praide to me in vaine: I graunt your willes, ſhe is a mayd againe. Thalia, neare the Caue where ſhe remaines The Fayries keepe, requeſt them of their paines, And in my name, bid them forthwith prouide, From that darke place, to be the Ladies guide. And in the bountie of their liberall minde, To giue her cloathes according to her kinde. Haſte againe. No time too ſwift, to eaſe a Louers paine. Graue Aramanthus, now I ſee thy face I call to minde, how tedious a long ſpace Thou haſt frequented theſe ſad deſarts here, Thy time imployed, in heedfull minde I beare: The patient ſufferance of thy former wrong, Thy poore eſtate, and ſharpe exile ſo long, The honourable port thou bor'ſt ſometime, Till wrongd thou waſt, with vndeſerued crime By them whom thou to honour didſt aduance, The memory of which thy heauy chance, Prouokes my minde to take remorſe on thee, Father henceforth, my clyent ſhalt thou bee: And paſſe the remnant of thy fleeting time, With Lawrell wreath, amongſt the Muſes nine. And when thy age hath giuen place to fate, Thou ſhalt exchaunge thy former mortall ſtate: And after death, a palme of fame ſhalt weare, Amongſt the reſt that liue in honor here. And laſtly know, that faire Eurymine Redeemed now from former miſerie Thy daughter is, whom I for that intent Did hide from thee, in this thy baniſhment: That ſo ſhe might the greater ſcourge ſuſtaine, In putting Phoebus to ſo great a paine. But freely now, enioy each others ſight: No more Eurymine: abandon quite That borrowed name, as Atlanta, ſhe is calde, And here ſhe woman, in her right ſhape inſtalde. No more of that, but mutually imbrace. Looke not ſo ſtraunge, it is thy fathers voyce. And this thy Loue: Atlanta now reioyce. Well lady, you are retransformed now, But I am ſure you did repent your vow. The penance was your owne, though I did faſt ▪ The matter is but ſmall. To weare a braunch of Lawrell in your Caull For Phoebus ſake, leaſt elſe I be forgot, And thinke vpon me, when you ſee me not. Nay Gemulo and Siluio, contented bee: This night let me intreate ye you will take, Such cheare as I and theſe poore Dames can make. To morrow morne weele bring you on your way. Then Ladies gratulate this happie chaunce, With ſome delightfull tune and pleaſaunt daunce. Meane ſpace, vpon his Harpe will Phoebus play, So both of them may boaſt another day And make report, that when their wedding chaunc'te, Phoebus gaue muſicke, and the Muſes daunc'te. Since painfull ſorrowes date hath end, And time hath coupled friend with friend: Reioyce we all, reioyce and ſing, Let all theſe groaues of Phoebus ring. Hope hauing wonne, diſpaire is vaniſht: Pleaſure reuiues, and care is baniſht. Then trippe we all this Roundelay, And ſtill be mindfull of the Bay.

Plot summary

Plot — The Maid's Metamorphosis
Considering Eurymine too low-born to be a suitable match for his son Ascanio, Duke Telemachus orders two of his gentlemen to escort her from the court and murder her. In the event, however, Phylander persuades his colleague that they have no obligation to follow unlawful orders. They plan to present the Duke with an animal’s heart, claiming it to be hers, and Eurymine agrees to live away from the court in country exile. She meets a forester and a shepherd, each of whom wants her to live with him; when they become competitive, she agrees to accept a cottage from the forester and a flock of sheep from the shepherd. Ascanio goes into the woods looking for Eurymine. Juno finds him asleep and, eager to prove her superiority to Venus, arranges for him to dream of where to find his love. Meanwhile, Eurymine meets Apollo, who is also attracted to her. To save herself from rape, she claims to disbelieve his claims of godhead, and asks him to prove himself by working a miracle: specifically, she asks him to change her sex. He does, but with an associated curse: as a man, she will fall hopelessly in love with another man, and long to be a woman again. Ascanio learns from an echo that Eurymine is nearby but inaccessible, and the hermit Aramanthus, who is also a dispossessed prince, explains her metamorphosis. This disappoints not only Ascanio but also her country admirers, and Aramanthus recommends that she should ask the Graces to intercede with Apollo on her behalf. Apollo eventually agrees and restores her original gender. Telemachus has a change of heart and sends Phylander to bring Eurymine back from the forest. Apollo reveals that she is in fact Aramanthus’ long-lost daughter, and so of royal blood: her status is after all no obstacle to her marriage with Ascanio.
