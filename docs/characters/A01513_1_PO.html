And why should I not talke with Dulippo as well as with any other, I pray you? A great mishappe I promise you: marie Gods blessing on their heart that sette suche a brouche on my cappe. And I pray you whome may I thanke but gentle nourse, that continually praysing him, what for his per sonage, his curtesie, and aboue all, the extreme passions of his minde, in fine you would neuer cease till I accepted him, delighted in him, and at length desired him with no lesse affection, than he earst desired me. Nay rather that he filled your pursse with bribes and rewards. No of honestie, I pray you, who first brought him into my chamber? who first taught him the way to my bed but you? fie Nourse fie, neuer speake of it for shame, you will make me tell a wise tale anone. Nay rather the author of my good happe gentle Nourse, for I would thou knewest I loue not Dulipo, nor any of so meane estate, but haue bestowed my loue more worthily than thou déemest, but I will say no more at this time. Nay I neither haue changed, nor will change it. Mary I say that I loue not Dulipo, nor any suche as he, and yet I neither haue changed nor wil change my minde. I can speake no plainer, I haue sworne to y co ̄ trary. Well, it is of greater importance than you thinke Nourse, yet would I tell it you vnder condition and pro mise that you shall not tell it agayne, nor giue any signe or token to be suspected that you know it. Well heare you me then: this yong man whome you haue alwayes taken for Dulipo, is a noble borne Sicilian, his right name Erostrato, sonne to Philogono, one of the worthiest men in that countrey. Holde thy talking nourse, and harken to me, that I may explane the whole case vnto thée: the man whome to this day you haue supposed to be Dulipo, is (as I say) Ero strato, a gentleman that came from Sicilia to studie in this Citie, & euen at his first arriuall met me in the stréet, fel en amored of me, & of suche vehement force were the passions he suffred, that immediatly he cast aside both long gowne and bookes, & determined on me only to apply his study: and to the end he might the more co ̄ modiously bothe sée me and talke with me, he exchanged both name, habite, clothes and credite with his serua ̄ t Dulipo, who ̄ only he brought with him out of Sicilia, and so with the turning of a hand, of Ero strato a gentleman, he became Dulipo a seruing man, and soone after sought seruice of my father, and obteyned it. Yea out of doubt, on the other side Dulippo took vppon him the name of Erostrato his maister, the habite, the credite, bookes, and all things néedefull to a studente, and in shorte space profited very muche, and is nowe estée med as you sée. Uery fewe that passe this way, and fewe or none that tarrie héere any time. That is a pollicie deuised betwéene them, to put Doctor Dotipole out of conceite, the olde dotarde, he that so instantly dothe lye vpon my father for me: but looke where he comes, as God helpe me it is he, out vpon him, what a luskie yonker is this? yet I had rather be a Noonne a thousande times, than be combred with suche a Coystrell.

Plot summary

Plot — Supposes
Polynesta loves Dulippo, whose suit has been promoted by her nurse, and who is supposed a poor servant. In fact he is not Dulippo at all but Erostrato, who two years ago gained access to Polynesta by swapping identities with his servant and entering her father Damon’s household. Meanwhile Cleander seeks her hand, and the supposed Erostrato (the true Dulippo) becomes his rival for trust with him. Cleander appears to offer the best dowry, but Damon gives ‘Erostrato’ a fortnight’s grace pending his father Philogano’s arrival to underwrite his counter-offer. ‘Erostrato’ duly arranges to produce a false Philogano, recruiting a Sienese gentleman for the role by persuading him that a diplomatic incident has made Ferrara an unsafe place for a Sienese visitor. Overhearing an argument between two women servants, Damon discovers that ‘Dulippo’ and Polynesta have been sleeping together. ‘Dulippo’ is apprehended and imprisoned in the dungeon of his master’s house, and therefore unable to warn his servant of the arrival of the true Philogano: he misses his son and has come to take him back to Sicily. By chance, Philogano meets the true Dulippo, who refuses to acknowledge him. After the locals confirm that they know the young man as Erostrato, Philogano suspects that he has murdered his master and stolen his identity, and seeks legal assistance from Cleander. However, Dulippo turns out to be Cleander’s son, whom he lost as a child when evacuating Otranto during the Turkish conquest; he was later captured by Sicilians and sold to Philogano. The exchange of identities is revealed, allaying Damon’s worries about his daughter’s reputation. Cleander withdraws his suit: he no longer needs to marry and beget an heir now that his lost son has been restored to him. The true Erostrato is confirmed as Polynesta’s future husband.
