Full speech

Why should you seeke, my Lord, to purchase glory By the disgrace of a silly maid? Well, well, my Lord, I haue no heart of flint; Or if I had, you know by cunning words How to out-weare it.—But. How shall I say I, when my feares say no? No; yes, yes, I would haue said, how my tongue trips. Oh, if you should proue false, and loue another? I shall Madam.— Good my Lord, Reclaime your incredulitie; my fault Proceeds from lawfull composition Of Wedlocke; he hath seal'd his oath to mine, To be my husband. Ay me, to me he did. Hey-ho, and so am I: But what shall's doe now? And so he knowes I am: I told him on't Last meeting in the lobby, and in troth The false deceiuer laugh'd. Those very words He vs'd to me; it fretted me to'th heart: I'le be reueng'd. My deere Ferentes, my betrothed Lord. Good Loue, let's walke. How, not me! Vnmatch'd Villaine. Come Gentlewomen, let's together then, Thrice happy maids that neuer trusted men.— I was too quickly wonne, you slaue. My Lord, the Dutchesse craues a word with you: In her chamber. Come foole, I'le giue thee plums enow, come foole.

Plot — 478
Eteocles and Polynices are elected co-kings of Thebes, ruling in alternate years. At the end of the first year, Eteocles refuses to stand down and banishes Polynices. Six champions join Polynices to besiege Thebes, but, after heavy losses, Polynices offers to settle the case by single combat. He and Eteocles kill one another, and Creon takes the crown. Tiresias prophesies that the walls of Thebes will stand only as long as the last of the seven champions remains alive. After Adrastus’ death, the Argive troops sack the city. 1569
