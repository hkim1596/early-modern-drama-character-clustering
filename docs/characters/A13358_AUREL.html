Thankes noble Polidor my ſecond ſelfe, The faithfull loue which I haue found in thee Hath made me leaue my fathers princelie court, The Duke of Ceſtus thriſe renowmed ſeate, To come to Athens thus to find thee out, Which ſince I haue ſo happilie attaind, My fortune now I doo account as great As earſt did Caeſar when he conquered moſt, But tell me noble friend where ſhal we lodge, For I am vnacquainted in this place. With all my hart, I will requite thy loue, But ſtaie; what dames are theſe ſo bright of hew Whoſe eies are brighter then the lampes of heauen, Fairer then rocks of pearle and pretious ſtone, More louelie farre then is the morning ſunne, When firſt ſhe opes hir orientall gates. Truſt me my friend I muſt confeſſe to thee, I tooke ſo much delight in theſe faire dames, As I doo wiſh they had not gone ſo ſoone, But if thou canſt, reſolue me what they be, And what old man it was that went with them, For I doo long to ſee them once againe. Why, is not her father willing to the match? But he ſhall keepe hir ſtill by my aduiſe, And yet I needs muſt loue his ſecond daughter The image of honor and Nobilitie, In whoſe ſweet perſon is compriſde the ſomme Of natures skill and heauenlie maieſtie. O might I ſee the center of my ſoule Whoſe ſacred beautie hath inchanted me, More faire then was the Grecian Helena For whoſe ſweet ſake ſo many princes dide, That came with thouſand ſhippes to Tenedos, But when we come vnto hir fathers houſe, Tell him I am a Marchants ſonne of Ceſtus, That comes for traffike vnto Athens heere, And heere ſirha I will change with you for once, And now be thou the Duke of Ceſtus ſonne, Reuell and ſpend as if thou wert my ſelfe, For I will court my loue in this diſguiſe. Tuſh feare not Valeria let me alone, But ſtaie, heere comes ſome other companie. Valeria as erſte we did deuiſe, Take thou thy lute and go to Alfonſos houſe, And ſay that Polidor ſent thee thither. I thanke you ſir, and if what I haue got, By marchandiſe or trauell on the ſeas, Sattins or lawnes or azure colloured ſilke, Or pretious firie pointed ſtones of Indie, You ſhall command both them my ſelfe and all. Why Valeria, will ſhe not learne apace? Well Valeria go to my chamber, And beare him companie that came to daie From Ceſtus, where our aged father dwels. Miſtreſſe you ſhall not need to buie of me, For when I croſt the bubling Canibey, And ſailde along the Criſtall Heliſpont, I filde my cofers of the wealthie mines, Where I did cauſe Millions of labouring Moores To vndermine the cauernes of the earth, To ſeeke for ſtrange and new found pretious ſtones, And diue into the ſea to gather pearle, As faire as Iuno offered Priams ſonne, And you ſhall take your liberall choice of all. Her manhood then is good I do beleeue. I find it ſo right worthie gentlemen, And of what worth your frendſhip I eſteme, I leue cenſure of your ſeuerall thoughts, But for requitall of your fauours paſt, Reſts yet behind, which when occaſion ſerues I vow ſhalbe remembred to the full, And for my fathers comming to this place, I do expect within this weeke at moſt. Valeria attend: I haue a louely loue, As bright as is the heauen criſtalline, As faire as is the milke white way of Ioue, As chaſt as Phoebe in her ſommer ſportes, As ſofte and tender as the aſure downe, That circles Cithereas ſiluer doues. Her do I meane to make my louely bride, And in her bed to breath the ſweete content, That I thou knowſt long time haue aimed at. Now Valeria it reſts in thee to helpe To compaſſe this, that I might gaine my loue, Which eaſilie thou maiſt performe at will, If that the marchant which thou toldſt me of, Will as he ſayd go to Alfonſos houſe, And ſay he is my father, and there with all Pas ouer certaine deedes of land to me, That I thereby may gaine my hearts deſire, And he is promiſed reward of me. He is: and Polidor ſhortly ſhall be wed, And he meanes to tame his wife erelong. Faith he's gon vnto the taming ſchoole. I: and Ferando is the Maiſter of the ſchoole. Faith I know not: but by ſom odde deuiſe Or other, but come Valeria I long to ſee the man, By whome we muſt compriſe our plotted drift, That I may tell him what we haue to doo. Agreed, then lets go. Now Senior Phylotus, we will go Vnto Alfonſos houſe, and be ſure you ſay As I did tell you, concerning the man That dwells in Ceſtus, whoſe ſon I ſaid I was, For you doo very much reſemble him, And feare not: you may be bold to ſpeake your mind. Thankes ſweet Phylotus, then ſtay you here, And I will go and fetch him hither ſtraight. Ho, Senior Alfonſo: a word with you. My father ſir is newly come to towne, And I haue brought him here to ſpeake with you, Concerning thoſe matters that I tolde you of, And he can certefie you of the truth. He is the Duke of Ceſtus thriſe renowned ſon, Who for the loue his honour beares to me: Hath thus accompanied me to this place. Sweetly reſolued faire Emelia, But would Phylema ſay as much to me, If I ſhould aske a queſtion now of thee, What if the duke of Ceſtus only ſon, Which came with me vnto your fathers houſe, Should ſeeke to git Phylemas loue from me, And make thee Duches of that ſtately towne, Wouldſt thou not then forſake me for his loue? Sweet Phylema bewties mynerall, From whence the ſun exhales his glorious ſhine, And clad the heauen in thy reflected raies, And now my liefeſt loue, the time drawes nie, That Himen mounted in his ſaffron robe, Muſt with his torches waight vpon thy traine, As Hellens brothers on the horned Moone, Now Iuno to thy number ſhall I adde, The faireſt bride that euer Marchant had. Pardon me father: humblie on my knees, I do intreat your grace to heare me ſpeake. Then let my death ſweet father end your griefe, For I it is that thus haue wrought your woes, Then be reuengd on me for here I ſweare, That they are innocent of what I did, Oh had I charge to cut of Hydraes hed, To make the topleſſe Alpes a champion field, To kill vntamed monſters with my ſword, To trauell dayly in the hotteſt ſun, And watch in winter when the nightes be colde, I would with gladneſſe vndertake them all, And thinke the paine but pleaſure that I felt, So that my noble father at my returne, Would but forget and pardon my offence, Not yet my Lord, Ile bring you to your ſhip. Faith if you will in triall of our wiues, Who will come ſowneſt at their husbands call. My wife comes ſooneſt for a hundred pound. How now Ferando you dare not lay belike. Faith father a waighty cauſe about our wiues Fiue hundred markes already we haue layd, And he whoſe wife doth ſhew moſt loue to him, He muſt inioie the wager to himſelfe. Vpon mine honour if I looſe Ile pay. Ile ſend for my wife firſt, Valeria Go bid your Miſtris come to me. Now for my hundred pound. Would any lay ten hundred more with me, I know I ſhould obtaine it by her loue. Truſt me Ferando I am ſure you haue, For you I dare preſume haue loſt it all. Now ſirra what ſaies your miſtris? I pray God your wife ſend you ſo good an an ſwere She may be buſie yet ſhe ſayes ſhele come. I thinke my wife for all ſhe did not come, Will proue moſt kinde for now I haue no feare, For I am ſure Ferandos wife, ſhe will not come. This is a wonder: almoſt paſt beleefe. Beſhrew thee Phylema, thou haſt Loſt me a hundred pound to night. For I did lay that thou wouldſt firſt haue come. Beleeue me father I reioice to ſee, Ferando and his wife ſo louingly agree.

Plot summary

Plot — The Taming of a Shrew
Forcibly ejected from an alehouse, Sly is sleeping off his drunkenness when he is found by a lord, who brings him home and tricks him into thinking himself a nobleman. A play is performed for him: The Taming of a Shrew. In the play, Aurelius of Sestos, newly arrived in Athens, falls in love with Philema; his friend Polidor already loves her sister, Emelia. To pursue his love, Aurelius pretends to be a wealthy merchant’s son and has his man Valeria assume his aristocratic identity. However, the girls’ father Alfonso has decreed that his eldest daughter Kate, a shrewish scold, must be married before them. The two wooers decide to persuade Ferando to marry Kate, but Alfonso has already promised him six thousand crowns to undertake the same task He and Kate meet unchaperoned, and they wrangle, but the marriage is arranged for the following Sunday; Kate seems outwardly unwilling but consents in an aside. Ferando goes to prepare his country house. Valeria is sent to teach Kate the lute, but she behaves aggressively. On the wedding day Ferando arrives in base clothes and forces Kate to leave with him immediately after the ceremony. When they arrive in the country, he claims that the dinner is burnt, and beats his servants. She is kept hungry, and denied her choice of clothing. Aurelius has Philotus pose as his father to persuade Alfonso that he is wealthy enough to marry Philema. He and Polidor are married to Kate’s younger sisters. Kate and Ferando visit them, after Kate submits to Ferando’s will. On the way, they meet the Duke of Sestos (who has come to Athens in disguise to spy on his son), and mock him. The Duke exposes Aurelio’s pretence and proposes to have those involved sent to prison, but Sly intervenes in the performance to prevent this. The three new husbands bet on whose wife will come when called. Ferando wins, and Kate explains to her sisters their duty as wives. Sly falls asleep during the performance. The lord puts him back where he was found. He returns home intending to tame his own wife.
