The Duke of Buckinghams Surueyor? Ha? Where's his Examination? Is he in perſon, ready? Well, we ſhall then know more, & Buckingham Shall leſſen this bigge looke. Whil'ſt our Commiſſion from Rome is read; Let ſilence be commanded. Bee't ſo, proceed. Madam, this is a meere diſtraction, You turne the good we offer, into enuy. Madam, you wander from the good We ayme at. Pray heare me. If your Grace Could but be brought to know, our Ends are honeſt, Youl'd feele more comfort. Why ſhold we (good Lady) Vpon what cauſe wrong you? Alas, our Places, The way of our Profeſſion is againſt it; We are to Cure ſuch ſorrowes, not to ſowe 'em. For Goodneſſe ſake, conſider what you do, How you may hurt your ſelfe: I, vtterly Grow from the Kings Acquaintance, by this Carriage. The hearts of Princes kiſſe Obedience, So much they loue it. But to ſtubborne Spirits, They ſwell and grow, as terrible as ſtormes. I know you haue a Gentle, Noble temper, A Soule as euen as a Calme; Pray thinke vs, Thoſe we profeſſe, Peace-makers, Friends, and Seruants. The Packet Cromwell, Gau't you the King? The late Queenes Gentlewoman? A Knights Daughter To be her Miſtris Miſtris? The Queenes, Queene? This Candle burnes not cleere, 'tis I muſt ſnuffe it, Then out it goes. What though I know her vertuous And well deſeruing? yet I know her for A ſpleeny Lutheran, and not wholſome to Our cauſe, that ſhe ſhould lye i' th' boſome of Our hard rul'd King. Againe, there is ſprung vp An Heretique, an Arch-one; Cranmer, one Hath crawl'd into the fauour of the King, And is his Oracle. Heauen forgiue me, Euer God bleſſe your Highneſſe. Sir, For Holy Offices I haue a time; a time To thinke vpon the part of buſineſſe, which I beare i' th' State: and Nature does require Her times of preſeruation, which perforce I her fraile ſonne, among'ſt my Brethren mortall, Muſt giue my tendance to. And euer may your Highneſſe yoake together, (As I will lend you cauſe) my doing well, With my well ſaying. What ſhould this meane? My Soueraigne, I confeſſe your Royall graces Showr'd on me daily, haue bene more then could My ſtudied purpoſes requite, which went Beyond all mans endeauors. My endeauors, Haue euer come too ſhort of my Deſires, Yet fill'd with my Abilities: Mine owne ends Haue beene mine ſo, that euermore they pointed To' th' good of your moſt Sacred Perſon, and The profit of the State. For your great Graces Heap'd vpon me (poore Vndeſeruer) I Can nothing render but Allegiant thankes, My Prayres to heauen for you; my Loyaltie Which euer ha's, and euer ſhall be growing, Till death (that Winter) kill it. I do profeſſe, That for your Highneſſe good, I euer labour'd More then mine owne: that am, haue, and will be (Though all the world ſhould cracke their duty to you, And throw it from their Soule, though perils did Abound, as thicke as thought could make 'em, and Appeare in formes more horrid) yet my Duty, As doth a Rocke againſt the chiding Flood, Should the approach of this wilde Riuer breake, And ſtand vnſhaken yours. What ſhould this meane? What ſodaine Anger's this? How haue I reap'd it? He parted Frowning from me, as if Ruine Leap'd from his Eyes. So lookes the chafed Lyon Vpon the daring Huntſman that has gall'd him: Then makes him nothing. I muſt reade this paper: I feare the Story of his Anger. 'Tis ſo: This paper ha's vndone me: 'Tis th' Accompt Of all that world of Wealth I haue drawne together For mine owne ends, (Indeed to gaine the Popedome, And fee my Friends in Rome.) O Negligence! Fit for a Foole to fall by: What croſſe Diuell Made me put this maine Secret in the Packet I ſent the King? Is there no way to cure this? No new deuice to beate this from his Braines? I know 'twill ſtirre him ſtrongly; yet I know A way, if it take right, in ſpight of Fortune Will bring me off againe. What's this? To th' Pope ? The Letter (as I liue) with all the Buſineſſe I writ too's Holineſſe. Nay then, farewell: I haue touch'd the higheſt point of all my Greatneſſe, And from that full Meridian of my Glory, I haſte now to my Setting. I ſhall fall Like a bright exhalation in the Euening, And no man ſee me more. Stay: Where's your Commiſſion? Lords, words cannot carrie Authority ſo weighty. Till I finde more then will, or words to do it, (I meane your malice) know, Officious Lords, I dare, and muſt deny it. Now I feele Of what courſe Mettle ye are molded, Enuy, How eagerly ye follow my Diſgraces As if it fed ye, and how ſleeke and wanton Ye appeare in euery thing may bring my ruine? Follow your enuious courſes, men of Malice; You haue Chriſtian warrant for 'em, and no doubt In time will finde their fit Rewards. That Seale You aske with ſuch a Violence, the King (Mine, and your Maſter) with his owne hand, gaue me: Bad me enioy it, with the Place, and Honors During my life; and to confirme his Goodneſſe, Ti'de it by Letters Patents. Now, who'll take it? It muſt be himſelfe then. Proud Lord, thou lyeſt: Within theſe fortie houres, Surrey durſt better Haue burnt that Tongue, then ſaide ſo. How much me thinkes, I could deſpiſe this man, But that I am bound in Charitie againſt it. What, amaz'd At my misfortunes? Can thy Spirit wonder A great man ſhould decline. Nay, and you weep I am falne indeed.

Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
