Full speech

A soueraigne salue for feare, is for to beare, Your selfe debonair to your subiects all. A worthier vertue 'tis, in countries syre, His people to defend with sword, and fyre. Nay rather frolick youthfull bloud appeares, To haue more néede of counsell wise, and graue. That heauenly powers your doings may alow. So much the more, bycause so much you may. Giue slender trust to fortunes flattering face: She topste turuie turnes hir whéele a pace. A princes prayse I counted haue alway, To do that same which with his honor stood, Not that which frantike fansie counteth good. And whome they hate with force they ouerquell. But faith more sure defence doth seeme to me. More méete of subiects for to be belou'd. What so by force of armes you do wring out, A grieuous worke it is to bring about. Will nothing then but that which well you may. What peoples voyce doth ioyntly bynde or lose, Let that confirmed stand. Ne. Swordes bloudy dynt Shall cause them else at me to take their hynt. God shéeld, and far that fact from you remoue. It is, O prince, a worthy famous thing, Amyds redoubted Lordes alone to ring: And wisely worke your countries praise to saue: And well your selfe to captiue folke behaue: From cruell brutish slaughter to abstaine: And voyde of moode to wreake your angry paine: And, to the worlde a quiet calme to giue, That all your age in peace their liues may liue. This is a Princes praise without all cryme: This is the path to heauen whereby we clyme. So is Augustus prince and father cald Of country first, in starbright throne ystald. Whome as a God in minsters we adorne, Yet troublous fortune tossed him beforne, A great while long on lands, and ruffling seas, Vntyll his fathers foes he could appease, And through wars diuers course could quel the ̄ quite. To you did fortune yelde hir power, and might, And raynes of rule without all bloud, and fyght. And to your beck both land, and seas hath bent. Grim deadly enuy daunted doth relent. The Senate Lordes gaue place with frée consent: The battaylous route of knightes with willing hearts (That same decre from sager syres departes) Vnto the lay mens choyse doe well agrée. Your grace the spring of peace they count to be. And chosen Iudge, and guyde of mortall stock. Your grace, your countries sacred syre, doth rock, And rule with princely gorgeous tytle bryght, The cyrcled worlde in rundell wysely ydight. Which myghty mounting name to kéepe so great, This noble city Rome, doth you entreate: And doth commend vnto your royall grace, Hir liuely lyms in charge for your lyues space. Your noble spouse, sprong forth of saincted péere, Of Claudius stock, the starbright Diamond cleere, That goddesse Iuno wise hir brothers bed Partaking, pressed downe with buttocks red, Your graces princely court shall garnish gay, With wondrous heauenly faire descended stay. In tender budding yeres, when loue supprest With blusshing, hydes the flames of burning brest, Scant plaine appeares the loue they beare in déede. Faith, meekenesse, maners mylde, & hashfull shame Of spouse, those ought an husbande to reclame. The perls of iudging minde, alone remaine, Not subiect once to any rulers raigne. The passing pryde of beawties numming grace Eche day appals, and bleamisheth a pace. O noble prince such blinde, unlawfull loue, (Do rashly credit nought) from you remoue. Swift winged loue, mens fanste fond, in vaine A mercy wanting God to be, doth fame: And armes his hands with wounding weapons kéene And bowes with burning bronds, for louers gréene: Of Venus to be sprong, they all accord, And blyndly forgde, of thunders limping Lorde. Bland loue the minds great torment sore, appeares, And buddeth first, in frollick youthfull yeares. Who, while we drinke of fortunes pleasaunt cup, With laysie pampring ryot, is nestled vp: Whome if to foster vp, you leaue at length It fleting, falles away with broken strength. This is, in all our life, as I suppose The greatest cause, how pleasure, first arose. Which sith mankinde by brooding, bydeth ayt, Through gladsome loue, that fierce wilde beasts doth sway It neuer can, from manly breast depart. The peoples griefe might neuer yelde to it: Nor vertue can, the same at all, permit. No tryfling toyes, the people lookes to haue Of him, that ought to rule with wisedome graue. Seeke rather for to please, and calme their mood. When nought that they require they can obtaine, They iustly then agrieued are againe. An hard thing 'tis the people not to haue That of their Prince, which they do iustly craue. Let not your subiects then so sore be raind. That no man trusts, that is of credit light. With countrie péeres to meddle, it is afraid. Your grace may easly couch that budding brute. Let saincted syres deserts with pliant sute, Your graces mynde, remoue: let spouses age, And curteous bashfull shame disrump your rage.

Plot summary

Plot — Octavia (Seneca's Ninth Tragedy)
PLOT With her mother executed, her father murdered, and her brother poisoned, Octavia is in a perilous state. Her husband, the Emperor Nero, has had his mother Agrippina murdered; after she survived an orchestrated shipwreck, a soldier was sent to kill her more directly. Now he has got Poppaea. Octavia is timid, pregnant and wants a divorce in order to marry Poppaea and legitimate her bastard. Octavia wants to take revenge on him for her brother’s death, but her nurse advises her to become pregnant herself and produce an heir who will restore the dynasty. Recalled from exile to serve at court, Seneca laments the fortune which has put him in harm’s way. He tries to encourage Nero to rule virtuously, but Nero finds his advice tiresome and plans to expedite his marriage to Poppaea. Agrippina’s ghost curses the nuptial and predicts Nero’s death. Poppaea’s wedding night is disturbed by a nightmare in which the ghost showed her a vision of her first husband’s death at Nero’s hands. The Roman people revolt in support of Octavia, invade the palace, and destroy images of Poppaea. After putting down the rebellion, Nero orders Octavia into exile, and makes arrangements for her to be murdered on arrival. Octavia leaves Rome, knowing she is going to her death.
