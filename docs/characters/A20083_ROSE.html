Full speech

Sibil, what news at London? Did Lacy send kind gréetings to his loue? O Sibill, how dest thou my Lacy wrong? My Rowland is as gentle as a lambe, No doue was euer halfe so milde as he. Will my loue leaue me then and go to France? Get thée to London, and learne perfectly, Whether my Lacy go to France, or no: Do this, and I wil giue thée for thy paines, My cambricke apron, and my romish gloues, My purple stockings, and a stomacher, Say, wilt thou do this Sibil for my sake? Do so good Sibill, meane time wretched I Will sit and sigh for his lost companie. Exit. Why Sibill wilt thou proue a forrester? Heark heark, the hunters come, y'are best take héed Theyle haue a saying to you for this deede. No, but two Does. Trust me, not I, It is not like the wild forrest déere, Would come so neare to places of resort, You are deceiu'd, he fled some other way. Why doe you stay, and not pursue your game? But not the déere (sir) which you had in chace. The strangest hunting that euer I see, But wheres your parke? Impale me, and then I will not stray. To loose your heart, is't possible you can? Alacke good gentleman. You by such lucke might proue your hart a hind. Now God and't be his wil send Luck into your way. Can it be possible you loue me so? No, no, within those eie-bals I espie, Apparant likelihoods of flattery, Pray now let go my hand. As deare as your owne heart? I iudge it right. Men loue their hearts best when th'are out of sight. Yet hands off now: If flesh be fraile, how weake and frail's your vowe? Then do not brawle, One quarrell looseth wife and life and all, Is not your meaning thus? Loue loues to sport, therfore leaue loue y'are best. I meane to liue a maide. Say sir I cannot, I haue made a vow, Who euer be my husband, tis not you. Yea, when wilt begin? What louerimes man? fie on that deadly sinne. How like my Lacie lookes yond shooe-maker. For his sake whose faire shape thou representst, Good friend I drinke to thée. Sibil What shal I do? That Haunce the shoemaker is my loue Lacie, Disguisde in that attire to find me out, How should I find the meanes to speake with him? Do this, and euer be assured of my loue. Swéete Lacie, since faire Oportunitie Offers her selfe to furder our escape, Let not too ouer-fond estéeme of me Hinder that happie hower, inuent the meanes, And Rose will follow thée through all the world. A man and want wit in extremitie, Come, come, be Hauns still, play the shoomaker, Pull on my shooe. Oh God it pincheth me, what will you do? Be not dismaid at this what ere befall, Rose is thine owne, to witnes I speake truth, Where thou appoints the place Ile méete with thée, I will not fixe a day to follow thée, But presently steale hence, do not replie. Loue which gaue strength to beare my fathers hate, Shall now adde wings to further our escape. Make haste sweet loue. If Rose were askt that question, sheed say, no. O my most gratious Lord! Can you diuide the body from the soule, Yet make the body liue?

Plot summary

Plot — The Shoemaker's Holiday, or The Gentle Craft
Before the start of the action, Lacy went bankrupt on a Continental tour, and learned the cobbler’s trade in Wittenberg. On his return to England, he fell in love with Rose Oatley, but they fall foul of disapproving relatives: the Earl of Lincoln contrives for Lacy to leave the country as a commander in the French wars, and her father takes her away to his country house. The newly married shoemaker Ralph is recruited into the army and takes a sad leave of his wife, Jane, and his colleagues. Lacy, however, deserts after the troops take ship: he sends his cousin to war in his place, and returns to London in the hope of making contact with Rose. Disguised as a Dutch shoemaker, Hans, he seeks employment with Simon Eyre and, though the business does not need another journeyman, he is accepted because Eyre’s other workmen like him. A Dutch ship arrives in London with a rich cargo of luxury items, which the skipper offers at a bargain price; Lacy helps Eyre to buy the goods, using the money Lincoln gave him for his expenses in France. Eyre grows prosperous and becomes first an alderman, then Sheriff, and makes over his shop to his foreman, Hodge. In the country, Rose meets Hammon during a hunt, and her father thinks him a suitable son-in-law; at the betrothal, however, she rejects him and declares that she will live a maid. Hammon decides to try his luck with a London shopkeeper instead. The English rout the French in battle with relatively few fatal casualties, but Ralph is injured and comes home lame. He finds that Jane, forced to make her own living in his absence, has disappeared. ===== PAGE 113 ===== 1599 Eyre is invited to dine with Oatley in the country, and the shoemakers accompany him to provide musical entertainment. Oatley hopes that the Eyres will talk some marital sense into Rose, but she recognizes one of the morris-dancing shoemakers as Lacy. She summons him the following day on the pretext of fitting her shoes, and they elope. Oatley and Lincoln are decoyed to wait at the wrong church, but the couple are married before they realize their mistake. Meanwhile Hammon courts Jane, who is working as a seamstress, and overcomes her lack of interest by showing her Ralph’s name on a list of the war dead. He orders a new pair of shoes for her to wear at the wedding, and sends one of her old ones as a model; Ralph recognizes it and his fellow shoemakers help him to intercept the wedding party before they reach the church.
