¶In faith Carisophus, you know your selfe best, But I will not call you noddie, but only in iest, And thus I assure you, though I came from schoole, To serue in this Court, I came not yet to be the Kinges foole, Or to fill his eares with seruile squirilitie, That office is yours, you know it right perfectlie, Of Parasites and Scicophants you are a graue bencher, The Kinge féedes you often from his owne trencher, I enuye not your state, nor yet your great fauour, Then grudge not at all, if in my behauiour: I make the Kinge mery, with pleasant vrbanitie, Whom I neuer abused to any mans iniurie. ¶ Why man in the Courte, doo you not sée, Rewardes geuen for vertue, to euery degrée? To rewarde the vnworthy that worlde is done, The Courte is changed, a good thread hath bin sponne Of Dogges woll heretofore, and why? be cause it was liked, And not for that it was best trimmed and picked: But now mens eares are finer, such grosse toyes are not set by, Therfore to a trimmer kynde of myrth my selfe I applye, Wherin though I please, it commeth not of my desert, But of the Kinges fauour. ¶ Bir Lady Carisophus, though you know not Philosophie, et surely you are a better Courtier then I, d yet I not so euyll a courtier that wyll séeme to dispise, uch an old courtier as you so expert and so wyse, ut where as you craue myne & offer your friendship so willingly, ith hart I geue you thankes for this your great curtesie: Assuring of friendship both with tooth and nayle, Whiles life lasteth neuer to fayle. Plot summary

Plot — Damon and Pithias
Damon and Pythias, close friends since their schooldays, arrive in Syracuse, only to discover that the ruler, Dionysius, is a tyrant given to executing his subjects for trivial reasons, and whose court is a magnet for mercenary men. Eager to cement his position there, the parasite Carisophus tries to trap Damon into indiscreet talk, then seizes on his tourist’s interest in the city as a pretext to brand him a spy. Damon is arrested and condemned to death. Pythias seeks the help of the philosopher courtier Aristippus, who refers him to Eubulus, whose advice has already been rejected by Dionysius. Eubulus argues that kings should inspire their subjects’ love, but Dionysius is convinced that terror is the only effective instrument of rule, and orders the execution to proceed. Damon asks for leave to return to Greece to put his affairs in order, with the promise that he will then return to face his death. Dionysius will not accept his word of honour, so Pythias offers himself as pledge. He is imprisoned and Damon released. Dionysius grows more suspicious, to the extent that he will allow only his own daughters to shave him. Two pages induce Grim the collier to pass on the common talk about the Damon case and the court in general. When he expresses interest in being shaved by the King’s daughters, the pages offer to do it in the same style, and take the opportunity to rob him. The day of Pythias’s execution approaches, and Damon has not returned. Carisophus falls from favour after Eubulus warns the King against flatterers in general and Aristippus drops hints against him in particular. However, Eubulus cannot dissuade Dionysius from executing Pythias. Damon arrives just as his friend is about to be beheaded, and they argue over which of them should die to save the other. Dionysius is stunned by the example: he realizes the value of friendship, pardons both, and eschews his former tyranny. The court is purged of vice and Carisophus beaten away.
