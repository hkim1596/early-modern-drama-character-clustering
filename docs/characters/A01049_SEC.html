Spadone, hold, what's the noise? Whom, whom my deare Spadoue, whom? Honest Spadone divine Morosa. Right, just as thon tould'st me, all's right, For which Sirrah Spadone, I will make thee a man, a man, dost heare? I say a man. How now fellow, how now, roring ripe indeed? Nay, nay, by my Mistresse faire eyes I meant no such thing. Hold take another Duccat, as I love new cloathe; Yes or cast old ones, I intended no injury. I know it is. For me never. Shee writes as much, and Spadone, when wee are married. We will revell in such variety of delights. Liveso sumptuously. Feed so deliciously. Enjoy the sweetnes of our yeers. Tumble and wallow in aboundance. That all the world should wonder. How doe the beauties (my dainty knave) live, wish, thinke, and dreame, sirrah ha. Commend me to my choisest, and tell her, the mi nute of her appointment shall be waited on, say to her, she shall find me a man at all points. I fly Nitido, Spadone remember me. Passing neat and exquisite, I protest faire creatures; These honours to our solemnity, are liberall and uncommon; my spouse and my selfe with our posterity, shall prostitute our services, to your bounties, shals not duckling? Ever mine owne, and ever. We are all suitors. Light as a feather. Saist so Spadone? Looke, looke, looke how officious the litle knave is—but— Iumpe with me, I smell a trick too, if I could tell what. Plot summary

Plot — 532
Three female courtiers tempt the King into a hedonistic lifestyle and away from sermons and the advice of his grave counsellors. They lull him to sleep and then transform him into a pig. While the court makes merry, two old men arrive and change him back to human form. The ladies and other courtiers vanish and he is sent for judgement; his plea is unsuccessful and he is carried away by evil spirits.
