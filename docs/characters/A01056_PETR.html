And I'le second My Nephewes suit with importunity. Dare I, my Lord! yes, 'lesse your fact were greater Than a bold womans spleene. Now Nephew, as I told you, since the Duke Hath held the reines of state in his owne hand, Much altered from the man he was before, (As if he were transformed in his mind) To sooth him in his pleasures, amongst whom Is fond Ferentes; one whose pride takes pride In nothing more then to delight his lust; And he (with griefe I speake it) hath, I feare, Too much besotted my vnhappy daughter, My poore Colona; whom, for kinreds sake, As you are noble, as you honour vertue, Perswade to loue her selfe: a word from you May win her more then my entreaties or frownes. His rouing eye, and her inchanting face, The onely dower Nature had ordained T'aduance her to her Bride-bed: She was daughter Vnto a Gentleman of Millaine, no better; Prefer'd to serue in the Duke of Millaine's Court: Where, for her beauty, she was greatly fam'd: And passing late from thence to Monacho, To visit there her Vncle, Paul Bagloone, The Abbot; Fortune (Queene to such blind matches) Presents her to the Dukes eye, on the way As he pursues the Deere: in short, my Lord, He saw her, lou'd her, woo'd her, won her, match'd her, No counsell could diuert him. She is; and to speake truth, I thinke right Noble In her Conditions. The Duke doth come. I shall for euer blesse Your Highnesse for your gracious kind esteems Of my dishartned kinsman; and to adde Encouragement to what you vndertake, I dare affirme, 'tis no important fault Hath caus'd the Dukes distaste. What's this? what's this? Here's fine jugling. Nephew, please you To see your friend to night? 'Tis true; you haue no enemy at Court But her, for whom you pine so much in loue: Then master your affections; I am sory you hug your ruine so. What say you to the proiect I propos'd? You are come as I could wish, my Cozen is resolu'd. Nephew, here is the thing you sent for: Come hither Foole, come 'tis a good foole. Hide not thy folly by vnwise excuse. Thou art vndone, Colona; no entreaties, No warning, no perswasion, could put off The habit of thy dotage on that man Of much deceit, Ferentes: would thine eyes Had seene me in my graue, e're I had knowne The staine of this thine honour. With child, Nibrassa? Alas, my Lord, by whom? How like you this, Colona, 'tis too true? Did not this man protest to be your husband? You counsell wisely, I approue your plot: Thinke on your shames, and who it was that wrought 'em. This fals out happily, Nibrassa. The Duke's on rising; are you ready ho? Within. All ready. I am resolu'd.—fresh lights, I heare 'em comming. Stay, I'le answer for my daughter. It attends your Highnesse. Couzen, one word with you: doth not this Cloud Acquaint you with strange nouelties? The Duke Is lately much distemper'd; what he meanes By journeying now to Luca, is to me A r … ddle; can you cleare my doubt? And see, He comes himselfe; I'le leaue you both together. May we giue credit to your words, my Lord? Speake on your honour. If it be thus, 'twere fit you rather kept A guard about you for your owne defence, Then to be guarded for security Of his Reuenge; he's extreamely mou'd. Well thought on; and in despight of all his rage, Rescue the vertuous Lady. Alas my Lord, this is a wise mans carriage.

Plot summary

Plot — 478
Eteocles and Polynices are elected co-kings of Thebes, ruling in alternate years. At the end of the first year, Eteocles refuses to stand down and banishes Polynices. Six champions join Polynices to besiege Thebes, but, after heavy losses, Polynices offers to settle the case by single combat. He and Eteocles kill one another, and Creon takes the crown. Tiresias prophesies that the walls of Thebes will stand only as long as the last of the seven champions remains alive. After Adrastus’ death, the Argive troops sack the city. 1569
