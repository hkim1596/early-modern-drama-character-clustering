Brother, looke vp, haue not I lost a father? Yes, and would a riuer of fresh teares Turne Lethes streame, and bring him from the wharf, With a North gale of windy blowing sighs, I would expire my soule, become all teares. Mother, pray wher's my father, wher's your husband, Haile to my gratious Queene, here's one at doore Enter Strophius, and speaks. Brings you a message, hee vvill not relate To any, but your selfe, he saies tis sad. Good mother doe your worst, No plague can euer make me more accurst, Nothing is worse then death, that I'll not flye. O my Orestes, O my dearest brother 'Tis he, 'tis he that thus hath drown'd himselfe. Feare not good vncle, there wil be a time To pull him downe, although he yet doth climbe: I hope our gracious mother cannot thinke We doe suspect her loue, witnesse this charge, Which you haue bless'd my armes and soule withall, And as your loue committed it with care, My care shall still defend it with my loue. You neuer heard from my brother, Vncle, Nor from your sonne, they haue beene long away? Vncle, his long delayes make mee surmise, Or he will neuer come, or come with prize; Hee, if now come, hee must not shew himselfe, But liue vnknowne, vnnam'd, or change his name. Come Nurse. Exeunt. In truth I had, but I can heare no news. They discouer themselues ▪ O see my son, welcome my dearest boy. Our brother, our Orestes is come home. What silent place hath smothered you so long! Of what great power haue you counsaile ta'ne, Concerning the great plot you had in hand. Why brother, is the child in any fault, That was vnborne when that our Father dide? And 'tis a lusty boy: O hurt not that. Shal't not, indeed. Why Grandsire, I herin no wrong do finde, Since all these goe, I would not stay behinde. THus neuer lesse alone, then when alone, Where to ourselues we sweetly tell our woes, Thou Vncle, cheife companion to our griefes, And sole partaker of our miseries, Why doe we liue, when now 'tis come to passe, It is scarce knowne that Agamemnon was, He dies far easier, who at first doth drowne, Then he which long doth swim, and then sinks down. O enuious Fates could you not vse me thus? Haue not I griefe inough to burst my heart? Was my life's thred twisted and knit so strong? That the keen edge of all these miseries Can neuer cut it off; Must I beare more? 'Tis all my safety now not to be safe, Are there so many wayes to rid ones life? And can I hit on none? they say that death Is euery where, and yet I finde him not: Tush, but I seeke him not, why my owne hand Might graspe him to me, if I did but striue. Now hand helpe ease my heart, and make a way Stabs her selfe To let out griefe, that hath so long dwelt here, Now knife tha'st don good seruice, there lie by, Heauen well decreed it, nothing life can giue, But euery thing can make vs not liue. NOw Priams ghost, haste, haste, I say to looke, With chearefull eyes on the sinister booke, And there to Hecuba my mother show The tragique story of thy conquered foe. And let Andromacha my sister see, What Agamemnons race is come to be. Now Troy gratifie that most sad doome, Conquered by those that thus themselus or'ecome, Let Greece so florish still, let Argos be Puft with the pride of their great victory. Let it beare Souldiers, so withall it beare Orestes too; now mother neuer feare Argos makes me to laugh, which made thee weep, The Troians in the graue now sweetly sleep. Their sorrow hath the end, now these begin To ouerflow themselues with mutuall sin: And after all, Orestes, we may see, Hath lost his reason, mans sole propertie.

Plot summary

Plot — Orestes
Agamemnon returns home after ten years of war. When he takes his neglected wife Clytemnestra to bed, she and her lover Aegisthus murder him. Roused by bad dreams, his son Orestes finds the body in bed. Feigning innocence, Clytemnestra curses the murderer, while the surviving Trojan glories in the death of the enemy commander: Troy is avenged. Recognizing that he is in danger, Orestes goes into hiding with Pylades until the murderer is identified, and sends a report that, unhinged with grief, he threw himself off a cliff into the sea, and that Pylades also went over trying to stop him. Aegisthus, the next in line, ascends the throne and marries Clytemnestra. She bears him a son, establishing his dynasty, and in celebration he proclaims a general pardon for all crimes. Orestes and Pylades consult the witch Canidia, who shows them a vision of Clytemnestra and Aegisthus committing the murder. They return to court in disguise. Their plotting is overheard by Misander, and they kill him to preserve secrecy, but are immediately arrested. In judging the case, Aegisthus faces two problems: the crime is covered by the general pardon, and each defendant, seeking to save the other, claims that he alone was the murderer. Aegisthus decides not to pass a death sentence on either, and is further impressed when Pylades introduces Orestes as a doctor who has perfected the secret of rejuvenation. Aegisthus asks for a demonstration, using himself and Clytemnestra as the subjects. Agamemnon’s ghost appears to Orestes, urging him to get on with the revenge and take care not to spare his mother. Pylades obtains custody of Aegisthus’ son, who is in the care of Orestes’ sister Electra. When Orestes meets with the King and Queen for their ===== PAGE 235 ===== 1620 rejuvenation, he ties them up, kills their son in front of them, forces them to drink the blood, and finally kills them. He demands his restoration to the throne, but the lords are unwilling to crown a murderer, and instead enthrone Tyndarus, whose first act is to banish Orestes and his accomplices, further stipulating that nobody must give Orestes food. Pylades’ father Strophius dies and Electra kills herself. Orestes goes mad and thinks himself tormented by the Furies. He and Pylades kill one another in a suicide pact, and Tyndarus announces that they are to be buried together in a single tomb.
