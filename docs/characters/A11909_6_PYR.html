And ſhall his ſprights haue no reward their angers to appeyſe? Proude tirant, while proſperity thy ſtomacke doth aduaunce, And cowardly wretch that ſhrinks for feare in caſe of fearefull chaunce. Is yet agayne thy breaſt enflamde, with brand of Venus might? Wilt thou alone ſo oft depriue Achilles of his right? This hand ſhall giue the ſacrifice, the which if thou withſtand. A greater ſlaughter ſhall I make, and worthy Pyrrhus hand. And now to long from Princes ſlaughter doth my hand abide, And meete it were that Polyxene were layde by Priams ſide. My fathers foes we haue them known, ſubmit themſelues humbly, And Pryam preſently yee wot, was glad to craue mercy. But thou for feare not ſtout to rule, lieſt cloſe from foes vp ſhit: Whil thou to Aiax, and Vlyſſes, doſt thy will commit. Great Hector then deſpiſing thee, Achilles ſonges did feare: And Theſſale ſhips in greateſt dread, in quiet peace yet weare. A poinct of mercy ſometime is, what liues in care to kill. Account yee cruell now her death whoſe ſacrifice I craue. Your own deere daughter once yee knowe, your ſelfe to th'aulters gaue. The law doth ſpare no captiues bloud nor wil'th their death to ſtay The conquerour what thing he liſt, may lawfully fulfill. Thus boaſt ye theſe as though in all ye onely bare the ſtroke When Pyrrhus looſed hath the greekes, from bond of ten yeres yoke. The ſeas it do encloſe. Thyeſtes noble ſtocke I know and A reus eke full well, And of the bretherns dire debate, perpetuall fame doth tell. Plot summary

Plot — Troas (Seneca's Sixth Tragedy)
After a ten-year war, Troy has fallen: King Priam had been murdered, the city burned, the women taken into slavery; but Queen Hecuba’s age means that none of the Greek conquerors are interested in her. Achilles’ ghost returns from the dead, demanding the blood of Hecuba’s daughter Polyxena in revenge for his own treacherous murder by Paris. Until she is sacrificed on his tomb, he will not allow the Greek fleet to sail home. Agamemnon is doubtful about this, but Pyrrhus reminds him that he was willing to sacrifice his own daughter to get the fleet to Troy, and Calchas confirms that the price of returning to Greece is the same as the price of leaving it: a virgin’s death. The ghost of Hector appears to Andromache in a dream and warns her to flee from Troy with their son: Calchas has demanded his blood as well as Polyxena’s. She hides the boy in Hector’s tomb, and tells Ulysses he is dead; but Ulysses insists on removing Hector’s ashes if the Greeks cannot have their sacrifice. Andromache produces the child, and Ulysses has him thrown to his death from a tower. Helen is sent to prepare Polyxena to marry Pyrrhus, but she tells Hecuba and Andromache that the Greeks intend a sacrifice, not a wedding. Pyrrhus leads Polyxena away and kills her. A messenger describes the two deaths. Not knowing whom she should grieve for most — husband, daughter, or nephew — Hecuba wishes for death. The women, Hecuba included, are taken away to live in slavery in Greece.
