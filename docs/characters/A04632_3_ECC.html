I. His name reuiues, and lifts me vp from earth. O, which way shall I first conuert my selfe? Or in what moode shall I assay to speake, That (in a moment) I may be deliuered Of the prodigious griefe I goe withall? Witnesse thy youths deare sweets, here spent vntasted, Like a faire taper, with his owne flame wasted. I will (kind MERCVRIE) be briefe as time. Vouchsafe me, I may doe him these last rites, But kisse his flowre, and sing some mourning straine Ouer his watrie hearse. Done presently (good HERMES) bide a little, Suffer my thirstie eye to gaze a while, But e'ene to taste the place, and I am vanisht. Here yong ACTEON fell, pursu'de, and torne By CYNTHIA'S wrath (more eager, then his hounds) And here, (ay me, the place is fatall) see The weeping NIOBE, translated hither From Phrygian mountaines: and by PHoeBE rear'd As the proud trophaee of her sharpe reuenge. But here, O here, the Fountayne of selfe-Loue , In which LATONA, and her carelesse Nymphs , (Regardles of my sorrowes) bathe themselues In hourely pleasures. O, stay: I haue but one poore thought to clothe In ayrie garments, and then (faith) I goe. Henceforth, thou trecherous, and murthering spring, Be euer call'd the Fountayne of selfe-Loue: And with thy water let this curse remaine, (As an inseparate plague) that who but tastes A drop thereof, may, with the instant touch, Grow dotingly enamor'd on themselues. Now, HERMES, I haue finisht. Well. Away. Leaue me. Hence.

Plot — Cynthia's Revels, or The Fountain of Self-Love
Cynthia proclaims a revels at which all are welcome. Mercury and Cupid, who is usually banned from her court, go disguised as pages. Resurrected by Mercury, Echo curses the fountain beside which Narcissus died, so that all who drink of it will be afflicted with self-love. Amorphus does so and reports its wonders, so that the water is much in demand. He undertakes to make Asotus, a citizen’s heir, into a courtier, and gives him intensive training in courtly behaviour and the practice of wooing, but Asotus’ plebeian tastes keep betraying him. While the court ladies impatiently await the arrival of the fountain-water, Asotus courts his father’s old love, Argurion. He accepts her gifts, but then gives them away to the other courtiers in an effort to ingratiate himself. Argurion faints, then transfers her affections to the page Morus. The water arrives and is eagerly consumed. Arete brings the courtiers Cynthia’s instructions that they are to prepare an entertainment for her that evening. They 1600 suggest a masque, but are offended when Arete decides to ask the scholar Criticus to write it: they will only be the performers. Cynthia makes her appearance, and the masque is performed: Anteros (played by Cupid ) introduces four virgins, and a page ( played by. Mercury ) introduces four brothers, all of them representing virtues associated with Cynthia.
