We are all dead men, yet fight So long as legges and Armes laſt. Wound for wound. For honours ſake come downe, and ſave thy Countrey. Tis Edwards, come reſcue Our Generall, and the noble Grimsby. Our Generall and ſtout Grimsby are encloſd With quick-ſets made of ſteele, come fetch them off, Or all is loſt. Loſt, loſt. Tis quite loſt. Yes. Stay noble Grimsby, ere he further paſſe One of us certifie our Generall, Perhaps hee'l not admit him to his preſence. Commonly, when Engliſh ſee at cuffs they are too weak, they fall to fiſhing, and then bait the hook with mercie, and the Kings pardon, at which who bites ha's his ſwallowing ſpoiled for ever, there's no Scot but ſcorns to hang his hope on your Kings promiſes, be it nere ſo ſmoothly gilded. I warrant he would pawn half his Dominions to ſhake hands with Wallace, and be friends. Ten thouſand Crowns. I could pick out five thouſand heads, That I durſt boldly ſell him at that rate. I and Court wind-falls too, Some Engliſh Earldome or ſo, here is none but friends, Should you betray the conference, I care not, I would deny it, and I would oreſway Your proofs tho neere ſo maſſie. Shall we earne this Engliſh gold, ten thouſand crownes? They are ours, hee's dead. Com. Lets be partakers, deare ſir. The happieſt day that ever ſhone on Scotland. How meet him? Our lips are ſeal'd. Wee'le paſſe the wood on foot. What face? he'le kill the Herald ſure. For my part, of ten thouſand crownes by this hand, I do wiſh you there. Time may come, In his Exchequer we may ſhare twice that ſumme. I have beſide with Wallace ſherife of life, Held private conference, who in Longſhancks name, Who ſweares to me we ſhall have good preferment, Beſide the promiſt gold. Enter Wallace. Is the Bruyce come? Who came along with you? Him muſt I kill. I'le looke if Bruce be in ſight yet— Exit. Be arm'd, Bruce with a force comes to betray thee, From ſome villaines hand thy foot-boy is murdered. Comyn my countryman and I have brought A jewell to your Highneſſe, which if 'twere right As 'tis known counterfeit, 'twere worth a kingdome, Wearied with warre, and pittying the deep wounds Which fainting Scotland beares upon her breaſt, And knowing that the onely ſword which gaſhes Her tender ſides, is grip'd in Wallace hands, I in my love to peace, and to the ſafetie Of two great Nations, am the man that layed Snares to entrap this monſter, that devoures So many thouſand lives, the Rebell's tane. I have brought him to your Engliſh Camp, Force would not doo't, but policie, we ſtruck the Stagge To the ground, and thought him dead, but heaven put backe The blow of purpoſe, hee's now come to life, From an aſtoniſhment when we thought him dead, To th'end the world may ſee the publique ſhame Of an Arch-traytor. It can, it doth, and at farre greater miſchiefs. Yes of mine. Here or in Hell.

Plot summary

Plot — The Valiant Scot
PLOT Scotland is under English rule. An attempt is made to dispossess Old Wallace of his office and lands; but Old Wallace partially circumvents it by transferring ownership of the land to his son. King Edward plans to make war on France, and appoints Bruce his general. Scotland is left in the charge of Queen Elinor and a panel of commissioners. Young Selby, son of one of the commissioners, abducts Peggy Graham, intending to marry her by force. Wallace intervenes and kills Young Selby in a duel. Commissioner Selby arrests Peggy and threatens her with execution for the murder. Wallace gives himself up, and Peggy is released. His own execution is forestalled when the Queen orders him brought to court for interrogation. He escapes en route, marries Peggy, and becomes a rebel. Friar Gertrid prophesies that he will be victorious until betrayed by his own blood. The English capture Peggy, Gertrid, and Old Wallace, and Selby has them killed. King Edward sends Wallace an ultimatum: if he will surrender and beg for mercy, he may be spared. Wallace sends an alternative ultimatum back: if Edward will admit himself to be a tyrant, he may be spared. Upon discovering that one of the ambassadors, the Queen’s nephew, has no diplomatic accreditation, he puts the young man to death. He then cuts out an ambassador’s tongue and blinds another, and accompanies them back to King Edward disguised as a lame soldier. The English plan to entice Wallace into a trap, and send another messenger to invite him to a parley; but the stratagem fails because the man appointed to escort the messenger to the Scottish camp is the lame soldier — Wallace himself. Wallace sails to seek help from France, but his ship is wrecked. On the shore, he meets first Bolt, from whom he forcibly takes possession of a washed-up chest of provisions, then Selby, who is now banished from England and penurious, and regrets the execution of Old Wallace. Wallace decides not to take revenge, but while he is away fetching food, another of the fallen commissioners turns up and kills Selby in an argument. Bolt returns and kills the murderer, taking him for Wallace. He is presented to King Edward and rewarded, but later discredited when Wallace turns out to be alive. The Scots prepare to fight, but Wallace is offended to be placed in the rearguard, and withdraws his services.
