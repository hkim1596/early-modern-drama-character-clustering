O poore comparison father, doe they use to line cloth of gold with cloth of gold; no, but with fine, gentle, and easie linings, and such my sister may be, for tho I say it that should not say it, my sister has a good face, a white necke, and a dainty hand, and that may serve for lining for the best cloth of gold in all Spaine. Away, away, speake not so like a Wood monger, I'le Put you downe with a caparison now, doe we not use To graft sweet apples upon crab-tree stocks, doe we Not use to enoculate your Malicatoon upon a Gooseberry? Such is my sisters case now, say that the noble man Would enoculate his Lordship upon my sisters yeomandry, What hurt were in this? would it grieve you to be a Lords brother, or this old woman to have her Lady Daughter to aske, Grauam, how doe you, will you ride Abroad in your Croarch, or your embroderd side-saddle? Pray father talk no more of the moon, but of your son, Not my selfe that am your son and heire, but of your Son in law that shall be, my noble L. Antonio, Lord of Barcelona, and his noble Lady my sister, that shall be. Those we can buy of the Heraulds, you know shee Has cryde Orenges the most of her time here in Ciuill, Now a fine Orenge for her crest, with Ciuillity Written round aboud it would speaks wondrous well, Then a Capon in a Scutchen with a gizard Vnder his left arme, with his spurs vpon his heeles Riding vpon a Leman. I, you say well Father there indeed, A Capon desires no Leman, and therefore Wele hope of both that neither the Lord Proue himselfe a Capon, nor my Sister a Leman. I marry, heres a Lady now will weare her owne haire. And brother eke also. And my brother. If you do, I will forget to call your Lady Sister. You haue my good will too brother. Shall I? why who am I pray? Tush, I can smell the rankest of them all. Well sister, heres my hand, and my heart is some where Here about me too, but I'de be loath to bring him Forth to witnesse, but I will be very carefull. Pha, d'e thinke me for A foole or your brother (sister) As for that, it is sufficient your Ladiship is my sister; oh ye little amiable rogue you, a good face is a good dowry, I see sometimes; when we two tumbled both in a belly together, little did our mother thinke which should have beene the Madam; I might have beene cut the tother way iffaith, if it had pleased the sisters three, if the Mid wife had but knowne my minde when I was borne, I had beene two stone lighter; but much good do thee with thy good fortunes; farewell honourable flesh and bloud, I will deliver to my noble brother, pretty trim Lady, I thinke we are eyde alike; fare thee well, I cannot chuse but see thee as long as I look upon thee. Exit. Plot summary

Plot — All's Lost by Lust
Spain is threatened by an invasion of Moors, but an ancient curse prevents King Roderick from opening his treasury to pay his troops. Julianus is appointed general and goes to the war. Roderick’s intention is to get Julianus out of the way while he attempts to seduce Jacinta, his daughter; Lothario engages a professional bawd to help. Jacinta resists Roderick’s advances, so he rapes her. She is then imprisoned in Lothario’s custody, but she escapes while he is asleep. Meanwhile, Antonio is in love with the base-born Margaretta, and procures her family’s consent to marry her before he leaves for the war. At the front, the Moors are reluctant to give battle, and seem undermanned. Antonio is attracted to Dionysia, the Castellan’s pugnacious daughter, and she encourages his advances. Lazarello advises him to keep quiet about his existing marriage, and pay Margaretta off. Margaretta learns that Antonio has married Dionysia bigamously, and sends her brother to the wars with a letter for him. Antonio is in a dilemma: he loves both women. Lazarello proposes that he should take Antonio’s place at the consummation of his marriage with Margaretta: she will mistake him for Antonio in the dark, and afterwards will be too ashamed to claim her husband. The plan is successful only to the extent that Lazarello is taken to be Antonio by the vindictive Margaretta: she strangles him in his bed. Julianus returns from war victorious, but Roderick orders him to stay away from court, and demands that he give up his prisoners. Jacinta tells him about the rape, and he allies with his generals.and Moorish prisoners to seek revenge. To cement the alliance, the Moorish King Muly Mumen asks for Jacinta as his wife; Julianus will not force her against her will, but gives him permission to woo her. With his army in revolt, Roderick needs to hire mercenaries, and breaks open the cursed treasury. There he sees a vision of his own defeat and deposition by the Moors. Lothario persuades Jaques the clown to hang him, and, after the battle goes against him, Roderick flees. Muly Mumen becomes King of Spain. Antonio criticizes Julianus for allowing the Moors in, and Julianus kills him. He leaves two grieving widows: Margaretta stabs herself and, not to be outdone, Dionysia wills herself to die too. Jacinta refuses to marry Muly Mumen, so he has her tongue cut out and Julianus blinded.
