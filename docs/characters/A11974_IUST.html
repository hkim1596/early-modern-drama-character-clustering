He that was in question for the rob'ry? What to Yorke? call him backe againe. I am sure he is to the hearing of any thing good, goe plucke him by the elbow, I must speake with him. Si Iohn Falstaffe, a word with you. I talke not of his maiesty, you would not come when I sent for you. Well, God mend him, I pray you let me speake with you. What tell you me of it, be it as it is. I think you are falne into the disease, for you heare no what I say to you. To punish you by the heeles, would amend the atten tion of your eares, and I care not if I doe become your phisitian. I sent for you when there were matters against you for your life to come speake with me. Wel, the truth is sir Iohn, you liue in great infamy. Your meanes are very slender, and your waste is great. You haue misled the youthfull prince. Wel, I am loth to gall a new heald wound, your daies seruice at Shrewsbury, hath a little guilded ouer your nights exploit on Gadshill, you may thanke th vnquiet time, for your quiet oreposting that action. But since all is well, keepe it so, wake not a sleeping wolfe. VVhat you are as a candle, the better part, burnt out. There is not a white haire in your face, but should haue his effect of grauity. You ollow the yong prince vp and downe, like his ill angell. It is very iust, look, here comes good sir Iohn, giue me your good hand, giue me your worshippes good hand, by my troth you like well, and beare your yeeres very well, welcome good sir Iohn. How doth the King? I hope not dead. I would his Maiestie had calld me with him: The seruice that I truely did his life, Hath left me open to all iniuries. I know he doth not, and do arme my selfe To welcome the condition of the time, Which cannot looke more hideously vpon me, Than I haue drawne it in my fantasie. O God, I feare all will be ouer-turnd. Peace be with vs, lest we be heauier. Sweet princes, what I did, I did in honor, Led by th impartiall conduct of my soule. And neuer shall you see that I will begge A ragged and forestald remission, If truth and vpright innocencie faile me. Ile to the King my maister that is dead, And tell him who hath sent me after him ▪ Enter the Prince and Blu t Good morrow, and God saue your maiestie. I am assurde, if I be measurde rightly, Your maiesty hath no iust cause to hate me. I then did vse the person of your father, The image of his power lay then in me, And in th'administration of his law, Whiles I was busie for the common wealth, Your Highnesse pleased to forget my place, The maiestie and power of law and iustice, The image of the King whom I presented, And strooke me in my very seate of iudgement, Whereon, as an offendor to your father,) I gaue bold way to my authority, And did commit you: if the deed were ill, Be you contented, wearing now the garland, To haue a sonne set your decrees at naught? To plucke downe Iustice from your awful bench? To trip the course of law and blunt the sword, That guards the peace and safeue of your person? Nay more, to spurne at your most royall image, And mocke your workings in a second body? Question your royall thoughts, make the case yours, Be now the father, and propose a sonne, Heare your owne dignity so much prophan'd, See your most dreadfull lawes so loosely slighted, Behold your selfe so by a sonne disdained: And then imagine me taking your part, And in your power soft silencing your sonne, After this cold considerance sentence me, And as you are a King, speake in your state, What I haue done that misbecame my place, My person, or my lieges soueraigntie. Haue you your wits? know you what tis you speake? I cannot now speake, I will heare you soone, take them away. exeunt. And so they are. He hath.

Plot summary

Plot — 2 Henry the Fourth
After the battle of Shrewsbury, there are contradictory rumours abroad as to the outcome: some say that the King and his son have vanquished Lord Hotspur of Lancaster, whereas in fact they won. Now Lord John of Lancaster is travelling north with a power, including Sir John Falstaff, to crush the regrouping remnants of the Percy rebellion. Mistress Quickly tries to have Falstaff arrested for debt, while threatens to prevent him setting out for London, but he talks his way out of it. Back from a military expedition in Wales, Prince Henry and Poins disguise themselves as drawers and eavesdrop on Falstaff’s conversation: he says uncomplimentary things about them, but Falstaff excuses himself inventively when confronted. Northumberland’s family persuade him to flee to Scotland rather than join the other northern rebels. Falstaff musters troops in Gloucestershire, but the best men bribe their way out, leaving him with the least suitable soldiers. Prince John sees the rebels and agrees to redress their grievances if they disband their army; but when they do so, he has them arrested for treason. Falstaff arrives in the north late, but meets the rebel Sir John Colville, who promptly surrenders without a fight, having heard Falstaff’s reputation. Prince John returns to court, but Falstaff goes via Gloucestershire to visit Justice Shallow. The sickly King prepares for death, and worries about the kingdom’s future in the hands of his eldest son. News arrives of the defeat of all the rebels. Prince Henry finds his father asleep with the crown beside him, and, thinking him dead, tries it on. The King wakes to find his crown gone. Brought back, the Prince excuses himself, and the King gives deathbed advice: he should pursue an aggressive foreign policy to distract from internal dissent. He dies in the Jerusalem chamber, fulfilling a prophecy that he will die in Jerusalem. Henry V is not the King everyone expected the Prince to be. He confirms in office the Lord Chief Justice, who had imprisoned him for his misdemeanours. Pistol brings to Gloucestershire news of his accession, and Falstaff anticipates power for himself and his associates. He is disappointed: when he accosts the King at his coronation, Henry refuses to acknowledge him, and banishes him — though with a pension to ensure that poverty does not lead him back into crime.
