¶Nor to doe it is our pretence, ¶Loue to good Gentleman. ¶Nay good Mayster Doctor let vs haue your helpe. ¶Why Syr are you a Iugler now, I pray you aunswere me, What is the iugling staffe you craue, if graunted it may be. ¶By God I would spende my best cote to fish within her poole, And yet my father in fishers arte was neuer but a foole. But what for that it forceth not, if we might spie a place, And time that well may serue the turne, I will put forth the case, But we are counted Elders here, and doe the people guide, And that we doe must secrete be, least that we be espide, For God, or for his threatninges, I passe it not a straw, But for myne honour in this world, is it I stand in aw. ¶Why friend know you not me? ¶Mayster Uoluptas for the Money force not, he is a good fellow, And will otherwyse deserue it, I doe right well know, ¶Oh excellent wit, and friendship irrecompens ble, We will requyte you with the lyke, i euer we be able. ¶As we two in the Orcharde walkte, and none but we alone, In came this woman with two maydes, but straight she sent them home. And wayghty charge she gaue them both, we could but muse wherfore In any case that they should shut, and spar the Orchard dore, With that a yong man straight slept forth, that hidden secrete lay. And came to her right shamefullye, and found her Uenus play. For vs to see the ende of this, in secrete corner stoode, The open sight of this fowle deede, appalde our auncient bloode. And when we saw with both our eyes, that they had lyen togither, Pretending for to take the wretch, our selues we did discouer. But when we layde our handes of him, we could not hold him still, His strength was of much fortitude, and ours very ill, This done, he hyde him to the gate, and so from thence away, For reason sayth one usty blood, two weake men cannot stay. Now when this woman thus we tooke, Dame (quoth we) what is he, That thus your husbandes bed hath fyld, as we with sorrow sée, But she because that tyme would helpe, to frame a lye full well, And craftily then could hold her peace, and spared that to tell. So this the matter is in déede, and both we witnesse be, And as we meane to aunswere it, we say but what we sée. ¶Under a Mulbery trée I saw their conference, And vnder that trée she committed her offence, And meaning not for to conceale that faulte, but it to tell, We marke the order and the trée, we also markt it well. ¶And to me Lord doe not account, the number of my sinnes, Reiect the same and heare me now, for now my plaint begins. And though the falshood of the flesh, in welth would let me know, Yet in this my extremitie, doe thou thy mercie show. The whole doe néede no medicine, it is bought for thée sicke, So mercie Lorde doe helpe the froward, and those against the kick. Not iustice Lord, but mercie we doe craue to ease our bandes. And by thy mercie both we yéelde our soules into thy handes.

Plot summary

Plot — The Most Virtuous and Godly Susanna
Satan commissions Ill Report to bring about the downfall of Susanna, a paragon of virtue. Since pride, gluttony, envy, sloth, and covetousness have failed to achieve this, Ill Report decides to use lust. He selects the elders Voluptas and Sensualitas as his instruments of temptation, and they are tempted in turn when they see Susanna. Susanna’s husband Joachim leaves to inspect his realm, leaving the elders to administer justice in his absence. The elders spy on Susanna as she bathes in her orchard after dinner, and proposition her for sex. When she refuses, they slander her with whoredom. Meanwhile Joachim discovers that the elders are corrupt and hated by the people. The elders’ perjured testimony ensures that Susanna is sentenced to death, but God intervenes, sending Daniel to reveal the truth. The elders are stoned, and die penitent. Ill Report is hanged, and a frustrated Satan arrives to carry him to hell and tormenters for his failure. Susanna and her family count their blessings.
