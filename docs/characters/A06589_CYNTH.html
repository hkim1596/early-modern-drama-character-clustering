IS the report true, that Endimion is striken into such a dead sleep, that nothing can either wake him or mooue him? What maketh you Tellus to bee so short? the time was Endimion onely was. What haue we heare before my face, these vn seemely and malepart ouerthwarts? I will tame your tongues, and your thoughts, and make your speeches an swerable to your dueties, and your conceits fitte for my dignitie, els will I banish you both my person and the worlde. If you bee so deere to him, howe happeneth it you neither go to see him, nor search for remedy for him? Presumptuous gyrle, I will make thy tongue an example of vnrecouerable displeasure, Corsites carry her to the Castle in the Deserte, there to remaine and weaue. No more Floscula, pittie dooth him no good, I would any thing els might, and I vowe by the vnspot ted honour of a Ladie he should not misse it: but is this all Giptes, that is to be done? Corsites what say you, will you vndertake this? Well, I will prouide others. Pithagoras and Giptes, you shall yet remaine in my Courte, till I heare what may be done in this matter. Let vs goe in. Eumenides, it cannot sinke into my heade, that I should bee signified by that sacred Fountaine, for many thinges are there in the worlde, to which those words may bee applyed. Will you not yet tell me her name? Well, let vs to Endimion. I will not be so state lie (good Endimion ) not to stoope to doe thee good: and if thy libertie consist in a kisse from mee, thou shalt haue it. And although my mouth hath beene heere tofore as vntouched as my thoughts, yet now to recouer thy life, (though to restore thy youth it be impossible) I will do that to Endimion, which yet neuer mortall man coulde bost of heretofore, nor shall euer hope for heereafter. Soft Eumenides, stand still. I commaund thee once againe stirre not: I wil stand behinde him. Endimion, speake sweete Endimion, knowest thou not Cynthia? I am Cynthia, and thou Endimion. You haue good cause to remember Eumeni des, who hath for thy safetie forsaken his owne solace. Well Endimion begin. A straunge sight. Giptes at our better leysure shall expound it. Poore Endimion, how wast thou affrighted? What els? I am content to bee dieted, therefore lette vs in. Eumenides, see that Endimion bee well tended, least eyther eating immoderatlie, or sleeping againe too long, hee fall into a deadly surfette, or into his former sleepe. See this also bee proclaimed, that whosoeuer will discouer this practise, shall haue of Cynthia infinite thankes, and no small rewardes. What younge againe? then it is pittie to pu nish Tellus. Endimion, you must nowe tell who Eumenides shrineth for his Saint. Semele Eumenides? is it Semele? the very waspe of all women, whose tongue stingeth as much as an Ad ders tooth? Nay sith Endimion is restored, wee will haue all parties pleased. Semele, are you content after so long triall of his faith, such rare secresie, such vnspotted loue, to take Eumenides? Why speake you not? Not a word? It is true Endimion. Eumenides, take Semele. Take her I say. Speakes the Parrat? shee shall nod heereafter with signes: cut off her tongue, nay her heade, that ha uing a seruant of honourable birth, honest manners, 〈◊〉 true loue, will not be perswaded. I thanke you Semele. What shall become of Tellus? Tellus you know Endimion is vowed to a seruice, from which death cannot remooue him.
