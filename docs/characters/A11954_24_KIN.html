Lady mine proceed. Taxation? Wherein? and what Taxation? My Lord Cardinall, You that are blam'd for it alike with vs, Know you of this Taxation? Still Exaction: The nature of it, in what kinde let's know, Is this Exaction? By my life, This is againſt our pleaſure. Things done well, And with a care, exempt themſelues from feare: Things done without example, in their iſſue Are to be fear'd. Haue you a Preſident Of this Commiſſion? I beleeue, not any. We muſt not rend our Subiects from our Lawes, And ſticke them in our Will. Sixt part of each? A trembling Contribution; why we take From euery Tree, lop, barke, and part o' th' Timber: And though we leaue it with a roote thus hackt, The Ayre will drinke the Sap. To euery County Where this is queſtion'd, ſend our Letters, with Free pardon to each man that has deny'de The force of this Commiſſion: pray looke too't; I put it to your care. It grieues many: The Gentleman is Learn'd, and a moſt rare Speaker, To Nature none more bound; his trayning ſuch, That he may furniſh and inſtruct great Teachers, And neuer ſeeke for ayd out of himſelfe: yet ſee, When theſe ſo Noble benefits ſhall proue Not well diſpos'd, the minde growing once corrupt, They turne to vicious formes, ten times more vgly Then euer they were faire. Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
