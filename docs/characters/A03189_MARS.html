Full speech

I knew loues Queene could not be long vnkind, Though (whil'st I absent, to teach Armes in Thrace ) You tooke th'aduangtage to forget your Mars, To doate on Adon, and Anchises too; Yet (those worne out) let vs renue our loues, And practise our first amorous dalliance. Come shall we now, whilst Vulcan plyes his forge, Sweats at his Anuill, choakes himselfe with dust, And labours at his bellowes, kisse and toy? Whom need we feare? Vnlesse the Sunne, who now the lower world Lights with his beames; I meane the Antipodes, The tell-tale blab is busie now else-where: And I will set to watch at the caues doore, My trusty groome, who (ere the Sunne shall rise With his bright beames to light our Hemispheare) Shall waken vs. Be that my charge. Wher's Gallus? Syrrah, you know this Lady. Syrrah attend, this night yon Queene and I Must haue some priuate conference, in yon caue, Where whilst we stay, 'tmust be thy care to watch That no suspicious eye pry through these chinks, Especially I warne thee of the Sunnes. See thou do call vs, e're the Sunne vprise, But sleepe not for by all my Armes I sweare, If by thy carelesse floth, or negligence We be describe, thy body I'le translate, To some strange Monster. Com enter then faire Queene, we are secure, Now safely maist thou claspe the God of warre, Spight of Sunne, Moone, or a iealous starre. Venus awake, wee haue ore-slept our selues, The Sunne's aboue in his diurnall taske, I saw his piercing beames pry through a cranny, And cast his right eye full vpon our bed. Nay rather, how will Vulcan tast our sport? He might suspect, but neuer proue till now, Where is the villaine Gallus set to watch? Awake thou drowsy Groome, thy chaflisement Shall exceed torture. Looke, hast thou eies? is not the Sun two howres Mounted aloft? hath he not seene theesleeping At the Caues dore, Yea beheld vs too? Speake, how canst thou excuse this? Behold, this is thy doome, thy negligence Thus I'le chastice, thou shalt thy humane shape Henceforth forgo, I will translate thy body Into a bird shall euer beare thy name, Bee Gallus still, a Cocke, and be thy nature Euer hereafter this; to watch the Sunne, And by thy crowes and clamours warne the world Two howres before he rise, that the Sunne comes Clap with thy wings, and with thy shrieking loud, Proclaime his comming when thou thrice hast crowed. I will to Thrace, go you to Lemnos. Content faire Queene of loue. For more, he cannot be much more displeas'd, Let's score on still, and make our reckoning full, As yet … alas faire Queene, the debts but small, Make vp the summe, and answere once for all. Once more in spight of Phoebus and these eies, That dog our pastimes, we are closely met, And whilst the Cuckold Vulcan blowes the fire, Our amorous soules their sportiue blisse conspire. I am arm'd for thee, prepare thee, for this night Il'e breast to breast dare thee to single fight. Betraid? bound? catcht? release me, or by Ioue, Thou dy'st what ere thou art. The Gods are all spectators of our shame, And laugh at vs.

Plot summary

Plot — The Brazen Age (The Age of Brass)
Hercules and the shape-changing Achelous fight for the hand of Dejanira; Achelous transforms himself into a series of monsters, but Hercules retains the upper hand, and Achelous concedes defeat after one of his bull’s horns is pulled off (later to become the cornucopia). Hercules takes Dejanira home, and en route they meet Nessus, the only survivor of Hercules’ massacre of the centaurs. Feigning friendship, the vindictive Nessus offers to carry Dejanira across the river on his back; but when he gets to the other side, he attempts to abduct her. Hercules shoots him and, dying, he tells Dejanira that his blood is a love-charm, which she should use on Hercules if he ever seems interested in another woman; in fact, it is poisonous. Hercules travels to Egypt, where King Busiris sacrifices foreigners, and kills him on his own altar, but refuses to accept his crown. During his absence, Diana, offended that King Oeneus has sacrificed to all the gods but her, sends a boar to ravage the Calydonian countryside. The lords of Greece gather to hunt the boar. One of them is Adonis, and Venus falls in love with him; he insists on hunting, the boar kills him, and she transforms him into a flower. Atalanta joins the hunt, and Meleager falls in love with her; when the boar is killed, he is awarded its head, but he offends his companions by presenting it to her. In the ensuing fight, Meleager kills his uncles. His mother Althea is torn between her duty to her brothers and to her son. Meleager’s life is in her power, for, when he was born, the Fates visited her, pointed out a brand in the fire, and foretold that the child would live until the brand was burnt up; she rescued it and has kept it safe ever since. Now she burns the brand, and he dies; in remorse, she kills herself with his sword. Hercules joins Jason and the Argonauts on their quest to retrieve the golden fleece from Colchis. On the way they stop off at Troy, where King Laomedon faces an unwelcome obligation to sacrifice his daughter Hesione: he accepted a loan from Neptune’s priests to finance building the city walls, but failed to repay it when the work was complete, and retribution came in the form of a sea monster who must be appeased with the monthly sacrifice of a young Trojan woman; this month the fatal lot has fallen on Hesione.
