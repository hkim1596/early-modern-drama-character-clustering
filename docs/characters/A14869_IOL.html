What does this meane, do you serue Proces on me? Why, what's this, I pray? In good season: I hope he will not haue my Allegiance stretcht to the vndoing of my selfe. Not for a Traytor, does he? Yet Kings many times Know meerly but mens outsides, was this commendation Voluntary, thinke you? You are my Brother. Sir, I haue often told you, I am so little my owne to dispose that way, That I can neuer be his. Now you expresse your selfe most nobly. Your Imprecation has vndone me for euer. No sir. Rather a damnable cunning, To haue me goe about to giu't away, Without consent of my soule. Husband? I hate my selfe for being thus enforst, You may soone iudge then what I thinke of you Which are the cause of it. Reach me the Caskanet, I am studying Sir, To take an Inuentory of all that's mine. To make you a Deed of gift. Oh sir, I am bewitcht. Most certaine, I am forespoken, To be married to another: can you euer thinke That I shall euer thriue in't? Am I not then bewitcht? All comfort I can teach my selfe is this, There is a time left for me to dye nobly, When I cannot liue so? By Lord Ercole, my Mother, and by Brother. But he may returne too soone. Fye vpon thee, prethee leaue vs. None vpon your honour? Oh that he should dye so soone. Oh take heed, a graue is a rotten foundation. How's this? issue by him, he dead, and I a Virgin! So then I conceaue you, My conceaued child must proue your Bastard. Tis a pretty feat this, but I am not capable of it. By Contarino, doe not knit the brow, The Precontract shall iustifie it, it shall: Nay, I will get some singular fine Churchman, Or tho he be a plurall one, shall affirme, He coupled vs together Your hopes are dasht then, since your Votaries issue Must not inherit the land. And what resemblance think you, would they haue To one another? Twinnes are still alike: But this is not your ayme, you would haue your child Inherite Ercoles Land,—Oh my sad soule, Haue you not made me yet wretched ynough, But after all this frostie age in youth, Which you haue witcht vpon me, you will seeke To poyson my Fame. No sir, I did but faine it, To a fatall purpose, as I thought. If you had lou'd or tendred my deare honour, You would haue lockt your ponyard in my heart, When I nam'd I was with child; but I must liue To linger out, till the consumption of my owne Sorrow kill me. Pray what's that sir? I remember since his hurt, Shee has bene very passionately enquiring, After his health. Professing as you say, Loue to my mother: wherefore did he make Me his heyre? To fight: oh well remembred, If he lou'd my mother, wherefore did he loose His life in my quarrell? How came you by this wretched knowledge? I neuer did find any thing ith world, Turne my blood so much as this: here's such a conflict, Betweene apparant presumption, and vnbeleefe, That I shall dye in't. Oh, if there be another world i'th Moone, As some fantasticks dreame, I could wish all men, The whole race of them, for their inconstancy, Sent thither to people that. Why, I protest, I now affect the Lord Ercoles memory, Better then the others. I doe call any thing to witnesse, That the diuine Law prescribed vs To strengthen an oath, were he liuing and in health, I would neuer mary with him. Nay, since I haue found the world So false to me, Ile be as false to it; I will mother this child for you. Most certainly it will be guile part of my sorrow. But doe you not thinke I shall haue a horrible strong breath now. Oh, with keeping your counsel, tis so terrible foule, Must I dissemble dishonestie? you haue diuers Counterfeit honestie: but I hope here's none Will take exceptions; I now must practise The art of a great bellyed woman, and goe faine Their qualmes and swoundings. Dine in my bed some two houres after noone. I haue a strange conceit now. I haue knowen some women when they were with child, Haue long'd to beat their Husbands: what if I, To keepe decorum, exercise my longing Vpon my Taylor that way, and noddle him soundly, Heele make the larger Bill for't. Oh my phantasticall sorrow, Cannot I now be miserable enough, Vnlesse I weare a pyde fooles coat: Nay worse, for when our passions Such giddy and vncertaine changes breed, We are neuer well, till we are mad indeed. Say friend, are you quicke with child? How could you know Of your first child when you quic ned? Within there. Beare this Letter To the Lord Ercole. Now wench, I am for thee All the world ouer. Sir, I was running away From the rumour of this Combate: I fled likewise, From the vntrue report my brother spread To his politike ends, that I was got with child.

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
