I maruell what the Duke meant to make such an idle vow. Is't not idle, to sweare to graunt his Daugh ter any thing she shall aske on her byrth day? she may aske an impossible thing: and I pray heauen she doe not aske an vnfit thing at one time or other; tis dan gerous trusting a mans vow vpon the discretion on's daughter. Hee's acquainted with 'em before. So is Leucippus her Brother. That were a folly in her, hauing refus'd all the Great Princes in one part of the world: Sheele dye a Maide. I know not yet I am so full of wonder, We shall be gods our selues shortly, And we pull'em out of heauen o'this fashion. The Duke hee's old and past it, he would Neuer haue brought such a plague vpon the land else, Tis worse then Sword and Famine: Yet to say truth, we haue deseru'd it, we haue liu'd So wickedly, euery man at his liuery, and wou'd that Wou'd haue suffis'd vs: we murmurd at this Blessing, that was nothing; and cryde out to the God for endlesse pleasures, he heard vs, And supplyed vs, and our women were new still As we need'em: yet we like beasts still cryde, Poore men can number their woers, giue vs Abundance: we had it, and this curse with all. Would I were married, som what has some sauour The race of Gentry will quite run out now, Tis onely left to husbands: if younger sisters Take not the greater charitie tis lawfull. Farewell my Lords, Ile eene take vp what Rent I can before the day, I feare the yeere will fall out ill. Gentlemen, this is a strange peece of Iustice, To put the wretched Dwarfe to death because She doted on him; Is she not a woman, and Subiect to those mad figaries her whole Sexe Is infected with? Had she lou'd you, or you, or I, Or all on's, (as indeed the more the merryer still With them) must we therefore haue our heads par'd With a Hatchet? So she may loue all the Nobility Out ath Duke dome in a month, and let the raskals in. I cannot tell, I would be loth to feele it: But the best is, she loues not proper men, we three Were in wise cases else: but make me know this need. Pray God she doe: but was the Sprat behea ded, or did they swing him about like a chickin, and so breake his necke. That might haue beene deducted. Faith I would haue had him rosted like a war den in a browne paper, and no more talke ont: or a teather stucke in's head, like a Quaile: or a hangd him in a Dog-coller: what should hee be beheaded? wee shall haue it grow so base shortly, Gentlemen will be out of loue with it. From an olde leacherous payre of breeches that lay vpon a wench to keepe her warme: for cer tainely they are no mans worke: and I am sure a Mon key would get one of the guard to this fellow, he was no bigger then a small Portmantu, and much about that making, if t'ad legs. Why then hee will come home I know not when: you shall pardon me, Ile talke no more of this subiect, but say gods be with him where ere he is, and sende him well home againe: For why, hee is gone, or when he will returne, let them know that directed him: Onely this, there's mad Moriscoes in the state; but what they are, Ile tell you when I know. Come, lets goe, heare all, and say nothing. I, I, the King may marry whom's list, lets talke of other matters. Yes, yes, hee was sent post for yesterday, lets make haste, weele see how his new Mother-in-law will entertaine him. But marke whats done. Nis. regard not shew Yes: and hauing had but meane bringing vp, it talks the pretlest and innocentlest, the Queene wilbe so angry to heare her betray her breeding by her lan guage: but I am perswaded shee's well dispos'd. No, thats too honest: we shall haue other Liberall Sciences taught vs too soone; Lying, and Flattering, those are the studies now: And Murther shortly I know, wil be humanity Gent. If we liue here we must be knaues, beleeue it. You must not be amongst vs then. This is one of her Ferrets that shee bolts bu sinesse out withall: this fellow, if hee were well ript, has all the linings of a knaue within him: how slye he lookes? Faith, and all the harme that I can finde in mine is, that they are not payd for, let him make what he can of that, so he discharge that. Come, lets goe. Can you blame it, and looke vpon such a deed as shall be done this morning? Within this houre they say. I care not if my throat were next: for to liue still, and liue heere, were but to growe fat for the Shambles. I a plague on him, hee's too excellent at annes. If I could doe him any good I would goe, The bare sight else will but afflict my spirit, my prayers shall be as neere him as your eyes: As you finde him setled, remember my loue and ser uice ro his Grace. Farewel to all our happinesse, a long farewel. Thou angry power, whether of heauen or hell, That layst this sharpe correction on our Kingdome For our offences, infinite and mighty, O heare me, and at length be pleas'd, be pleas'd With pitty to draw backe thy vengeance, Too heauy for our weaknesse; and accept, (Since it is your discretion, heauenly Wisedomes, To haue it so) this sacrifice for all, That now is flying to your happinesse, Onely for you most fit: let all our sinnes suffer in him. Gods, whats the matter! I hope tis ioy. How now my Lords? How! Heauen, heauen I thanke thee, What a slaue was I to haue my hand so farre from This braue rescue, 'tad beene a thing to bragge on When I was olde. Shall we runne for a wager to the Next Temple and giue thanks? She is dead already. There is a full point. I would your Maiestie would haste for setling of the people.

Plot — Cupid's Revenge
Leontius, King of Lycia, promises his daughter Hydaspes anything she asks for on her birthday. She wants him to ban the cult of Cupid in Lycia: the god’s naked statues are to be taken down and burnt, and Cupid-worship to be made a capital offence. Leontius agrees, persuaded that the religion is the cause of national immorality. His nephew Ismenus demurs: he will promulgate the Duke’s orders, but will himself take no hand in obeying them. As his temples are dismantled, Cupid declares vengeance on the kingdom. The Lycian royal family begins to behave with inappropriate amorousness. Hydaspes fixates on Zoilus, a deformed dwarf, and asks to be allowed to marry him; her father’s response is to have him beheaded, and she pines to death. Prince Leucippus falls for Bacha, a strumpet only interested in his wealth and power, but, questioned by his father, denies sexual misbehaviour and praises her virtue and chastity. Leontius is so impressed that he sends Leucippus away ===== PAGE 378 ===== and woos Bacha for himself, affecting youthful clothing and behaviour. Bacha becomes Queen of Lycia. When Leucippus returns to court, Bacha tries to seduce him again. He refuses to cuckold his father, and, annoyed at his refusal, she plots his death; this will leave her daughter Urania as the only heir to the throne. She makes Leontius jealous of his son’s popularity with the people and courtiers, and persuades him that Leucippus is plotting his assassination. The court parasite Timantus tells Leucippus that the King is to discuss his arrest at a council, and arranges to place him where he can eavesdrop and be forewarned of the charges. It is a ruse: Leucippus is arrested and sentenced to death, and Urania is proclaimed heir to the throne. At the execution, the citizens rise in rebellion and rescue the Prince from the scaffold. As Leontius sickens, Bacha schemes to marry Urania to a foreign prince; but she is in love with Leucippus, locates him through the good offices of Ismenus, and follows him posing as a page. Bacha engages Timantus to murder Leucippus. He finds the Prince by intercepting a letter from Ismenus; but his attack fails when Urania interposes her own body and is mortally wounded. Timantus is killed, but reveals her true identity before he dies. Ismenus brings Leucippus news that Leontius is dead. He declares that he will leave Bacha to heaven’s justice, but she stabs him.
