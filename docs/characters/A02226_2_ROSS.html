Behold! I stand amaz'd: Sir! ease my heart. A King lesse than a man! more than a God! I know not where to stay, nor how to part. God hath ordain'd that wickednesse shall die: Sir! who is guiltie? Mustapha, or I? Who will endure the sentence he may giue, Betweene you two? He must be King that liues. Your graue prepared is among your owne: Neighbours, Church, People, Souldiers, made the Stage, Where Hope, and Youth shall ruine Feare, and Age. Most wretched I, rais'd to be ouerthrowne. If you will die, then am I lost in you; And die you must, if you beleeue your owne. If he shall liue; then am I prou'd vntrue, Hated by him, whom you haue plac'd aboue, Lost vnto you, and ruin'd by my loue. " Ah Confidence! thou Glorie of the ill! The latitude of Princes is Desire, Which all, it hath enioy'd, still carries higher. Say you the World is left to my deuotion? Who question'd am both in my State, and Fame, Must lose my Will, and cannot lose my Shame: For Mustapha, long since condemn'd to die, Now liues againe. To boast of mariage then what ground haue I? Ah seruile Sex! must Yokes our honor be, To make our owne loues our captiuity? No Rossa, no: looke not in Languisht Wit, For none can stand on Fortunes steepe with it. " Thinke Innocencie harme; Vertue dishonor; " Wound Truth; and ouerweigh the scale of Right: Sexes haue wayes apart; States haue their fashions: The vertues of Authoritie are Passions. Those silly natures apt to louingnesse, Which euer must in others power liue, With Doubt become more fond, with Wrong more thral: Feare here wants eyes, Hate hath no sting at all. This masse of Passions who can deale withall? Too nice, and subtile is Inconstancy. Shall Wrong faire-written still in Patience be? Must my desire so many Cautions haue, And waite on those thoughts that haue worshipt me? I cannot beare this Mediocrity. Yet tell: What is the worst? O cruell Fates! that doe in Loue plant Woe, And in elights make our Disasters grow. But speake: What hath she done? Plot summary

Plot — Mustapha
Seeking to secure the succession for her son Zanger, Rosa persuades her husband Suleiman that his son Mustapha is plotting against him. Suleiman summons Mustapha to court. His pasha Achmat tries to persuade him of Mustapha’s innocence. Mustapha’s stepsister Camaena attempts to warn him that his life is in danger, but her message is not delivered: it comes instead to Rosa’s attention, and precipitates Camaena’s murder by her mother. Rosa tells Suleiman that her unwarned actions substantiate her tale of Mustapha’s conspiracy: Camaena was also implicated, so she had to die. Heli the priest advises Mustapha not to throw his life away by going to court, but he considers it a higher duty to obey a royal command. He presents himself before Suleiman, who has him strangled. Rosa realizes that the greatest influence on Suleiman is now Achmat, and makes arrangements to have him murdered. But all her plans come to nothing when a disgusted Zanger confronts her with her crimes and commits suicide. The murder also precipitates a popular rebellion against Suleiman, but Achmat refuses to act against him: whatever his guilt as a man, as a ruler he must still command his subjects’ loyalty.
