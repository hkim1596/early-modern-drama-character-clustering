Mighty Priest, as you Respect the safety of the King, you must Make haste, the Court is up in armes, and he Calls for his sword. His owne wilde thoughts, and apprehension Of what, he sayes, was in his sleepe presented, He calls upon his Guard, and railes upon 'em, When they appeare with no more armes, and swearos That every man shall weare a Tun of Iron. You will finde Ilands Peopled with Squirrils, Rate, and Crowes, and 〈◊〉 , Where you may better plant, my reverend Moles. I am his eldest son, he shall find me reasonable, He may doe worse, considering how long I have been of age. He is well agen. Love innocence, the gods have thus reveng'd In your sonnes tragedy: Draw not a greater Vp on your self and this faire Iland, by Threatning the temples, and the gods themselves, Looke on them still with humble reverence, Or greater punishments remaine for you To suffer; and our ghosts shall never leave To fright thy conscience, and with thousand stings Afflict thy soule to madnesse and despaire: Be patient yet and prosper, and let fall Thy anger on the Christians, that else Will poyson thy faire kingdome. Where are we yet Endarius? And we are lost, our feare to die i'th sight Of men, hath brought us hither with our blood To quench the thirst of wolves: Or worse, to starve. Now that lust is punish'd, Which fed our hope, if we had staid i'th Temple To have polluted it, with foule embraces: How wearinesse, with travell, and some fasting Will tame the flesh. Take heede, It may be a Lion, or a fierce wolves den; How nature trembles at the thought of death: Though it be prest downe, with the weight of life. The worst is welcome, with our clamor, rouse What ever doth inhabite here, or man Or beast appeare, if any such dwell in This Cave? We can meet charity or death. We are no spirits, but your living sons, Preserv'd without the knowledge of the King, By Archimagus, till a new mis-fortune Compell d us hither to meet death, we feare, In want of food.
