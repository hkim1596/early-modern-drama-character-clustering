Sweet Lady, I'm your humble servant, 'tis Well knowne what I am, where I live, my father Died since I was of age; and left me, thank him, A younger brothers portion: I know, sweet Lady, what you'd say, My father Had no more children, but I must speake modestly Of my estate, I have land I know enough For two or three wives; I have a horse in towne, Your daughter, if we please, shall ride behind me, Sweet Lady, did you ever see the Countrey? Why any Countrey living, Sweet Lady, I am your humble servant, if You affect hawking, hunting, or drinking, There be good fellowes will beare you company; but you have better Sack, sweet Lady, is there good Tobacco in London? Sweet Sir, I am your humble servant, you Seeme to be a Gentleman, will you fetch me a pipe? There's halfe a peece, and I be not troublesome: Perhaps, sweet Lady, 'twill offend you, then Let it alone. Ta la la la lero, &c. Sweet Lady, is this your daughter? That may be too: what Gentleman is that? Sweet Sir, I am your humble servant likewise. Sweet Sir, 'tis all one, A leg or an arme is not cast away Among friends, I am a Countrey Gentleman All the world knowes, sweet Sir, I ha no businesse In towne. That may be too, sweet Lady, pray uncase me, I honour your faire daughter; for I know, As well as another, what belongs to a Gentlewoman: She's not the first, sweet Lady, I'have lov'd I'th' way of matrimony. Sweet Sir, no ▪ all men are not alike. Sweet Sir, I do confesse it: But wit is neuer goodwill it be bought. They say there are good wits in towne, I ha brought money a purpose 〈◊〉 mee for it; If any will sell me a penny-worth, I'll Give him a hundred peeces, 'cause I would carrie A little downe into the Countrey. Sweet Sir, no; There's plentie. My servants have, sweet Sir; but 'tis all one; This Lady shall be Lord o' the Soyle: I wo'not Give any man six pence for a bushell o' money; I am a Gentleman, my father was A Yeoman; but sweet Lady, howsoever I'm yours, and everie limb is at your service; My hands shall walk, my feet shall run. By this gold they shall. Not run? my Grandfather was a Nobleman Foot-man, and indeed he run his countrey; my father did out 〈◊〉 the Con stable. If I live. Sweet Lady, I am your servant: will this Gentleman dine with us? Sweet Sir, I invite no body, if you love any body here? Sweet Sir, I shannot take it kindly, I do not use to quarrel. Sweet Sir, 'tis dinner time: faire Lady: Exeunt. If thou dost fear they'll drowne, Nurse, I can give thee Lighter, I have some want weight. There. A pox of love, if this be the reward on't; Some call it fire, but I finde no such matter; I am frozen to the Blanket, and my teeth Strike one another, and keep time like hammers That beat a Psalme upon the vertuous Anvile; I do beleeve if they were beaten out, They would make false Dyce, there's quicksilver in 'em, I know already by their dancing.
