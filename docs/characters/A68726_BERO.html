Com'on then, I will ſweare to ſtudy ſo, To know the thing I am forbid to know: As thus, to ſtudy where I well may dine, When I to faſt expreſſely am forbid. Or ſtudie where to meete ſome Miſtris fine. When Miſtreſſes from common ſenſe are hid. Or hauing ſworne too hard a keeping oth, Studie to breake it, and not breake my troth. If ſtudies gaine be thus, and this be ſo, Studie knowes that which yet it doth not know, Sweare me to this, and I will nere ſay no. Why? all delightes are vaine, but that moſt vaine Which with payne purchaſ'd, doth inherite payne, As paynefully to poare vpon a Booke, To ſeeke the lyght of trueth, while trueth the whyle Doth falſely blinde the eye-ſight of his looke: Light ſeeking light, doth light of light beguyle: So ere you finde where light in darknes lyes, Your light growes darke by looſing of your eyes. Plot summary

Plot — Love's Labor's Lost
The King of Navarre has proclaimed a three-year period of study during which his courtiers must live abstemiously and refrain from seeing women; no woman may even approach the court. Unfortunately the Princess of France is due to arrive on a diplomatic mission concerning a dispute over money and territory, and the King is forced to meet her; however, the evidence in support of France’s claim against Navarre has not arrived, and the issue is deferred. Costard is caught canoodling with Jaquenetta and is remitted to Don Armado’s custody. Armado is himself enamoured of Jaquenetta, and sends Costard to her with a love-letter. Biron also pays Costard to carry a similar letter to his love, Rosaline, and Costard mixes up the two letters. Armado’s causes amusement to the French ladies when they receive it; Biron’s causes embarrassment to himself when, directed by Holofernes, Jaquenetta brings it to the King just after Biron has attacked him and the other lords for falling in love. Biron argues the illegitimacy of their original vows, and they set about wooing the ladies, first with gifts, then by appearing in a masque of Russians. The ladies confound the latter by wearing masks themselves and swapping gifts so that each lord woos the wrong lady; they also refuse to dance. Don Armado and the locals offer the court a pageant of the Nine Worthies, devised by the schoolmaster: it is mocked by the lords and breaks up in confusion when Costard accuses Don Armado of fathering the pregnant Jaquenetta’s baby. News then arrives that the King of France is dead. The lords propose marriage to the ladies, who refuse: the court of France is in mourning, and the lords must wait a year; given particular conditions by their ladies, the King will spend it in a hermitage and Biron trying vainly to entertain dying men. The play ends with a song of winter and spring, the owl and the cuckoo.
