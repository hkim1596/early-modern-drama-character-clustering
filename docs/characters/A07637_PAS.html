You are welcome ſir, what M. Garland ? I'ſt you, you know my mind; one tooth is drie, Since yeſternight I haue not had one drinke; I am ſo colde. What did you ſay? ſure I remember not; My wits want freſhing. Will driue cold out, and keep my belly warm. Faſt a ſleepe, his troubled head is ſo vext With this worlds cares. Yes that I will, and more then that; Ile doe for you, or for your Brothers ſake; Like burning Drakes ile ſplit the empty ayre, And run through thicke or thin, at noon or night: If you commaund, poore Paſquill will obey. Out from the ſmoaking of my Muſquets mouth Fetcht from the fragments of ſome Poetrie: My nimble Muſe comes from the Aquilone, And flaps her wings gainſt Auſters frothy beard; While Eurus blaſts doe pinch my tender ſides. And gentle Zephir, glads the Seamans heart; Driuing his ſhippe, croſſe Neptunes foaming front. You are diſpoſde to ieſt M. Ganland. I haue many ſuch conceites without booke. Once more with patience, ſilence, & be ſtill: You ſhall haue Rhetoricke gainſt your will. Mount thee my Phlegon Muſe, and teſtifie How Saturne ſitting on an Ebon cloud, Diſroabde his Podex, white as Iuorie, And through the Welkin thundered all aloud. My Grandame taught me, & I learnt by heart This riddle of Saturnes far-fetcht ſigh, But here me Sir, you know that honeſt man M. Flutterkin our Iouiall Hoaſt. While you do talke, then I am ſure of drinke. I goe, I runne, I haſte, I skippe, I flye; With nimbler heeles then ere did Mercurie. This way he went, and here they were ere while, But now they are fledde like birds that cut the ayre, With clipping wings, and leaue no trace behind. But Hans my noſe is quicke and ſharpe of ſent, Like thoſe great Beares in noua Zembla found, Whoſe ſmelling ſence was better then their ſight. I cannot ſee, but ſmell where they are gone. An Engliſh ſhilling: hey; did ſhe indeed? Ile drinke her health vpon my bended knees, Vntill the Welkin roare, and ground lookes blew; Two ſhillings haue I, not one penny ſpent; I bleſſe my ſtarres, good fortune ſet me free; This dayes mine owne. I ſaw no hurt, ile ſayt, and ſweare it too: But good cauſe why; thou couldſt nor go, nor ſtand Thou wert ſo drunke: vnfit for Venus game, A cart became thee better then a Coach. That had been pitty, which way went the ſhot O hold my head my thumbe begins to ake, From hence to France if this be not a lye. I haue no manners: had I ſuch an one As Amptill is, to which ſeuen Parkes belong, I would keepe thee to be my worſhips foole. In the Fayery land. Where men eate mutton, pigge, and gooſe, and beefe, Rabbets and chickens, partridge, pheaſants, quailes, And drinke rich wine, that France or Spaine ſends in, And ſtrong March Beere, of fiue or ſixe yeeres old, But on with thy tale. Tis not farre of, weele goe to Flutterkins, And talke more on't. Hang him ſayes nay. Were you a Younker, made of beaten golde, You ſhould haue this; What nor your Fathers health? All well ſit; nothing: I am pretily well, And ſoes mine hoaſt, I thinke his braines doe crow. I thanke you ſir, I want but Holydayes. Pay-dayes I meane, To make one meet another, and ſhake hands, On euen termes, is all that I care for. He was indeed, but went before you came About his buſineſſe. Plot summary

Plot — Hans Beer-Pot (See Me and See Me Not)
Hans is sent to town to sell corn for his master, Cornelius; his mistress, Hanneke, gives him money to give to her younger son, a student, anda shilling on top for beer-money. Cornelius suspects that Hans is cheating him, and follows him to town. There he finds his son and son-in-law drinking with Hans at a tavern; they have been losing bets with Sergeant Goodfellow about his linguistic ability, and discussing the relative merits of infantry and cavalry (with extensive reference to Turkish history). Hans sneaks away, thinking he has not been spotted; but in fact he has. The town characters go to the country as Cornelius’ guests at Christmas dinner. There they discuss the decline in public respect for soldiers, making extensive reference to the great military men of the Elizabethan era, including some with whom Cornelius served in his younger days.
