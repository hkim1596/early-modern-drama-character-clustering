My Lord your Sonne, drew on my Maſter. There might haue beene, But that my Maſter rather plaid, then fought, And had no helpe of Anger: they were parted By Gentlemen, at hand. On his command: he would not ſuffer mee To bring him to the Hauen: left theſe Notes Of what commands I ſhould be ſubiect too, When't pleas'd you to employ me. I humbly thanke your Highneſſe. It was his Queene, his Queene. And kiſt it, Madam. No Madam: for ſo long As he could make me with his eye, or eare, Diſtinguiſh him from others, he did keepe The Decke, with Gloue, or Hat, or Handkerchife, Still wauing, as the fits and ſtirres of's mind Could beſt expreſſe how ſlow his Soule ſayl'd on, How ſwift his Ship. Madam, ſo I did. Be aſſur'd Madam, With his next vantage. Madam, I ſhall. And ſhall do: But when to my good Lord, I proue vntrue, Ile choake my ſelfe: there's all Ile do for you. Madam, a Noble Gentleman of Rome, Comes from my Lord with Letters. I was going Sir, To giue him welcome. Madam, you're beſt conſider. Alas good Lady. Good Madam, heare me. No, on my life: Ile giue but notice you are dead, and ſend him Some bloody ſigne of it. For 'tis commanded I ſhould do ſo: you ſhall be miſt at Court, And that will well confirme it. I heard no Letter from my Maſter, ſince I wrote him Imogen was ſlaine. 'Tis ſtrange: Nor heare I from my Miſtris, who did promiſe To yeeld me often tydings. Neither know I What is betide to Cloten, but remaine Perplext in all. The Heauens ſtill muſt worke: Wherein I am falſe, I am honeſt: not true, to be true. Theſe preſent warres ſhall finde I loue my Country, Euen to the note o' th' King, or Ile fall in them: All other doubts, by time let them be cleer'd, Fortune brings in ſome Boats, that are not ſteer'd. He hath bin ſearch'd among the dead, & liuing; But no trace of him. It is my Miſtris: Since ſhe is liuing, let the time run on, To good, or bad. Wake my Miſtris. How fares my Miſtris? My Lord, Now feare is from me, Ile ſpeake troth. Lord Cloten Vpon my Ladies miſſing, came to me With his Sword drawne, foam'd at the mouth, and ſwore If I diſcouer'd not which way ſhe was gone, It was my inſtant death. By accident, I had a feigned Letter of my Maſters Then in my pocket, which directed him To ſeeke her on the Mountaines neere to Milford, Where in a frenzie, in my Maſters Garments (Which he inforc'd from me) away he poſtes With vnchaſte purpoſe, and with oath to violate My Ladies honor, what became of him, I further know not.

Plot summary

Plot — Cymbeline, King of Britain
Imogen has been the heir to Cymbeline’s kingdom since the disappearance of her infant brothers twenty years ago. Now her father intends her to marry her foolish stepbrother Cloten; but she pre-empts this by marrying Posthumus instead. Cymbeline objects to Posthumus, whom he took in as an orphan and brought up at court, and banishes him. The Queen pretends support for the couple, but is really the source of Cymbeline’s hostility; her objective is to bring the succession into her own family line, if necessary by destroying Imogen. Claiming to be studying toxicology, she asks Cornelius to give her some poison for experimental purposes. He mistrusts her motives: the ‘poison’ he supplies is really only a sleeping potion. She then gives it to Posthumus’ servant, Pisanio, claiming it to be a powerful medicine. Posthumus goes to Rome, leaving his bracelet behind with Imogen as a love-token, and taking her ring. There he boasts of his wife’s beauty and chastity, and bets Iachimo the ring that she will never succumb to his seduction. Iachimo comes to Britain, tells Imogen that Posthumus is behaving adulterously in Rome, and tries to persuade her to do the same. When she refuses, he claims he was only testing her, and asks her to take charge of a chest of valuables, partly paid for by Posthumus, and lodge them for the night in a safe place, her bedroom. He hides in the chest and emerges when she is asleep, giving him the opportunity to inspect the room, look at her in bed, and take Posthumus’ bracelet from her arm. Back in Rome, he claims to have enjoyed Imogen sexually, and the bracelet convinces Posthumus that he is telling the truth. Iachimo takes the ring and Posthumus sends two letters home: one to Imogen asking her to meet him at Milford Haven, the other to Pisanio ordering him to kill her on arrival there. Pisanio escorts her as planned, but then gives her boy’s clothes to enable her to escape from Britain; he also passes on the ‘medicine’ he had from the Queen, then writes to tell Posthumus that she has been killed as ordered. Cymbeline has neglected to pay the tribute to Rome agreed by his predecessors. Caius Lucius comes to demand it, but gets a dusty answer. Rome prepares its 1610 forces for an invasion. Imogen is found to be absent from court, and, stung by her contemptuous rejection, Cloten follows her wearing a suit of Posthumus’ clothes, intending to rape her and bring her back humiliated to court.
