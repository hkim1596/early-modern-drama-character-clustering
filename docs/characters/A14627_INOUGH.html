Godlines is great riches if a man can be content, When God hath ſent him plenty and Inough: Let vs praiſe him for our food and raiment, And liue godlyly all our liues through, For we muſt tread the paths of death ſo ſharp and rough. And then ſhall we be ſure to cary as little away: As we brought with vs thus Saint Paule dooth ſay. Yea, hardely my fréend ſay what you would. Your minde vnto me you may boldely declare. If you had required a greater thing of me, I would haue doon it for you I tel you certain: Sir, one of yonder men which you doo ſee, Would ſpeak with you alone very fain. Be not raſh in taking of a fréend Ariſtotle dooth ſay: Nor when thou haſte taken him, caſt him not away. Admit not thy fréend either high or lowe: Except his behauiour to others thou dooſt knowe. For look how before he hath ſerued his other fréend: Euen ſo wil he ſerue thée alſo in the end. Inough is as good as a feaſt, wel you wot: More then inough: a man needeth not. Whether it be lands, money, freends or ſtore: if he haue inough: what néedeth he any more? It is an olde prouerbe and of an ancient time: Which ſaith, it is not all Golde, that like Golde dooth ſhine. No more are all fréends that freendſhip pretend: As it approoued with many in the end. Plot summary

Plot — Enough Is as Good as a Feast
Remembering his father’s bankruptcy, the Worldly Man hoards treasure for the future, seeing such behaviour as provident thrift, whereas the Heavenly Man relies on God to look after him and regards wealth as a distraction from spiritual things. He and Contentation persuade the Worldly Man to change to a godly lifestyle, but the vices, using innocuous aliases to inveigle their way into his confidence, cause him to backslide into covetous materialism. He becomes a miser and maltreats his tenants, servants, and hirelings: he raises rents, fails to pay the wages he owes, and seizes property adjacent to his own. After a bad dream about God’s punishment, he falls ill and dies. Satan carries him to hell on his back, advising the audience to live their lives in the same way. The virtues advise the contrary.
