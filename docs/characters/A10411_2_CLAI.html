Full speech

I am as you are, mortall; 'tis my skill In Physick, and experience in the rare Vertue of herbes, that wrought this miracle; No Divinity, or power in me. Thus like a bat, or owle I spend my age In night or darknesse, as asham'd of day, And fearefull of the light: the sunne and I Dare never be acquainted. O guilt, guilt, Thou and thy daughter feare are punishments Perpetuall, every whistling of the wind Doth seeme the noise of apprehenders ▪ shadowes Affright me more then men. Each step I tread Is danger. Life? why to live longer should we Not live at all: I heare a noise: false timorousnesse Deceive me not,—my eyes instruct me too, Heaven shield me— Fain I would enquire of them For Amaryllis, but if one of these Bee Damon, I am lost. Then I have hearbs to cure it: heaven I thank thee That didst instruct me hither! still the bloud Flowes like a scarlet torrent, whose quick streame Will not be checkt: speak Amarillis, quickly, What hand this sinne hath stain'd, upon whose soule This bloud writes murther; till you see the man Before your eyes, that gave the hurt, all hope In Physick is despaire:—She will not speak, And now the cure growes to the last. Yet here I have a Recipe will revive her spirits, Applies a medicine and rubs her te ̄ ples. And 'till the last drop of her blood be clean Exhausted from those azure veines, preserve her; But then shee's lost for ever! Then, O Ceres, If there be any in these groves, men, virgins, Beast, bird, or trees, or any thing detesting This horrid fact, reveale it! Sacred grasse Whose hallowed greene this bloudy deed hath stain'd, Aske nature for a tongue to name the murtherer! I'le to the Temple:—If this place containe Any Divinity, Piety, or Religion, If there be any God at home, or Priest, Ompha, or Oracle, Shrine, or Altar, speake Who did it: who is guilty of this sinne, That dyes the earth with bloud, & makes the heavens Asham'd to stand a witnesse? I charge thee by that duty Thou ow'st to me, Amarillis, that thou owest to me Who gave thee life.— In his lookes He carries guilt, whose horror breeds this strange And obstinate silence: shame, and his conscience Will not permit him to deny it. Amarillis, prethee call Amyntas to mee, And Thestylis: I faine would have mine eye Behold them once again before I dye. O what a showre of joy falls from mine eyes! The now too fortunate Claius! my Amyntas, My Amarillis, how shall I divide My teares and joyes betwixt you! Pilumnus, let us now grow young againe, And like two trees robb'd of their leafy boughes By winter, age, and Boreas keener breath, Sprout forth and bud again: This spring of joy Cuts forty yeares away from the gray summe. Once more in triumph let us walke the Village!

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
