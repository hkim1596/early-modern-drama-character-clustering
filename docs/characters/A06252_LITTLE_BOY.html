He will be presently. Sir you must pardon vs, the plot of our Plaie lies contrarie, and 'twill hazard the spoiling of our Plaie. Why sir he shall, but if anie thing fall out of order, the Gentlemen must pardon vs. In good faith sir we cannot, you'le vtterly spoile our Play, and make it to be hist, and it cost money, you will not suffer vs to go on with our plot, I pray Gentlemen rule him. Will you giue me your hand of that? I'le send him to you presently. No indeed forsooth: Neither. Sir, you need not feare, I haue my lesson here, and cannot misse it: The men are ready for you, and what else Pertaines to this imployment. Faith sir 'twere rare To see so yong a purchaser: I flye, And on my wings carry your destinie. You shall haue what you will sir. Beleeue me sir, that will not doe so well, 'tis stale, it has beene had before at the red Bull. Sir, if you will imagine all this to be done already, you shall heare them talke together: but wee cannot pre sent a house couered with blacke veluet, and a Lady in bea ten gold. Besides it will shew ill-fauouredly to haue'a Gro cers prentice to court a kings daughter. It shall be done, it is not our fault gentlemen. Sir, I take it you are the Maister of this house. Then to your selfe sir comes this letter. From him that was your seruant, but no more Shall that name euer be, for hee is dead, Griefe of your purchas'd anger broke his heart, I saw him die, and from his hand receiu'd This paper, with a charge to bring it hither, Reade it, and satisfie your selfe in all. 'Tis here without sir. Faire Mistresse let me not adde greater griefe To that great store you haue already; Iasper That whilst he liu'd was yours, now dead, And here enclos'd, commanded me to bring His body hither, and to craue a teare From those faire eyes, though he deseru'd not pitty, To decke his funerall, for so he bid me Tell her for whom he di'de. At hand sir. 'Tis done already. Your seruant sir. I will sir. God blesse your VVorships health sir. Sir, they say they know all your mony is gone, and they will trust you for no more drinke. Sir, your pleasure. Why sir you do not thinke of our plot, what will be come of that then? Well sir hee shall come out, but if our play miscar ry, sir you are like to pay for't. God saue you sir. Yes sir, I can sing, but 'tis not so necessary at this time. Sir, sir, if you knew what I haue brought you, you would haue little list to sing. A Coffin sir, and your dead son Iasper in it. 'Tis long of your selfe sir, wee haue nothing to doe with his part. 'Twill be very vnfit he should die sir, vpon no occa sion, and in a Comedy too.

Plot summary

Plot — The Knight of the Burning Pestle
At the theatre, a citizen and his wife object to the satirical subject matter of the play, The London Merchant: they insist on a more romantic offering starring their apprentice, Rafe, as a heroic grocer. The boy actors do their best to provide this within the play they have rehearsed. In the play, Luce prefers her father Venturewell’s apprentice Jasper to his chosen son-in-law, Humphrey; so Venturewell discharges Jasper, and he goes home to a family impoverished by the irresponsible merriment of his father, Old Merrythought. His mother, who 1607 favours his younger brother Michael, insists that he be paid his inheritance and sent out into the world. Several of the characters go to Waltham Forest. Luce elopes with Humphrey, intending to meet Jasper there. Rafe the grocer, a voluminous reader of chivalric romance, becomes the Knight of the Burning Pestle and leaves in search of heroic deeds. He scares Mistress Merrythought, who leaves behind her jewels. These are found and taken away by Jasper, who then meets the eloping couple, beats Humphrey, and takes Luce. In the audience, the citizen and his wife take Jasper to be the villain and Humphrey the hero; they demand that Rafe return to fight Jasper, but Jasper beats him. Notified by Humphrey, Venturewell confronts Jasper’s father, but is offended by his refusal to take the matter seriously. Jasper tests Luce’s love by threatening her with violence. She accepts her fate, but Humphrey and her father seize her before he can admit he was only pretending. Rafe stays at the Bell inn, but cannot understand that he must pay for its hospitality. (The citizen settles the bill for him.) The host sends him to fight the giant Barbaroso, better known as Nick the barber, who has imprisoned many knights and ladies — actually clients and venereal disease patients. Rafe defeats the ‘giant’ and releases the ‘knights’. The citizens then have a scene inserted in which he meets a foreign king’s daughter, but refuses her offer oflove. Later interpolated scenes show Rafe as the city’s May Lord, and mustering troops at Mile End. Venturewell incarcerates Luce in her room, but Jasper gains access by pretending to be dead: Venturewell accedes to a request that his body should be brought to Luce, and then has the coffin sent on to Old Merrythought, unaware that Luce has taken Jasper’s place inside.
