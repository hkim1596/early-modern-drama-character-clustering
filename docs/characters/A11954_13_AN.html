Marry but you ſhall haue, and heeres my hand. Wherefore (ſweet-heart?) What's your Meta phor? Neuer in your life I thinke, vnleſſe you ſee Ca narie put me downe: mee thinkes ſometimes I haue no more wit then a Chriſtian, or an ordinary man ha's: but I am a great eater of beefe, and I beleeue that does harme to my wit. And I thought that, I'de forſweare it. Ile ride home to morrow ſir Toby. What is purquoy ? Do, or not do? I would I had beſtowed that time in the tongues, that I haue in fencing dancing, and beare-bayting; O had I but followed the Arts. Why, would that haue mended my haire? But it becoms we wel enough, doſt not? Faith Ile home to morrow ſir Toby, your niece wil not be ſeene, or if ſhe be it's four to one, ſhe'l none of me: the Count himſelfe here hard by, wooes her, Let me yet know of you, whither you are bound. Excellent: Why this is the beſt fooling, when all is done. Now a ſong. There's a teſtrill of me too: if one knight giue a I, I. I care not for good life. O Miſtris mine where are you roming? O ſtay and heare, your true loues coming, That can ſing both high and low. Trip no further prettie ſweeting. Iourneys end in louers meeting, Euery wiſe mans ſonne doth know. Excellent good, ifaith. A mellifluous voyce, as I am true knight. Very ſweet, and contagious ifaith. Moſt certaine: Let our Catch be, Thou Knaue. 'Tis not the firſt time I haue conſtrained one to call me knaue. Begin foole: it begins, Hold thy peace. Good ifaith: Come begin. I, he do's well enough if he be diſpos'd, and ſo do I too: he does it with a better grace, but I do it more naturall. 'Twere as good a deede as to drink when a mans a hungrie, to challenge him the field, and then to breake promiſe with him, and make a foole of him. O, if I thought that, Ide beate him like a dogge. I haue no exquiſite reaſon for't, but I haue reaſon good enough. I hau't in my noſe too. And your horſe now would make him an Aſſe. O twill be admirable. Before me ſhe's a good wench. I was ador'd once too. If I cannot recouer your Neece, I am a foule way out. If I do not, neuer truſt me, take it how you will. And we do not, it is pittie of our liues. Piſtoll him, piſtoll him. Fie on him Iezabel. Her C's, her Ʋ 's, and her T's : why that? So could I too. Nor I neither. Or o' mine either? Ifaith, or I either? I hope ſir, you are, and I am yours. Will either of you beare me a challenge to him? Heere's the Challenge, reade it: I warrant there's vinegar and pepper in't. Plague on't, and I thought he had beene vallant, and ſo cunning in Fence, I'de haue ſeene him damn'd ere I'de haue challeng'd him. Let him let the matter ſlip, and Ile giue him my horſe, gray Capilet. You do miſtake me ſir. Nay let him alone, Ile go another way to worke with him: Ile haue an action of Battery againſt him, if there be any law in Illyria: though I ſtroke him firſt, yet it's no matter for that.

Plot summary

Plot — Twelfth Night, or What You Will
Orsino is in love with Olivia, but she is in mourning and has renounced the company of men. Shipwrecked in Illyria and believing her twin brother dead, Viola enters Orsino’s service posing as a eunuch, ‘Cesario’. She falls in love with him, but is sent to woo Olivia on his behalf. Olivia in turn falls in love with ‘Cesario’. Olivia’s uncle, Sir Toby, is trying to marry her off to his idiot acquaintance, Sir Andrew. After her steward Malvolio interrupts their raucous nocturnal sing-song, and unjustly accuses her gentlewoman Maria of complicity, the offended parties plot to humiliate him. Maria writes a letter, in handwriting similar to Olivia’s, and leaves it for him to find. It encourages him to believe possible his fantasies of marrying his mistress, and induces him to behave and dress inappropriately in her presence. Olivia thinks he is mad or possessed, and has him confined in Sir Toby’s charge. Sir Toby and his accomplices take the opportunity to mock him. Sebastian, Viola’s brother, is alive after all: he was rescued from drowning by Orsino’s enemy Antonio. They too come to Illyria, and Antonio entrusts Sebastian with his purse. Sir Toby encourages Sir Andrew to challenge ‘Cesario’ to a duel, and makes each afraid of the other. They are on the point of fighting when Antonio intervenes to help the youth, believing him to be Sebastian. When he is arrested by Orsino’s officers, Viola is unable to return the purse he gave to Sebastian; he reproaches ‘Sebastian’s’ ingratitude, and Viola realizes her brother has survived. Taking ‘Cesario’ for a coward, Sir Andrew and Sir Toby renew the quarrel when they encounter Sebastian, but Olivia intervenes. After arranging for a priest, she contracts to marry Sebastian, thinking him “Cesario’. Orsino realizes that ‘Cesario’ has become an obstacle in the pursuit of his love, and considers killing ‘him’ until it emerges that Olivia is now married. Sir Toby and Sir Andrew are wounded after attacking Sebastian, whose arrival on stage resolves all confusions. Orsino transfers his affections to Viola. Malvolio is released, but goes away swearing vengeance. Feste sings.
