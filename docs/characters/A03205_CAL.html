What meane these horrid & these shrill alarmes That fright the peacefull Court with hostle cryes? Feare and amazement hurry through each chamber; Th'affrighted Ladies light the darkest roomes With their bright beauties: whence (ô whence ye Gods) Are all you grones, cryes, and inhumane sownds Of bloud and death: Licaon, where is he? Why in this dire and sad astonishment Appeares not he to comfort my sad feares, And cheere me in this dull distemprature? You afright me with your steele. You'r one too many, and of all the rest, That beare mens figure, we can spare you best. What are you sir? Sir, I shall proue your loue. You'l grant it me my Lord. You'l not reuoke your word? This onely freedome to your captiue giue That I a Nunne and profest maid may liue. Tush, 'tis all in vaine, Dian I am now a seruant of thy traine. I claime your oath, all loue with men adue, Dianae's Cloyster I will next pursue. Madam, deuotion drew me to your seruice, And I am now your hand-maid. Hast gentle Lady, we shall loose our traine, And misse Dianai's pastime in the chase, Hie then to staine our Iauelings guilded points In bloud of yon swift Stag, so hot pursu'de. Will you keepe pace with mee? And loose the honour to be first at fall? Rather then leaue you, I will loose the sport Come shal's lye downe a little. You gripe too hard. Leane on me then. How? I do not like this kissing. You kisse too wantonly. Nay fye what meane you? A man, how then? You are too wanton, and your hand too free. My barenesse I haue hid from sight of skyes, Therefore may barre it any Ladyes eyes. Oh God you tickle me. Lord how you wooe, I am weary of this toying. Thus would I rise, And fill the Dales and mountaines with my cryes. A man! (Oh heauen) to gaine Elisiums blisse, I'de not be sayd that I a man should kisse. Come, lets go wound the Stagge. Gods, Angels, men, help all a maid to free. To do me right, Helpe fingers, feet, nailes, teeth, and all to fight. Help, help, for heauen sake help, I am pursu'd, And by my sonne, that seemes to threate my life, What's he? false Iupiter? Oh thou most false, most treacherous, and vnkind, Behold Calisto by her sonne pursu'd, Indeed thy sonne: this little sauadge youth Hath liued' mongst Tygers, Lyons, Wolues, and Beares, And since his birth partakes their cruelty. Archas his name: since I Diana left, And from her chast traine was diuorc't, this youth I childed in a caue remote and silent. His nurture was amongst the sauadges. This day I by misfortune mou'd his spleene, And he pursu'd me with reuenge and fury, And had I not forsooke the shades and forrests, And fled for rescue to these walled Townes, He had slaine me in his fury: saue me then, Let not the sonne the mother sacrifice Before the fathers eye. No thou false man, for thy periurious lusts I haue abandoned humaine subtelties: There take thy sonne, and vse him like a Prince, Being sonne vnto a Princesse. Teach him Arts, And honoured armes. Plot — The Golden Age, or The Lives of Jupiter and Saturn
King Uranus of Crete is dead. Titan is the eldest son, but his younger brother Saturn is the popular choice to ascend the throne. Titan agrees to make way for him provided that he agrees to settle the succession on Titan’s issue and kill his own sons as soon as they are born. Saturn is declared a god, and under his benevolent rule, the people learn agriculture, ship- building, and architecture, and begin to make weapons for hunting. His wife, Sibylla, becomes pregnant, but Saturn keeps his promise to Titan and kills the baby. During a second pregnancy, an oracle foretells that the child will depose Saturn. This time Sibylla demands the privilege of killing her own child; but neither she nor her women can bring themselves to do it, so the baby is sent to a foster mother, the Princess of Epirus, and Saturn is deceived with the blood of a goat. Jupiter grows up in the court of King Melisseus of Epirus, and is sent as an envoy to conclude a peace with King Lycaon of Pelasgia; but Lycaon has murdered the Epirote hostage and offers his limbs to Jupiter as a cannibalistic banquet. Jupiter deposes Lycaon and the people elect him their King. During the conflict, he encounters Callisto, Lycaon’s daughter, and is attracted to her. She asks him to grant her a boon to prove his love; he promises, but her request is to live a chaste life as a nymph of Diana. She joins Diana, but the nymphs are already paired off, so she must await the next recruit to have a bedfellow. This turns out to be Jupiter in female disguise. During a stag hunt, he diverts her, reveals his identity and rapes her. Months later, the nymphs undress to bathe together; Callisto is reluctant, and is forcibly stripped. Saturn learns that Sibylla has deceived him, and sends to Jupiter for help. Meanwhile, Jupiter is curious about his parentage. He meets Callisto, pursued by their son Archas, whom she has brought up among beasts in a remote cave.
