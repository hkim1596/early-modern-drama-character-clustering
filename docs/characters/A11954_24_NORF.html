Full speech

I thanke your Grace: Healthfull, and euer ſince a freſh Admirer Of what I ſaw there. Be aduiſ'd; Heat not a Furnace for your foe ſo hot That it do ſindge your ſelfe. We may out-runne By violent ſwiftneſſe that which we run at; And loſe by ouer-running: know you not, The fire that mounts the liquor til't run ore, In ſeeming to augment it, waſts it: be aduiſ'd; I ſay againe there is no Engliſh Soule More ſtronger to direct you then your ſelfe; If with the ſap of reaſon you would quench, Or but allay the fire of paſſion. Say not treaſonous. Faith, and ſo it did. I am ſorry To heare this of him; and could wiſh he were Somthing miſtaken in't. Not almoſt appeares, It doth appeare; for, vpon theſe Taxations, The Clothiers all not able to maintaine The many to them longing, haue put off The Spinſters, Carders, Fullers, Weauers, who Vnfit for other life, compeld by hunger And lack of other meanes, in deſperate manner Daring th' euent too th' teeth, are all in vprore, And danger ſerues among them. Well met my Lord Chamberlaine. What's the cauſe? Tis ſo; This is the Cardinals doing: The King-Cardinall, That blinde Prieſt, like the eldeſt Sonne of Fortune, Turnes what he liſt. The King will know him one day. How holily he workes in all his buſineſſe, And with what zeale? For now he has crackt the League Between vs & the Emperor (the Queens great Nephew) He diues into the Kings Soule, and there ſcatters Dangers, doubts, wringing of the Conſcience, Feares, and deſpaires, and all theſe for his Marriage. And out of all theſe, to reſtore the King, He counſels a Diuorce, a loſſe of her That like a Iewell, ha's hung twenty yeares About his necke, yet neuer loſt her luſtre; Of her that loues him with that excellence, That Angels loue good men with: Euen of her, That when the greateſt ſtroake of Fortune falls Will bleſſe the King: and is not this courſe pious? We had need pray, And heartily, for our deliuerance; Or this imperious man will worke vs all From Princes into Pages: all mens honours Lie like one lumpe before him, to be faſhion'd Into what pitch he pleaſe. Let's in; And with ſome other buſines, put the King From theſe ſad thoughts, that work too much vpon him: My Lord, youle beare vs company? If you will now vnite in your Complaints, And force them with a Conſtancy, the Cardinall Cannot ſtand vnder them. If you omit The offer of this time, I cannot promiſe, But that you ſhall ſuſtaine moe new diſgraces, With theſe you beare alreadie. But my Lord When returnes Cranmer ? Who waits there? Doe you thinke my Lords The King will ſuffer but the little finger Of this man to be vex'd?

Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
