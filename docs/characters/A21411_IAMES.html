Good morrow sir. A marriage sir? Because tis rich. In whom sit, if I may be bold. How ignorance pleades nonage, in his eie He knowes me not, tis not the Lyons kinde, Whose nature challenges right property. Of perfect being, if it were, Humil would Humill know, that him begot, To be what now he is married againe, And her first husband liuing, blame her not, Tis my owne proiect, thanke my letters, That falsifide our deaths black funerall, Into her mornefull hearing, follow it, Thinke on thy soule, diuide that bitter band, Knit by the closure of a mutuall hand. Tis done too late to aske why so. Tardie intrusion as a Cipher plaste. Fils but vp roome, while substance in the other Makes number pretious, I am that round O, Which with a sigh, as sad as is my soule, Grieue all too late, what now befits my mone, But black despaire, and die in't do, To make thy selfe knowne, but represents Mellow fruit falling into danger, ô I am that Rotten ruin'd, and vnrellisht substance, Which on my owne vpgrowing tree falles off, By the times negligence, vndone I am, Would I had dide indeede, and not in word. So faire and fortunate to be thus false, Wedded to two. O you all wondring eies, Gaze till your fire flame, your eie-balles drop In moist imagination of this act, Before the first be dead to wed a second. ô What then, was that a iust excuse To varnish ouer this base counterfeiting? no, Il'e make it knowne. Had I beene thus aduiz'd, but all too late Acquainted with your speede, I had preuented what now is past and done. I am from my selfe in this, what shall I doe? O I am madde, and mischiefe mennassis vnwitting of all purpose. I know not how. To ly with him the while. I build on this. Our sonne. Sound proclamation, It is inacted by the bride and bride-groome, And by our selfe chiefe in authoritie, That all receiue their pleasures From the most high in this assembly To the lowest, all pastimes are made free, Dauncing, carding, dicing reuelling. And other dues oftimes fit merriments, —Vnto the bride and bride-groomes health. There take your places. And in your sweetest key of musique strokes Sound pleasant melody, eccho those sounds Which true-loue-hearts, in concords chiefest grounds Haue their blest being, vse art in times, Which may giue welcome to our noblest guests. Peace, we on you do impose command. Yeeld duty in it: hall, a hall there. Musique sound, and to the bride do consecrate this round. A coople more, too makes no show, our measure is for three. Then I with her. Harke. My warrant wins, where his dos loose the right. God continue this good league. No. That you are then possest. New broken sir. Tis true your mother challeng'd it: but he as an gry as the raging morne, whose choller breathing shakes high battlements, puts her off with a pause of contrarie, I know it sir, her ioynter is subscrib'd too, which else to doe, sooner should earth to heauen presume a progresse, then the grant make firme what the antecedent challenges, your mo ther vpon this abandons from his bed, vowing bold absence, he inrag'd, giues way to all maligne and stubborne fashion of contempt, such a cloze to day neuer had practise, such a wedding night, till this sad first neuer had purchase: you shall well agree them sir, to attone this iarre, vse meanes I pray you, t'will become ye, well, when wrangling wrestles with such violent iniurie, tis the sonnes office. You had a father loued ye better. Haue patience. So. Now swims vpon the maine, such shipwrack-soules, As the windes rage splits on the rocks of danger. Plot summary

Plot — The Two Maids of More-Clacke
Before the action, James Humil wrote to his wife from what he took to be his deathbed. Believing herself widowed, she made swift arrangements for her remarriage to Sir William Vergir, with the support of her son, who hoped to marry one of Sir William’s daughters, Now, on her way back from the wedding, she sees James in the street. She loses her glove, and offers a reward for its discovery: the person who finds . it will have the honour of deciding the festivities in celebration of her second marriage. This is a ruse to enable her to see James, who has found the glove. She tells him that, though she genuinely thought him dead, she contracted her second marriage as a means of paying his debts and securing a desirable match for ===== PAGE 471 ===== 1608 their son. She takes on James as a servant, and tells her second husband that she will only consummate the marriage once Young Humil is married to his elder daughter, Mary. Since both daughters are already engaged, Sir William tries to put off the fiancés by setting impossible conditions: Toures will only be allowed to marry Mary after she has died and come back to life; Young Filbon will get Tabitha only if he becomes a woman. Filbon sends a letter to Tabitha via Touch, Sir William’s servant, but the letter is found and Touch dismissed; Tabitha becomes estranged from her father. Toures visits Mary disguised as a tinker, and they go off to Putney to get married. Tabitha has two other suitors ~ Auditor, Toures’s father, and the Welsh knight Sir Robert, who is really Touch in disguise — but she tells them she is only interested in Filbon. The creation of Sir Robert is a ruse to allow Filbon access to her, despite her father’s opposition: when he releases her to be married to the Welsh knight, Touch hands over the role to Filbon. Toures and Mary are married, and escape by ship, but Mary falls ill during a storm at sea. She appears to die and is buried with all her possessions in a trunk on the beach in the Scilly Isles. The Governor of the Scillies digs up the trunk, thinking it booty, and finds her inside, still alive; he happens to be her uncle. Humil finds his mother in bed with James, and tells Vergir. He is unable to substantiate the charge of adultery because, by the time Vergir arrives to see for himself, James has left the bed (but is still in the room).
