Mania? Braine-sick, why start'st thou so? Approch yet nearer, and thou then shalt know The will of Ioue, which he will breath from me, The same Mania, and Ioue greets thee thus, Though seuerall power to thee, and charge he gaue, T'enclose in thy Dominions such as raue Through blouds distemper, how durst thou attempt T'imprison Entheus, whose rage is exempt From vulgar censure? it is all diuine Full of celestiall rapture, that can shine Through darkest shadowes, therefore Ioue by me Commands thy power strait to set Entheus free. —Let not feare in vaine Trouble thy crazed fancie, all againe Saue Entheus to thy safeguard shall retire, For Ioue into our musick will inspire The power of passion, that their thoughts shall bend To any forme or motion we intend: Obey Ioues willing then, go, set Entheus free. See Ioue is pleas'd, Statues haue life & moue, Goe new-borne men, and entertaine with loue, These new-borne women, though your number yet Exceedes their's double, they are arm'd with wit, To beare your best encounters, Court them faire: When words and Musicke speake, let none despaire. WOoe her, and win her, he that can, Each woman hath two louers, So shee must take and leaue a man, Till time more grace discouers; This doth Ioue to shew that want, Makes beautie most respected; If faire women were more skant, They would be more affected. Courtship and Musicke, suite with loue, They both are workes of passion, Happie is he whose words can moue, Yet sweete notes helpe perswasion. Mixe your words with Musicke then, That they the more may enter; Bold assaults are fit for men, That on strange beauties venture.

Plot summary

Plot — The Lord's Masque (Wedding Masque of Stars and Statues Made Human)
Entheus, representing poetic fury, has been imprisoned by Mania, but Jove needs a wedding masque to be written and sends Orpheus to command his release. Mania protests that all the frantics are confined in the same cave, and she cannot let one out without releasing all the others; a mad dance ensues, but she succeeds in confining the non-literary lunatics. Entheus calls on Prometheus, who introduces eight stars. Orpheus’ music transforms them into human beings, the male masquers. Jove, who turned Prometheus’ first female creations into statues, rescinds his decree and makes them women again; at first there are only four women, but Jove creates four more to even up the numbers of male and female 1613 masquers. Dancing ensues. Sibylla speaks Latin prophecies about the bride and groom’s future, and Orpheus, Entheus, and Prometheus wish them well The masquers dance away.
