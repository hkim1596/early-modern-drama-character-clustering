Full speech

God spede you syr heartily, and well to fare, I reioyce much that I chaunce you here to fynde, In good soth I was sory, and toke muche care That I had no tyme to declare to you my mynde. We know that you do much good in the countrey here Wherfore the liuyng God is glorified: You heale the sicke persons both farre and nere, Like as it hath ben credibly testified: For a season it behoueth vs to haue pacience, I shewed you the reason wherfore of late: At this season I pray you do your diligence, And semble rather to loue hym than to hate. Shall it please you syr, this day to take payne With me at my house to take some repast, You shal be welcome doubtlesse I tell you playne, No great puruiance for you I entend to make. All things be in maner ready I thinke verily, In the meane season in my gardein we will walke. Take the paines to go with me, I pray you heartily, Till dinner be ready, of matters we will talke. Sirr , you see how that we are appointed now, Make all thyngs ready without delay. Sir now are you welcome, I pray you come nere, Fetche in meate syrs, I pray you quickly. I promise you I byd you for no good chere, But such as it is, you ar welcome hartily. Can you not a while dissemble the matter? It is no tyme to talke of suche geare now. Will you sit sir, bryng hither a cushion and a stoole. Set it down I say there, there at the tables ende. I pray you my guest his mynde do you satisfie, It is said, that the sonne of God you do your self call. Wel sir, you ar welcom, I wold not haue you to thi ̄ k That I did byd you hither to tempt or to proue, But that I would haue you both to eate and drinke, Euen as my entier friend, and for very loue. Wherfore any thing that is here done or sayd, Shalbe layd vnder foote, and go no further, For surely if your wordes should be betrayd, As a blasphemer the people would you murder, Syrs, take away here, we will no more now, This fyrst: Are you in such things to be tought? What meane you, wherabout do you looke, I maruell wherabout you do occupy your thought. Mary, he to whom most was forgiuen I suppose, In few wordes truly you haue heard my sentence. Sir, you take vpon you very presumptuously, I haue bydden you vnto my house here of good will, And you reason of matters here contemptuously: But take your pleasure, it shall not greatly skill. I see the wordes whiche I haue heard, proued true. Men say that you are new fangled, and friuolous, Goyng about the law and our rulers to subdue, Introducyng sectes perillous and sedicious. Thinke you vs ignorant of gods law and will, Which vpon our garments do them weare. Who but we doe the law of God fulfill, For his precepts with vs in all places we beare. Away with this geare, how long shall we syt here? At once: We haue somewhat els to do I thinke. Fare ye well: for me you shall no countes render, All shall be layd vnder the feete that is here spoken. It shall behoue you to dog him from place to place, Note whether openly he teache suche doctrine: If he doe, accuse hym before his face, For I will cause the byshops hym to examine. And where as he willeth you vs to expell, Well the tyme of our euenyng seruice is at hand, We must depart, the sacrifice to prepare.

Plot summary

Plot — The Life and Repentance of Mary Magdalene
Christ has recently come to Jewry, and Infidelity is keen to interfere with his work: his plan is to influence the religious establishment against Christ and thereby ensure his crucifixion. Meanwhile, he encourages Mary Magdalen to enjoy her youth while she can, and introduces her to three companions who will help: Pride, Cupidity, and Carnal Concupiscence (all of whom go under less damaging names). They recommend fashionable clothes and hairstyles, which will attract men, but also advise her against marriage. Mary meets The Law of God and Knowledge of Sin, and experiences the stirrings of conscience: she despairs, thinking herself surely damned and God unjust. Christ casts seven devils out of Mary and drives away Infidelity: she is given the grace to repent. Simon the Pharisee is suspicious of Christ because he is lower-class and uneducated, and invites him to dinner. During the meal, Mary washes his feet with her tears and anoints them. Simon is outraged, but Christ emphasizes the universality of forgiveness: Mary is a genuine penitent, whereas Simon is merely a hypocrite wanting to show off his wealth and presumed holiness. Simon is offended and sets Infidelity and Malicious Judgement to follow Christ, spy on his preaching, and have him brought to trial. Mary ends the play in the company of Love and Justification, who emphasize that she is saved through her faith alone.
