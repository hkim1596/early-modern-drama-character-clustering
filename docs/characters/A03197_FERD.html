O brother! Thou art too yong to reach the depth of griefe, That is immur'd within my hearts deepe closet, A thousand sighes keepe daily centinell, That beate like whirle-winds all my comfort backe, As many sobbes guard my distressed heart, That no reliefe comes neere to ayde my soule, Millions of woes like bands of armed men, Stop vp the passage of my sweete reliefe: And arte thou then perswaded that thy wordes, Can any comfort to my soule afford? No, no, good Franke, deere brother then forbeare, Vnlesse with griefe in me youle take a share. Wilt thou not leaue me to my selfe alone? A child, a little little boy thats blind. I, such a boy as thou canst neuer see, And yet ere long mayst feele his tirranie: Hee's not visible, yet aimes at the heart, Wo be to those that feele his wounding dart; And one of them I am: wounded so deepe, That in my passions I no meane can keepe: Vnhappy time, woe to that dismall howre, When loue did wound me with faire Phillis flowre: O Phillis, Phillis ; of flowers sweetest flower, That euer garnish'd any princely bowre: Farewell, farewell, my woes will ne're remoue, Till I inioy faire Phillis for my loue. Exit. Shall I exclaime gainst fortune and mishap. Or raile on Nature who first framed me? Is it hard chance that keepes me from my loue? Or is this heape of loathd deformitie, The cause that breeds a blemmish in her eye? I know not what to thinke, or what to say, Onely one comfort yet I haue in store, Which I will practise though I ne're try more. I haue a brother riuall in my loue; I haue a brother hates me for my loue; I haue a brother vowes to winne my loue; That brother too, he hath incenst my loue, To gaine the beauty of my deerest loue; What hope remaines then to inioy my loue? I haue no meanes of priuate conference, So narrowly pursues my hinderer, No sooner am I entred the sweet court Of louely rest, my loues rich mansion, But riuall loue to my affection Followes me, as a soone enforced strawe, The drawing vertue of a sable jeat: This therefore's my determination, Within the close wombe of a sealed paper, Will I right downe in bloody Characters, The burning zeale of my affection: And by some trusty messenger or other, Conuey the same into my loues owne hand; So shall I know her resolution, And how she fancies my affection. About affaires that doe require some haste. But you would reape the haruest of my paine: Farewell good brother, I must needes be gone, I haue serious businesse now to thinke vpon: Yet for I feare my brother Anthonie, Ile step aside and stand a while vnseene, I may perchance discry which way he goes; Thus policie must worke twixt friends and foes. Aside. And shall it so, good brother Anthonie? Were you so neere when we in secret talk'd? Wilt ne're be otherwise? will you dog me still? Welcome sweete Frank ; such news I haue to tell, As cannot chuse but like thee passing well: Thou knowest my loue to Phillis ? Thou likewise art acquainted with my riuall, And I doe build vpon your secrecie. I dare professe as much, and there-vpon Make bold to craue thy furtherance, in a thing Concernes me much. Then thus it is; my brother all in haste Is gone, to write a letter to my loue, And thinkes thereby to crosse me in my sute, Sending it by a Porter to her hand; If euer therefore thou wilt aid thy brother, Helpe me in this, who seekes helpe from no other. It is enough; then brother Ile prouide A Porters habite, alike in euery point, Will you but so much humble your estate, To put your selfe in that so base attyre, And like so meane a person waite his comming, About his doore which will not be ore long, Thou shalt for euer binde me to thy loue. For this ile loue thee euerlastingly. Heere is a Porters habite, on with it brother. This first, that thou make haste to Anthonies, Aske for a burden and thou shall be sure To haue his letter to my deere loue Phillis, Deliuer it not, but keepe it to thy selfe, Till thou hast giuen this paper to her hands, Whose lines do intimate thy chaste desires. This is the summe of all, good Franke, make haste, Loue burnes in me, and I in loue doe waste. Exit. Franke. What hast thou giuen the letter to her hand, And staid my brother Anthonies withall? O vnkinde answere to a Louers letter; Let me suruay the end once more: For I neuer looke you in the face but I am sicke: and so prai ing God to continue me in health, by keeping you from mee. Is shee so farre from yeelding? is this fort Of her chaste loue yet so impregnable? What shall I doe? this is the furthest way, A labour of impossibilities: This way to winne her? I will once againe, Challenge the promise that her father made me; To him ile write, and he I know will pleade My loue to Phillis, and so winne the maide. Exit. Brother Anthony, what newes from Venice ? Are your ships return'd? I had rather aside. Heare newes from Phillis : Oh, brother Franke, Thy absence makes me burne in passion. Yonders a friend of mine acquaintance, With whom I would gladly haue some conference, I pray thee stay I will returne immediatly. But two words more at most: You haue not then receiu'd any such letter? A vengeance take the lazie messenger; Brother if I liue, ile quittance thee for this, Yes. I know our businesse tends to one effect: O that villaine Franke, it mads my soule I am so wrong'd, by such a foolish Boy. Well, since I haue such opportunitie, Ile trust no longer to vncertaintie. Oh sight intollerable. Franke thou art a villaine, thou shalt know't ere long. For proffering me such vndeserued wrong. Exit. M. Flower, I come to claime your promise. Sir, sir, I wrote no letter. Y faith good brother haue you ore reach,d vs so? For all this cunning, I will breake the match. Brother you lie, you got her with a shift. That's not so, t'was I. Thy loue. I wish but loue. Impatience puls me hence, for this disdaine, I am resolu'd neuer to loue againe. Exit.

Plot summary

Plot — The Fair Maid of the Exchange
Phyllis and Ursula are assaulted by two ruffians as they go to deliver wares to a customer at night. A cripple rescues them, and a second assault is foiled by the intervention of Frank Golding; the cripple promises to help Frank if ever he can. Frank's two brothers are both in love with Phyllis, and, though he scorns amorous passions, Frank has fallen for her too. Phyllis, however, loves the cripple, and woos him by giving him a handkerchief to embroider “for a friend’. Meanwhile, Bobbington poses as a sailor going on a voyage, and borrows money from Phyllis’s father, Flower, leaving a diamond ring with him as security. Ferdinand and Anthony overhear one another's plans to woo Phyllis, but neither knows that Frank is also a rival. Ferdinand writes to her; Anthony plans to intercept the letter and substitute his own, but Ferdinand disguises Frank as a porter and gets him hired as Anthony's messenger. Planning to double-cross them both, Frank enlists the cripple's help. The cripple supplies him with two rejection letters to take back to his brothers, as from Phyllis. They each enlist the help of one of Phyllis’s parents, ===== PAGE 378 ===== who then quarrel over which brother shall marry their daughter. _ Knowing Phyllis loves him but thinking himself unworthy, the cripple lends Frank his clothes. Frank courts Phyllis in disguise, and she gives him her ring; then. he brings the parents forged letters from his brothers withdrawing their suits. Each parent decides to:spite the other by offering Phyllis to Frank instead of the other’s favoured son-in-law. The thwarted brothers realize what has happened, but Phyllis asks to be allowed a say in her.own marriage — and chooses Frank. As the play ends, Flower is arrested for receiving stolen property — Bobbington’s ring — and sent for trial. In the sub-plot, Mall Berry is in love with Barnard, but suffers the unwanted attentions of Bowdler. Barnard is surety for a £100 debt to Mall’s father; the original borrower is dead and the debt about to fall due, but Berry refuses to allow Barnard any more time to find the money. Bowdler persists in his pursuit of Mall, using lines he remembers from Venus and Adonis. Berry engages sergeants to arrest Barnard. Mall agrees to marry Bowdler, but the cripple points out that, through a party wall, he has overheard her talking about Barnard in her sleep: he is the man she truly loves.
