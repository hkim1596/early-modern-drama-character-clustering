Did her not see her true Loves, As her came from London, Oh, if her saw not her fine prave Loves, Randall is quite undone, Well, was neuer mortall man in Wales, coo'd have wagd pra ver, finers, and nimblers, then Randals have done, to get service in Londons: whoope, vvhere vvas her novv, just upon a pridge of stone, between the legs of a couple of pretty hils, but no more nere mountaines in Wales, then Clim of the Cloughes bowe, to her Cozen Davids Harpe, and now her prattle of Davie, I thinke yonder come prancing down the hils from Kingstone a coupl of her tother cozens Saint Nicholas Clarks. the morning was so red as an egge, and the place ferry full of dangers, perills, and bloody busi nesses by reports: augh her swords was trawne, God plesse us and her cozen Hercules was not stand against too, which shall her take if they take Randalls, will rippe Randals cutts out, and then Ran dals shall see Pawles steeples no more, therefore her will goe di rectly vnder the pridge, here was but standing to knees in little fine coole faire waters, and becat if her have Randalls out, her shall come and fetch Randalls and her will, were her nineteene Nicholas Clearkes. Exit. Plot — A Match at Midnight
Old Bloodhound is training his younger son Tim in the family pawnbroking business, since Alexander, the elder son, is a drunken wastrel. He intends to marry his daughter Mary to the informer Earlack, even though she prefers Ensign Young, while he himself will marry the young widow Wag. The widow has other suitors, including her servant Jarvis, but wants to get rid of them. She arranges a midnight assignation with Old Bloodhound, intending not to turn up. Jarvis arranges a substitute, and tells Bloodhound that his mistress has come incognito and must be addressed as Mistress Coot; in fact it really is a Mistress Coot, an old bawd, whom Bloodhound takes home that night in preparation for a wedding the next morning. Alexander gets into the widow’s bedroom and hides under her bed, then forces her to agree to marry him by threatening to undress just as neighbours appear to be arriving. The various wedding plans unravel in the morning. Earlack has sneaked the opportunity to sleep with the widow — who is really Coot the bawd and a somewhat less desirable prospect in bed once seen in daylight. Meanwhile, Tim has married Sue, not knowing her true profession until it is too late, and Mary has married Young, to the dismay of Randall, who finds that he has accidentally married a servant. Only Alexander seems on course for the wedding he wants, until the widow points out that Jarvis has a prior claim: when he tried to seduce her months ago, she demanded that he should prove himself by living in her household as her servant. Now, however, she intends to honour her dead husband by never remarrying. Jarvis reveals his true identity: he is Wag himself, the widow’s husband, still alive, and satisfied that his ruse has proven his wife’s fidelity.
