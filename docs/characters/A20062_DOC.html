Full speech

I sent for him my Lord: at last he came, And did receiue all speech that went from me, As gilded pilles made to prolong his health: My credit with him wrought it: for, some men. Swallow euen empty hookes, like fooles. that feare No drowning where tis deepest, Cause tis cleare: In th'end we sat and eate: a health I dranke To Infaelices sweete departed soule, (This traine I knew would take.) He fell with such deuotion on his knees, To pledge the same. That had he beene inflam'd with zeale of prayer, He could not power't out with more reuerence. About my necke he hung, wept on my cheeke, Kist it, and swore, he would adore my lippes, Because they brought forth Infaelices name. The cup he lifs vp high, and thus he said, Here noble maid: drinkes, and was poisoned. And died my Lord. Thankes gracious Lord: My honoured Lord: I doe beseech your grace to bury deepe, This bloudy act of mine. You throw an angry forehead on my face, But be you pleas'd, backward thus for to looke, That for your good, this euill I vndertooke, And onely for your loue. Nor let it stand against me as a bar, To thrust me from your presence: nor beleeue (As Princes haue quicke thoughts,) that now my finger Being deept in blood, I will not spare the hand, But that for gold (as what can golde not doe?) I may be hi'rde to worke the like on you, Tis from my hart as far. leaue me. Enter Hipolito. Come you must doff this blacke: die that pale cheeke, Into his owne colour; goe: Attire your selfe Fresh as a bridegroome, when he meetes his bride, The Duke has done much treason to thy loue, Tis now reuealed, tis now to be reuengde, Be mery honord friend, thy Lady liues. Infaelice, Shees reuiude; Reuiude: alacke! death neuer had the hart, To take breath from her. By that loue, Which I haue euer borne you, what I speake Is trueth: the maiden liues: that funerall, Dukes teares, the morning, was all counterfet, A sleepy draught cozend the world and you, I was his minister and then chambred vp, To stop discouery. He cannot hope so certainely for blisse: As he beleeues that I haue poysond you, He wode me toot, I yeelded, and confirm'd him, In his most bloudy thoughts. Her did he closely coach to Bergamo, And thither? You shall to her, but not to Bergamo, How passion makes you fly beyond your selfe. Much of that weary iourney I'ha cut off, For she by letters hath intelligence, Of your supposed death, her owne interment, And all those plots, which that false Duke, (her father) Has wrought against you: And sheele meete you. Nay see: how couetous are your desires, Ea ely to morrow morne. At Bethlem monasterie: are you pleasd now? Weele steale away, this night in some disguise, Father Anselmo, a most euerend Frier, Expects our comming, before whom weele lay, Reasons so strong, that he shall yeeld, in bonds, Oh holy wedlocke, to tie both your hands. Let all your ioyes then die in misbeliefe, I will reueale no more. Weele hence this night, much must be done, much said But if the Doctor faile not in his charmes, Your Lady shall ere morning fill these armes.

Plot summary

Plot — 1 The Honest Whore (1 The Converted Courtesan) (The Patient Man and the Honest Whore)
Count Hippolito attempts to make a scene at the funeral of the Duke’s daughter, Infelice, but is restrained. In fact, Infelice is not dead: the Duke administered a sleeping draught to prevent her from marrying Hippolito. When she wakes, he tells her Hippolito is dead and sends her away to Bergamo, then accepts Dr Benedict’s offer to poison Hippolito. Bellafront the courtesan falls in love with Hippolito when his friend and her client Matheo brings him to meet her. He mistrusts her protestation that, for him, she will become honest, and speaks harshly against whores. But she fulfils her promise: dismisses her pander Roger (who quickly finds alternative employment) and sends away her former clients, including Matheo. On a Monday, she goes to visit Hippolito, disguised as a page; she finds him in solemn meditation (since Infelice supposedly died on a Monday), and he rejects her again. Dr Benedict tells the Duke that he has been poisoned, and is banished for it; but in fact he has done no such thing, and tells Hippolito that Infelice is not only alive, but waiting at the madhouse to marry him. Meanwhile, Fustigo returns to Milan to find that his sister has married Candido, a man apparently incapable of anger. This is demonstrated in a wager between three gentlemen who try to make him lose his temper: first Castruccio poses as a customer and makes unreasonable demands, which Candido meets even though it means spoiling a piece of cloth; then Fluello steals a beaker from the household, for which Candido has them arrested, but then genially invites them to dinner. Viola also wants to make her husband angry, and has Fustigo pose as a swaggerer and attempt to seduce her. When he tries, however, Candido’s apprentices take it upon themselves to beat him while their master is away, and Candido’s equanimity remains intact. Viola continues to behave provocatively: she refuses Candido the key to fetch his gown for a senate meeting, so he goes wearing a carpet instead; she has George dress in his clothes and 1604 pose as him on his return, but Candido’s good-natured response is to dress himself as an apprentice; and he even looks on the bright side of a beating administered by ruffians hired by Fustigo to avenge his injury. Finally Viola has him committed to the madhouse, but soon changes her mind and petitions the Duke for his release.
