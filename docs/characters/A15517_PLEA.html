Pleasure hath soar'd as dooth his ympreze show, To lo ke alooffe on earthly Ladies all, Yet neuer could my curious eie discerne A Dame of woorth, for Londons Pleasures loue, But one, and she dooth shine as siluer Dooue. Of selfe bred soile, of London is her race, For whom in challenge I my shield aduaunce. I seeke but one and her vnto my selfe. Hearke ye. Whisper in his eare. VVhy pompe ? But pleasure honors Lucre most. Will, be not wanton nor of waiward mood, To Will. Waite as doo these, vse faith and diligence, And marke him well that dare disdaine this shield, Which Londons Lord that pleasure hath to name, Hath here aduaunc'd in honor of his Dame. I bid thée marke him well, what e'er he be, That Londons pleasure dooth in malice scorne, For he's a Rascal, or a straunger borne. Good boy marke well his iesture and his looke, His eie, his gate, his weapon and attire, And dog him to his lodging, or his denne, For I will make him scomme and scorne of men, No better boy than Wil, when Wil is pleasde, Be pleasd my boy, and so be my good Wil. Wil can do something too when pleaseth him. At libertie, thou spotlesse Magistrat , That of the cause doost carie all regard, Carelesse of bribes, of birth and parentage, Because thy selfe art onely borne to blisse, Blesse vs so much that Lordes of London are, That those thrée Ladies borne and bred with vs May by our suites, release of thraldome nd. Thou from perpetuall prison maist reuoke. To marie them, and make them honest wiues. And doth forget the faults that were before. Their same is more then cause or reason would, May one of these be pleasures paragon? May we not sée their beautie what it is? A daintie face. A gallant grace. Swéet creature of the world. And Londons pleasure, peerles in delightes, Wil deigne to make one of these Dames his owne, Who may with him in more contentment liue, Than euer did the Quéene of Ethiope. And Conscience by sincerity. So I affirme, and so I wil maintaine That Ladie Lucre ought of right be his. So I affirme, and so I wil maintaine That pleasure ought by right Dame Lucre haue, To beare the charge of sportes and of delightes. What pleasure can be fostered without cost: Go, we abide thy doome til thy returne, If she loue happie life, she wil be mine, Women loue Pleasure. If pleasure may for wife faire lucre gaine, Her life shall be an earthly Paradice. Were Conscience halfe so sweete as is thy selfe, Her would I seek with suites and seruices. But on this Dame hath pleasure fixt his hart, And this or death the period of his loue. And art thou Conscience ? welcommer to me than either loue or Lucre, Go seeke els where, for here's no place for such. I think it good, and wish the enemie come. Boies, take our sheildes and speares, for they come on. this Conscience fauour, she my mistresse is. Agréed, here Fealty, hang them vp a space. At Tyrannies wil I bestow my blow, wishing the maister. with pageants, plates, and what delights may be to entertaine the time and companie. I like ye verie well, stay a while for your answere, Why foole, tis for thy good to giue thée money for that, that might lie and rust by thée. Thou shalt haue helpe, speake, what is the matter? Is this true M. Skil? Wisely perfourm'd, but soft sirs, where is Fraud ? O noble vil laine, gone whiles we beheld the other: Who loosed him? Who let him slip? wel, one day he wil pay for all: vnblind Simplicity. Well now thou art satisfied, I wish all here as wel contented, And we my Lords that praise this happie day, Fall wee on knees and humbly let vs pray. On all the rest that in this Land doo dwell, Chiefly in London, Lord poure downe thy grace, Who liuing in thy feare and dying well, In heauen with Angels they may haue a place.

Plot summary

Plot — The Three Lords and Three Ladies of London
The three lords of London, in want of wives, plan to marry the three ladies. There are two complications. ===== PAGE 450 ===== First, it emerges that they all want the same lady, Lucre. More pressingly, all three ladies are in prison. The lords agree to secure their freedom and worry later about who marries whom. But Judge Nemo has heard petitions for the ladies’ release before: many men wanted Lucre, some wanted Love, and nobody cared about Conscience. His conditions are that either all three ladies go free or none of them, and that, if they do, he shall decide which lady marries which lord. The ladies are taken from prison and sat on uncomfortable penitential stones. They are visited by Lucre’s former servants, who were banished from London but now want their old jobs back; Lucre is unable to accommodate them. Falsehood and Double- Dealing offer the ladies clothes to replace their prison rags, but they refuse. Nemo arranges for them to have more virtuous garments, the first stage in their rehabilitation. When the three lords continue to quarrel over which of them should have Lucre, Nemo agrees to give up his right to make the marital dispositions, on condition that the lords accept the lady’s own choice. He then has Conscience pose as Lucre. Each lord offers her a different benefit — power, magnificence, and hedonism - and she eventually bestows herself on Pleasure. He is happy with his fiancée even when he learns of the deception. Conscience declares that Policy should marry Love and that Lucre is best suited to Pomp. Before the lords can proceed with their marriages, however, they must repel a Spanish invasion. Three lords of Spain arrive and plan to take the ladies for themselves. The London lords defeat them and present their captured shields to their ladies. Nemo grants the two remaining ladies to the appropriate lords, but three lords of Lincoln intervene, claiming pre-contracts with the ladies. The London lords outface their rivals, who are sent away with the penitential stones in lieu of the ladies. In the sub-plot, Fraud sells Simplicity some cheap ‘gold’, which turns out to be mere copper, so Simplicity repeatedly attempts to have him arrested. Posing as Skill and Fair Semblance, Fraud and Dissimulation seek jobs with the lords, but are recognized.
