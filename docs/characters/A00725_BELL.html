You are insolent, nay strangely sawcie Sir, To wrong me in this publicke fashion. Then this Crosse accident doth rellish ours. Sister, this resolution is not good, Ill thriues that Marriage that begins in blood. Letter: Ha? What doost thou mocke me? Heere is nothing writ. Oh Basiliske, remooue thee from my sight, Or thy harts bloud shall pay thy rash attempt. Ho, Who attends vs there? Oh, I am sicke of my corruption, For Godsake do not speake a word more to me. Oh peace, for you speake sharpnesse to my soule More torturous, then hels plagues to the damn'd, For loue sake heare me speake. Tyrant, my knees shall beg, Till they get liberty for my tongue to speake, Drown'd almost in the Riuers of mine eyes. Alas I was enforst, first by the threats Of a seuere Father, that in his hand Did gripe my fortunes; next to that, the fame Of your neglect, and liberall tongue, Which bred my honour an eternall wrong. Oh! still retaine her, deare Scudmore heare me. Oh rather shoot me friend, Then let me heare thee speake such bitternesse. Oh pitty me, redeeme from the hell That in this Marriage I am like to feele, Ile rather flye to barren wildernesses, and suffer all wants with thee Scudmore, then Liue with all plentie in this husbands armes, Thou shalt perceiue I am not such a woman, That is transported with vaine dignities, Oh thy deare words haue knockt at my harts gates, and entred: They haue pluckt the Diuels Vizard. (That did deforme this face, and blinde my soule) Off, and thy Bellafront presents her selfe, (Lau'd in a Bath of contrite Virginall teares,) Cloath'd in the Originall beautie that was thine: Now for thy loue to God, count this not done, Let time go backe, and be as when before it, Or from thy memorie race it for euer. Heare me, and thou shalt go: I do confesse I do deserue all this, Haue wounded all the faith my sex doth owe, But will recouer it, or pay my life: Striue not to go, for you shall heare me first, I charge thee Scudmore, thou hard-hearted man, Vpon my knees: thou most implacable man, 〈…〉 since penitence and satisfaction to, gets not thy pardon, I charge thee vse some meanes to set me free, Before the Reuels of this night haue end, Preuent my entering to this marriage bed; Or by the memorie of Lucretiaes knife, Ere Morne Ile die a Virgin, though a wise. Exit. Titles and State de'e call it: Oh Content! Thou art both beauty, meanes, and all in marriage: Ioy dwels not in the Princes Pallaces, They that enuie em do not know their cares, Were I the Queene of Gold, it could not buy An houres ease, for my oppressed heart. Oh, were this Wedlocke knot to tie againe, Not all the State and glorie it containes, Ioyn'd with my Fathers fury, should enforce My rash consent; but Scudmore thou shalt see, This false heart (in my death) most true to thee. My Lord, my Father, all the Companie Did note my sodaine sadnesse now at Supper, Yet came I out, and put on faigned mirth, And meane to sit out this nights Reuels too, To auoide all suspect may grow in em, Least my behauiour should my intent reueale: Our greefes (like loue) we hardly can conceale, Yon comes my Sisters: Are the Maskers ready? Ha? Euen to death. In short know, that I am married to this Gent. To whom I was contracted long ago: This Priest the inuiolable knot hath ty'de, What ease I finde being vn-Ladifyed.

Plot summary

Plot — A Woman Is a Weathercock
Scudmore is in love with Bellafront, and his friend Nevill persuades him, with difficulty, to show the love- letter he has received from her. There is a slight complication: Nevill is on his way to .Bellafront’s wedding. She is to marry Count Frederick that morning. The whole family is mired in matrimonial competition. Bellafront’s sister Lucida also loves Frederick, and has sworn never to marry for his sake; but she is being courted by the foolish Sir Abraham Ninny, who has got himself knighted expressly to make himself a more eligible son-in-law. The youngest daughter, Katherine, is also to be married that morning, to Strange the merchant, but she too has another admirer, Captain Pouts. Unbeknown to Scudmore, Nevill impersonates the parson who is to officiate at the wedding. Scudmore tries to catch Bellafront at the church door, but she refuses him; this convinces him that all women are perfidious. After the wedding, Pouts publicly declares that Katherine has slept with him on many previous occasions. Strange seems reluctant to seek legal redress for this slander, but only because he doesn’t want anyone to stop him secretly taking a more immediate revenge. He tries to see Pouts at his lodging, but Pouts assumes he has come to serve a writ and will not let him in. Pouts then prepares to flee England and his creditors, but Strange intercepts him disguised as a soldier, claiming that Katherine engaged him to murder her new husband and that she loves only Pouts. Pouts admits that he slandered her; Strange wounds him in a fight and forces him to sign a document affirming her innocence. By a pretext, Scudmore gets in to see Bellafront, rebukes her, and returns her love-letters. She tells him that her father forced her to marry Frederick, and that she will kill herself rather than consummate the marriage. He mistrusts her, but Nevill arranges for him to participate in a wedding masque that evening. He and Bellafront sneak away during the dancing and are married, this time by the right parson. Suspicions of polyandry are dispelled when Nevill explains that the parson that morning was really him, so that the marriages are invalid. Strange brings Pouts to apologise to the gathering, but is almost arrested for his own murder until he reveals his identity. Pouts returns to the war, abjuring women.
