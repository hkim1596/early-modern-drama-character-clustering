Full speech

Master Major! We thanke you for your entertainment, And for your princely present, a cup of gold! In gratefullnes we backe returne the keyes With all the embleames of your government; We in our progresse, are a sojourner, Not an inhabitant, we will be so with you; A welcome fuller of bounty, vertue, love, We have not seene; therefore to gratulate As a small token of our princely love, On, to your former motion made for Trent. You'd have it navigable to Gainsborough So to Boston, Kingston, Humber, and Hull; But, what are the causes? I have tasted your welcome, and would faine Grant your designe, soe you give reason. Ya'r a merry man Master Major. On, to your Charter. You shall enjoy your wishes; VVe thanke you; VVelcome renowned Iohn of Wilton, And you the war like Heroes of his traine, VVar-like Clifton; fame has ben before thee, And with her shrill Trumpe sent your praises home, E're your arivall; rise noble John of Wilton; The onely champion of Elizabeth. And they shall have them; welcome bold Arguile, Thanke thou the god of batles, that hast given Prosperity to our first enterprise, Being the first Batle that we ever wag'd, Link'd victory unto a virgin's arme, For which we render thee all attributes, Guarded by thee, and these our loving subjects, VVe feare noe Spanish force, nor French-mens braves, Let Austria bragge; and Rome, and Italy Send out their poyson'd Darts; dreadlesse we stand Protected by thy never failing power, Lord Grey, returne you governour of Barwicke, The Duke of Norfolke, for some speciall causes VVe must recall; Arguile shall have his pleadges, VVe but reserv'd them to preserve our selves, Clifton; be thou our deputy Leivetenant, And Lord warden of Nottingham Castle, Our selfe wilbe Leivetenant of the County. For Howard, Pellham, Leake, and all the rest That in this victory shar'd with dangers, They shall participate our princely loves, I know not how to dignifie your deedes VVithout a large premeditation; Grey, and Clifton, Clifton, and war-like Grey Fought for our father, brother, and sister At Dennis, Roan, Bullen, and at Callice The bloudy sweat that Muslborough bredd At Edenborough, and now againe at Leith, In all which we fortunately conquer'd, Thankes unto heaven, next your valiant hands. Master Maior, and noble Iohn of Wilton, And war-like Clifton with all your men of warre VVe this night doe invite you, for our gueasts, To sup with us; to morrow wee'le survey The underminings, and unpaced greise That Mortimer, and Isabell did devise To steale their sportive daliancies in, Of whom your stately fortresse does retaine The Labyrinth (now called Mortimers hole) Heaven for our victory we first will pay, And praise our subjects that redeem'd the day, Proud France, and poysening Spaine, if heave'n us blesse A virgin's arme shall quell your mightines.

Plot summary

Plot — The Vow Breaker, or The Fair Maid of Clifton
Though Young Bateman is betrothed to Anne Boot and loved by her, her father reneges on the ===== PAGE 347 ===== 1628 arrangement. Meanwhile, war looms: England enters an alliance with a federation of Scottish lords hostile to the French Queen Regent of Scotland, and troops are being recruited to march north. Bateman joins up, hoping that his absence will make Old Boot’s heart grow fonder, and Anne vows to love only him; but her father puts pressure on her to marry German, and, seduced by his wealth, she agrees. The French are vanquished, and Bateman distinguishes himself in battle; but he is troubled by dreams about Anne, and returns home on the day of her wedding, just missing the ceremony. German goes away on a long business trip, leaving Anne in her father’s house. Old Bateman sues Boot for breach of the pre-contract; Young Bateman hangs himself outside Anne’s door, and his ghost haunts Anne, invisible to the rest of her family. Anne gives birth to a baby girl. While the women around her sleep, she sees the ghost and runs out of the house; following her footsteps in the snow, the household find her drowned in the river. The two bereaved fathers, Boot and Bateman, commiserate together. In Scotland, Sir Gervase Clifton insults the Queen Regent by drawing an unfavourable comparison with Queen Elizabeth, and captures Martigues in a single combat, but releases him with honour. Joshua the Puritan intends to hang his cat for Sabbath-breaking - she killed a mouse on Sunday - but the cat is saved by the arrival of a French ambassador. The English attack Leith, and Clifton captures a French colonel’s armour. Hostilities are ended by diplomatic means, and Scotland is to be demilitarized. The English and French lords feast together, and Joshua gets drunk. The English troops return south to meet the Queen at Nottingham; the common soldiers plan to put on a play for her, but decide on a morris dance instead. She meets the civic authorities and rewards her military commanders.
