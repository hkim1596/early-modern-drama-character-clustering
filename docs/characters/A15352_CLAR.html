Troth Sir, carrying no watch about me but mine eyes, I answer you: I cannot tell. Yet I am big enough to keepe my sclfe. I know not that neither, til I am better acquainted with a man. To distinguish betwixt himselfe and my selfe. O Lorde Sir, three or foure thinges: I Loue my meate, choise of Suters: Cloathes in the Fashion: and like a right woman I loue to haue my will. Let me first know, what you think of me for a wife? Do you but thinke so? It is great pitty then I should be alone without a proper man. And I say God forbid Sir: I am a great deale to young. O pray you do not so, for then you stray from the steps of Gentility, the fashion among them is to marry first, and loue after by leisure. You will not inforce me I hope Sir? For better, for worse. Why then I thanke you Sir, and now I am like to haue that I long lookt for: A Husband. How soone from our owne tongues is the word sed, Captiues our maiden-freedome to a head. We being thus subdued, pray you know then, As women owe a duty, so do men. Men must be like the branch and barke to trees, Which doth defend them from tempestuous rage, Cloth them in Winter, tender them in age, Or as Ewes loue vnto their Eanlings liues, Such should be husbands custome to their wiues. If it appeare to them they haue straid amisse, They onely must rebuke them with a kisse, Or Clock them, as Hens Chickens, with kind call, Couer them vnder their wing, and pardon all: No iarres must make two beds, no strife deuide them, Those betwixt whom a faith and troth is giuen, Death onely parts, since they are knit by heauen: If such a husband you intend to be, I am your Clare, and you are fit for me. Aduise before you sweare, let me remember you, Men neuer giue their faith, and promise mariage, But heauen records their oth: If they proue true, Heauen smiles for ioy, if not it weepes for you, Vnlesse your hart, then with your wordes agree, Yet let vs part, and lesse vs both be free. Till when you please, where ere you liue or lie, Your loues here worne, your presence in my eie. Such welcome as befits my Scarborrows brothers, From me his troth-plight wife be sure to haue, And tho my tongue proue scant in any part, The bounds be sure are large, full in my hart. Returnd so soone from London? Whats the newes? A Letter from my Scarborrow, giue it thy mistris. I am glad your gone, Shall I now opent: no, Ile kisse it first, Because his outside last did kisse his hand. Within this fould, Ile calt a sacred sheet, Are writ blacke lines, when our white harts shall meet, Before I ope this dore of my delight, Methinkes I gesse how kindly he doth write, Of his true Loue to me, as Chuck, Sweet-hart, I prethee do not thinke the time too long, That keepes vs from the sweets of marriage rites, And then he sets my name and kisses it, Wishing my lips his sheet to write vpon, With like desire methinkes as mine owne thoughts, Aske him now heere for me to looke vpon, Yet at the last thinking his loue too slacke, Ere it arriue at my desired eyes, He hastens vp his message with like speed, Euen as I breake this ope, wishing to read: Oh: whats hear? I know my Scarborrowes constancie to me, Is as firme knit, as faith to Charity, That I shall kisse him often, hug him thus, Be made a happy and a fruitfull Mother Of many prosperous children like to him, And read I, he was maried? He has set his name too't to, O periury? within the harts of men Thy feasts are kept, their tongues proclaimeth them. Kind Sir, excuse me I pray you a little, Ile but peruse this Letter and come straight. He was contracted mine, yet he vniust Hath married to another: whats my estate then? A wretched maid, not fit for any man, For being vnited his with plighted faiths, Who euer sues to me commits a sinne, Besiedgeth me, and who shal marry me: Is like my selfe, liues in Adultery, (O God) That such hard Fortune, should betide my youth. Plot summary

Plot — The Miseries of Enforced Marriage
The ward William Scarborow betroths himself to Clare Harcop, but on his return to London he is forced to marry Katherine, the niece of his guardian, Lord Falconbridge. He is rewarded with control of his own’ and his younger brothers’ property. He writes to tell Clare the news of his unwelcome marriage; upon receiving the letter, she dies (possibly by suicide). ===== PAGE 337 ===== 1606 Scarborow takes her death badly: he blames his wife, and becomes prodigal. Ilford fakes his own arrest for debt in order to induce Scarborow to guarantee the supposed loan, thereby defrauding him of his money. This still leaves him with the patrimony of his younger brothers, Thomas and John; but he spends it, and drunkenly assaults his friends and kinsmen. The brothers and the honest family servant Butler are forced into theft to keep themselves from starvation. They steal £300 from Harcop, Clare’s father, which they use to make their sister appear to be a rich woman. Butler tricks Ilford into marrying her, but on learning that she is penniless her new husband throws her out; Butler offers to support her out of his meagre wages. Thomas confronts and fights with his elder brother, but saves his life when they are attacked by Ilford. Now penitent, Thomas declares his intention to confess to Harcop that he and John stole the money, even though it will mean hanging for them both. John falls out with him over this, but they are reconciled by Butler. Dr Baxter, who married Scarborow to Katherine, talks him into repentance. On his deathbed, Lord Falconbridge also repents and leaves the Scarborow brothers double their original wealth in restitution, along with a dowry for their sister.
