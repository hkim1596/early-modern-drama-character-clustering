I, and the best o'the Kastrills, I'lld be sory else, By fifteene hundred, a yeare Where is this Doctor? My mad Tobacco-Boy, here, tells me of one, That can doe things. Has he any skill? To cary a business, manage a Quarrell, fayrely, Vpon fit termes. Sir, not so yong, but I haue heard some speech Of the angry Boyes, and seene'hem take Tobacco; And in his shop: and I can take it too. And I would faine be one of'hem, and goe downe And practise i'the countrey. How? to take it? But, dos he teach Liuing, by the Witts, too? No, I'll not come there. You shall pardon me. There's gaming there, and tricks. I, 'twill spend a man. What, three thousand a yeare? Are there such? Doe you not gull one? Will the Doctor teach this? I faith! Is he such a fellow? Gods will, my Suster shall see him. Pray thee Tobacco-Boy, Goe fetch my Suster, I'll see this learned Boy, before I goe: And so shall he. Where's the Captaine? Gone? You lie. Brauely, i-faith! Doe you thinke so? Where do's he carry her? Agreed. I loue a Spanish Boy, with all my heart. I, I knew that, at first. This match will aduance the House of the Kastrils. Why, Her name is so, by her other Husband. The Widdow Pliant. Knew you not that? Yes, but doe you thinke, Doctor. I e'er shall quarrell well? Where is he? Which is he? He is a Slaue What ere he is, and the Sonne of a Whore. Are you The Man, Sir, I would know? Then you lie, i' your throte. You lie, And 'tis no matter. Be gone Sir, quickly. I, I know—Away, you talke like a foolish Mauther. Yes, how then Sir? I will. Sir, if you get not out o' doores, you lie, And you are a Pimpe. It is my humor: you are a Pimpe, and a Trig, And an Amadis de Gaule, or a Don Quixote. Ile keepe peace, for no man. Is he the Constable? Then you are an Otter, and a Shad, a Whit, A very Tim. I will not. Will you be gone, Sir? Be gone Sir. Hence Sir. Did I not quarrell brauely? Nay and I giue my minde to't, I shall do't, I'll re-turne him, then. What Rogues, Baudes, Slaues, you'il open the dore anone. Punque, Cocatrice, my Suster. By this light I'll fetch the Marshall to you. You are a Whore, To keepe your Castle. The baudy Doctor, and the Cosening Captaine, And Pus my Suster. I haue heard all their tricks, told me twice ouer, By the fat Knight, and the leane Gentleman. My Suster's there. I, I will fetch the Scauenger, and the Cunstable. You will not come then? Punque, Deuise, my Suster? I'll raise the street. 'Slight, ding it open. Doxey, my Suster. The Nun my Suster. Plot — The Alchemist
With his master absent to avoid the plague in London, Face has set up an occult consultancy in his house in association with the formerly itinerant rogues Subtle and Doll Common: Face fetches in the clients and Subtle poses as the alchemist. They quarrel over the relative merits of their contributions to the enterprise, but resolve to collaborate competitively in the day’s ===== PAGE 80 ===== business. They are visited by successive clients: Dapper, who wants a familiar to help him win at gambling; Drugger, who wants advice on the most auspicious layout for his shop; Sir Epicure Mammon, who seeks the philosopher’s stone to fulfil his fantasies of wealth, luxury, and voluptuousness, and who brings along with him the sceptic Surly; and the religious sectarians Ananias and Tribulation, who want both the stone and Subtle’s claimed expertise in counterfeiting foreign coins. Dapper is persuaded that he is the Fairy Queen’s nephew and heir, and is promised an introduction to her. Mammon brings various domestic articles for aurification, which Subtle then sells on to Ananias and Tribulation, saying they belong to orphans. Clients come thick and fast. Drugger introduces his neighbours, Kastril (who wants te learn how to quarrel) and the wealthy widow Pliant (who wants her fortune told and whom Face and Subtle both want to marry). They are sent away so that the tricksters can deal with Dapper, who in his turn is secreted in the privy while they attend to Mammon. The knight courts Doll, who is presented to him as a lord’s sister prone to religious mania, which he activates by ill-chosen words; but he is then told that his lust has caused a setback in the alchemical process, which demands moral purity. Meanwhile, Surly returns, disguised as a Spanish count seeking sexual favours; but, since Doll is busy with Mammon, he is foisted on Dame Pliant, who is tempted with the chance of becoming a Spanish countess. His attempt to tell her the truth is outmanoeuvred when Face and Subtle convince her that he is an impostor and that the real Count is on his way; Drugger is persuaded to impersonate him and sent off to borrow a Spanish suit for the role. Lovewit, the master of the house, returns to hear Tumours of strange goings-on. Face’s explanation is confuted by the cries of Dapper, forgotten in the privy, so he confesses everything.
