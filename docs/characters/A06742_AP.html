Good Ladyes, your conceites in iudgement erre, Becauſe you ſee me dumpiſh, you referre The reaſon to ſome ſecret griefe of mine: But you haue ſeene me melancholy many a time, Perhaps it is the glowing weather now, That makes me ſeeme ſo ill at eaſe to you. No Ladies, you deceiue your ſelues in mee: What likelihood or token do ye ſee, That may perſwade it true that you ſuppoſe? Alas faire dames, to make my ſorows plain, Would but reuiue an auncient wound again. Which grating preſently vpon my minde, Doth leaue a ſcar of former woes behinde. Good Ladies, though I hope for no reliefe, Ile ſhewe the ground of this my preſent griefe. This time of yeare, or there about it was, Accurſed be the time, tenne times alas: When I from Delphos tooke my iourney downe, To ſee the games in noble Sparta Towne, There ſaw I that, wherein I gan to ioy, Amilchars ſonne a gallant comely boy, Hight (Hiacinth) full fifteene yeares of age, Whom I intended to haue made my Page, And bare as great affection to the boy, As euer Ioue, in Ganimede did ioy. And yet no Goddeſſe, nor of heauenly kinde She is, whoſe beautie thus torments my minde. Plot summary

Plot — The Maid's Metamorphosis
Considering Eurymine too low-born to be a suitable match for his son Ascanio, Duke Telemachus orders two of his gentlemen to escort her from the court and murder her. In the event, however, Phylander persuades his colleague that they have no obligation to follow unlawful orders. They plan to present the Duke with an animal’s heart, claiming it to be hers, and Eurymine agrees to live away from the court in country exile. She meets a forester and a shepherd, each of whom wants her to live with him; when they become competitive, she agrees to accept a cottage from the forester and a flock of sheep from the shepherd. Ascanio goes into the woods looking for Eurymine. Juno finds him asleep and, eager to prove her superiority to Venus, arranges for him to dream of where to find his love. Meanwhile, Eurymine meets Apollo, who is also attracted to her. To save herself from rape, she claims to disbelieve his claims of godhead, and asks him to prove himself by working a miracle: specifically, she asks him to change her sex. He does, but with an associated curse: as a man, she will fall hopelessly in love with another man, and long to be a woman again. Ascanio learns from an echo that Eurymine is nearby but inaccessible, and the hermit Aramanthus, who is also a dispossessed prince, explains her metamorphosis. This disappoints not only Ascanio but also her country admirers, and Aramanthus recommends that she should ask the Graces to intercede with Apollo on her behalf. Apollo eventually agrees and restores her original gender. Telemachus has a change of heart and sends Phylander to bring Eurymine back from the forest. Apollo reveals that she is in fact Aramanthus’ long-lost daughter, and so of royal blood: her status is after all no obstacle to her marriage with Ascanio.
