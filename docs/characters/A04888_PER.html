That wer pretie I faith, to see Honesty know a knaue by his cap, Tis more than I can do with al the skil I haue: But tel me I pray thee, how I should know a knaue. You see father, this marchant wil be witnes That he saw so much money tendred, And you receiued it, being full satisfyed, As the last payment for the farme he bought, And if this marchant take his oath against you, That seuen daies past he saw the mony tendered, I must passe sentence then against you needs. But wil you sweare on the bible this is true? God saue you gentlemen, the king greets you, and at this time, Hauing some occasion, to vse mony, hath sent to know What you that be Knightes and Squires wil lend his Grace, And you maister Farmer, be brief (sir) for I cannot stay. Very wel, but what saith the Farmer? What can he spare the King? Why thats well said, and I commend thy honest mind, Would all men wer of thy mind: I warrant thee, thou art an honest man, & one that loues the king But tel me, what wouldst thou haue me doe? Sir, feare not, I wil do it for you, I warrant you, For I tel you, I can do much with the King. I thanke you sir hartily. Trulie Honesty, if I were furnisht with money, I would not stick to giue thee thy dinner, But now thou seest I am but a guest my selfe. It is my gratious Lord and honest man, And one it seemes that loues you Maiestie: For as your Grace gaue me in charge, I went about into the countrey, to see what summes of money I could make, Among the cheefest of the communaltie: And mongst the richest Knights that I could fynd, They would lend your Grace at most but twenty ponnd, And euery Squire would lend you Grace but ten: Then came I mongst the rest to this plaine man, And asked him what he would lend the king, He aunswered sir, you see I am but poore, Not halfe so wealthy as a Knight or Squire, And yet in signe of dutie to his Grace, I wil lende his Maiestie two hundred pound. My gratious Lord, Ethenwald hath dissembled with your Maiesty, For Alfrida is fair and vertuous: For last night, being in priuate conference, He tould me he had deuised a mean To collour with the King by forged excuse, No, no, (quoth he) my Alfrida is faire, As is the radiant North star Christaline, That guides the wet and wearie Traueller, Soust with the surge of Neptunes watery main, And thus my Lord, he fell to praising her, And from his pocket straight he drew this counterfeit, And said twas made by beauteous Alfrida. Nothing but to haue a license to brew strong Ale thrise a we k, and he that comes to Goteham, and will not spende a penie on a pot of Ale, if he be a drie, that he may fast. My Lord, this is not Alfrida, this is the kitchin maid, Why Dunston, you haue seene as wel as I, That Ethenwald hath dissembled with the King, My gratious Lord, first cut that Traitor downe. And then will others feare the lyke amysse.

Plot summary

Plot — A Knack to Know a Knave
. . Concerned for the good order of his kingdom, King Edgar commissions Honesty to seek out and expose knaves. The dying Bailiff of Hexham advises his four sons to live by fraud and deceit in their various: professions, and to enrich themselves at the expense of the commonweal and the poor..He dies and is carried off by the devil. While Edgar deals with the case of 1592 Philarchus, a courtier who has abused his father, Honesty observes the corrupt dealings of the Bailiff’s sons: Cuthbert, a cony-catcher, who bears false witness in an attempt to take possession of a farm; Walter, a farmer, who oppresses his neighbours and exports corn to feed the enemy; John, a priest, who refuses to give to charity; and Perin, a courtier, who misuses the royal seal and sells Walter an export licence for his corn under pretext of raising revenues for the crown. Meanwhile honest members of the state, from knight to ploughman, complain of their economic decline. Edgar sends Ethenwald to woo Alfrida by proxy, but Ethenwald falls in love with her himself and marries her. The King forgives him and visits the couple (receiving a petition from the mad men of Gotham on the way), but the jealous Ethenwald is convinced that Edgar plans to cuckold him, and has Alfrida and the kitchen maid swap identities during the royal visit. An outraged Edgar sees through the subterfuge and plans to take revenge, but Bishop Dunstan, with the help of the devil, persuades him to pardon Ethenwald instead. Honesty exposes the various knaves he has observed, and, with royal permission, sentences them all to gruesome and unusual punishments.
