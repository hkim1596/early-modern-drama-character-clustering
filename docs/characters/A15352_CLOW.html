I haue heard of many blacke Iacks Sir, but neuer of a blew Bottle. No Sir, I am twenty yardes without, and the house stands without me. He that dwels in it Sir. He that owes it. I was none of his God-father. Ile giue you a rime for that Sir, Sicke men may lie, and dead men in their Graues, Few else do lie abed at noone, but Drunkards, Punks, & knaues. What am I the better for thy question? Why then of nothing comes nothing. Then I that am a foole by Art, am better then you that are fooles by nature. As men do in hast, to make an end of their busines. My busines is this Sir, this Sir, and this Sir, No Sir. Because as the learned haue very well instructed me, Qui supranos, nihil ad nos, and tho many Gentlemen will haue to doe with other mens busines, yet from me know, the most part of them proue knaues for their labor. As you by your good cloaths, tooke them vpon trust, & swore I would neuer pay for em. Well, I could alter mine eies from filthy mud into fair wa ter: you haue paid for my teares, and mine eyes shal proue banke routs, and breake out for you, let no man perswade me, I will cry, and euery Towne betwixt Shoreditch-church and Yorke bridge, shall beare me witnesse. O mistris, if euer you haue seene Demoniceacleare look in to mine eyes, mine eyes are Seuerne, plaine Seuerne, the Thamee, nor the Ryuer of Tweed are nothing to em: Nay all the rayne that fell at Noahs floud, had not the discretion that my eyes haue: that drunke but vp the whole world, and I ha drownd all the way be twixt this and London. My newes mistres, Ile tell you strange newes, the dust vp on London way, being so great, that not a Lorde, Gentleman, Knight, or Knaue could trauell, least his eies should bee blowne out: At last, they all agreed to hyre me to go before them, when I looking but vpon this Letter, did with this water, this very water, lay the dust, as well as if it had raind from the beginning of Aprill to the last of May. Plot summary

Plot — The Miseries of Enforced Marriage
The ward William Scarborow betroths himself to Clare Harcop, but on his return to London he is forced to marry Katherine, the niece of his guardian, Lord Falconbridge. He is rewarded with control of his own’ and his younger brothers’ property. He writes to tell Clare the news of his unwelcome marriage; upon receiving the letter, she dies (possibly by suicide). ===== PAGE 337 ===== 1606 Scarborow takes her death badly: he blames his wife, and becomes prodigal. Ilford fakes his own arrest for debt in order to induce Scarborow to guarantee the supposed loan, thereby defrauding him of his money. This still leaves him with the patrimony of his younger brothers, Thomas and John; but he spends it, and drunkenly assaults his friends and kinsmen. The brothers and the honest family servant Butler are forced into theft to keep themselves from starvation. They steal £300 from Harcop, Clare’s father, which they use to make their sister appear to be a rich woman. Butler tricks Ilford into marrying her, but on learning that she is penniless her new husband throws her out; Butler offers to support her out of his meagre wages. Thomas confronts and fights with his elder brother, but saves his life when they are attacked by Ilford. Now penitent, Thomas declares his intention to confess to Harcop that he and John stole the money, even though it will mean hanging for them both. John falls out with him over this, but they are reconciled by Butler. Dr Baxter, who married Scarborow to Katherine, talks him into repentance. On his deathbed, Lord Falconbridge also repents and leaves the Scarborow brothers double their original wealth in restitution, along with a dowry for their sister.
