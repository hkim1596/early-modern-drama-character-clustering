Is he gone: is he blowne of? now out vpon him vnsuffe rably iealous foole. Didst thou giue him the fam'd report? do's he beleeue I am with child? do's he giue faith? That's most full truth, he hath most vniustly seuered his sheetes euer since the old Duke Pietro (heauen rest his soule.) Euer since the old Duke Pietro, the great Deuill of hell torture his soule. Why? tis indifferent to them what you say foole, but do's my Lord rauell out, do's he fret? for pitie of an aflicted Ladie load him soundly, let him not goe cleere from vexation, hee has the most dishonourably, with the most sinfull, most vitious obstinacie, perseuered to wronge mee that were I not of a male constitution, twere impossible for mee to suruiue it, but in madnesse name, let him on, I ha not the weake fence of some of your soft-eyed whim pering Ladies, who, if they were vs'd like me, would gall their fin gers with wringing their handes, looke like bleeding Lucresses, and shed salt water ynough to powder all the beefe in the Dukes lar der. No, I am resolute Donna Zoya ; ha; that wiues were of my mettall, I would make these ridiculously iealous fooles, howle like a starued dogge, before he got a bit, I was created to be the affli ction of such an vnsanctified member, and will boyle him in his owne sirupe. If any man ha the wit, now let him talke wantonly, but not baudily; come Gallants who'le be my seruants: I am now ve rie open harted, and full of entertainment. Or all, I am taken with you all, with you all. Excellent Faunus I kisse thee for this, by this hand. No, good sir Amorous, your teeth hath taken rust, your breath wants ayring, and indeed I loue sound kissing. Come Gal lants, who'le run a Caranto, or leape a Leualto. No matter, now I ha the sleight, or rather the fashion of it, I feare no barrennesse. Husband? husband? as if women could haue no chil dren without husbands. No matter, thei'le be like their father, tis honour ynough to my husband, that they vouchsafe to call him father, and that his land shall discend to them (do's hee not gnash his very teeth in anguish) like our husband? I had rather they were vngroand for, like our husband? prooue such a melancholy iealous asse as he is: Do's he not stampe? Faith good ynough face for a husband, come gallants Ile daunce to mine owne whistle, I am as light now as: ah, She sings and daun ces. a kisse to you, to my sweet free seruants dreame on me, and adue. Exit Zoya. Deare my Lord husband. What fellowes that. I will ha no mercie, I wil not relent. The order shall stand, I am separated, and I wil be se parated. Hence fellow, I am none of thy wife, noe I will be ty rannous and a most deepe reuenger, the order shal stand, I will marry a fellow that keepes a fox in his bosom, a goat vn der his arme holes, and a polecat in his mouth, rather then re accept thee. Was I not handsom, generous, honest enough from my foote to my feather, for such a fellow as thou art? But goe thy waies & wiue with whome thou wilt for my part, thou hast spun a fair thread, whole kisse thee now? whole court thee now? whole ha thee now? So it is your destiny that I should thus reuenge your abuse: No the Irishman shall hate Aquauitae, the Welshman cheese and the Dutchman salt butter before ile loue or receiue thee, dos he crie? dos the babe pule? t'is to late now, thou shoul dest ha cried before, t'is to late now, goe bury thy head in silence, and let obliuion be thy vtmost hope. Well, vpon your apparant repentance, that all modest spectators may witnes, I haue for a short time onely thus fay nedly hated you, that you might euer after truely loue me, vp pon these cautions I reaccept you: first you shall vow. Faine loue to my waiting woman or chamber maide. Neuer promise them such a farme to their mariadge. If sheele discouer but whom I affect. Or if they know none that theil but take a false oath; I do, onely to be ridde of mee. That if I chance to haue a humor to be in a maske, you shall not grow Iealous. Or grudge at the expence. You shall vowe to weare cleane linnen, and feede wholsomelie.

Plot summary

Plot — Parasitaster, or The Fawn
After failing to persuade his son Tiberio to marry, Hercules, the widowed Duke of Ferrara, plans to marry Princess Dulcimel of Urbino, and sends Tiberio to negotiate the treaty. Hercules goes to Urbino himself, assumes the alias Faunus, meets Nymphadoro and Herod, and hears some unflattering remarks about himself in his other persona, Hercules. He gets them to introduce him at court, and becomes a favourite with the Duke of Urbino. Dulcimel would rather marry Tiberio than Hercules, and contrives to woo him using her father, Duke Gonzago, as an intermediary. She brings him a scarf which she has embroidered with a message, and tells him that it was sent by Tiberio; the outraged Gonzago gives it to the young man, thinking he is returning an unwanted would-be lover's gift. She then reports to ===== PAGE 176 ===== Gonzago that Tiberio intends to climb up the tree by her bedroom window and surreptitiously marry her. Gonzago repeats the plan back to Tiberio, revokes his diplomatic status, and orders him to leave Urbino. Instead, Tiberio. puts the plan into effect and marries Dulcimel in her bedroom. Faunus: observes the foolish behaviour of the courtiers at Urbino. Herod cuckolds his brother, the impotent Sir Amoroso, and gets his sister-in-law with child — in effect, dispossessing himself of the estate by producing an heir apparent. Donna Zoya, who bas not slept with her husband Zuccone for four years, feigns pregnancy, and the gossip spreads rapidly through the court, each person telling the next on condition that they keep it strictly to themselves. Zuccone repudiates her, then regrets it on learning she is not really an adulteress, but she will only take him back on new terms which give her the upper hand in the marriage. Faunus lays on a court entertainment: a parliament of Cupid to consider the many grievances lovers have sent to heaven. Cupid’s statutes are read, and several of the foolish courtiers are tried. Nymphadoro is convicted as a multiple philanderer and sent to the ship of fools, Herod for falsely boasting of love, and Granuffo for affecting silence to seem wiser than he is. Sir Amoroso (charged with wooing on false pretences) and Zuccone are spared. The last fool to be exposed is Gonzago, for procuring the seduction of his own daughter. Resuming his own identity, Hercules thanks Gonzago for securing Tiberio’s marriage.
