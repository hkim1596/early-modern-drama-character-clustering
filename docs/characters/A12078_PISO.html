Why should I loue her? because shes faire, because shees faire; because shee's a whore: for if she wore not faire, she would not be a whore; & if she were not faire, I should not loue her: Ergo, if shee were not a whore I shoulde not loue her: well concluded witte, well concluded wit; there is no man breathing could loue her but I, shee's a whore, yet her beautie haunts me like a Ghost, I cannot sleepe for't, her remembrance rides me like the Mare a nights, I cannot rest for't, what shall I doe? Let little children feare the shallow Brookes, for I can swim though't be through Seas of blood; let foolish feare goe dwell with women, for bloodie resolution shall not part from me, Ile kill them both euen with mine owne hands, Ile doo't. Vntoucht? weele doo't. Weele fight with them. But for the meanes. In which ile mingle mingle such a dram, as they shall ride to heauen in post, vnlesse they misse the way. I haue a Countrey-man in towne an Apothecarie, one Signeur Aluino, a fellow that is well stor'd, and will sell me of the best. Still tonguelesse night put off thy sable robe, Thou needs not mourne, my villanies were done By day, thou hadst no hand in them, O I am great, as is a woman that is neare her time: And life's the burthen that I beare. But t'is a bastard for that I am asha'md on't. The Law I hope is a skilfull midwife, and will soone deli uer me; grim Iustice doe thy worst, Thy crueltie shall prooue a curtesie, And baile me out of prison. Lie there thou selfe-consuming Taper, true patern of my life, I haue consumde my selfe for others, as thou hast done for mee, and nowe shee has extinguisht my life as I this light. O how obedient was my bountie, still To her commaund? my liberalitie Did fatten mischiefe, and hath made her prowde: O that too much of any thing shuld be so ill in euery thing The Suns all seeng eye, with too much vntemperate heate makes wither what it made to flourish. The earth being mother to all wholsome hearbes, With too much fatnesse oft produceth weedes. A sute of cloath doth keepe the bodie warme, When richer garments makes the wearer proude. O, the meanes the sweetest Musicke; Contentment reuels when that string is toucht; But O, the time will come she will repent My death: for when she lookes on vices face Vnmaske like mine; she will detest and loath it. Plot summary

Plot — The Fleer
Before the start of the action, Piso deposed Antifront, whose daughters thereupon fled from Florence. Antifront now travels in search of his missing children. He arrives in London under the alias Fleer, and meets some gentlemen. One of them, Spark, gets him a job with two of his countrywomen, a pair of Florentine courtesans working in London. Antifront is dismayed to discover that they are his missing daughters. Two sisters, Susan and Nan, are interested in Spark and his friend Ruffle, who are among the courtesans’ many clients. They dress up as boys and try to get the gentlemen to employ them as pages; Spark and Ruffle refuse, and the ‘pages’ end up working for two other clients, Sir John Havelittle and Piso junior, the son of the Florentine usurper. ‘Fleer’ wants to engineer a marriage for his daughters, selecting Havelittle and Piso as the eligible bachelors. The daughters prefer Spark and Ruffle, but are refused; taking offence, they ask Piso and Havelittle to kill the gentlemen, and suggest poison as the safest method. The plot is overheard by ‘Fleer’ and the ‘pages’. ‘Fleer’ gets himself another job looking after an apothecary’s shop, and sells Piso and Havelittle a poison which is really a sleeping potion. Spark and Ruffle dine with Piso and Havelittle and apparently die. The hosts are at once arrested on suspicion of poisoning and are committed for trial. Fleer is sent on an errand to the senior judge, who is unable to attend through illness, and sends word back for the other two judges to proceed without him. Given a ring to authenticate the message, Fleer instead uses it to get himself deputized as the third judge. He produces a letter which establishes that the guilt extends to the daughters as well as the poisoners, and all four are sentenced to death. News arrives that Piso’s father has died, making him the new Duke of Florence; but-he tells the Florentines to recall the deposed Antifront instead, and offers to make amends for his father’s treason by marrying Antifront’s elder daughter. Antifront reveals his identity and says Piso can do just 1606 that; his younger daughter will marry Havelittle. He produces the murder victims, still alive, and bestows them on Nan and Susan.
