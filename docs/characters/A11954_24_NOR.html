'Twixt Guynes and Arde, I was then preſent, ſaw them ſalute on Horſebacke, Beheld them when they lighted, how they clung In their Embracement, as they grew together, Which had they ▪ What foure Thron'd ones could haue weigh'd Such a compounded one? Then you loſt The view of earthly glory: Men might ſay Till this time Pompe was ſingle, but now married To one aboue it ſelfe. Each following day Became the next dayes maſter, till the laſt Made former Wonders, it's. To day the French, All Clinquant all in Gold, like Heathen Gods Shone downe the Engliſh; and to morrow, they Made Britaine, India: Euery man that ſtood, Shew d like a Mine ▪ Their Dwarfiſh Pages were As Cherubins, all gilt: the Madams too, Not vs'd to toyle, did almoſt ſweat to beare The Pride vpon them, that their very labour Was to them, as a Painting. Now this Maske Was cry'de incompareable; and th' enſuing night Made it a Foole, and Begger. The two Kings Equall in luſtre, were now beſt, now worſt As preſence did preſent them: Him in eye, Still him in praiſe, and being preſent both, 'Twas ſaid they ſaw but one, and no Diſcerner Durſt wagge his Tongue in cenſure, when theſe Sunnes (For ſo they phraſe 'em) by their Heralds challeng'd The Noble Spirits to Armes, they did performe Beyond thoughts Compaſſe, that former fabulous Storie Being now ſeene, poſſible enough, got credit That Beuis was beleeu'd. As I belong to worſhip, and affect In Honor, Honeſty, the tract of eu'ry thing, Would by a good Diſcourſer looſe ſome life, Which Actions ſelfe, was tongue too. As you gueſſe: One certes, that promiſes no Element In ſuch a buſineſſe. All this was ordred by the good Diſcretion Of the right Reuerend Cardinall of Yorke. Surely Sir, There's in him ſtuffe, that put's him to theſe ends: For being not propt by Aunceſtry, whoſe grace Chalkes Succeſſors their way; nor call'd vpon For high feats done to' th' Crowne; neither Allied To eminent Aſſiſtants; but Spider-like Out of his Selfe-drawing Web. O giues vs note, The force of his owne merit makes his way A guift that heauen giues for him, which buyes A place next to the King. Greeuingly I thinke, The Peace betweene the French and vs, not valewes The Coſt that did conclude it. Which is budded out, For France hath flaw'd the League, and hath attach'd Our Merchants goods at Burdeux. Marry is't. Like it your Grace, The State takes notice of the priuate difference Betwixt you, and the Cardinall. I aduiſe you (And take it from a heart, that wiſhes towards you Honor, and plenteous ſafety) that you reade The Cardinals Malice, and his Potency Together; To conſider further, that What his high Hatred would effect, wants not A Miniſter in his Power. You know his Nature, That he's Reuengefull; and I know, his Sword Hath a ſharpe edge: It's long, and't may be ſaide It reaches farre, and where 'twill not extend, Thither he darts it. Boſome vp my counſell, You'l finde it wholeſome. Loe, where comes that Rock That I aduice your ſhunning. What are you chaff'd? Aske God for Temp'rance, that's th' appliance onely Which your diſeaſe requires. Stay my Lord, And let your Reaſon with your Choller queſtion What 'tis you go about: to climbe ſteepe hilles Requires flow pace at firſt. Anger is like A full hot Horſe, who being allow'd his way Selfe-mettle tyres him: Not a man in England Can aduiſe me like you: Be to your ſelfe, As you would to your Friend. O feare him not, His ſpell in that is out: the King hath found Matter againſt him, that for euer marres The Hony of his Language. No, he's ſetled (Not to come off) in his diſpleaſure. Beleeue it, this is true. In the Diuorce, his contrarie proceedings Are all vnfolded: wherein he appeares, As I would wiſh mine Enemy. All mens. Marry Amen. This ſame Cranmer 's A worthy Fellow, and hath tane much paine In the Kings buſineſſe. So I heare. Obſerue, obſerue, hee's moody. He's diſcontented. He is vex'd at ſomething. My Lord, we haue Stood heere obſeruing him. Some ſtrange Commotion Is in his braine: He bites his lip, and ſtarts, Stops on a ſodaine, lookes vpon the ground, Then layes his finger on his Temple: ſtraight Springs out into faſt gate, then ſtops againe, Strikes his breſt hard, and anon, he caſts His eye againſt the Moone: in moſt ſtrange Poſtures We haue ſeene him ſet himſelfe. It's Heauens will, Some Spirit put this paper in the Packet, To bleſſe your eye withall. Heare the Kings pleaſure Cardinall, Who commands you To render vp the Great Seale preſently Into our hands, and to Confine your ſelfe To Aſher-houſe, my Lord of Wincheſters, Till you heare further from his Highneſſe. Thoſe Articles, my Lord, are in the Kings hand: But thus much, they are foule ones. Then, That in all you writ to Rome, or elſe To Forraigne Princes, Ego & Rex meus Was ſtill inſcrib'd: in which you brought the King To be your Seruant. And ſo wee'l leaue you to your Meditations How to liue better. For your ſtubborne anſwer About the giuing backe the Great Seale to vs, The King ſhall know it, and (no doubt) ſhal thanke you. So fare you well, my little good Lord Cardinall.

Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
