Well, they may talke of Dunkerke, or of Callis, Enricht with forraine booties, but if euer A little Garriſon, or ſconce, as this, Were ſo fild vp with ſpoyles, let me be carted. Did not I purchaſe it? And am not I the Lady of the Mannor? And who ſhall dare to queſtion mee? I hope, I ſhall be able to defend my Fort, From the inuaſion of the painted ſtaffe, Or the tempeſtuous paper Engine, ſafe, As a Moale in a Trench, and worke at hie midnight. When their wiſe heads are layd, wee'l rayſe the ſpirits Of our dead pleaſures, vſe the benefit Of youth, and dance our Orgyes by the Moonelight. Why ſo? We defye The force of any man, who's that knocks ſo? Go bid the watch looke out, and if their number Be not too plurall, then let them come in. But if they chance to bee thoſe ruffian Souldiers, Let fall the purcullis. All they can do, Is to diſcharge a volley of oathes at me. Ile take no tickets, nor no future ſtipends. Tis not falſe titles, or denominations Of offices can do it. I muſt haue money. Tell them ſo, draw the bridge. Ile make them know, This is no widdowes houſe, but Marcus Manitius, Is Lord of the Iland, Who waſt? What would he haue? Pox on the Marſhall, and the Conſtable. There cannot be a Myſtery in a Trade, But they muſt peepe into it. Mercileſſe varlets, That know how many fall by our occupation, And yet would haue their Venery for nothing. A chambermayd can't haue a Ruffe to ſet, But they muſt bee poking in it; Now they haue brought vs vnder contribution, They vex vs more then the Venetians doe The whole Corporation of Curtezans: But we muſt giue good words, ſhew them a roome. M r . Ardelio, you haue beene a ſtranger. You are growne rich of late. Yes ſomewhat purſy for want of exerciſe. We were wont to be your ſubiects to worke on, And ſince you ſcorne vs, yet you cannot ſay, But you haue found good dealing at our hands. Truely ſir you know, I keepe as good creatures at liuery, And as cheape too, as any poore ſinner Of my profeſſion. Indeede I haue So much adoe to keepe my family ſound, You would wonder at it ▪ and ſuch as are ſo, They are taken vp preſently. But I haue one, I dare commend to you, for wind and limme. Pleaſe you walke in, ſir. The fortreſſe, ſir, is mine, and none come here, But pay me cuſtome. Very well, ſir. I doe not vſe the faſhions of thoſe Countries, That keepe a ſtranger out foure weekes at ſea, To know if hee bee ſound. I make no ſcruple, But giue free traffique to all Nations. If you haue payd your due, you may put in, There is the way, Ile follow preſently. I thinke our ſouldiers are all come, lets in And ſet the watch. Nay, if you are vnruly, we ſhall tame you. Sir, doe you ſpeake with no more reuerence To me? it ſeemes you know me not Did you attempt but the leaſt iniury, There be in readines, would vindicate The wrongs, and credit of my houſe. How came you in? haue you performd all duties? Then you are wel-come, ſir. You haue ſayd enough, ſir. And for requitall, I will ſhew you in, Where you ſhall read the titles, and the prices. Plot — Holland's Leaguer
Six years ago, Faustina’s father sent her abroad to keep her away from her love, Fidelio, and made her swear not to marry for seven years. Now the old man is dead, she has returned to London, known only to Fidelio, but insists on keeping her oath for the last remaining year. Her brother Philautus is in love with himself, which distresses his friends and offends his wife, but gives his servant Ardelio the opportunity to steal from him by conveying away property into storage at an accomplice’s house. Fidelio plans to make him fall in love with Faustina; this will both cure him and test her fidelity. He explains it to Faustina as a plan to humiliate a lord who has cast aspersions on women. Philautus makes advances to Faustina on the assumption that all women are libidinous and enjoy being raped from time to time; but he is impressed by her militant chastity, and accedes to her demand that he prove his honour in battle. He later returns from the wars, cured of narcissism, and asks Faustina’s pardon for his earlier insolence. She identifies herself as his sister, and he gives his blessing to her marriage to Fidelio; so she agrees to abandon her oath before the seven years are out. On learning that Ardelio is a villain, Philautus dismisses him. He goes to collect the stolen goods, only to find that his partner in crime will not acknowledge that the items even exist. The ensuing argument is overheard, and the miscreants are both arrested, In the sub-plot, Agurtes intends to cheat the courtly name-dropper Trimalchio by introducing him to a young heiress - who is, in reality, his daughter and lifelong accomplice Millicent.
