O My ſweete wyfe, my pretye Conye. ¶ O Lorde wath pleaſures and great commodytie, Are heaped togyther in Matrimonye? ¶ What ſmylynge: what laughyng? What ſporte, paſtyme, and playenge? ¶ The man with the wyfe is wholly delyghted And with many cauſes to laughter enforced. ¶ Somtymes to their Garden foorth they walke And into the fyeldes ſomtymes they go, With mery trickes, and geſtures they talke As they do moue their feete to and fro. ¶ Somtymes abrode they go, to ſee playes, And other trym ſyghtes, for to be holde: When often they meete in the hye wayes Muche of their aquaintaunce they knewe of olde. ¶ Somtymes at home, at cardes they playe Somtymes at this game ſomtymes at that They nede not with ſadnes to paſſe the daye Nor yet to ſyt ſtyll, or ſtande in one plat. ¶ I thynke that a man might ſpende a whole daye Declarynge the Ioyes, and endles blys: Which maryed perſones receyue alwaye, If they loue faythfullye, as meete it is. ¶ If they do not, it maye be a ſhame For I loue you hartele I you aſſure: Or els I were truely greatly to blame Ye are ſo louynge, ſo kynde, and demure. ¶ Who then merye Maryage can diſco ̄ mende And wyll not with Ariſtotle in his Ethickes agree ▪ But wyll ſaye, that myſerye is the ende When otherwyſe I fynde it to be: A polyt que man wyll marye a wyfe As the Phyloſopher makes declaration, Not onely to haue chyldren by his lyfe, But alſo for lyuynge, helpe, & ſuſtentacion. ¶ Pythagoras, Socrates, and Crates alſo Whiche truely were men of very ſmall Subſtaunce As I harde my father tell longe ago, Dyd take them wyfes with a ſafe conſcience: And dwelled togyther, ſuppoſynge that they Were vnto Philoſophy, nother ſtoppe nor ſtaye. ¶ Awaye with thoſe therfore that Mariage deſpyſt And of daungers therof inuent many lyes. ¶ Come on then my wyfe, if it be ſo, Let vs departe hence for a ſeaſon: For I am not well tyll I do knowe Of that mannes commynge the very reaſon. ¶ Do ye nowe prouyde and gyue a regarve For Victualles hereafter to be preparde? ¶ To trouble me nowe, and make me vexed, This miſchieuous meane haſt thou inuented. ¶ Yet for a tyme, if it maye thee pleaſe Let me be quit, and take myne eaſe. ¶ I woulde not we ſhulde for hungre be kylled. ¶ Begynneſt thou euen now to be paynefull & greuous And to thy Huſbande, a woman ſo troublous? ¶ O myrth, O Ioye, O paſtyme and pleaſure, How lyttell a ſpace, do you endure? ¶ Alas good wyfe, good wyfe, alas, alas, Stryke not ſo harde, I praye thee hartelye, Whatſoeuer thou wylte haue brought to paſſe It ſhalbe done with all ſpede poſſyblye. ¶ O Lorde what howe myſerable men be thoſe Whiche to their wyfes as wretches be wedded And haue them conty nually their mort all foes Seruynge them thus, as Slaues that he hyred. Nowe by experience true I do fynde, Whiche oftentymes vnto me heretofore My father dyd ſaye, declarynge his mynde That in Matrymonie was payne euermore, What ſhall I do moſt pityfull Creature? Iuſte cauſe I haue alas to lament: That franticke woman my death wyll procure If ſo he this daye without gayne be ſpent For vnleſſe for my wood ſom Money be taken Lyke a dogge, with a Cudgell I ſhalbe beaten. Ho thou good felowe which ſtandeſt ſo nye Of theſe heauy bundelles eaſe my ſore backe: And ſomewhat therfore gyue me by and by Or els I dye, for Syluer I do lacke. Nowe that I haue ſome Monye receyued For this my burden home I wyll go: And leſt that my wyfe be diſcontented, What I haue take, I wyll her ſhowe. Wyfe I am come, I went a longe waye And here is the profyte, and gaynes of this daye. ¶ By Goddes precious I wyll not vnwyſely ſuffes To do as I haue done, any longer. ¶ Scratche and thou dare, for I haue a knyfe, Perchaunce I wyll ryd the of thy lyfe. ¶ Good wyfe be content, forgeue my this faulte I wyll neuer agayne do that which is naught. ¶ I wyll do your co ̄ maun dementes whatſoeuer. O mercyfull God, in what lamentable ſtate Is he, of whom the wyfe is the mayſter? Wolde God I had bene predeſtinate, On my Maryage daye, to haue dyed wyth a Feuer. O wretched creature, what maye I do? My grieuous wyfe ſhall I returne vnto? Lo wyfe beholde without further delaye The water ye ſent for, here I do brynge. ¶ I weepe not forſothe, not crye not as yet. ¶ Wyfe I wyll thyther hye me faſte. ¶ O howe vnhappye and eke vnfortunate Is the moſte parte of maryed mennes condytion? I woulde to death I had bene agate When my Mother in be arynge me made lamentation. What ſhall I do whyther ſhall I turne, Moſt carefull man nowe vnder the Skye? In the flamynge fyre, I had rather burne, Then with extreme payne, lyue ſo heauylye, There is no wyfte, to my wyfe I muſt go Whom that I dyd wed, I am full o. Where are ye wyfe, your clothes are waſhte cleane Is whyte as a lylly without ſpot or ueyne. ¶ Alas, alas, I am almoſt quyte dead, My wyfe ſo pytyfully hath broken my head. ¶ The ſlyinge and fiende go with my wyfe And in her Iourney ill maye ſhe ſpeede: I praye God almighty to ſhorten her lyfe The earth at no tyme doth beare ſuche a weede. Althoughe that I be a Gentelman borne And come by my Auncetours of a good blood, Yet am I lyke to weare a Cote torne And hither and thither go catye wood. But rather then I this lyfe wyll abyde To morowe mornynge I do intende, Home to my Father agayne to ryde, If ſome man to me his Horſe wyll lende. She is to her Goſſypes gone to make mery, And there ſhe wyll be for three or foure dayes: She cares not thoughe I do nowe miſcarye And ſuffre ſuch payne and ſorowe alwayes. She leaueth to me neyther Breade nor drynke But ſuch as I iugde no bodye wolde eate: I myght by the wailes lye dead and ſtynke For any great holſomnes in my meate. She walketh a brode, and taketh her pleaſure Her ſelfe to cheryſſhe is all her care: She paſſeth not what griefs I endure, Or howe I can lyue with noughty fare: And ſynce it is ſo, without further delaye To my father to morowe I wyll awaye.

Plot summary

Plot — The Disobedient Child
A rich man’s son, afraid of corporal punishment, refuses to go to school. He also rejects his father’s suggestion that he find gainful employment, perhaps as a soldier; instead, against his father’s advice, he insists on marrying. His father disinherits him. He leaves his home in London for St Albans, gets into debt, and marries a young woman. They have a lavish and expensive wedding feast. His father refuses to pay the money he owes. When he proves reluctant to go out to earn a living, his wife becomes shrewish, beats him, and sends him on domestic errands. He regrets his marriage, and goes home to his father. Satan reveals that the marital discord was his doing. The rich man refuses to take in his son and insists that he return to the wife he chose, but promises him a little money. The moral is explained in a peroration: spare the rod and spoil the child.
