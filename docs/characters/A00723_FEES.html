One and thirty good-morrows to the fairest, wisest, chastest, richest VViddow that euer conuersation coapt withall. Gentlewoman, I crie thee mercie, but ti's fault in all Lords, not in mee only, we doe vse to sweare by our Ho nors: and as we are Noble, to dispatch such a businesse for such a Gentleman and wee are bound, euen by the same Honors wee sware by, to forget it in a quarter of an houre. And looke as if wee had neuer seene the Partie, when wee meete next, especially if none of our Gentlemen haue bin considered. He quarrell? Bould: hang him, if he durst haue quar rel'd, the world knowes hee's within a mile of an oke ha's put him too't, and soundly, I neuer car'd for him in my life, but to see his sister, hee's an asse, pox an arrant asse, for doe you thinke any but an arrant asse, would offer to come a wooing, where a Lord atempts? he quarrell: hee dares not quarrell. I a poxe, hang him old gouty foole, he neuer brought me vp to any Lordly exercise, as fencing, dancing, rumbling, and such like: but forsooth I must write and reade, & speake languages, and such base qualities, fit for none but Gentle men. Now sir would I tell him, Father you are a Count, I am a Lo: a poxe a writing and reading, and languages, let mee be brought vp as I was borne.
