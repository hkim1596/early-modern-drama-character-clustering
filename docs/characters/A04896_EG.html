Addresse thy selfe sweet warbling Instrument, My sorrowes sad Companion; to tune forth Thy melancholly notes; somewhat to slake Those furious flames that scorch my tender heart. Vpon the blacke Rocke of despaire My youthfull ioyes are perish'd quite, My hopes are vanish'd into ayre, My day is turn'd to gloomy night: For since my Rhodon deare is gone, Hope, light, nor comfort, have I none. A Cell, where griefe the Landlord is, Shall be my palace of delight; Where I will wooe with votes and sighes, Sweet death to end my sorrowes quite; Since I have lost my Rhodon deare, Deaths fleshlesse armes why should I feare? What Fury sent you hither, Caitiffes vile, Thus to prolong my sorrow, and my toyle. Death is a plaister for all ills (they say) What mischiefe then can be in death, I pray. Were I not plunged in a grievous plight, Perhaps I would not thinke thy counsell light. For being great, I am malign'd by Fate, For being faire, I am unfortunate. Doe this, and I will live (Poneria) To give thy merit ample satisfaction. I will adore thy skill, and thee adorne With what may make thee famous through all Thessaly. My deare Poneria, I am truly thine. But tell me, I prethe, what grave S r . is this That lookes like one of Greeces Sages; His reverent Countenance makes me surmise That he's a man of sublime qualities. His silence argues something extraordinary. Aye me, Poneria. I doe admire his skill, and see (by happe) Good stuffe may be beneath a fatten Cap. I tooke a dram of it this morning, According to your appointment. And I am of the same minde: But 'twas my Maids fault. I thinke she goes about utterly to undoe me: She is as good a servant as ere was Married to the whipping. post. Nay, I could not for my heart perswade The wicked pertinacious harlot, To lay more colour on then pleased her fancy; Bat if I live I will cashiere the queane. How lik'st thou the colour of my haire. Me thinks the hue is not high enough. Discreet Poneria, thy wise approbation Doth give my fancy ample satisfaction. But heare me Poneria, will you undertake That I shall meet with the Shepheard Rhodon, As you oft have promis'd me. But 'tis a thing impossible I feare. Because I heare he deeply is ingag'd To Iris, that proud Damsell of Hymettus. Nor Circes drugs, nor all Vlysses wits, I tell thee Beldame, can accomplish this. I prethe blesse my eares with this relation. Tis bravely plotted sweet Poneria: But what houre wilt thou allot for this designe. Very good. Now most auspicious be thy stars and mine, Let all good lucke attend our great designe. Poneria, retire: I will addresse my selfe unto him. Make no doubt on't: thou hast arm'd me For all assaies. (Deare Rhodon) who, like the vernall Sunne, Dost lend refreshing heats to my affections. Tak't not amisse, that I have chose this houre And unfreqrented place t'enioy thy company. But from a maidens modesty (faire Sir) It may seeme much to derogate, To be abroad so late at night. Great pitty tis indeed, Sir, that true love Should be disparag'd, because 'tis so true. The sodaine newes of this late kindled warre, Wherein I heare (to my great griefe) you are ingag'd, Made me transgresse the bounds of modesty so farre, That I desir'd once more to see your face, Ere your departure to the field of danger. And here on you I doe bestow this viall, Which such a precious dosis doth containe, That it doth farre exceed the height of value. It is a potion made by wondrous art, Nectar is no more comparable to it, Then Bonniclabar is to Husquobath; And Aurum potabile is as far short of it, As poore Metheglin is of rich Canary: All the confections even from the lowest degree Of Sage-ale, to the height of Aqua-Celestis, Are no more like it then the beere of the Low-countries Is to the High-country wine: A dram of it taken before you goe to bed Cheeres the heart, prevents the Incubus And all frightfull dreames; cheeres the blood, Comforts the stomacke, dispels all collickes, Cures all aches, repayres the liver, helpes The lungs, rectifies the braine, quencheth All the senses, strengthens the memory, refresheth The spirits. Taken fasting it breaks the stone in bladder Or kidnyes, cures the gout, expels a quartane ague: Outwardly apply'd it kils the gangrene, And destroyes the wolfe, heales all sorts of wounds, Bruses, boyles, and sores. And not to use more multiplicity of words, I tell you gentle Rhodon you shall finde, It cures all griefes of body and of minde. But (gentle Sir) although your constitution So well attemper'd seemes, that no disease Can either hurt or over-throw your health, Yet if my counsell might prevaile with you, I should perswade you to make tryall of this Rare water this night before you sleepe. Since these nocturnall distillations May be offensive to your health (sweet Rhodon) I will be well contented to be gone, Though wondrous loth from you to part so soone.

Plot — Rhodon and Iris
: Aiming to sow dissension among the flowers, Poneria and Agnostus disguise themselves as a virtuous maiden and a wise man. Rhodon has recently breken off his suit to Eglantine, and is now expected to marry Iris. His sister Violetta appeals to him for help against the marauding Martagon, who has trampled on her flowers and forced her to live away from her home. Martagon conspires with Eglantine’s brother to forestall Rhodon’s match with Iris. The jilted Eglantine grows melancholy, but Poneria stops her killing herself and gets her to tart herself up for an assignation with Rhodon. It becomes clear that Martagon has no intention of making restitution to Violetta, so Rhodon will have to take him on by force: a floral war seems inevitable. Iris gives Rhodon a gern which makes the wearer victorious in battle; Violetta gives him a lettuce which guarantees sustenance and prevents poisoning. Martagon suborns Poneria to kill Rhodon, so she gives Eglantine a poison, telling her it is a love-philtre. Posing as Iris, Eglantine gives it to him, claiming it to be a restorative, and expects him to fall in love with her.
