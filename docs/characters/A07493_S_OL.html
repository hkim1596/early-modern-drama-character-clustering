Be content ſweet Wife. Patience ſweet Wife. Nay pray thee ceaſe, I'le be at more coſt yet, Thou know'ſt we are rich enough. Sweet Wife haue patience. I know 'tis great, but what of that Wife? I cannot doe with all, there's things making By thine owne Doctors aduice at Poticaries, I ſpare for nothing Wife, no if the price Were fortie markes a ſpoone-full, I'de giue a thouſand pound to purchaſe fruitfulneſſe, 'Tis but bating ſo many good workes In the erecting of Bridewels and Spittle-houſes, And ſo fetch it vp againe, for hauing none I meane to make good deeds my Children. Hang thee, thou haſt had too many. O horrible, dar'ſt thou call me breuitie? Dar'ſt thou be ſo ſhort with me? Talke not on't pray thee, Thou'lt make me play the Woman, and weepe too. They may be all deceiued, Be but you patient Wife. Suffer thy Heart out, a Poxe ſuffer thee. Come, come, I ha' done, You'le to the Goſſiping of M r Allwits Child? Sorrow conſume thee, thou art ſtill croſſing me, And know'ſt my nature. O that, that, that or nothing. Iſt poſſible? I'le venture that I faith. No matter, what ſerues wealth for? Thou lyeſt Barrenneſſe. 'Tis thy fault. Thine, 'tis thou art barren. I'le be deuorc't. I'le giue vp Houſe, & keepe ſome fruitfull whore, Like an old Batcheler in a Tradſmans Chamber, She and her Children ſhall haue all. What with her? Neuer. You worke all in vaine. Good Sir pardon me, I'me Maſter of this Houſe, Which I'le ſell preſently, I'le clap vp Billes this Euening. Thou art nothing of a Woman. Nay prethe what do'ſt meane? I faith thou art a good Soule, he lyes that ſayes it, Buſſe, buſſe, pretie Rogue. Is the Drinke come? I hope to ſee thee wench within theſe few yeeres, Cirkled with Children, pranking vp a Girle, And putting Iewels in their little Eares, Fine ſport I ſaith. Had I bin ought, hang thee, had'ſt thou bin ought, But a croſſe thing I euer found thee. A Pox on thee. I will not take it now Sir, And I were ſure to get three Boyes ere Midnight. Hang ſtrumpet, I will take it now in ſpight. I meane ſo, Within there? Saddle the white Mare, I'le take a Whore along, and ride to Ware. I'le plague you euerie way, Looke ye, doe you ſee, 'tis gone. I curſe and ſpare not now. Nay I'me not giuen to ſtanding. I neuer could ſtand long in one place yet, I learnt it of my Father, euer figient, How if I croſt this Sir? What ſay you to a Table thus high Sir? Or elſe I had a bad Head: you muſt receiue Sir foure hundred pounds of me at foure ſeuerall payments: One hundred pound now in hand. Another hundred when my Wifes is quicke: the third when ſhe's brought a bed: and the laſt hundred when the Child cryes, For if it ſhould be ſtill borne, it doth no good Sir. Not a whit Sir, I'me in an excellent pace for any Phyſicke, I ſhall vp preſently: One kiſſe, and farewell. Expect me about three. Ho my Wiues quickned, I am a Man for euer, I thinke I haue beſtur'd my ſtumps I faith: Run, get your Fellowes altogether inſtantly, Then to the Pariſh-Church, and ring the Belles. Vpon my loue I charge you Villaine, that you make a Bon-fier before the Doore at night. A thwacking one I charge you. Run, tell a hundred pound out for the Gentleman That gaue my Wife the Drinke, the firſt thing you doe. A bargaine, as our ioyes growes, We muſt remember ſtill from whence it flowes, Or elſe we proue vngratefull multiplyers: The Child is comming, and the Land comes after, The newes of this will make a poore S r Walter. Plot — A Chaste Maid in Cheapside
Sir Walter Whorehound lives high on the expectation of an inheritance: unless Sir Oliver Kix produces an heir, his estate will pass to Sir Walter — and Sir Oliver is impotent. Sir Walter maintains Allwit and his wife, paying for everything, in return for exclusive sexual access to Mistress Allwit: he is the true father of the children presumed to be Allwit’s. But Sir Walter is ===== PAGE 327 ===== 1613 planning to discontinue the arrangement and get married: the goldsmith Yellowhammer has agreed to marry his son Tim to a Welsh gentlewoman whom Sir Walter has introduced as his niece, and Sir Walter hopes himself to marry Tim’s sister Moll. Moll has other ideas: her sweetheart Touchwood plans to elope with her, and has Yellowhammer himself make the wedding ring, umaware of the bride’s identity. The elopement is foiled, however: Yellowhammer and Sir Walter intervene just as Touchwood is putting the ring onto Moll’s finger, and Moll is taken home and locked up. Touchwood’s elder brother is poor but virile, and cannot afford to cohabit with his wife for fear that she may produce a legitimate child he will not have the means to support. He has fathered a prolific number of bastards, and refuses to acknowledge one of them when a former paramour, a country wench, brings it to him. She then disposes of the baby by duping two corrupt promoters set to prevent the illicit trade in meat during Lent: she hides the child in a basket and allows them to confiscate it as contraband. Lady Kix’s maid suggests that Touchwood Seniors excessive fertility might solve Sir Oliver’s problems in begetting an heir; Sir Oliver agrees to pay him £400 for the job. Mistress Allwit gives birth to another of Sir Walter’s children. Tim Yellowhammer arrives home from Cambridge and is sent to see his mother at the christening party; he is priggish about being in female company, contrary to university rules, and the women are drunkenly embarrassing. Encouraged to woo his fiancée, Tim finds it heavy going: he tries to converse with her in Latin, she with him in Welsh. Allwit learns of Sir Walter’s plans for Moll, and tries to forestall them by telling Yellowhammer that his prospective son-in-law is a notorious whoremonger, but Yellowhammer is unconcerned by this. Moll disappears from home: she and Touchwood Junior have eloped again, with the help of the London watermen; but her mother drags her home by the hair.
