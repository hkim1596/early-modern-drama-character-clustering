He that's a womans keeper, should have eyes A hundred more then Argus, and his eares Double the number: Now the newes, what letters? What posy, ring or bracelet wooes to day? What Grove to night is conscious of your whispers? Come tell mee, for I know your trusty squire, Your litle closet blabbes into your eare Some secret, let me know it. Indeed I doe, Cupids a dangerous boy, and often wounds The wanton roving eye. So I would have thee, but not yet my Girle, Till loves prove happier, till the wretched Claius Hath satisfied the Gods. Hast thou not heard it? Tis impossible. Why I will tell thee, Thou knowest Pilumnus? Yes: This Pilumnus had a sonne Philaebus, Who was, while yet he was, the only joy The staffe and comfort of his fathers age, And might have still beene so, had not fond love Vndone him. Why thus; one Lalage, a beauteous Nymph As ever eye admired, Alphestus daughter, Was by her father promis'd him in marriage. But only promis'd: for the sheapherd Claius, (A name accursed in Sicilian fields!) Being rich, obtain'd the beauteous Lalage From sweet Philaebus: he sad heart being rob'd, Of all his comfort, having lost the beauty Which gave him life and motion; seeing Claius Injoy those lips, whose cherries were the food That nurs'd his soule, spent all his time in sorrow, In melancholy sighes and discontents; Look'd like a witherd tree oregrowne with mosse, His eyes were ever dropping Iceacles. Disdaine and sorrow made Pilumnus rage, And in this rage, he makes his moane to Ceres, ( Ceres most sacred of Sicilian powers;) And in those moanes he prosecutes revenge, And that revenge to fall on Lalage. Silly maid, His passions were not causelesse; and with what justice Could she deny Pilumnus? how oft hath he sprinkled, The finest flowre of wheat, and sweetest myrrhe Vpon her Altars! Lalage ru'd the time She flowted brave Philaebus. Now she was great With two sweet twins, the faire chast Amaryllis, And mad Amyntas; (an unlucky paire!) These shee brought forth, but never liv'd to see them: Lucina caus'd her sorrowes stop her breath. Leaving this matchlesse paire of beauteous infants, In whom till now she lives. Worse Then ever: Shee being dead whose life was his, Whose lookes did hold his eyes from shutting up, He pin'd away in sorrowes, griefe it was To see she was not his, but greater farre That she was not at all. Her Exequies being past, He casts him down upon that turfe of earth, Vnder whose roofe his Lalage was hous'd, And parlied with her ashes, 'till his own lampe Was quite extinguish'd with a fatall dampe. Here ended th'noble sheapheard. Why the Ompha, Having denounc'd against him, and he knowing The hate of old Pilumnus fled away, I think hee's sayl'd to the Antipodes. No tydings can be brought what ground receives him, Vnlesse Corymbus make a happy voyage; Corymbus that will search both East and Occident And when he finds him spill his captive blood. Which Ceres grant he may! Tender Laurinda Now dost thou see the reason of my care, And why my watchfull eyes so close observe Thy steps and actions.

Plot summary

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
