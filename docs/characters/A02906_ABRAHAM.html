Full speech

Yet mercyfull lorde, thy gracyousnesse remembre, To Adam and Noah, both in thy worde and promes. And lose not the sowles, of men in so great nombre, But saue thyne owne worke, of thy most dyscrete goodnes. I wore thy mercyes, are plentyfull and endles. Neuer can they dye, nor fayle, thy self endurynge, Thys hath faythe fixed, fast in my vnderstandynge. Tell me blessed lorde, where wyll thy great malyce lyght, My hope is, all fleshe, shall not perysh in thy syght? All that wyll I do, by assystence of thy goodnes. Pytiefull maker, though they haue kyndled thy furye, Cast not awaye yet, the iust sort with the vngodlye. Parauenture there maye, be fiftye ryghteouse persones, Within those cyties, wylt thu lose them all at ones? And not spare the place, for those fyftye ryghteouse sake? Be it farre from the, soch rygoure to vndertake. I hope there is not, in the so cruell hardenesse, As to cast awaye, the iust men with the rechelesse, And so to destroye, the good with the vngodlye. In the iudge of all, be neuer soche a furye. I take vpon me, to speake here in thy presence, More then become me, lorde pardon my neglygence. I am but ashes, and were lothe the to offende. Happlye there maye be, fyue lesse in the same nombre. For their sakes I trust, thu wylt not the rest accombre? What if the cytie, maye fortye ryghteouse make? Be not angrye lorde, though I speake vndyscretelye. Parauenture there maye, be thirty founde amonge them. I take vpon me, to moche lorde in thy syght? No lesse I suppose, than twenty can it haue? Ones yet wyll I speake, my mynde, and than no more, And what if there myght, be x. good creatures founde? O mercyfull maker, moche is thy tolleracyon, And sufferaunce of synne. I se it now in dede, Wit saue yet of fauer, out of those cyties to leade, Those that be faythfull, though their flocke be but small. Great are thy graces, in the generacyon of Sem. Lorde so regarde me, as I receyue it with gladnesse. I wyll not one Iote, Lorde from thy wyll dyssent, But to thy pleasure, be alwayes obedyent, Thy lawes to fullfyll, and most precyouse commaundement. Thankes wyll I rendre, lyke as it shall behoue me. Euerlastynge prayse, to thy most gloryouse name. Whych sauedyst Ada ̄ , through faythe in thy swete promes, Of the womannys sede. And now confirmest the same, In the sede of me. Fosoth great is thy goodnes I can not perceyue, but that thy mercye is endles. To soch as feare the, in euery generacyon, For it endureth, without abreuyacyon. Thys haue I prynted in depe consyderacyon, No worldly matter, can race it out of mynde. For ones it wyll be, the fynall restauracyon, Of Adam and Eue, with other that hath synde. Yea, the sure helthe, and rayse of all mankynde. Helpe haue the faythfull, therof, though they be infect, They condempnacyon, where as it is reiect. Mercyfull maker, my crabbed voyce dyrect, That it maye breake out, in some swete prayse to the, And suffre me not, thy due lawdes to neglect, But lete me shewe forth, thy commendacyons fre. Stoppe not my wynde pypes, but geue them lyberte, To sounde to thy name, whych is most gracyouse, And in it reioyce, with hart melodyouse. O most myghtye gouernour, of thy people, and in hart most desyred, the harde rocke and true corner stone, that of two maketh one, vnynge the Iewes with the gentyles in one churche, come now and releue mankynde whom thu hast four med of the vyle earthe.

Plot — The Chief Promises of God unto Man (God's Promises)
Though mankind is sinful and deserves death, God promises life through his Son. He curses Adam for his disobedience, but Adam’s repentance persuades him to promise the chance of salvation. But mankind proves a disappointment, and God plans to annihilate his creation with a flood. Noah pleads for the human race and is saved; God promises never again to seek mankind’s destruction in this way, and confirms it with the covenant of the rainbow. Mankind persists in sin, and God plans further reprisals: having promised not to use water, he chooses fire as his weapon to destroy the Sodomites. Abraham, who honours God and is therefore to be spared, asks his maker not to destroy the righteous people in Sodom along with the wicked. God agrees to spare the city if it is found to contain 50 righteous people, and Abraham talks him down to 10. God makes his third promise: Abraham will be the father of a blessed race; the sign of the covenant is circumcision. Man does not improve, so plague is to be God’s next instrument of judgement. Moses pleads for mercy: God has spared his people from the plagues of Egypt, helped them to escape from bondage, and kept them alive in the wilderness. But they are ungrateful: while Moses has been talking with God, they have erected a golden idol. God makes his fourth promise: a prophet will arise from the Jewish people; the sign of the covenant is the Passover lamb. Given the promised land, the Israelites persist in their ingratitude to God, and resume idolatry, so God plagues them with war. He is angry that David, whom he made King and a victorious war leader, has committed adultery and murder. He allows David to choose his punishment: seven years’ famine, three months’ exile, or three days’ plague. David leaves the decision up to God, who picks the plague epidemic; but David asks for the penalty to be visited on him alone. His contrition impresses God, who promises that his descendant will establish an everlasting kingdom on earth. Isaiah speaks against the proliferation of sin, and asks God to send the people a prophet. God seems to despair: whatever authority he places over his people, they always disobey it! Isaiah points to the successes of the present king, Hezekiah, and asks God to send the people comfort. He responds with his sixth promise: the coming of a messiah born of a virgin.
