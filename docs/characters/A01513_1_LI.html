Marie fir, we had thoughte you had béene on sléepe within, and therefore we thoughte best to make you: what doth Erostrato? I cannot tell you what I shoulde saye sir, the worlde is large and long, there maye be moe Philoganos and moe Erostratos than one, yea and moe Ferraras, moe Sicili as, and moe Cathaneas: peraduenture this is not that Fer rara which you sent your sonne vnto. Sir, I like it as euill as may be, but haue you not often heard tell of the falsehood of Ferrara, and now may you sée, it falleth out accordingly. Well, there is neuer a barrell better herring, be twene you both: but in déed your officers are most to blame, that suffer such faultes to escape vnpunished. Nay, I thinke they will knowe as litle as may be, specially when they haue no gaines by it, but they ought to haue their eares as open to heare of such offe ̄ ces, as the In gates be to receyue guests. By the masse I am afearde that we shal be proued fooles both two. I would thinke best we should go séeke Erostrato him selfe. Sure, my mind giues me that we shall find a new E rostrato ere it be long. Why, doubte you of that? Did I not tell you of the falsehood of Ferrara master? Dulippo hath lerned to play the knaue indifferently well since he came hither. Master, nowe you may sée the falsehood of these fel lowes, this honest man your hoste, is of counsaile with him, and would face vs downe that it is Erostrato: beware of these mates. If there be many such witnesse in this countrey, me ̄ may go aboute to proue what they will in controuersies here. Sir, he that will goe to the ciuill lawe, must be sure of foure things: first, a right and a iust cause: then a righte ous doctor to pleade: next, fauour Coram Iudice: and aboue all, a good purse to procure it. Fauour cal I, to haue a friend néere about the Iudge, who may so sollicite thy cause, as if it be right, spéedie sen tence may ensue without any delayes: if it be not good, then to prolong it, till at the last, thine aduersarie being wearie, shal be glad be compound with thée. And the other halfe a Deuill, I hold a pennie. He hath a moulde there in déede: and an hole in an o ther place too, I woulde your nose were in it. Sir, také you héede, least he leade you to some mis chiefe. Well, I haue tolde you my minde Sir, doe you as you please. COme hither you olde kallat, you tatling huswife, tha the deuill cut oute your tong: tell me, howe could Pasi philo know of this géere but by you?

Plot summary

Plot — Supposes
Polynesta loves Dulippo, whose suit has been promoted by her nurse, and who is supposed a poor servant. In fact he is not Dulippo at all but Erostrato, who two years ago gained access to Polynesta by swapping identities with his servant and entering her father Damon’s household. Meanwhile Cleander seeks her hand, and the supposed Erostrato (the true Dulippo) becomes his rival for trust with him. Cleander appears to offer the best dowry, but Damon gives ‘Erostrato’ a fortnight’s grace pending his father Philogano’s arrival to underwrite his counter-offer. ‘Erostrato’ duly arranges to produce a false Philogano, recruiting a Sienese gentleman for the role by persuading him that a diplomatic incident has made Ferrara an unsafe place for a Sienese visitor. Overhearing an argument between two women servants, Damon discovers that ‘Dulippo’ and Polynesta have been sleeping together. ‘Dulippo’ is apprehended and imprisoned in the dungeon of his master’s house, and therefore unable to warn his servant of the arrival of the true Philogano: he misses his son and has come to take him back to Sicily. By chance, Philogano meets the true Dulippo, who refuses to acknowledge him. After the locals confirm that they know the young man as Erostrato, Philogano suspects that he has murdered his master and stolen his identity, and seeks legal assistance from Cleander. However, Dulippo turns out to be Cleander’s son, whom he lost as a child when evacuating Otranto during the Turkish conquest; he was later captured by Sicilians and sold to Philogano. The exchange of identities is revealed, allaying Damon’s worries about his daughter’s reputation. Cleander withdraws his suit: he no longer needs to marry and beget an heir now that his lost son has been restored to him. The true Erostrato is confirmed as Polynesta’s future husband.
