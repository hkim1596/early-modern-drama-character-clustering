I haue iuſt cauſe, as hath eche Chriſtian hart, To waile and weepe, to ſhed out teares of bloud: When as I call to minde, the torments and the ſmart, Which thoſe haue borne, who honeſt be and good, For nought els, but becauſe, their errors they withſtood: Yet ioyed I much, to ſee how paciently, They boare the croſe of Chriſt, with conſtancie. In the new Teſtament, we may alſo réede, That our Sauiour Chriſt, euen in his Infancy, Of Herod the King might ſtand in great dread: Who ſought to deſtroy him, ſuch was his inſolency: Afterward of the Phariſes, he did with conſtancy, Suffer ſhamefull death, his Apoſtles alſo, For teſtimonie of the trueth, did their croſſes vnder go. It is no new thing, I doo now perceiue, That Chriſtes Church doo ſuffer tribulation, But that the ſame croſſe I might better receiue: I requeſt you to ſhew me for my conſolation: What is the cauſe, by your eſtimation: That God doth ſuffer, his people be in thrall ▪ Yet helpe them ſo ſoone as they to him call. This is the ſumme of all your talke, if that I geſſe a right, That God doth punniſh his electt to kéepe their faith in vre, Or leaſt that if continuall eaſe, and reſt enioy they might: God to forget through hautineſſe, fraile nature ſhould procure: Or els by feeling puniſhment, our ſinnes for to abiure: Or els to proue our conſtancy, or laſtly that we may, Be inſtruments in whom his might, God may abroad diſplay. With right good will, I will attend on you, your houſe vnto: Or els goe you with mée to mine, the longer iourney ſaue: For it is now high dinner time, my ſtomack meat do th craue: Goe you before, and I will come behinde with all my harte.

Plot summary

Plot — The Conflict of Conscience
Satan, concerned about the spread of godliness on Earth, procures Hypocrisy, Tyranny, and Avarice to counteract it by propagating vice through the Roman Catholic church. Philologus and Mathetes discuss the danger to Protestantism, and conclude that worldly torments are sent by God to test men’s faith. After arguing about their mutual precedence, the vices resolve to help the papal legation, and adopt innocuous aliases. Tyranny is commissioned as an inquisitor to hunt heretics, while Avarice accepts their bribes to save them from arrest. Caconos alerts the vices to the heresies of Philologus, who is arrested and tried by the Cardinal in consistory court. He is examined on the topics of papal authority and transubstantiation, and found guilty. His property is confiscated and his family’s distress used to put psychological pressure on him; he is also threatened with imprisonment, torture, and execution, but promised money and power if he recants. He does, persuaded by the glass of vanity given by Sensual Suggestion. Spirit and Conscience call on Philologus to renounce his apostasy and embrace martyrdom, but he rejects them. In prosperity, he is visited by Horror, who torments him with the knowledge of his own damnation. Theologus and Eusebius urge the continuing availability of God’s grace, but Philologus blasphemes and curses God. Despairing of salvation, he hangs himself. (In the B-text, he is given grace to repent at the last moment.)
