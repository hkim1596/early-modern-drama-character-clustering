Full speech

Cease then to lead thy life in a study, pinned with a fewe boardes, and endeuour to be a courtier to liue in emboste rouffes. Why? Why do you thinke in court any vse to dis semble? You haue no reaso ̄ for it, but an old reporte. I, but this is the court of Sapho natures mi racle, which resembleth the tree Salurus, whose roote is fastened vpon knotted steele, & in whose top bud leaues of pure gold. The vertue of the tree is not the cause: but the Easterly wind, which is thought commonly to bring cankers and rottenesse. Why, then you conclude with me, that Sapho for vertue hath no copartner. We wil thither streight. Why, there you may liue stil. Howe like you the Ladies, are they not passing faire? Yet am I sure that in iudgemente you are not so seuere, but that you can be content to allowe of bewtie by day or by night. I think all your stuffe are bundles of paper: but now must you learne to turne your library to a war drope, & see whether your rapier hang better by your side, then the penne did in your eare. Phao, the Ferrie man of Syracusa. No Madame, this is he that Venus determined among men to make the fairest. You wil much delight in the flattering greene, which now beginneth to be in his glory. Sapho is falne sodenly sick, I cannot guesse the cause. Softe, I am not content, that a male and Female content, should go together. You shall not fall out? for Pigions after byting fall to billing, and open iarres make the closest iestes. I am sorie for Sapho: because shee will take no Phisicke, like you Pandion, who being sick of the sul lens, will seeke no friend. Is it not loue? Nothing, but that I hope it be not. Why, what doe you think of our Ladies? I will not tempt you in your deepe Mela ̄ choly, least you seeme sowre to those, which are so sweete. But come, let vs walke a litle into the fieldes, it may bee the open ayre will disclose your close conceites.

Plot — Sappho and Phao
Menus plans to visit Syracuse to make SAppH6 fall in love, and travels there on PHa0’s ferry-boat. She makes Phao beautiful; and, unknown to her, @iipi@ makes him irresistibly attractive to women, even though he is not interested in them. Sibyllaj whom the gods have given long life but not eternal youth, counsels Phao not to waste his prime years. He falls in love with Sappho, and Sibylla advises him on the technique of wooing. Sappho is taken ill; Phao, who has some pharmaceutical knowledge, is summoned to minister to her. In fact, her malady is love for Phao, whose base origins make him unsuitable for her. Venus too falls for Phao, and procures from Wailea new arrows for Cupid, with which he can break her rival Sappho’s infatuation and turn Phao’s affections her way. Cupid carries out the first part of his mission, but also tells Sappho what he has done; so she persuades him to use another of the new arrows to make Phao hate Venus. Cupid decides to be Sappho’s son instead of Venus’, and gives her his arrows: now she, not Venus, will be the lady of love. Venus swears to get even, and, convinced of love’s pointlessness, Phao leaves Sicily.
