Full speech

Pray god 't prooue well. Good morrow my good Lord of Sussex. The like to you my Lord: As you were speaking With all my hart Lord Howard. Wher's the Princesse. Goe tell her we must and will. It shall not neede, presse after her my Lord. Sorry we are sweet Lady, to behold you in this sad plight. Doctor Owine and Doctor Wendith, You are the Queenes Phisitions truly sworne, On your alegeance, as before her highnes you will answere it, Speake, may the Princesse be remoou'd with life. Madam, we take no pleasure to deliuer so strict a messuage. To morrow earlie then you must prepare. We found the Princesse, sicke and in great danger; Yet did we vrge our strickt Commission, She much intreated that she might be spar'd, Vntill her health and strength might be restor'd. What answere you to Wiats late rebellion, Madam, tis thought that you did set them on. Rowme for the Lords there. Exeunt Counsell. Madam it raynes. A gentleman in fauor with the Queene. Alas Sir Harry these are honest contrey men, That much reioyce to see the Princesse well. And myne as great as yours. Bells. Madam this night your lodging's at my house, No prisoner are you Madame for this night. No, no prisoner, what I intend to do ile answere, Madame, wil't please you goe. Exit Eliz: Bening: & Tame. You much amaze me sir, els heauen forefend. And I with you would spend my deerest blood, To do that vertuous Lady any good. Sir Harry, now my charge I must resigne, The Ladye's wholy in your custody, Yet vse her kindly as she well deserues, And so I take my leaue, Madame adue. Exit Tame How now my masters what's here to do. And you do well: and yet me thinke 'twere fit, To spend some funerall teares vpon her hearce. VVho while she liu'd was deere vnto you all. Did you not loue her father when he liu'd, As deerly as you ere did loue any, And yet reioyced at his funerall: Likewise her brother 〈…〉 em'd him deere, Yet once departed, ioyfully you sung, Runne to make Bone-fiers, to proclaime your loue Vnto the newe, forgetting still the old: Now she is gone, how you mone for her, VVere it not fit a while to mone her hearse, And dutyfully there reioyce the tother; Had you the wisest and the louingst Prince, That euer swayd a Scepter in the world, This is the loue he shall haue after life: Let Princes while they liue haue loue or feare 'tis fit, For after death, ther's none continues it. I blame you not, nor do I you commend, For you will still the strongest side defend. Exit. This Garter with the order of the George, Two Ornaments vnto the Crowne of England, I here present.
