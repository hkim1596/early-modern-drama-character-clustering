Were euen in their Enemies made happie; The Macedonian Courage tryed of old, And the new greatneſſe of the Syrian power: But he for Phillip, and Antiochus, Hath found more eaſie enemies to deale with, Turpuus, Pammenes, and a rout of Fidlers. I ſaw't, but turn'd away my Eyes, and Eares, Angry, they ſhould be priuie to ſuch ſights. Why doe I ſtand relating of the ſtorie, Which in the doing had enough to grieue me? Tell on, and end the tale, you, whom it pleaſeth; Mee mine owne ſorrow ſtops from further ſpeaking. Nero, my loue doth make thy fault, and my griefe greater. ex. Se. O Rome, the enuy late, But now, the pitie of the world thee gets, The men of Choleos at thy ſufferings griue, The ſhaggy dweller in the Scithian Rockes; The moſt condemned to perpetuall ſnowe That neuer wept at kindreds burials, Suffers with thee, and feeles his heart to ſoften. O, ſhould the Parthyan heare theſe miſeries, He would, (his low and natiue hate apart) Sit downe with vs. and lend an Enemies teare, To grace the funerall fires of ending Rome. Exeunt. I, now my Troy lookes beautious in her flames, The Trirhnen Seas are bright with Roman fires, Whileſt the amazed Marriner a farre, Gazing on th'vnknowne light, wonders what starre Heauen hath begot, to eaſe the aged Moone. When Pirrhus, ſtryding ore the cynders, ſtood On ground, where Troy late was; and with his Eye Meaſur'd the height of what he had throwne downe, A Citie, great in people, and in power: Walls built with hands of Gods; He now forgiue The ten yeares length, and thinkes his wounds well heald, Bath'd in the blood of Priams fifty ſonnes. Yet am not I appeas'd, I muſt ſee more Then Towers, and Collomns tumble to the ground; 'Twas not the high built walles, and guiltleſſe ſtones That Nero did prouoke; Themſelues muſt be the wood To feed this fire, or quench it with their blood. What meanes your mourning, this vngratefull ſorrow? Where are your precepts of Philoſophie? Where our prepared reſolution, So many yeeres fore-ſtudied againſt danger? To whom is Neroes cruelty vnknowne? Or what remained after mothers blood, But his inſtructers death? Leaue, leaue theſe teares, Death from me nothing takes, but what's a burthen, A clog, to that free ſparke of Heauenly fire: But that in Seneca, the which you lou'd, Which you admir'd, doth, and ſhall ſtill remaine Secure of death, vntouched of the graue. Be not afraid my ſoule, goe cheerefully, To thy owne Heauen, from whence it firſt let downe, Thou loath by this impriſoning fleſh putſt on, Now lifted vp, thou rauiſht ſhalt behold The truth of things, at which we wonder here, And fooliſhly doe wrangle on beneath; And like a God ſhalt walke the ſpacious ayre, And ſee what euen to conceit's deni'd. Great ſoule oth' world, that through the parts defus'd Of this vaſt All, guid'ſt what thou doſt informe; You bleſſed mindes, that from the Pbeares you moue, Looke on mens actions not with idle eyes; And Gods we goe to, Aaid me in this ſtrife, And combat of my fleſh, that ending, I May ſtill ſhew Seneca, and my ſelfe die.

Plot summary

Plot — Nero (Piso's Conspiracy)
The Emperor Nero celebrates his latest triumph : not a military victory, but success in a singing and acting competition against the Greeks; he also has one of his critics executed. He gives a performance in the role of Orestes; the theatre is guarded so that the audience cannot escape, and those who do not respond with due enthusiasm are marked down for execution. Deciding that he has treated Rome too lenien:tly, he has the city burned down . The wind blows the fire back towards the palace, and his own possessions are destroyed. . Antonius loves _ the Empress Poppaea, despite Petronius' efforts to persuade him to settle for someone of lower rank. Poppaea refuses Antonius, preferring Nymphidius, who has cultivated her affections as a means to make himself Emperor. Scaevinus broaches --a conspiracy to assassinate Nero, thinking it demeaning to have _an Emperor so dedicated to artistic pursuits. Piso is recruited, and the plotters discuss the best way to assassinate Nero; but they are overheard by the freedman Milichus, who reports them to Nero. Piso commits suicide; other conspirators are arrested, and Nero institutes a purge of their sympathizers. Petronius is arrested and executed simply because Tigellinus has -a score to settle with him. A young man is executed merely for pitying Piso's death; Poppaea tries to save him because he resembles a former lover of hers, so Nero kicks her to death in a rage. News arrives from France that Vindex has revolted and chosen · Galba as Emperor. Nymphidius spreads rumours that Nero has resigned, and tries both to curry favour with Galba's agents and to buy military support for his own ambitions. Nero is condemned by the Senate and commits suicide . Nymphidius is killed by Galba's supporters.
