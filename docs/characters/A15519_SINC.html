Full speech

Good coossen Simplicitie do something for me. Alas I am not able to giue thee a new. Nay cossen Simplicity I got my liuing hardly but yet I hope iust And with good Conscience too although I am restrained from my lust. But this it is coosen Simplicitie, I would request you do for me: Which is, to get Lady Loue, and Lady Conscience hand to latter: That by their menes I may get some benefice to make me liue the better Lady, my name is Sinceritie. I came from Oxford, but in Cambridge I studied late, Hauing nothing, thought good if I could, to make better my state. But if I had in stead of Diuinitie, the Law, Astronomie, Astrologie, Phislognomie, Palmestrie, Arithmetike, Logicke, Mas cke, Phis cke, or any such thing, I had not doubted then, but to haue had some better liuing. But Diuines that preach the word of God sincerely and truly, Are in these dayes little or nothing set by. God grant the good Preachers be not taken away for our vnthankfulnes There was neuer more preaching and lesse following, the people lyue so amisse: But what is he that may not on the Saba th day attend to heare Gods word? But we wil rather run to bowls, sit at the alehouse, than one hour affoord: Telling a tale of Robin hood, sitting at Cards, playing at kettels, or some other vaine thing, That I feare Gods vengeance on your head it will bring. God graunt amendment, but Lady Conscience I pray, In my behalfe vnto Lucar do what ye may. I yeeld you most hartie thanks my good lady. Dissimulation, out vpon him, he shall be no spokeman for me. Seeing thou wilt haue it, heere receiue it, but it greeues my har that this dissembling wretch should speake on my part. Thou art a kin to the lawyer, thou wilt do nothing without a fee, But thou, Fraud, Usurie, nor yet Symonie shall haue nothing of me. And thou wilt do it, do it, and thou wilt not chuse: Both thee and their dealing I hate and refuse. Thou speakst the truth Simplicitie, for flatterers now a dayes, liue Gentlemen-like, and with prating get praise. Lady, I shall neuer get his goodwill for want of abilitie, For he will do nothing except one bring money. And if you graunt it not, then it is past all doubt, I shall be neuer the nearer, but go quite without. Thou maiest well be Simplicitie for thou shewest thy folly. I haue a parsonage, but of what? of saint Nihil, and Nihil is nothing. Then where is the Church, or any Bels for to ring? Thou vnderstandest her not, she was set for to out, I thought comming in their names I should go without, Tis calle to see that Lucar oues not Loue and Conscience: But God I trust will one day yeeld her iust recompence. Good Simplicitie hold thy peace, my state is yet nought, I will helpe thee sure, if euer I get ought. But here comes sir Nicholas Nemo, to him I will go, And see if for their sakes he will any thing bestow. You shall haue, but what? a liuing that is blowne downe with the winde. What if thou canst not get it, then how wilt thou eate? why his name was Nemo, and Nemo hath no beeing.

Plot — The Three Ladies of London
Lady Love and Lady Conscience are concerned that Lady Lucre now rules in London, but Fame assures them that she and her adherents will meet their due punishment in the end. Four knaves and Simplicity, a penurious miller, go to London in the hope of employment by one of the ladies. Conscience rejects their applications, but Love agrees to take on Simplicity; the knaves find positions in Lucre’s household. Out of work because foreigners have taken all the jobs, Artifex also seeks a place with Lucre. Simplicity procures a testimonial from Conscience for his cousin Sincerity, who seeks a benefice; but he is unable to get anything from Simony because he has no bribe to offer. Dissimulation fobs him off with the parsonage of St Nihil — nothing - and an invitation to dine with Nemo — nobody — is likewise worthless (though Simplicity has _ difficulty understanding this). Simony employs Sir Peter Pleaseman, who can be either Protestant or Catholic as it suits his circumstances, as Lucre’s chaplain. Conscience is forced to sell her cottage to Lucre, but hopes to stay on as a sitting tenant. Usury threatens eviction; there is a proposal to pack the house with foreigners who will require less living space than English tenants. Eventually he relents and quadruples the rent instead. Love and Conscience are reduced to sending Usury their clothes in lieu of payment, but Dissimulation gives Simplicity money to redeem one of the gowns. He intended it to be Love’s, since he cares nothing for Conscience, but Simplicity returns Conscience’s clothes and delivers Love’s to Usury. Lucre enters an agreement with the Italian merchant Mercadore, whereby he will export essential materials and foodstuffs from England, and import foreign trinkets for gentlewomen. Mercadore goes to Turkey to procure knick-knacks, and encounters the Jew Gerontus. Two years ago, he borrowed a lot of money from Gerontus and avoided paying it back by skipping the country. Gerontus is happy to help in the supply of 1581 worthless tat to England, but eventually loses patience with Mercadore’s promises to pay his debt, and has him arrested. Mercadore proposes converting to Islam, which will legally void all his past obligations. Fearing that he might be blamed for a Christian’s apostasy, Gerontus cancels the debt, whereupon Mercadore’s Muslim convictions evaporate.
