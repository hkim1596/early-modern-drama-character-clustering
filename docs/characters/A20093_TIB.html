A meere slip, you have kept good diet my lord. You see it doe's in him. I'me ill, exceeding ill. Sure I did surfet at Lord Vannies. No, but I had a stomack to one dish, and the not tasting it, makes me sick at heart. Flesh sure, if I hit the marke right. Would I had beene a thousand leagues from thence, When I sat downe at's table or bin partner With Angelo Lotti in his banishment; Oh! sister Alphonsina, there I dranke My ba e the strongest poison that e're man Drew from a Ladies eye, now swelling in me. 'Tis a greene wound indeed. I am and with such mortall Arrowes pierc't I shall fall downe— And dye unlesse he her pitty Send me a quicke and sweete recovery. Faire Dariene, the Lord Vannies wife— Could love be like a subject, tied to lawes, Then might you speake this language. You shall see me then do so. If this be all the comfort you will lend me, Would you might leave me— Even what you will must out Yes sure my stomack would goe against it: 'Tis onely that which breeds in me despaire. Her walls of chastitie cannot be beaten downe. Sister you do but stretch me on the racke And with a laughing cheeke increase my paine, Be rather pitifull and ease my torments By teaching me how in this dreadfull storme, I may escape ship-wrack and attaine that shore Where I may live, heere else I'me sure to die. I doe conjure you by that noble blood Which makes me call you sister, cease to powre Poison into a wound, so neere my heart, And if to cure Loves-paines there be an Art. Woman me thinkes should know it cause she breeds it, I shall for ever thanke you. For heavens sake. Oh! fie, fie, fie— So so. Y'ar mad, y'ar mad. You split my very heart in pieces. Away, away, cruell you are to kill, When to give life, you have both power and skill. Thou think'st I lie in, heere's such a gossiping, as if 'twere a Child-bed Chamber. I would thy tongue were a man then, to lie. Good sister Alphonsina, you still play The bad Phisicion, I am all on fire, And you to quench mee, powre on scoopes of oyle; I feele ten thousand plummets at my heart, Yet you cry, Lay on more and are more cruell Then all my tortures. Gentle sister, The easiest thing ith' world to begge, I crave, And the poorest Almes to give. A friendly counsell, loe that's all. Embers? I wo'd you telt em, 'tis a fire— And shee'le condemne me; no, I will not write. Against your charmes, Witch, thus I stop mine eares. Are you mad? No, no, none else must, none shall, none can, My hunger feede but this; downe will I dive, And fetch this Pearle, or nere come up alive— Oh fie, fie. Plot summary

Plot — The Wonder of A Kingdom
The Duke of Florence has betrothed his daughter, Fiametta, to the Prince of Pisa, and, to facilitate the political marriage, banished her lover, Angelo. Fiametta sickens for her absent love, and he returns to court disguised as a French doctor, offering the Duke his services to save her life. He diagnoses sexual frustration, and proposes a cure which requires the patient to drink a potion made from the beloved’s powdered heart: she will then hate him. In order to obtain the key ingredient, Angelo must be murdered. Angelo reveals his presence to Fiametta, but she insists on identifying him to her father, in the expectation that the Duke will relent. Angelo passes this off as an insane delusion, then flees to sanctuary. He is found and brought to court with a friar. He seems to reject Fiametta, and she agrees to marry Pisa the following day, but asks for the friar to hear her confession overnight. In fact, he secretly marries her to Angelo, pre-empting Pisa; the Duke and Prince are annoyed, but recognize there is nothing to be done about it. In the sub-plot, Lord Vanni has amorous inclinations younger than his physical age, and sends a love-letter and jewel to the chaste Alphonsina Neri. Meanwhile, Alphonsina’s brother Tibaldo has fallen sick for love of Vanni’s wife Dariene. He asks Alphonsina to accept Vanni’s advances, and Vanni duly excuses himself from Dariene’s bed. Tibaldo has arranged for himself to be present disguised as Alphonsina’s sister, and offers himself as an alternative, ‘female’ bedfellow for Dariene. His plan is aborted when he learns that Vanni’s daughter Alisandra loves Trebatio and Trebatio’s son. When Vanni pays his nocturnal visit, he is scared off by Trebatio’s lute-playing, then caught by his wife and blamed for the adultery he never committed. Alphonsina and Tibaldo marry Trebatio and Alisandra. In the third plot, the hospitable Jacomo has built a house in which to entertain his guests: his uncle, who is suspected of getting rich through supernatural agency, gave him a deathbed injunction to spend his inheritance virtuously. Torrenti envies Jacomo his new house, and plans a magnificent lifestyle of his own; but whereas Jacomo entertains the poor and needy, Torrenti is only interested in luxury and guests of a high social standing.
