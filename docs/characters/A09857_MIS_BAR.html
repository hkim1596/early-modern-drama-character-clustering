Full speech

Whats a clocke sirra? Nor I, but that I thinke sheele play me false. Come, ther's my stake. Throw for the Dice: Ill luck they are yours. Play right I pray. Where stands your man? Right by the Lord, a plague vpon the bones. Not so sweet husband. I would ye did, I faith are ye so hasty? Doe ye heare daughter, you shal stay my leasure Well lustie guts, I meane to make ye stay, And set some rubbes in your mindes smothest way. I sir, haue patience, and see your father To rifle vp the treasure of my loue, And play the spend-thrift vpon such an harlot? This same will make me haue patience, will it not? You disobedient ruffen, carelesse wretch, That said your Father loude me too well, Ile thinke on't when thou thinkst I haue forgotten it: Whose with thee else? how now minion you? With whom? with him? why what make you heere sir? And thus late too, what hath your mother sent ye To cut my throate, that heere you be in waite? Come from him mistris, and let go his hand, Will ye not sir? Calst thou me mother, no thy mothers name Carryes about with it, reproche and shame: Giue me my daughter, ere that she shall wed, A strumpets sonne, and haue her fomislead, Ile marry her to a Carter: come I say, Giue me her from thee. Well, I will finde a time to be reueng'd: Mean time Ile keep my daughter from thy sonne, Where are you minion? how now are yee gone. Shees hid in this same Warren Ile lay money. But I will make ye. Will ye? A plague ont, is she there? No but I will not. O Lord what haste you make to hurt your selues Good Phillip vse some good perswasions To make them friends.

Plot summary

Plot — 1 The Two Angry Women of Abingdon
The Gourseys share a meal with their neighbours, the Barnes family, and the two wives fall out over a game of chance. When Barnes admonishes his wife, she suspects him of adultery with Mistress Goursey. With a view to patching up the wives’ quarrel, Barnes plans to marry their daughter Mall to Goursey’s son, Frank, but Mistress Goursey finds out about it and goes to pick a fight at the Barnes house. ; Frank successfully woos Mall, but Mistress Barnes’s intervention sends the lovers fleeing, intending to rendezvous in Oxford. They get lost in a nearby rabbit warren, where Sir Ralph Smith is also out hunting, determined to bag some venison if it takes him all night. The other characters search for the lovers in the dark; there is an extended sequence of mistaken identities, during which Will, Sir Ralph’s woodman, gets the mistaken idea that his master is looking for a wench. Sir Ralph meets one — Mall. She tells him her story and he offers to intervene to reconcile the two mothers. The husbands find the wives brawling over possession of a torch. Mistress Barnes’s tendency to call Mistress Goursey a strumpet leads Goursey to suspect that there is no smoke without fire, and that his wife is having an affair with Barnes. Philip Barnes sees things are getting out of hand, and persuades the two angry women to retract their harsh words and make up their quarrel and agree to the marriage. Sir Ralph arrives to find there is no work for him as peacemaker; instead, he asks the two families to dinner.
