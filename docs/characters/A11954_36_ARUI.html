What ſhould we ſpeake of When we are old as you? When we ſhall heare The Raine and winde beate darke December? How In this our pinching Caue, ſhall we diſcourſe The freezing houres away? We haue ſeene nothing: We are beaſtly; ſubtle as the Fox for prey, Like warlike as the Wolfe, for what we eate: Our Valour is to chace what flyes: Our Cage We make a Quite, as doth the priſon'd Bird, And ſing our Bondage freely. I am weake with toyle, yet ſtrong in appetite. Ile make't my Comfort He is a man, Ile loue him as my Brother: And ſuch a welcome as I'ld giue to him (After long abſence) ſuch is yours. Moſt welcome: Be ſprightly, for you fall 'mongſt Friends. Or I, what ere it be, What paine it coſt, what danger: Gods! The Night to' th' Owle, And Morne to th' Larke leſſe welcome. I pray draw neere. Brother, ſtay heere: Are we not Brothers? If it be ſinne to ſay ſo (Sir) I yoake mee In my good Brothers fault: I know not why I loue this youth, and I haue heard you ſay, Loue's reaſon's, without reaſon. The Beere at doore, And a demand who is't ſhall dye, I'ld ſay My Father, not this youth. Brother, farewell. You health.—So pleaſe you Sir. Thus did he anſwer me: yet ſaid heereafter, I might know more. Wee'l not be long away. How Angell-like he ſings? He cut our Rootes in Charracters, And ſawc'ſt our Brothes, as Iuno had bin ſicke, And he her Dieter. Nobly he yoakes A ſmiling, with a ſigh; as if the ſighe Was that it was, for not being ſuch a Smile: The Smile, mocking the Sigh, that it would flye From ſo diuine a Temple, to commix With windes, that Saylors raile at. Grow patient, And let the ſtinking-Elder (Greefe) vntwine His periſhing roote, with the encreaſing Vine. None in the world: you did miſtake him ſure. In this place we left them; I wiſh my Brother make good time with him, You ſay he is ſo fell. Let Ord'nance Come as the Gods fore-ſay it: howſoere, My Brother hath done well. Would I had done 't: So the Reuenge alone purſu'de me: Polidore I loue thee brotherly, but enuy much Thou haſt robb'd me of this deed: I would Reuenges That poſſible ſtrength might meet, wold ſeek vs through And put vs to our anſwer. Poore ſicke Fidele. Ile willingly to him, to gaine his colour, Il'd let a pariſh of ſuch Clotens blood, And praiſe my ſelfe for charity. The Bird is dead That we haue made ſo much on. I had rather Haue skipt from ſixteene yeares of Age, to ſixty: To haue turn'd my leaping time into a Crutch, Then haue ſeene this. Starke, as you ſee: Thus ſmiling, as ſome Fly had tickled ſlumber, Not as deaths dart being laugh'd at: his right Cheeke Repoſing on a Cuſhion. O' th' floore: His armes thus leagu'd, I thought he ſlept, and put My clowted Brogues from off my feete, whoſe rudeneſſe Anſwer'd my ſteps too lowd. With fayreſt Flowers Whil'ſt Sommer laſts, and I liue heere, Fidele, Ile ſweeten thy ſad graue: thou ſhalt not lacke The Flower that's like thy face. Pale-Primroſe, nor The azur'd Hare-bell, like thy Veines: no, nor The leafe of Eglantine, whom not to ſlander, Out-ſweetned not thy breath: the Raddocke would With Charitable bill (Oh bill ſore ſhaming Thoſe rich-left-heyres, that let their Fathers lye Without a Monument) bring thee all this, Yea, and furr'd Moſſe beſides. When Flowres are none To winter-ground thy Coarſe— Say, where ſhall's lay him? Bee't ſo: And let vs (Polidore) though now our voyces Haue got the manniſh cracke, ſing him to' th' ground As once to our Mother: vſe like note, and words, Saue that Euriphile, muſt be Fidele. Wee'l ſpeake it then. If you'l go fetch him, Wee'l ſay our Song the whil'ſt: Brother begin. 'Tis true. So, begin. Feare no more the frowne o' th' Great, Thou art paſt the Tirants ſtroake, Care no more to cloath and eate, To thee the Reede is as the Oake: The Scepter, Learning, Phyſicke muſt, All follow this and come to duſt. Nor th' all-dreaded Thunderſtone. Thou haſt finiſh'd Ioy and m ne. Nor no witch-craft charme thee. Nothing ill come neere thee. What pleaſure Sir, we finde in life, to locke it From Action, and Aduenture. It is not likely, That when they heare their Roman horſes neigh, Behold their quarter'd Fires; haue both their eyes And eares ſo cloy'd importantly as now, That they will waſte their time vpon our note, To know from whence we are. By this Sunne that ſhines Ile thither: What thing is't, that I neuer Did ſee man dye, ſcarſe euer look'd on blood, But that of Coward Hares, hot Goats, and Veniſon? Neuer beſtrid a Horſe ſaue one, that had A Rider like my ſelfe, who ne're wore Rowell, Nor Iron on his heele? I am aſham'd To looke vpon the holy Sunne, to haue The benefit of his bleſt Beames, remaining So long a poore vnknowne. So ſay I, Amen. One Sand another Not more reſembles that ſweet Roſie Lad: Who dyed, and was Fidele: what thinke you? In that he ſpake too farre. Your danger's ours. I my good Lord. You holpe vs Sir, As you did meane indeed to be our Brother, Ioy'd are we, that you are.

Plot summary

Plot — Cymbeline, King of Britain
Imogen has been the heir to Cymbeline’s kingdom since the disappearance of her infant brothers twenty years ago. Now her father intends her to marry her foolish stepbrother Cloten; but she pre-empts this by marrying Posthumus instead. Cymbeline objects to Posthumus, whom he took in as an orphan and brought up at court, and banishes him. The Queen pretends support for the couple, but is really the source of Cymbeline’s hostility; her objective is to bring the succession into her own family line, if necessary by destroying Imogen. Claiming to be studying toxicology, she asks Cornelius to give her some poison for experimental purposes. He mistrusts her motives: the ‘poison’ he supplies is really only a sleeping potion. She then gives it to Posthumus’ servant, Pisanio, claiming it to be a powerful medicine. Posthumus goes to Rome, leaving his bracelet behind with Imogen as a love-token, and taking her ring. There he boasts of his wife’s beauty and chastity, and bets Iachimo the ring that she will never succumb to his seduction. Iachimo comes to Britain, tells Imogen that Posthumus is behaving adulterously in Rome, and tries to persuade her to do the same. When she refuses, he claims he was only testing her, and asks her to take charge of a chest of valuables, partly paid for by Posthumus, and lodge them for the night in a safe place, her bedroom. He hides in the chest and emerges when she is asleep, giving him the opportunity to inspect the room, look at her in bed, and take Posthumus’ bracelet from her arm. Back in Rome, he claims to have enjoyed Imogen sexually, and the bracelet convinces Posthumus that he is telling the truth. Iachimo takes the ring and Posthumus sends two letters home: one to Imogen asking her to meet him at Milford Haven, the other to Pisanio ordering him to kill her on arrival there. Pisanio escorts her as planned, but then gives her boy’s clothes to enable her to escape from Britain; he also passes on the ‘medicine’ he had from the Queen, then writes to tell Posthumus that she has been killed as ordered. Cymbeline has neglected to pay the tribute to Rome agreed by his predecessors. Caius Lucius comes to demand it, but gets a dusty answer. Rome prepares its 1610 forces for an invasion. Imogen is found to be absent from court, and, stung by her contemptuous rejection, Cloten follows her wearing a suit of Posthumus’ clothes, intending to rape her and bring her back humiliated to court.
