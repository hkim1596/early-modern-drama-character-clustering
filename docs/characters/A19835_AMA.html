Full speech

Dorinda, you are yet in happie case, You are belou'd, you need not to complaine; 'Tis I haue reason onely to bewaile My fortunes, who am cast vpon disdaine, And on his rockie heart that wrackes my youth With stormes of sorowes, and contemnes my truth; 'Tis I that am shut out from all delight This world can yeeld a maide, that am remou'd From th'onely ioy on earth, to be belou'd: Cruell Carinus scornes this faith of mine, And lets poore Amarillis grieue and pine. How may that be Dorinda? you know this, You can enioy but one, and one there is Ought to possesse your heart, and loue alone, Who hunts two Hares at one time, catches none. Well Cloris yet he may, for ought I see Before you come, vnlesse you make more haste. Ah cruell maide, she little knowes the griefe Of such a heart that's desperate of reliefe, Nor vnderstands she her owne happinesse, To haue so true a louer as he is. And yet I see sh'is toucht, if not too late, For I perceiu'd her coulour come and goe, And though in pride she would haue hid her woe, Yet I saw sorrow looke out at her eyes. And poore Amyntas if thou now be gone, Thou hast (like to the Bee that stinging dyes, And in anothers wound leaft his owne life) Transpierced by thy death, that marble heart, Which liuing thou, couldst touch by no desert. And if thou shalt escape, thou hast suruiu'd Her crueltie, which now repents her wrong, And thou shalt by her fauours be reuiu'd, After the affliction thou hast suffred long. Which makes me thinke, that time, and patience may Intenerat at length the hardest heart, And that I may yet after all my woe, Liue t'ouertake Carinus mercie too. Indeed Dorinda you haue done him wrong, But your repentance, and compassion now May make amends, and you must learne to do As I long time haue done, indure and hope, And on that turne of Fortunes Scene depend, When all extremities must mend, or end. Ah gentle Lelaps, prety louing dogge, Where hast thou leaft thy maister, where is hee, That great commander ouer thee and mee? Thou wert not wont be far off from his feete, And ô no more would I, were he so pleasd; But would as well as thou go follow him, Through brakes, and thickers, ouer cliffes and rockes So long as I had life to follow him, Would he but looke vpon me with that eye Of fauour, as h'is vs'd to looke on thee. Thou canst be clapt, and strookt with that faire hande That thrustes away my heart, and beates it back From following him, which yet it euer will And though he fly mee I must after still. But here he comes, me thought he was not farre. Alas the least of this regarde before, Might haue holpe all, then when 'twas in her power, T'haue sau'd his heart, and to reuiue his minde, Now for all this, her mercie is vnkinde; The good that's out of season, is not good. There is no difference now twixt cruelty, And the compassion thats not vnderstood. There may for me, if now Carinus thou Wilt stand but to thy word, as thou hast said. O deare Carinus blest be this good howre, That I haue liu'd to ouertake at last That heart of thine which fled from me so fast.

Plot — The Queen's Arcadia (Arcadia Reformed)
Arcadia is no longer the place of pastoral simplicity it once was: old shepherds have grown litigious, young ones are falling in love unwisely and unrequitedly, and the nymphs, who seem less beautiful than before, have abandoned the modest simplicity of traditional Arcadian dress in favour of quirky new fashions. The problems arise from foreign influences on this previously sequestered land. Colax has travelled abroad, and brought back with him the city woman Techne, and they are selfishly interfering in the love- lives of the nymphs and shepherds: they have made Silvia and Palaemon jealous of one another with mendacious tales of infidelity; and Colax has persuaded Dorinda to jilt Myrtillus in his favour, making her the latest in a string of sexual conquests. Meanwhile Lincus, a lawyer, and Doctor Alcon have both come from town to set up practices in Arcadia. Lincus has found the local amity bad for business: the Arcadians do not covet one another’s property and so never go to law. For Alcon, however, the Arcadians’ fellow-feeling is profitable, because when one falls ill, others follow suit, and they all want the same medicine from him — which is just as well, because he has only one to offer, and that is a placebo. Both are keen to drum up trade, so Lincus has set Montanus and Acrisius against one another, and Alcon has introduced the Arcadians to an unhealthy habit: tobacco-smoking. Carinus (who has recently jilted Amaryllis) and Amyntas are rivals for the love of Cloris, and argue about which of them has the greater command of her affections; but in fact Cloris loves neither of them, and has sworn chastity until she finds a worthy man. Colax now wants a change of girlfriend, and asks Techne to make Cloris love him. Techne offers to give Cloris a new outfit, and arranges for them to meet at a cave so she can try it on; but it is Colax who keeps the appointment, and attempts to rape her. Techne herself has fallen for Amyntas, and arranges for him to see Cloris meeting Colax. Amyntas becomes suicidal, so Techne sends Cloris to stop him; but she arrives too late, and he has already taken poison.
