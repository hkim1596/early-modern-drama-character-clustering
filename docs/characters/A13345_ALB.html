Nay, if I goe not now, I might be gelded ifaith. Albert aſcends, and being on the top of the ladder, puts outs the candle. I heard the ſteps of ſome comming this way, Did you not heare Albert paſſe by as yet. Then hee intends iuſt at the breake of day, To lend his truſty helpe to our departure: Tis yet two howres time thether, till when lets reſt, For that our ſpeedy flight will not yeeld any. Iuſt at the inſtant, feare not of his Care: If I doe not doe ſo, then hate me euer, Ile then retourne alone to fetch you hence, Sooner ile deceaue my ſoule, and ſo I feare I haue. Till when, this touch of lips be the true pleadge, of Carracus conſtant true deuoted loue, But you did lend a hand vnto my entrance. How haue I wrong'd my friend, my faith full friend, Robd him of whats more precious then his blood, His earthly heauen th'unſpotted honor, Of his ſoule-ioying Miſtres, the fruition of whoſe bed, I yet am warme of, whilſt deere Carracus, Wanders this cold night, through th'unſheltering field, Seeking me treacherous man, yet no man neither, Though in an outward ſhew of ſuch appearance, But am a Diuel indeed, for ſo this deed. Of wronged loue and friendſhip rightly makes me, I may compare my friend, to one that's ſicke, Who lying on his death-bed, cals to him, His dear'ſt thought friend and bids him goe, To ſome rare gifted man that can reſtore, His former health, this his friend ſadly heares, And vowes with proteſtations to fulfill, His wiſht deſires, with his beſt performance, But then no ſooner ſeeing that the death, Of his ſicke friend, would ad to him ſome gaine. Goes not to ſeeke a remedy to ſaue, But like a wretch hides him to dig his graue, As I haue done for vertuous Carracus, Yet Albert be not reaſonleſſe, to indanger, VVhat thou maiſt yet ſecure, who can detect, The crime of thy licentious appetite, There ones pace tis ſurely Carracus. Tis wronged Carracus by Alberts baſeneſſe, I haue no power now to reueale my ſelfe, What ere you are that call, you know my name. O Carracus, you are a ſlow pac't louer. Your credit had been toucht, had I not beene: Why I excuſd you to the faire Maria; Who would haue thought you elſe, a ſlacke performer. For comming firſt vnder her chamber window, Shee heard me tread, and cald vpon your name, To which I anſwered with a tongue like yours: And told her, I would goe to ſeeke for Albert, And ſtraight retourne. Yes but hearing of ſome Company, Shee at my warning, was fore't to put it out: And had I bin ſo too, you and I too had ſtill bin happy. Yes, and your ſeruant honored Lady. May you continew euer ſo: But its time now to make ſome haſt to horſe: Night ſoone will vaniſh: O that it had power For euer to exclude day from oureies, For my lookes then will ſhew my villany: O friend, no more, come, you are ſlow in haſte, Friendſhip ought neuer be diſtruſt in words, Till all her deeds be finiſh't, who looking in a booke, And reades but ſome part only, cannot iudge What pray ſo the whole deſerues, becauſe his knowledge Is grounded but on part, as thine friend is Ignorant of that black miſchiefe I haue done thee. Yes I do follow, would I had done ſo euer, And nere had gone before. My buſineſſe is not ſo troubleſome: Is a in health with his late eſpouſed wife I me truly glad ont, farewel good friend. You may ſay, one Albert riding by this way, onely inquir'd their health. How like a poiſonous Doctor haue I come, To enquire their wel-fare, knowing that my ſelfe Haue giuen the portion of their nere recouery; For which I will afflict my ſelfe with torture euer: And ſince the earth yeelds not a remedy, Able to ſalue the ſores my luſt hath made, Ile now take fare-wel of ſociety, And th'aboade of men to entertaine a life Fitting my fellowſhip, in deſart woods; Where beaſts like me conſort, there may I liue, Farre off from wronging vertuous Carracus; Theres no Maria that ſhall ſatisfie My hatefull luſt, the trees ſhall ſhelter This wretched trunke of mine, vpon whoſe backe. I heard the Eccho anſwere vnto one, That by his ſpeech cannot be far remote From of this ground, and ſee I haue diſcried him: Oh heauens! its Carracus, whoſe reaſons ſeate Is now vſurpt by madnes, and diſtraction; Which I the author of confuſion Haue planted here, by my accurſed deeds. Good ſir, if any ſparke do yet remaine Of your conſumed reaſon, let me ſtriue. But though your words do ſauor ſir of iudgement, Yet when they derogate from the due obſeruance Of fitting times, they ought not be reſpected, No more, then if a man ſhould tell a tale Of fained mirth in midſt of extreame ſorrowes. Good ſir walke with me, but where you ſee The ſhaddowing Elmes, within whoſe circling round There is a holy ſpring about incompaſt, By dandling ſiccamores and viol es, Whoſe waters cure all homane maladies: Few drops thereof being ſprinkled on your temples, Reuiues your fading memory, and reſtores Your ſences loſt vnto their perfect being. Moſt pleaſant to the taſte, pray will you goe. Which vnto you befall, thereof moſt worthy: But vertuous ſir, what I will now requeſt From your true generous nature, is, that you would Be pleas'd to pardon that repentant Wight Whoſe ſinfull ſtories vpon you trees barke, Your ſelfe did reade, for that you ſay, to you Thoſe wrongs were done. I rather ought to thanke the heauens Creator, That he vouchſaf't me ſuch eſpeciall grace, In dooing ſo ſmall a good, which could I howerly Beſtowe on all, yet could I not aſſwage The ſwelling rancor of my fore-paſt crimes. Then ſee falſe Albert proſtrate at your feete, Deſiring Iuſtice for his haynous ill. So pleaſe you, vertuous paire, Albert will ſtay, And ſpend the remnant of this weary ſome life In theſe darke woods. Lou'd Carracus, I haue found in thy conuerſe Comfort ſo bleſt, that nothing now but death, Shall cauſe a ſeparation in our being. Heauen ſo continue it. Ile ſee that peace concluded.

Plot summary

Plot — The Hog Hath Lost His Pearl
Haddit has spent all his money and mortgaged his lands to Hog the usurer, and now ekes out a living selling jigs to players. He aims to solve his financial problems by marrying Rebecca, Hog’s daughter, whose affections he had previously refused in the belief that she was too low-born for him. Hog intends Rebecca to marry young Lord Wealthy, but she refuses, and helps Haddit trick her father. Haddit’s uncle, Lightfoot, helps to get the servant Peter out of the way by picking a quarrel, to give him a pretext for making peace with a drinking contest; Peter imbibes himself insensible in the cellar. Rebecca steals Hog’s keys, giving Haddit and his accomplices access to his bedroom, where he keeps his gold. Lightfoot appears to Hog as the ghost of Croesus, come to make the usurer rich: a spirit will ===== PAGE 291 ===== 1613 take his gold and transform it into pearl, which is even more valuable; in reality, Haddit steals the money and retrieves his mortgage, then absconds with Rebecca and marries her. Hog goes to complain to old Lord Wealthy, but his supernatural experiences induce a conversion: he renounces avarice and gives the couple his blessing. In the sub-plot, Carracus plans to elope with young Lord Wealthy’s sister Maria, and entrusts his friend Albert with the arrangements. Albert visits Maria early, posing as Carracus, and has sex with her; she then leaves with Carracus as planned, and marries him: Albert suffers remorse and decides to leave human society. Some time later, Carracus notices that Maria is wearing Albert’s ring, which he gave her on the night of the elopement. Both realize what Albert did; Maria faints and Carracus is told that she has died of shame. In fact, she disguises herself as a page and flees to the woods, where Albert has carved penitential verses on every tree. Posing as a hermit, Albert finds her almost dead from starvation; he takes her to his cave; and feeds her. Carracus also goes to the woods, where an echo tells him that Maria is alive and innocent. They meet up, and revelations ensue: the page is chaste Maria and the hermit is penitent Albert. Carracus and Maria forgive him, and make their peace with Maria’s family.
