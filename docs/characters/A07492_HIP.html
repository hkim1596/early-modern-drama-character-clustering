I am a new man Sister, and now cry a pox a that ho nor, that must haue none but Barber-Surgions to waite vpon't, and a band of poore stragling rascals, that euery twinckling of an eye, forfeit their legs and armes into the Lords hands Wenches by Mars his sweatty Buffe Ierkin, (for now all my 〈◊〉 must smell a the Soldado) I haue seene more mens head 〈◊〉 vp and downe like foote-balles at a breake-fast, after the hungry Cannons had pickt them; than are Maiden-heads in Venice: and more legs of men seru'd in at a dinner, then euer I shall see legs of Capons in one platter whilst I liue. Not so many as thou hast done with that villanous eye by a thousand. S'foote if all the wit in this company haue nothing to set it selfe about, but to run Deuision vpon me, why then Eene burne off mine eares indeed, but my little Mer-maides, Signi or Camillo does this, that I now might describe the Niniuiticall motion of the whole battle, and so tell what hee has done: and come, shall I begin? But I can tell you one thing, I shall make your hayre stand vp an end at some things. Yes, yes, I haue a tricke to make vs vnderstand one an other and we fumble neuer so— The law at logs then. Agree'd so that we go not out of the compasse of those things that are fit for Lords. Ide knocke as long as I thought good, but haue my braines knockt out when I entred, if I were he. Will your French prisoner drinke well? or else cut his throate. The pox he can, s'light me thinks a French man shold haue a good courage to wine, for many of them be exceeding hot fiery whoresons, and resolute as Hector, and as valliant as Troilus; then come off and on brauely and lye by it, and sweate fort too, vpon a good and a millitary aduantage. Enough of this, come wenches shake your heeles. Trickes: trickes? kerry merry buffe; how now lad, in a traunce? Nay sweet roague, wh wouldst thou make his face a vizard, to haue two loope-holes onely? when he comes to a good face, may he not doe with his eyes what he will: s'foote if I were as he, Ide pull them out, and if I wist they would anger thee. Hercules stand betweene them. Your foote with a pox? I hope y'are no Pope Sir, his lips shall kisse my Sisters soft lippe: and thine, the tough lips of this: nay Sir, I doe but shewe you that I haue a toole; doe you heate Saint Dennis, but that we both stand vpon the nar row bridge of Honour, I should cut your throate now, for pure loue you beare to my Sister, but that I know you would set out a throate. Saint Marke set his markes vpon me then: stab? Ile haue my shinnes broken, ere Ile scratch so much as the skin off, a the law of Armes: shall I make a French-man cry oh, before the fall of the Leafe? not I by the Crosse of this, Dandy prat. I shall lay too heauie a crosse vpon thee then. But sirra Camillo, wilt thou play the wise and venerable bearded Master Constable, and commit him indeede, because he would be medling in thy Precincte and will not put off the cap of his Loue to the browne-bill of thy desires? Well, thou hast giuen the Law of Armes a broken pate alreadie, therefore if thou wilt needes turne Broker, and be a cut-throate too, doe: for my part, Ile goe get a sweet ball, and wash my handes of it. Ist you Sir Pandarus, the broking Knight of Troy, are your two legs the paire of tressels, for the French-man to getvp vpon my Sister? With her heeles? O heer's a sure pocket Dag, and my Sister shootes him off snipsnap at her pleasure. Sirra Mephosto philes, did not you bring letters from my Sister to the French-man? Did not he follow your cracke-ship, at a becke giuen? But were staide thus. Not for this wager of my Sisters loue, run; away Dandi part, catch True-penny, & hold him, thy selfe shal passe more cur rant. Nay Gods lid after him Camillo, reply not but away. For I know that the onelie way to win a wench, is not to woe her: the onely way to haue her fast, is to haue her loose: the onely way to tryumph ouer her, is to make her fall; and the way to make her fall,— Are you so cunning Sir? Well Sir, you know the Gentlewoman that dwels in the midst of Saint Markes streete. A pox on you; the flea-bitten fac'd Ladie. Not as I doe, I hope Sir. Well sir slip goe presently to her, and from me deliuer to her owne white hands, Fontinels picture. She does, le keepe my sisters eyes and his painted face a sunder; tell her besides, the Maske holdes and this the night, & nine the houre; say we are all for her away. I swore first. Come. Let him goe, I knew what hooke would choake him, and therfore baited that for him to nibble vpon: an old combe peckt rascall, that was beaten out a'th Cocke-pit, when I could not stand a hye-lone without I held by a thing, to come crow ing among vs: hang him lobster; come, the same oath that your Fore-man tooke, take all, and Sing. Loue is like a Lambe, and Loue is like a Lyon, Fly from Loue, he fights; fight, then does he fly on: Loue is all in fire, and yet is euer freezing, Loue is much in winning, yet is more in leezing. S'foote how now Don Dego; Sanguine cheekt? dost thinke their faces haue been at Cutlers? out you roring-tawny fac'd rascall, t'were a good deede to beate my hyltes about's coxcombe, and then make him Sanguine cheekt too. Mary this Sir; heere's a Yellow-hammer flew to me with thy water, and I cast it, and finde, that his Mistris be ing giuen to this newe falling-sicknesse, will cure thee: the Frenchman you see has a soft Mermaladie heart, and shall no sooner feele Imperiaes liquerish desire to licke at him, but straight hee'll sticke the brooch of her longing in it: then Sir, may you sir, come vpon my Sister sir, with a fresh charge Sir; Sa, sa, sa, sa, once giuing backe, and thrice comming forward, she yeeld and the towne of Brest is taken. Boneioure Monsieur. Hark swaggerer, there's a little dapple-colour'd rascal: ho a Bona Roba; her name's Imperia, a Gentlewoman by my faith of an auntient house, and has goodlie rents, and comminges in of her owne, and this Ape would faine haue thee chayn'd to her in the holie state: Sirra, shee's falne in loue with thy picture, yes faith too her, wooe her, and win her: leaue my Sister, & thy ran some's paide; all's paide Gentlemen; bi'th Lord Imperia is as good a girle as any is in Uenice. After him Frisco, inforce thy Mistresses passion, thou shalt haue accesse to him, to bring him loue-tokens: if they preuaile not, yet thou shalt still be in presence, bee't but to spite him: In honest Frisco. Come, wilt thou goelaugh, and lye downe? nowe sure there be some rebels in thy bellie, for thine eyes doe nothing but watch and ward, tho'ast not slept these three nights. Heere sweet Sister. S'light, she meanes her French garsoon. You will not leaue my friend in this poore taking: You scuruey Tyt: s'foote, scuruey anything, Doe you heare Susanna: you, puncke, if I geld not your Muske-Cat; Ile doo't by Iesu; lets goe Camillo. Rareroage! fine! Swounds holde. As for that light Hobby-horse my Sister, whose foule name, I will race out with my Poniard; by the honour of my Familie (which her lust hath prophaned) I sweare (and Gentlemen be in this, my sworne brothers) I sweare that as all Uenice does admire her beautie, so all the world shall be amazed at her punishment, follow therefore. And betweene both, my Sisters. At so, Saint Marke my Pistoll, thus death flyes. Puncke you may laugh at this, Heer's trickes, but mouth Ile stop you with a kisse. There be many of your Countrymen in Ireland Signi or, trauaile to them.

Plot summary

Plot — Blurt, Master Constable, or The Spaniard's Night Walk
Camillo and Hippolito return home to Venice after a war with France. Camillo brings the French lord Fontinelle as a prisoner, and presents him to his love Violetta. She falls for the Frenchman, so Camillo sends him to prison. Hippolito takes charge of the situation. He makes the courtesan Imperia dote on Fontinelle by sending her his picture; but Fontinelle refuses to accept her love and is taken back to his dungeon. Meanwhile Camillo’s attempts to woo Violetta back are met with rejection. Fontinelle escapes by changing clothes with Frisco, and, using Violetta’s page as a go-between, makes an assignation at a chapel, where they are secretly married. Afterwards, though, he takes up with Imperia. Camillo swears revenge on all three and leads a party of armed men to kill them and hang out their dead bodies. Violetta also goes to find her husband at Imperia’s, and is invited to join him in bed. Camillo’s men, the watch, and the Duke all arrive simultaneously at the brothel. The Duke prevents murder being done, but has Fontinelle arrested for adultery, which is a capital crime in Venice. Violetta denies that infidelity has occurred: it was all a plot laid by her and Fontinelle. In the sub-plot, Lazarillo also arrives in Venice from the wars and gets lodgings with Blurt the constable. He goes to Imperia’s house to lecture the ladies on Spanish marital conduct, and makes a nocturnal assignation with Imperia. Simperina makes another assignation with old Curvetto, but when he tries to attract her attention he is doused with water. Lazarillo is equally unlucky in his date: he falls through a trapdoor. Curvetto returns and is climbing up a rope ladder let down by Frisco when his foot slips; Frisco accuses him of trying to burgle the house, and he is arrested by Blurt. Lazarillo climbs out and he too is doused, with urine, and then arrested by Blurt. Curvetto refuses to 1601 marry Imperia to escape the death penalty, but the Duke pardons him and Lazarillo anyway, and agrees to pay the money the latter owes Blurt.
