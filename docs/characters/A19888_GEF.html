What Roger, al amort, me thinkes th'art off o'th'hookes? Faith I was never on a merrier pinn, Nor my breaſt lighter hearted. Ha? Thou wilt not ſwagger, Hodge? Are we not of one Houſe? Pander? I could anſwer thee, but that I ſpy two Gentlemen, With whom I have buſineſſe of my Maſters. All's plotted, we want money, ſell we muſt, The rate we live at muſt have freſh ſupply; Ile give you the whole project. I have gone through ſir. A Thouſand pounds brought in to night. Trifles ſir, acknowledging a Statute, nothing elſe; 'Tis finiſht in a moment. Three ſir. It ſhall be done, Ile be your Mercury. If any thing to whet dull appetite, To heighten pleaſure, and inflame delight Be this night wanting, ere Sun riſe to morrow Caſhiere me from your ſervice. Good Wine, good Victualls, Liveries; And the countenance of a good Maſter. That's as your worſhip ſhall be pleaſed to call it. All this ſir? And how for mine? If this he all, as where no more is left What more can be expected? this's my portion, Ile husband't for my ſelfe; he that gets this, Or part of this, muſt have more ſhare in me Than either man or Maſter. And my neglect; ſo I be ſtor'd my ſelfe, Which hand with him goes forward. 'Tis moſt true, you never ſaw a man ſo ſtrang debauch't; He hath not onely run out all his fortune, But even his ſences; I had once my Lord, Some ſmall dependance on him, but his riot Hath almoſt ruin'd me. Your Lordſhips Cloth and countenance. One of my fellowes once. Brave my Lord? And nothing more than truth. Speake it not, Ile not beleeve it Miſtris. But all this Miſtris, makes not me a Pander. Where I'le try how neere of Kin I am to this Gentleman, and ſhew my ſelfe an Eate-well. Exeunt. Him I ſaw but now croſſe by the doore. Sir I ſhall doe't. Remember Iſlington, my Hoſt, my Hoſteſſe, and the Conſtable, Should we be ſilent, every Oyſter-wife, Fiſh-wife, and thoſe cry Milke and Orenges Can ſpeake of your rare ſupper; fye, Frier Iohn, And ſo dainty to your friends? A man of Art, no more; ſo much we know you are. Offer my ſelfe to the Divill before my time? You might accuſe my folly. What, growne ſo brave? he hath ſure lighted on ſome cheate? A Bankerupt idle fellow brave your Lordſhip? Beate him to's wits, or let me kicke him for you. Endure this? Daſh out's braines. I thinke we made him fly, for I am ſure ſome there Gave ſhamefull ground. Here comes your husband, he hath perhaps ſome newes He ſayes true ſir, Let all your anger then conclude in this, And bid the Divell take him. Faith And I. Would I were hence. My lord theſe Friers I know. My Lord ſee, my Fellow Roger's damn'd, for he's got in Among the Fiends and Furies. Is ſhe not dead to night, and Now her Ghoſt there walkes? Now I have't; Upon my life the Divell can be no better; ſhe the illuſive Spirit by whom this roome was haunted, contracted Now unto yon deſperate Ruffian.

Plot summary

Plot — A New Trick to Cheat the Devil
Anne Changeable wants to marry Slightall, with her father’s consent, but her mother persuades her that marriage to Lord Scales will be better for her social standing. Slightall takes rejection badly: he turns to misogyny and voluptuous hedonism. One of his servants, Roger, tries unsuccessfully to dissuade him from squandering his money, while the other, Geoffrey, helps make an arrangement with a usurer to supply him with a further £1,000 — the usurer’s objective being to acquire his land. Lord Scales visits Anne, and she finds that he is not a good kisser: Slightall is the better catch in every respect save his lack of a title; but when she tries to reactivate the romance, Slightall rejects her. He has been ruined by prodigality and pays off his servants; Geoffrey is taken on by Lord Scales, but Roger declines a similar offer. Anne insults everyone who persuaded her to jilt Slightall, but this only makes Lord Scales more attracted to her. Changeable promises Anne that he will get Slightall back for her. Driven out of his wits with melancholy, Slightall conjures up Beelzebub, and makes a contract: the devil will give him enough money to pay his debts in exchange for the right to take his soul when he no longer owes anyone anything. On the day his debt to the usurer falls due, he turns out to have ample money to pay, so the usurer gets his money back rather than the lands he coveted. Asked by Anne to give Slightall some financial support, Lord Scales finds his offer rejected with contempt. The Changeables’ house is troubled by a female spirit; Slightall agrees to help exorcise it by spending the night in the haunted room. When he does so, he is visited by the ghost in Anne’s form. Beelzebub comes for Slightall’s soul, but Slightall demonstrates that he is not yet clear of debt: he paid the usurer with money loaned by Beelzebub. The devil accepts the situation and offers to marry him to the she-devil who haunts the Changeables’ house. Anne’s mother and Lord Scales give their consent, and watch as a friar performs the ceremony. In fact it is not a ghost in Anne’s form, but Anne herself; and ‘Beelzebub’ was really Changeable: Anne has finally got her man back. In the sub-plot, two friars are detained until late by charitable visitation, and find themselves locked out of their abbey. They ask Nan to give them a bed for the night.
