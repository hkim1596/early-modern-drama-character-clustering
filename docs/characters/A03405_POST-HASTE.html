Lett's make vp a company of Players, For we can all sing and say, And so (with practise) soone may learne to play. I'le teach yee to play true Politicians. Why proper-fellowes, they play Lords and Kings, Content; Scriuener, hoe, You must tye a knott of Knaues togither. Your names he meanes; the man's learn'd. I Maister Posthast the Poet. O stately Scriuener, thats wher dwell yee? Politician Players. Whose but the merry Knight's, sir Oliuer Owlets, There was neuer a better man to Players. What a greazie phrase: This playing will furnish yee. Heeres halfe a dozen good fellowes. The liker to thriue. A speakes to you players; I am the poett. Tis not to be dealt on without store of drinke. Nay tis well said, for drink must clap vp the bargaine. Lets away. Haue you cry'd the Play, maisters? O sirs, my wit's grown no lesse plentiful then the time. Ther's two sheets done in follio, will cost two shillings in time. I that you shall, I sweare by the Sunne-sit down sirs, When Aucthours quill, in quiuering hand, His tyred arme did take: His wearied Muse, bad him deuise, Some fine play for to make. And now my Maisters in this brauadoe, I can read no more without Canadoe. Enter the Prodigall Child; fill the pot I would say, Huffa, huffa, who callis for mee? I play the Prodigall child in iollytie. Enter to him Dame Vertue: My Sonne thou art a lost childe, (This is a passion, note you the passion?) And hath many poore men of their goods beguild: O prodigall childe, and childe prodigall. Read the rest sirs, I cannot read for teares, Fill mee the pot I prethe fellow Gulch. My maisters, what tire weares your lady on her head? O amiable good, 'tis excellent. I'le doo't extempore. Why Lords we are heere to shew you what we are, Lords wee are heere although our cloths be bare, In steed of flowers, in season, yee shall gather Rime and Reason? I neuer pleas'd my selfe better, it comes off with such suauity. Why man? The glasse is run our play is done, Hence Time doth call, wee thanke you all. No matter so they thump vs not, Come, come, we poets haue the kindest wretches to our Ingles One whose hands are hard as battle-dores with clap ping at baldnesse. Content. Why his reward is worth the Maior & all the towne. Sir, we haue carowst like Kings, For heere is plenty of all things. God blesse my Lord Mauortius, & his merry men all, To make his honour merry, we sing in the hall. We hope (for causes) in the house, though drinke be in our heads. Because to Plenty we carowse, for beefe and beere, and beds. For mine owne part, though this summer season I am desperate of a horse. A Gentleman's a Gentleman, that hath a cleane shirt on, with some learning, and so haue I. Mother Gurtons neadle; ( a Tragedy. ) The Diuell and Diues; ( a Comedie. ) Arusset coate, and a Knaues cap; ( an Infernall ) A prowd heart and a beggars purse; ( a pastorall. ) The Widdowes apron-strings; ( a nocturnall. ) My Lords, of your accords, some better pleasure for to bring, if you a theame affords, you shall knowe it, that I Post-hast the Poet, extempore can sing. Your Lord? what, shall our paines be soundly recom penc'd? With open hand of honours srancke reward? But are you sure that none will want the weight? To wey downe our expence in sumptuous Clothes? O sir, your morall lines were better spent, In matters of more worthy consequent. Preach to the poore; looke Steward, to your compt, Direct your houshold, teach not vs to mount: Sir as concerning halfe a score angells Or such a matter for a man in my place. Faith Ile eene past all my ballads together, And make a coate to hold out pistoll-proofe; Well sirs, I haue no stomacke to these warres, Goody sharpe, be not so short, Ile pay you, when I giue you mony. What and be vnder fifty? Faith sir, Warre hath so pinch't vs we must pawne. What sixepence an Egge, and two and two at an Egge. Fellowes bring out the hamper choose somew-what out o'th Stocke. What will you haue this cloke to pawne, what thinke you it's worth? You may see what it is to laugh at the Audience. Sir, (at few words we shar'd but xv. pence last weeke. Who are not are glad to bring such as they can get. Fall to worke after playing vnpossible. Sauing your sad tale, will you take a pot or two. Cunstable doe you know what you doe. Know you our credit with Sir Oliuer? Ile boldy fall to ballading againe. It's an ill winde blowes a man thus cleane out of ballading.

Plot — Histriomastix, or The Player Whipped
Under the benevolent rule of Peace, various orders of society turn to learning in their different ways: Mavortius and his fellow noblemen and gentlemen become students of the liberal arts, professional people appoint the scholar Chrisoganus as their lecturer, and some artisans organize themselves as a company of players, Sir Oliver Owlet’s Men. However, the new reign of Plenty tempts many of them away from their studies, leaving only Chrisoganus loyal. While the lawyers and merchants take advantage of the leisure of the vacation to go hunting, Mavortius entertains his guests with a play given by Owlet’s Men. Plenty is succeeded by Pride, who encourages a dedication to fashion and a neglect of proper decorum: the lawyers and merchants enrich themselves unjustly, Mavortius dismisses many of his servants to release more money to spend on pleasure, and both Chrisoganus (who has turned playwright) and Owlet's Men price themselves out of the market. Next, Envy comes to power, causing class resentment in the realm: the citizenry’s adoption of the latest fashions causes resentment among the nobility, envious behaviour disrupts the players’ rehearsal, and Mavortius becomes belligerent. The next ruler is War, under whom the players are conscripted as soldiers, Mavortius fights with his friends, the common people rise in rebellion against their social superiors, and the Spanish invade the realm. Out of this chaos comes Poverty, leading aristocrats, merchants. and peasants alike to debt, bankruptcy, and famine. Owlet’s men, unable to pay their taxes, are banished from the realm. Finally Poverty and her associates are expelled by the return of Peace and Plenty. Queen Elizabeth also appears, in the guise of Astraea, and the play ends with the five lesser rulers resigning their sceptres to Peace.
