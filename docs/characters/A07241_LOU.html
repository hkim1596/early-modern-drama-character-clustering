Louing Youth; Till what I purpose be put into act, Doe not o're-prize it, since you haue trusted me With your soules nearest, nay her dearest secret, Rest confident 'tis in a cabinet lock'd, Treachery shall neuer open, I haue found you (For so much to your face I must professe, How er'e you guard your modesty with a blush for't) More zealous in your loue, and seruice to me Than I haue beene in my rewards. Such your Gratitude calls 'em: Nor am I of that harsh, and rugg'd temper As some Great men are tax'd, with who imagine They part from the respect due to their Honours, If they vse not all such as follow 'em, Without distinction of their births, like slaues. I am not so condition'd: I can make A fitting difference betweene my Foot-boy, And a Gentleman, by want compell'd to serue me. I allow it; And to giue you assurance I am pleas'd in't, My carriage and demeanor to your Mistrisse Faire Margaret, shall truely witnesse for me I can command my passions. Why do you sigh? can you be doubtfull of mee? By that faire name, I in the warres haue purchas'd, And all my actions hitherto vntainted, I will not be more true to mine owne Honour, Than to my Alworth. So young, and iealous? Speake your doubts, and feares, Since you will nourish 'em in plainer language, That I may vnderstand 'em. Loue hath made you Poeticall, Alworth. Why shall I sweare? Suspend Your iudgement 'till the triall. How far is it 'T Ouerreach house? Sir, you meete your trouble. Your hand good Sir. I misse Sir your faire daughter, To crowne my welcome. You'le be obey'd Sir. As I liue a rare one. I am happy In such a scholler: but. You see faire Lady, your father is sollicitous. To haue you change the barren name of Virgin Into a hopefull wife. But o're your duty. Bend rather sweetest: Thinke of your yeares. Doe you thinke I am old? I can aduance you. Pray you trust my eare then. Ha! I heard some noise. Lady, I vnderstand you; And rest most happy in your choice, beleeue it, I'le be a carefull pilot to direct Your yet vncertaine barke to a port of safety. How! the Lady Alworth! Noble Lady, This is a fauour to preuent my visit, The seruice of my life can neuer equall. Your bounties are so great they robbe me, Madam Of words to giue you thankes. I waite on you Madam, farwell good Sir Giles. This parting kisse: you shall euery day heare from me By my faithfull page. Nay, doe not melt: This ceremoniall thankes to mee's superfluous. 'Tis he, oh here's your letter: let him in. You are an early riser, Sir Giles. And you too master Greedie, vp so soone? Pray you vse your pleasure. 'Tis a wholesome aire, And well built pile, and she that's mistresse of it Worthy the large reuennue. Impossible. I dare not owne What's by vniust, and cruell meanes extorted My fame, and credit are more deare to me, Than so to expose 'em to be censur'd by The publike voice. Are you not frighted with the imprecations, And curses, of whole families made wretched By your sinister practises? I admire The toughnesse of your nature. I hope that is past doubt now. Hee's gone, I wonder how the Earth can beare Such a portent! I, that haue liu'd a Souldier, And stood the enemies violent charge vndaunted To heare this blasphemous beast, a'm bath'd all ouer In a cold sweat: yet like a mountaine he Confirm'd in Atheisticall assertions, Is no more shaken, than Olimpus is When angry Boreas loades his double head With suddaine drifts of snow. No good Madam; For your owne sake I am glad you came no sooner. Since this bold, bad man, Sir Giles Ouerreach Made such a plaine discouerie of himselfe, And read this morning such a diuellish Matins, That I should thinke it a sinne next to his; But to repeat it. Which is the reason, that the politicke, And cunning Statesman, that beleeues he fathomes The counsels of all Kingdomes on the earth Is by simplicity oft ouerreach. May it to my selfe Proue so good Lady in my suite to you: What thinke you of the motion? You are too modest, And vnderualue that which is aboue My title, or what euer I call mine. I grant, were I a Spaniard to marry A widdow might disparage me, but being A true borne Englishman, I cannot find How it can taint my Honour; nay what's more, That which you thinke a blemish is to me The fairest lustre. You alreadie Madam Haue giuen sure proofes how dearely you can cherish A Husband that deserues you: which confirmes me, That if I am not wanting in my care To doe you seruice, you'le be still the same That you were to your Alworth, in a word Our yeares, our states, our births are not vnequall, You being descended nobly and alli'd so, If then you may be wonne to make me happy, But ioyne your lipps to mine, and that shall be A solemne contract. If I returne not with all tendernesse, Equall respect to you, may I die wretched. It is a thankefulnesse that well becomes you; You could not make choice of a better shape, To dresse your mind in. I am ready. Feare not I am your guard. How he fomes! Plot summary

Plot — A New Way to Pay Old Debts
Sir Giles Overreach’s schemes for the accumulation of lands and money include a campaign against his prodigal nephew, Welborne, and a plot to marry his daughter Margaret to Lord Lovell. Welborne has sold Overreach all his land to fund a prodigal lifestyle, and now Overreach’s agent Marrall has him ejected from his alehouse lodging: the objective is to force him into a choice between hunger and theft, and so procure his death either by starvation or on the gallows. Welborne goes to see the recently widowed Lady Alworth, who is refusing to see her many suitors, including Overreach’s associate Justice Greedy. Welborne forces his way in and persuades her, trading on his friendship with her dead husband, to entertain him and a guest to dinner. Welborne brings Marrall, who infers from their cordial reception that Welborne is to marry the widow. He is therefore likely to be worth more alive than dead, so Overreach changes his plans and lends him £1,000, with which Welborne pays his old debts. When Overreach attempts to close the trap and force him to sign away Lady Alworth’s lands, however, Welborne refuses, and insists that his own estate is wrongfully detained by Overreach. His uncle produces the deeds to prove otherwise, but Marrall, fed up with Overreach’s maltreatment, has tampered with them and they are invalid. To redeem his lost reputation, Welborne joins the army to fight for king and country. In the other plot, Lord Lovell goes to dinner at Overreach’s house, where another unexpected guest is Lady Alworth. Overreach plans to tempt him into sex with Margaret, so that he will then be obliged to restore her honour by marrying her. Margaret confides to Lovell that she prefers his page, Lady Alworth’s stepson, and, knowing that the attraction is mutual, Lovell agrees to help. He proposes that she should deceive her father with a display of amorousness, and that Alworth should be introduced into the equation as a carrier of love letters between them. Overreach agrees to this, is pleased to overhear that the first letter seems to solicit an elopement, and makes arrangements for his chaplain to perform the marriage. He is reeling from the failure of his Welborne scheme when the news arrives that the marriage has taken place — but the bridegroom has been Alworth, not Lovell. The extortioner goes mad and is sent to Bedlam, tormented by his conscience for his many financial misdeeds. Lovell asks Lady Alworth to marry him.
