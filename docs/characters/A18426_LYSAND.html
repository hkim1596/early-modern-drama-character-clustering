MOrrow Brother; Not readie yet? So much for the face. None, but Fortune send you well to weare it: for shee best knowes how you got it. Not all? A thing to bee heartily wisht; but brother, take heede you be not gull'd, be not too forward. May we not know't? The qualitie of it may bee such as a laugh will not be ill bestow'd vpon't; pray heauen I call not Arsace sister. How's that? how's that? But if this Deitie should draw you vp in a basket to your Countesses window, and there let you hang for all the wits in the Towne to shoot at: how then? All this sauors of the blind Goddesse you speake of. I am therein most confident. As for example. What of that? You lose time Brother in discourse, by this had you bore vp with the Ladie and clapt her aboord, for I knowe your confidence will not dwell long in the ser uice. It shall goe hard but weele heare your entertaine ment for your confidence sake. A good pleasant dreame. These diseases doe, brother, take heede of them: Fare you well; Take heede you be not baffeld. This brauing wooer, hath the successe expected; The fauour I obtain'd, made me witnesse to the sport; And let his Confidence bee sure, Ile giue it him home. The newes by this, is blowne through the foure quarters of the Cittie. Alas good Confidence: but the happinesse is he has a forehead of proofe; the staine shall neuer stick there whatsoeuer his reproch be. WHat? in discourse? What? my noble Lord? What meanes your Lordship then to hang that head that hath beene so erected; it knocks Sir at your bo some to come in and hide it selfe. I hope by this time it needes feare no hornes. What blanqueted? O the Gods! spurn'd out by Groomes like a base Bisogno? thrust out by'th head and shoulders? What has thy wits fine engine taken cold? art stuff't inth head? canst answere nothing? Now the Gods forbid that this opinion should run in a bloud. Dogges and Cats doe. Well Brother, in earnest, you haue now set your confidence to schoole, from whence I hope 't has brought home such a lesson as will instruct his master neuer after to begin such attempts as end in laughter. My confidence? in what? Wherein? Why brother know you ought that may impeach my confidence, as this successe may yours? hath your ob seruation discouered any such frailtie in my wife (for that is your aime I know) then let me know it. That's scarce a Brothers speech. If there be ought wherein your Brothers good might any way be question'd can you conceale it from his bosome? Then must I presse you further. You spake (as to your selfe, but yet I ouer-heard) as if you knew some dispo sition of weaknesse where I most had fixt my trust. I chal lenge you to let me know what t'was. Why? What then? I thinke none: I know not. Passing ill. No: doe you know cause to make me? I can discouer nothing in her lookes. Goe, Ile not be long. Ile follow you. SO now we are our selues. Brother, that ill relisht speech you let slip from your tongue, hath taken so deepe hold of my thoughts, that they will neuer giue me rest, till I be re solu'd what 'twas you said, you know, touching my wife. By truth it selfe you did: I ouer-heard you. Come, it shall nothing moue me, whatsoeuer it be; pray thee vn fold briefly what you know. You shall not leaue mee so vnsatisfied; tell mee what tis you know. Perhaps I shall. A deare experiment, then I must rise againe to bee resolu'd. All the Furies in hell attend thee; has giuen me a Bone to tire on with a pestilence; slight know? What can he know? what can his eie obserue More then mine owne, or the most piersing sight That euer viewed her? by this light I thinke Her priuat'st thought may dare the eie of heauen, And challenge th'enuious world to witnesse it. I know him for a wild corrupted youth, Whom prophane Ruffins, Squires to Bawds, & Strumpets, Drunkards, speud out of Tauerns, into'th sinkes Of Tap-houses, and Stewes, Reuolts from manhood; Debaucht perdu's, haue by their companies Turn'd Deuill like themselues, and stuft his soule With damn'd opinions, and vnhallowed thoughts Of womanhood, of all humanitie, Nay Deitie it selfe. I pray thee wife shew but thy selfe a woman; and be silent: question no more the reason of my iourney, which our great Viceroies charge vrg'd in this letter doth enforce me to. But let me wonder at this frailtie yet; would shee in so short time weare out his memorie, so soon wipe from her eies, nay, from her heart, whom I my selfe, and this whole Ile besides, still remember with griefe, the impres sion of his losse taking worthily such roote in vs; howe thinke you Wife? Plot summary

Plot — The Widow's Tears
Renouncing Fortune and devoting himself instead to the goddess Confidence, Tharsalio intends to woo the widowed Countess Eudora. His wooing technique is grossly sexual, and he also quarrels with a rival, Rebus. Rebuffed and mocked by his family, he seeks out Arsace and bribes her to advise Eudora not to marry him, but when he returns to her and throws out Rebus, he is rejected a second time. Finally, however, she accepts him, and their wedding is marked by a masque. ===== PAGE 180 ===== Lysander is concerned about his wife Cynthia’s constancy, and plans to test it by faking his own death: with the help of Lycus he undertakes a journey and sends back a report that he has been murdered by mountain bandits. His body is interred in the family monument; Cynthia and her waiting-woman go to live in the tomb in order to be near him. Lysander returns incognito, disguised as a soldier assigned as the sentinel of the cemetery. He successfully seduces Cynthia, but she insists that their liaison be conducted privately to avoid compromising her reputation. Tharsalio sees them kissing inside the tomb and takes revenge on the ‘sentinel’ by stealing one of the crucified bodies he is supposed to be watching. When this is discovered, Cynthia suggests disinterring Lysander’s body and substituting it for the stolen one. Lysander goes to fetch a crowbar, removes his disguise, and meets Tharsalio, who deduces what is happening and informs Cynthia. She is thus able to pretend that she knew who the ‘sentinel’ was all along. Lycus is arrested for Lysander’s murder, and the tomb is opened to test whether his presence will make the corpse’s wounds bleed; but the coffin proves to be empty, and the ‘sentinel’ is found hiding in the tomb. Both men are sent for trial by the Governor, recently appointed with a hard-line law-and-order policy. Eudora intervenes to explain the truth, and Lycus is released; after Tharsalio replaces the stolen body, so is Lysander. Tharsalio allows him to believe that Cynthia was never tempted to infidelity.
