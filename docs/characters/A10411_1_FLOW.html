Or you sell feathers brother. But are they not hang'd for it? Is not this flat conjureing, To make our Ghosts to walke ere we be dead? Impiety! O that men indued with reason Should have no more grace in them! I have heard our Vicar Call Play-houses the Colledge … of Transgression, Wherein the seven deadly sinnes are studied. It was a zealous prayer I heard a Brother make, concerning Play-houses: That the Globe Wherein (quoth he) reigns a whole world of vice, Had been consum'd! The Phoenix burnt to Ashes. The Fortune whipt for a blind whore: Blackfriers He wonders how it scapd demolishing I'th' time of reformation: lastly he wish'd The Bull might crosse the Thames to the Bear-garden, And there be soundly baited! Indeed it something pricks my Conscience, I come to sell 'em Pins and Looking-glasses. Pray sir what serve they for? And so impious? So unwarrantable? To live by sinne? The sight is able To unsanctify our eyes, and make 'em Carnall. I am content. That moralizing I doe approve, it may be for instruction. How did she say? a Masse? Brother fly hence, Fly hence, Idolatry will overtake us. See Brother, if mens hearts and Consciences Had not been sear'd, and cauterized, how could they Affect these filthy harbingers of hell! These Procters of Belzebub, Lucifers Hinch-boyes! I thought you taught two vices for one vertue! For pitty sake fret not the good old Gentleman. A good Doctrine marke it. Now shame take thee for A Luke warme formalist. Base belly-God, licentious Libertine! Insooth it is the common shore of lewdnesse. Now out upon thee thou lewd reprobate! Thou man of sinne, and shame, that sowest cushions Vnto the elbowes of iniquity. You shall have the devotions Of all the Elders. But this foppinesse Is weary some, I could at our Saint Antlings, Sleeping and all, sit twenty times as long, Pray sir continue still the moralizing. In sooth I ' have heard as wholsome instructions From a zealous wicker chaire, as e're I did From the carv'd Idoll of wainscoat. Indeed a Frock is not so Ceremonious. My Spirit within me too fumeth, I say Fumeth, and steemeth up, and runneth ore With holy wrath, at these delights of flesh. Stay brother, I doe find the spirit grow strong. I begin to think 'em lawfull recreations. Very good insooth—la And edifying. Now verily I find the devout Bee May suck the hony of good Doctrine thence, And beare it to the hive of her pure family, Whence the prophane and irreligious spider Gathers her impious Venome! I have pick'd Out of the Garden of this Play, a good And wholesome salad of instruction! What doe you next present? Will Vertues dance? I hate a Vertue in a Morrice-dance! O vile, absurd, may pole—maid-marrian vertue Who's this? Mother of Pearle I thinke, she is so gawdy She looketh like the Idoll of Cheap-side. They seeme to mee the Family of Love, But is there such a Glasse; good Roscius? Good Brother lets goe see it! Saints may want something of perfection. We will goe post to reformation. This ignorance even makes religion sin, Sets zeale upon the rack, and stretches her Beyond her length—Most blessed Looking-glass … That didst instruct my blinded eyes to day, I might have gone to hell the Narrow-way!

Plot summary

Plot — The Muses' Looking Glass
Bird and Mistress Flowerdew, Puritan residents of Blackfriars, bring their wares to the theatre, but are appalled when Roscius tells them they have been ordered for use as props in a filthy stage play. Roscius argues that they themselves make a living from the vice of vanity, and invites them to see the play before they condemn it; he will give them a running commentary on its moral import. Despite Roscius’ high claims, Comedy and Tragedy seem initially more concerned about the order of their precedence; they agree to spend a year in competition, staging examples of their respective genres, and whichever is adjudged the better will thereafter have the theatre to herself. The play is a series of episodes showing specific virtues to be the mean: an excess or a deficiency constitutes a vice. A lack of courtesy makes Dyscolus churlishly ready to pick quarrels, whereas too much of it makes Colax sycophantic, to such an extent that he hangs around for the rest of the play flattering all the other vices. Aphobus is over-confident and Deilus cowardly, afraid of his own shadow: they have respectively too much fortitude and too little. Temperance is the next example, illustrated by Acolastus the epicure and Anaisthetus the ascetic, followed by liberality, missing in Aneleutherus the miser and carried to excess in Asotus the prodigal. The over-ostentatious Banausus, full of extravagant schemes for public works, overdoes magnificence, whereas Microprepes balks at replacing a church steeple. (The Puritans in the audience 1630 approve.) Chaunus overvalues himself, and seeks inappropriate high offices, whereas Micropsychus thinks himself unsuited to even the meanest position. Meekness is quite absent from quarrelsome Orgylus, who persistently tries to provoke patient Aorgus to a fight, without success. The arrogant Alazon and the modest Eiron deviate from truth: one falsely claims personal qualities he lacks, the other denies those he does have. Philotimia is unduly fastidious about her appearance, whereas her slovenly husband Luparus would rather clothes had never been invented. The impudent bawd Anaiskyntia brings Kataplectus to visit Philotimia, but he is too bashful to speak to her in English. Philotimia appoints him her gentleman usher, and they go off together; it looks as if Luparus is going to be cuckolded, but he doesn’t care.
