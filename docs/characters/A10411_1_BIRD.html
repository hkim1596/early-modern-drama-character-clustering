Sister, were there not before Innes, Yes I will say Inns, for my zeale bids me Say filthy Innes, enough to harbour such As travell'd to destruction the broad way; But they 〈◊〉 more and more, more shops of Satan. Good workes are done. But th'bad abound, surround, yea & confound us ▪ No matveile now if Play-howses increase, For they are all grown so obscene of late That one begets another. Nay and I have heard That in a—Tragedy, I think they call it, They make no more of killing one another, Then you sell pinnes. Law growes partiall, And findes it but Chance-medly: And their Comedies Will abuse you, or me, or any body; We cannot put our monies to increase By lawfull Vsury, nor Breake in quiet, Nor put off our false wares, nor keep our wives Finer then others, but our Ghosts must walke Vpon their stages. Thats nothing M rs Flowrdew, they will play The Knave, the Foole, the Divell and all for mony. Be there not other Vocations as thriving, and more honest? Bailies, Promooters, Iaylors, and Apparitours, Beadles, and Martialls men, the needfull instruments Of the Republique; but to make themselves Such monsters? for they are monsters, th'are monsters, Base, sinfull, shamelesse, ugly, vile, deform'd Pernitious monsters ▪ Why then the City will in time be made An Vniversity of Iniquity. We dwell by Black-Friers Colledge, where I wonder How that prophane nest of pernitious Birds Dare roost themselves there in the midst of us, So many good and well disposed persons. O impudence! For Charity what is it? A good prayer. I have their custome too for all their feathers: 'Tis fit that we which are sincere Professors Should gain by Infidels. Plot summary

Plot — The Muses' Looking Glass
Bird and Mistress Flowerdew, Puritan residents of Blackfriars, bring their wares to the theatre, but are appalled when Roscius tells them they have been ordered for use as props in a filthy stage play. Roscius argues that they themselves make a living from the vice of vanity, and invites them to see the play before they condemn it; he will give them a running commentary on its moral import. Despite Roscius’ high claims, Comedy and Tragedy seem initially more concerned about the order of their precedence; they agree to spend a year in competition, staging examples of their respective genres, and whichever is adjudged the better will thereafter have the theatre to herself. The play is a series of episodes showing specific virtues to be the mean: an excess or a deficiency constitutes a vice. A lack of courtesy makes Dyscolus churlishly ready to pick quarrels, whereas too much of it makes Colax sycophantic, to such an extent that he hangs around for the rest of the play flattering all the other vices. Aphobus is over-confident and Deilus cowardly, afraid of his own shadow: they have respectively too much fortitude and too little. Temperance is the next example, illustrated by Acolastus the epicure and Anaisthetus the ascetic, followed by liberality, missing in Aneleutherus the miser and carried to excess in Asotus the prodigal. The over-ostentatious Banausus, full of extravagant schemes for public works, overdoes magnificence, whereas Microprepes balks at replacing a church steeple. (The Puritans in the audience 1630 approve.) Chaunus overvalues himself, and seeks inappropriate high offices, whereas Micropsychus thinks himself unsuited to even the meanest position. Meekness is quite absent from quarrelsome Orgylus, who persistently tries to provoke patient Aorgus to a fight, without success. The arrogant Alazon and the modest Eiron deviate from truth: one falsely claims personal qualities he lacks, the other denies those he does have. Philotimia is unduly fastidious about her appearance, whereas her slovenly husband Luparus would rather clothes had never been invented. The impudent bawd Anaiskyntia brings Kataplectus to visit Philotimia, but he is too bashful to speak to her in English. Philotimia appoints him her gentleman usher, and they go off together; it looks as if Luparus is going to be cuckolded, but he doesn’t care.
