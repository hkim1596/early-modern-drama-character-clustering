Why all the begging Mynſtrills by the way, He tooke along with him, and forc'd to ſtriue That he might ouercome, Imagining Himſelfe Imortall, by ſuch victories. But they were Neroes men, like Nero arm'd With Luts, and Harpes, and Pipes, and Fiddle-caſes: Souldyers to th ſhadow traynd, and not the field. I and anger too, Had you but ſeene him in his Chariot ryde. That Chariot in which, Auguſtus late His Triumphs ore' ſo many Nations ſhew'd, And with him in the ſame a Minſtrell plac'd, The whil'ſt the people, running by his ſide, Hayle thou Olimpick Conqueror did crye, O haile thou Pithian, and did fill the skie With ſhame, and voyces, Heauen would not haue heard. I doe commend in Seneca this paſsion; And yet me thinkes our Countries miſerie, Doth at our hands craue ſomewhat more then teares. Nothing is hard to them that dare to die. This Noble reſolution in you Lords, Heartens me to diſcloſe ſome thoughts that I — The matter is of waight and dangerous. Nay, Nay, although the thing be full of feare. Faith let it goe, it will but trouble vs, Bee hurtfull to the ſpeaker, and the hearer. Why ſhould I feare to tell them? Why is he not a Parricide, a Player? Nay Lucan is he not thine Enemie? Hate not the Heauens, as well, as men, to ſee That condem'd head: and you O righteous Gods Whither ſo ere you now are fled, and will No more looke downe vpon th'oppreſſed Earth; O ſeuere anger of the higheſt Gods, And thou ſterne power, to whom the Greekes aſsigne Scourges, and ſwords to puniſh proud mens wrongs, If you be more then names found out to awe vs, And that we doe not vainely build you alters, Aid that iuſt arme, that's bent to execute What you ſhould doe. Whoſe within I for as great matters, as we are about. Long want of loſſe hath made it dull, and blunt: See, Melichus, this weapon better edg'd. We ſhall haue blowes on both ſides, Melichus; Prouide me ſtore of cloathes to bind vp wounds; What an't be heart, for heart, Death is the worſt; The Gods ſure keepe it, hide from vs that liue How ſweet death is, becauſe we ſhould goe on And be their bailes: There are about the houſe Some ſtones that will ſtanch blood, ſee them ſet vp: This world I ſee hath no felicitie, Ile trie the other. Me thinkes, I ſee him ſtruggling on the ground, Heare his vnmanly outcries, and loſt prayers Made to the Gods, which turne their heads away. Nero, this day muſt end the worlds deſi es, And head-long ſend thee, to vnquenched fires. exit. If by the State, this gouernment you meane, I iuſtly am an enemy vnto it. That's but to Nero, you, and Tigellinus: That glorious world, that euen beguiles the wiſe, Being lookt into, includes but three, or foure. Plot summary

Plot — Nero (Piso's Conspiracy)
The Emperor Nero celebrates his latest triumph : not a military victory, but success in a singing and acting competition against the Greeks; he also has one of his critics executed. He gives a performance in the role of Orestes; the theatre is guarded so that the audience cannot escape, and those who do not respond with due enthusiasm are marked down for execution. Deciding that he has treated Rome too lenien:tly, he has the city burned down . The wind blows the fire back towards the palace, and his own possessions are destroyed. . Antonius loves _ the Empress Poppaea, despite Petronius' efforts to persuade him to settle for someone of lower rank. Poppaea refuses Antonius, preferring Nymphidius, who has cultivated her affections as a means to make himself Emperor. Scaevinus broaches --a conspiracy to assassinate Nero, thinking it demeaning to have _an Emperor so dedicated to artistic pursuits. Piso is recruited, and the plotters discuss the best way to assassinate Nero; but they are overheard by the freedman Milichus, who reports them to Nero. Piso commits suicide; other conspirators are arrested, and Nero institutes a purge of their sympathizers. Petronius is arrested and executed simply because Tigellinus has -a score to settle with him. A young man is executed merely for pitying Piso's death; Poppaea tries to save him because he resembles a former lover of hers, so Nero kicks her to death in a rage. News arrives from France that Vindex has revolted and chosen · Galba as Emperor. Nymphidius spreads rumours that Nero has resigned, and tries both to curry favour with Galba's agents and to buy military support for his own ambitions. Nero is condemned by the Senate and commits suicide . Nymphidius is killed by Galba's supporters.
