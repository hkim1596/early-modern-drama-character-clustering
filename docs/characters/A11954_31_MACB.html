So foule and faire a day I haue not ſeene. Stay you imperfect Speakers, tell me more: By Sinells death, I know I am Thane of Glamis, But how, of Cawdor? the Thane of Cawdor liues A proſperous Gentleman: And to be King, Stands not within the proſpect of beleefe, No more then to be Cawdor. Say from whence You owe this ſtrange Intelligence, or why Vpon this blaſted Heath you ſtop our way With ſuch Prophetique greeting? Speake, I charge you. Into the Ayre: and what ſeem'd corporall, Melted, as breath into the Winde. Would they had ſtay'd. Your Children ſhall be Kings. And Thane of Cawdor too: went it not ſo? The Thane of Cawdor liues: Why doe you dreſſe me in borrowed Robes? Glamys, and Thane of Cawdor: The greateſt is behinde. Thankes for your paines. Doe you not hope your Children ſhall be Kings, When thoſe that gaue the Thane of Cawdor to me, Promis'd no leſſe to them. Two Truths are told, As happy Prologues to the ſwelling Act Of the Imperiall Theame. I thanke you Gentlemen: This ſupernaturall ſolliciting Cannot be ill; cannot be good. If ill? why hath it giuen me earneſt of ſucceſſe, Commencing in a Truth? I am Thane of Cawdor. If good? why doe I yeeld to that ſuggeſtion, Whoſe horrid Image doth vnfixe my Heire, And make my ſeated Heart knock at my Ribbes, Againſt the vſe of Nature? Preſent Feares Are leſſe then horrible Imaginings: My Thought, whoſe Murther yet is but fantaſticall, Shakes ſo my ſingle ſtate of Man, That Function is ſmother'd in ſurmiſe, And nothing is, but what is not. If Chance will haue me King, Why Chance may Crowne me, Without my ſtirre. Come what come may, Time, and the Houre, runs through the rougheſt Day. Giue me your fauour: My dull Braine was wrought with things forgotten. Kinde Gentlemen, your paines are regiſtred, Where euery day I turne the Leaſe, To reade them. The ſeruice, and the loyaltie I owe, In doing it, payes it ſelfe. Your Highneſſe part, is to receiue our Duties: And our Duties are to your Throne, and State, Children, and Seruants; which doe but what they ſhould, By doing euery thing ſafe toward your Loue And Honor. The Reſt is Labor, which is not vs'd for you: Ile be my ſelfe the Herbenger, and make ioyfull The hearing of my Wife, with your approach: So humbly take my leaue. The Prince of Cumberland: that is a ſtep, On which I muſt fall downe, or elſe o're-leape, For in my way it lyes. Starres hide your fires, Let not Light ſee my black and deepe deſires: The Eye winke at the Hand; yet let that bee, Which the Eye feares, when it is done to ſee. Hee's heere in double truſt; Firſt, as I am his Kinſman, and his Subiect, Strong both againſt the Deed: Then, as his Hoſt, Who ſhould againſt his Murtherer ſhut the doore, Not beare the knife my ſelfe. I haue no Spurre To pricke the ſides of my intent, but onely Vaulting Ambition, which ore-leapes it ſelfe, And falles on th' other. How now? What Newes? I am ſettled, and bend vp Each corporall Agent to this terrible Feat. Away, and mock the time with faireſt ſhow, Falſe Face muſt hide what the falſe Heart doth know. A Friend. So is he mine: and in ſuch bloody diſtance, That euery minute of his being, thruſts Againſt my neer'ſt of Life: and though I could With bare-fac'd power ſweepe him from my ſight, And bid my will auouch it; yet I muſt not, For certaine friends that are both his, and mine, Whoſe loues I may not drop, but wayle his fall, Who I my ſelfe ſtruck downe: and thence it is, That I to your aſſiſtance doe make loue, Masking the Buſineſſe from the common Eye, For ſundry weightie Reaſons. Plot summary

Plot — Macbeth
With the aid of some treacherous noblemen, the Norwegians have attacked Scotland. In the ensuing battle, Macbeth is instrumental in expelling the invaders, and is created Thane of Cawdor. Before he learns of this honour, he is greeted with the title by three witches who tell him he is to be King; they also tell Banquo that, though he will not be King himself, his descendants will. King Duncan names his son Malcolm the heir apparent, and invites himself to Macbeth’s castle, where Macbeth murders him, abetted by his wife. The night is troubled by unnatural, portentous events. When the assassination is discovered, Malcolm and Donalbain flee the country; suspicion falls on them, and the succession on Macbeth. Macbeth is troubled by the witches’ prophecy about Banquo. He has Banquo murdered, but Fleance escapes; Banquo’s ghost then appears when Macbeth wishes him present at a feast. Hecate rebukes the witches for failing to involve her in their dealings with Macbeth. Disgraced after his failure to attend Macbeth’s feast, Macduff goes to England to rally military support against Macbeth, who has become an egregious tyrant. The witches show Macbeth three apparitions which warn him to beware of Macduff, but also assure him that he cannot be harmed by anybody born of a woman, and that he shall never be defeated until Birnam Wood comes to Dunsinane; he is also shown a vision confirming that Banquo’s heirs will rule Scotland. Learning of Macduff's defection, Macbeth has his wife and family killed; grief steels Macduff's resolve. Lady Macbeth begins sleepwalking, disintegrates psychologically, and dies, possibly by suicide. When the English army enters Scotland with Malcolm at its head, the thanes join it, but Macbeth puts his trust in the witches’ prophecies. They prove to have been misleading: the English army disguise their numbers by carrying branches from Birnam Wood as they advance on Dunsinane, and Macduff proves not to have been born of woman but prematurely ripped from his mother’s womb. Macduff kills Macbeth, and Malcolm is established as King of Scotland; he awards his thanes the new style of earls.
