To viſite you, ſo you be pleaſ'd to grace That viſite with a welcome, is a bleſſing No place has power to leſſen, it would make Hells ſaddeſt caue a faire Elyſium. Aemon neuer Was tax'd of flattery, nor will your worth Admit it; gentle Lady, be but pleaſ'd To thinke my heart ſpeakes in my tongue to you. Oh giue me leaue but to confeſſe my flame, Which neuer can be hid; a better fire More chaſt, more true, and full of conſtancy, (I dare maintaine it) warmes no breaſt on earth. No earthly power but ſweete Antigone Can ſentence me to bliſſe or endleſſe woe. Oh ſaue that creature that depends on you. Make me immortall by a faire returne Of grace from you and fauour. (That title, though I hated you, your worth Would challenge from my truth) I loue you better Then ſo to worke your ruine; Loue and wed-locke Haue ſtill beene fatall in our family. Plot summary

Plot — Antigone, the Theban Princess
Self-blinded and banished from Thebes, Oedipus wishes only for death. His daughter Antigone temporarily persuades him to give up such thoughts, but it is not long before he is dead. Thebes itself is at war: Eteocles has dishonoured a power-sharing agreement with his brother Polynices, and seven Greek champions have besieged the city in support of the latter’s claim. After sustaining heavy casualties, Polynices proposes settling the case in single combat with Eteocles. Both are killed. The new King, Creon, decrees that no Theban shall bury any enemy soldier, including Polynices. He also places Antigone under house arrest and refuses his son Haemon permission to marry her. In defiance of Creon, Antigone goes to the battlefield in search of Polynices’ body. There she meets his widow, Argia, and together they convey the corpse past the watchmen and give it a funeral. Creon too comes to the battlefield, to consult the witches who are gathering Parts of the dead bodies for magical use. They make a dead body tell his fortune: he will die soon, and disorder will come to Thebes. Antigone and Argia are arrested. Creon holds Argia, a foreign princess, for ransom, but sentences Antigone to be entombed alive and left to starve to death. By threatening to take his own life, Haemon persuades his father to allow her release, but it is too late: she has taken poison to hasten her death. Haemon kills himself. Thebes is attacked by Theseus, who has been summoned by another widow to intervene for the unburied. Argia is released and Creon killed, but Theseus allows him the burial he denied his enemies.
