A rape, a rape, a rape! Why what is't but a Rape to force a wench to marry, since it forces her to lie with him she would not? Verily, your grauitie becomes your perish'd soule, as hoary mouldinesse does rotten fruit. And verifie the Prouerbe. The nearer the Church; the further from God.—Poore wench. For thy sake, may his habilitie die in his appetite; that thou beest not troubled with him thou louest not. May his appetite moue thy desire to another man: so hee shall helpe to make himselfe Cuckold. And let that man be one that he payes wages too: so thou shalt profit by him thou hatest. Let the Chambers be matted, the hinges oyl'd, the curtaine rings silenced, and the chamber-maid hold her peace at his owne request, that he may sleepe the qui etlier. And in that sleepe let him be soundly cuckolded. And when hee knowes it, and seekes to sue a diuorce; let him haue no other satisfaction then this: Hee lay by and slept: the Law will take no hold of her, because he wink'd at it. That but mitigates The heate; rather imbrace and entertaine A younger brother; he can quench the fire. Vnder the protection of his present absence. Yes. Let me encounter you so; Ile perswade Your meanes to reconcile me to his loue. Within this halfe houre? Nothing yet; but I hope I shall.— And you very valiant; for you met mee at full Ca riere. A word or two, Madame? that you doe for mee, will not be contain'd in lesse then the compasse of two sheetes. But in plaine termes, shall wee take the opportunitie of priuate nesse? To dance the beginning of the world after the Eng lish manner. Fie. They dance it preposterously; backward. I can shake my heeles. Measure me from top to toe; you shall not finde mee differ much from the true standard of proportion. Now by the hand of M rcurie. My Annuitie. How would you ha'me liue? Yes. Sir, I confesse in particular respect to your selfe, I was somewhat forgetfull. Gen'rall honestie possess'd me. Inflict any punishment vpon me. The seueritie shall not discourage me, if it be not shamefull; so you'l but put mo ney i'my purse. The want of money makes a free spirit more mad then the possession does an Vsurer. Would you ha'me turne purse-taker? T'is the next way to doe't. For want is like the Racque; it drawes a man to endanger himselfe to the gallowes rather then endure it. What art thou? speake. The Spirit of Charlemont? I'le try that. 'Fore God thou sayest true, th'art all Spirit. You lye. A good honest fellow. Sir, I haue got a scratch or two here for your sake. I hope you'l giue mee money to pay the Surgeon. I thanke you sir.—Gen'rous libertie.—that is to say, freely to bestow my habilities to honest purposes. Me thinkes I should not follow that instruction now; if hauing the meanes to doe an honest office for an honest fellow, I should neglect it. Charlemont lyes in prison for a thousand Crownes. And here I haue a thousand Crownes. Honestie tels meet'were well done to release Charlemont. But discretion sayes I had much a doe to come by this; and when this shall be gone I know not where to singer any more: especially if I employ it to this vse, which is like to endanger mee into my Fathers per petuall displeasure. And then I may goe hang my selfe, or be forc'd to doe that, will make another saue mee the labour. No matter. Charlemont! Thou gau'st mee my life and that's some what of a purer earth then gold as fine as it is. T'is no courte sie I doe thee but thankefulnesse. I owe thee it and Ile pay it. Hee sought brauely, but the Officers drag'd him villanously. Arrant knaues! for vsing him so discourtcously; may the sins o'the poore people be so few, that you sha'not be able to spare so much out o'your gettings, as will pay for the hyre of a lame staru'd hackney to ride to an execution. But goe a foote to the gallowes, and be hang'd. May elder brothers turne good hus bands, and younger brothers get good wiues; that there be no neede of debt-bookes, nor vse of Serieants. May there be all peace but i'the warre, and all charitie but i'the Diuell; so that prisons may be turn'd to Hospitals, though the Officers liue othe beneuolence. If this curse might come to passe, the world would say, Blessed be he that curseth. Here. Take my sword.—How now my wilde Swag'rer? y'are tame enough now; are you not? Plot summary

Plot — The Atheist's Tragedy, or The Honest Man's Revenge
Montferrers has forbidden his son Charlemont from going to war and refused him the money necessary to equip himself. Charlemont gets a subvention from his uncle, D’Amville, and this persuades Montferrers to change his mind. But D’Amville is an atheist who believes that he must make his happiness in this world rather than wait for reward or punishment in the next, and he has an ulterior motive for wanting his nephew out of the way. Castabella loves Charlemont, and has privately agreed to marry him when he returns. They entrust their secret only to the Puritan Languebeau Snuffe. D’Amville bribes Snuffe to betray them and persuade Belleforest, her father, to make her marry Rousard, his son. Castabella is pressured into the match, despite the protestations of Sebastian, Rousard’s younger brother. As soon as he is married, Rousard falls ill with symptoms which include impotence. On the wedding day, D’Amville’s accomplice Borachio brings false news that Charlemont has been killed in battle. Montferrers makes a new will in D’Amville’s favour. D’Amville gets Montferrers’s torchbearers drunk at the wedding celebrations, and tricks them into quarrelling with one another when lighting their master home; the torches go out in the fight, allowing Borachio to murder Montferrers under cover of darkness. Montferrers’s ghost appears to Charlemont, tells him of the murder, and enjoins him not to take revenge. Charlemont returns to France to find himself presumed dead and his fiancée unhappily married. He confronts D’Amville and has an altercation with Sebastian, but another appearance by the ghost prevents him from getting carried away. D’Amville fabricates an unpaid debt in order to have Charlemont imprisoned, but Sebastian, who has been slightly injured, asks for money to pay a surgeon and uses it to pay the supposed debt and get Charlemont released. D’Amville takes steps to secure the future of his estate. Reasoning that Rousard will beget no son and that Sebastian, a libertine, will never marry, he decides that he will have to produce a bastard to be his heir, and selects Castabella as the mother. He commissions Borachio to murder Charlemont in the churchyard, but Charlemont kills Borachio instead. Snuffe uses the same churchyard for an assignation, intending to scare off any unwanted attention by disguising himself as the ghost of Montferrers, but he is himself frightened by Charlemont and runs away leaving the disguise behind.
