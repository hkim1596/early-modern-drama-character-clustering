Be aſſurd you may. What finde I heere? Faire Portias counterfeit. What demy God hath come ſo neere creation? moue theſe eyes? Or whither riding on the balls of mine ſeeme they in motion? Heere are ſeuerd lips parted with ſuger breath, ſo ſweet a barre ſhould ſunder ſuch ſweet friends: heere in her haires the Paynter playes the Spyder, and hath wouen a golden meſh tyntrap the harts of men faſter then gnats in cobwebs, but her eyes how could he ſee to doe them? hauing made one, me thinkes it ſhould haue power to ſteale both his and leaue it ſelfe vnfurniſht: Yet looke how farre the ſubſtance of my praiſe doth wrong this ſhadow in vnderpryſing it, ſo farre this ſhadow doth limpe behind the ſubſtance. Heeres the ſcroule, the continent and ſummarie of my fortune. You that chooſe not by the view Chaunce as faire, and chooſe as true; Since this fortune falls to you, Be content, and ſeeke no new. If you be well pleaſd with this, and hold your fortune for your bliſſe, Turne you where your Lady is, And claims her with a loving kis. A gentle ſcroule: Faire Lady, by your leaue, I come by note to giue, and to receaue, Like one of two contending in a prize That thinks he hath done well in peoples eyes: Hearing applauſe and vniuerſall ſhoute, Giddy in ſpirit, ſtill gazing in a doubt vvhether thoſe peales of praiſe be his or no, So thrice faire Lady ſtand I euen ſo, As doubtfull whether what I ſee be true, Vntill confirmd, ſignd, ratified by you.

Plot — The Merchant of Venice (The Jew of Venice)
Bassanio plans to woo the heiress Portia, hoping that, if successful, her wealth will get him out of debt. His friend and principal creditor, the melancholy merchant Antonio, agrees to finance the venture, but all his money is tied up in commercial activity. The Merchant of Venice Shylock is resentful of Antonio’s attacks on him for taking interest on loans, but frames a ‘merry bond’ which will entitle him only to a pound of Antonio’s flesh should the money not be repaid on the due date. Lancelot Gobbo leaves Shylock’s service and is hired by Bassanio, who is planning a going-away feast. Lancelot brings a letter from Shylock’s daughter Jessica to Lorenzo, whom she wishes to marry. Shylock is invited to the supper, and, while he is away, Lorenzo helps Jessica to escape from her father’s house; afterwards, it is unclear whether Shylock is more distressed by the loss of his daughter or the money she took with her. Bassanio leaves for Belmont, accompanied by Gratiano. Portia’s marital prospects are regulated by the will of her dead father: her suitors must choose which of three caskets contains her picture, and she may only wed the suitor who gets it right, while suitors who get it wrong must remain unmarried ever after. The Prince of Morocco chooses the golden casket, signifying a much-desired object, but gets only a death’s head. The Prince of Aragon chooses silver, signifying the suitor’s desert, and gets a fool’s head. Bassanio chooses lead, signifying that the suitor must risk everything, and gets Portia. She gives him a ring, which he promises never to remove. oo Rumours circulate in Venice that Antonio is ruined by the loss of all his ships. Unable to pay his debt to the vindictive Shylock, he writes to Belmont for Bassanio’s help. Lorenzo also comes to Belmont with Jessica, and, leaving her household in their care, Portia. secretly follows her new husband to Venice. Having acquired a disguise and a testimonial from a lawyer kinsman, she takes part in the hearing of Antonio’s case before the Duke. Shylock refuses either to accept the repayment of his money or to be merciful; but Portia points out that the bond entitles him to take no blood with his pound of flesh.
