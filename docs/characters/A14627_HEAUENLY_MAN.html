Building therwith to them ſelues a good foundation, That is to lay héer on Earth treaſure great ſtore: to purchace a kingdome that laſteth euer more. And I wil prouide to kéep thée from daunger and ſtrife: Onely ſeek thou to liue a godly and good life. If this be true as vndoutedly it is, What men are more wicked, wretched and miſerable: Then thoſe that in riches account their bliſſe, Béeing infected with Ambition that ſicknes vncurable. Though the Worldly man doo folowe their luſt, Crying on Earth is our felicitie and pleaſure: Yet God dooth ſo rule the harts of the Iuſt, That their ſtudy is, cheefly to get Heauenly treaſure. Alas why ſhould you not haue that in eſtimation, Which God hath prepared for his déer elect: Should not our mindes reſt in ful contentation, Hauing truſt in that treaſure moſte high in reſpect, Saint Paule whome the Lord ſo high dooth erect. Saith, it paſſeth the ſence, our memoryes and minde: Much leſſe can our outwarde eyes the ſame finde. As for the treaſure that you poſſeſſe héer, Through ficklenes of Fortune ſoon fadeth away: The greateſt of renown and moſte worthy Peer, Somtime in the end falleth to miſery and decay. But theſe be ſufficient plainly to prooue: How ſoon and vncertainly riches dooth remooue. Plot summary

Plot — Enough Is as Good as a Feast
Remembering his father’s bankruptcy, the Worldly Man hoards treasure for the future, seeing such behaviour as provident thrift, whereas the Heavenly Man relies on God to look after him and regards wealth as a distraction from spiritual things. He and Contentation persuade the Worldly Man to change to a godly lifestyle, but the vices, using innocuous aliases to inveigle their way into his confidence, cause him to backslide into covetous materialism. He becomes a miser and maltreats his tenants, servants, and hirelings: he raises rents, fails to pay the wages he owes, and seizes property adjacent to his own. After a bad dream about God’s punishment, he falls ill and dies. Satan carries him to hell on his back, advising the audience to live their lives in the same way. The virtues advise the contrary.
