Factum, ô fortuna, nequitèr, quod istum obiecisti jàm tàm inep tum, tàm importunum mihi. Etsi nequicquam placeat, ta mèn cùm inhumanam esse haud deceat; audiam quae loquatur. Aliam reperias quaeso, quam illudas; ego id agam sedulò sempèr, vt honesta sim, utcunque tibi videor. Praestaret domum reverti me, quàm hic irrideri facetijs. Quin potiùs auferas animam hinc denuò. Frustrà laboratis, meos enìm amores jam ante alter possidet. Mihi abeundum est, cùm vi deam vos irasci tantoperè. Convalescas ut lubet: me mul ta manent domi negotia. Tibi verò nulla sit quies, qui me vbique vexas. Vtinàm te oculi mei vel expi rantem jàm cernerent. Ego nec tu quid dicas, nec, ille me amet necnè, scio. Noui satis amores vestros, uno momento & incalescitis, & frige tis denuò. Esto: an itaque cuivis me ut dem, qui amat me? Dignissime Maecenas, con temnas tu istam, Rex tibi dabit conju gem praestantiâ dignam tuâ. Te verò mentis inopem, quae oblatum hoc res puis aurum; hic tibi omnia tanquàm virgulâ diuinâ suppeditasset. Hei! nonne, nonne, no Pedantj. Num mater tua chartis vesci solita, ut tu libros è mammis ejus sugeres? Eo quidèm minùs gratus hic mihi, acceptusque est, te quod habeat tàm ineptum adjutorem sibi. Quiescas quaeso tandèm, hoc que responsum feras, non posse me animum inducere meum te vt amem. Quare ne sis ampliùs posthac mihi molestus. Tandèm spero liberatam esse me importunitate tantâ stolidi hujus, ex quo coetera faciliora erunt omnia: vt sciat haec Crobolus meus, quam pri mùm curabo. Et ego certè Pedantium hunc, etsi rejeci saepè (quod solemus omnes, ubi amamus plurimùm) tamèn exosa sum nun quàm profectò. Sed metuo malè, ne (cum re pulsam toties tulerit) jàm demùm fri geat totus. Credo te hic excubias agere perpetuò, sic semper occurris mihi. Tu imperita es Tuscidilla, nec nôsti doctorum technas. Quid attinet reprimere diu tius quod nequit celari? Ego te (mi Pedanti) jàm diù quidèm amavi plu rimùm: sed, ut tuam certiùs fidem experirer, non ausa sum id profiteri. Nunc cùm te tentaverim satis, gaude, habe, accipe tuam tibi. Tibi quod gratum est, mihi & idem est gratissimum. Ego, ut hoc homine repudiato inertissimum illum Crobolum elige rem, Nunquàm faciam. Ad corvos? Crobole. Ego nisi te, volo neminem; sine quo vitam mihi acerbam ducam. Parvula quaedam restat nobis ista in re difficultas. Nam senex hic intùs decrepitus Charendas, nisi minas acci piat triginta, liberam me non di mittet. Triginta, nec obolo minùs. Nunquid tu Lydiam tuam minoris estimares? Magna certè res agitur: non solet iste ita diu deliberare, quicquid est benè coctum dabit. Tum ego dabo tibi me in per petuum, Pedanti.

Plot summary

Plot — Pedantius
Crobolus and the schoolmaster Pedantius are rivals. for the love of Lydia. Crobolus asks his servant Pogglostus to bilkk money from Pedantius and his friend Dromodotus. After failing with a hard-luck story, Pogglostus robs them at sword-point. Crobolus wins Lydia’s love by entertaining her to dinner. He is slow to pay his landlady for the meal, but assures her he can easily trick the money out of Pedantius. Through the parasite Tyrophagus, he lets Pedantius know that a former pupil, Leonidas, has secured a position at court, and intends to use his influence to get a royal appointment for his old tutor. On the strength of this, Pedantius is persuaded to loan Leonidas money. Tyrophagus is bringing the cash back to Crobolus when he is robbed by Pogglostus, who doesn’t know that he and his master are in league. Pedantius tries to woo Lydia, but has less success, partly because he does it using academic logic and partly because she has already accepted Crobulus. In any event, whoever wins her will need to pay her avaricious master to manumit her. She feigns interest in him after hearing of his supposed successes at court, and he tries to raise the money by offering to sell his books to the audience. The tailor Gilbert pursues him for payment of his bills, but Pedantius only gives him a string of classical authorities and quotations; Gilbert threatens to sue him. Pedantius sends Lydia money and a ring, but the news comes back that she is dead. She has sent him back his ring, and, with her last breath, told him not to grieve for her, and to leave the country. Assured of her love for him, Pedantius complies and goes into exile. With him out of the way, Crobulus is free to marry Lydia, who is not really dead, using the money he has tricked out of the pedant. |
