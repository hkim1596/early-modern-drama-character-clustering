Faith I haue a dozen at the least, and their deserts are all so good, I know not which I should loue most: and one last day did court me thus: O had my tongue the influence to lead thy faire thoughts as thy faire lookes do mine: then shouldst thou be his prisoner who is thine. I seeing my poore Gentleman likely to be drownd in the depth of Hellespont, deliuered him this verse to catch hold of: O be not faire, and so vnkinde: misshapen stuffe, is of behauiour boystrous and rough. What was a for a man? Why, a was a man for a wo man, what should a be? and yfaith he was a neate lad too, for his beard was newly cut bare; marry it showed some thing like a Medow newly mowed: stubble, stubble. What, i'st Sir Iohn Haue-little that gallant Knight that Courts delicate Ladies? spare not the sweate of my bo die, man was made to labour, vse my creation, women to bear, ile vse yours: Birds to flye, Fish to swimme, &c. And then sweares by my co ̄ science Lady, I esteem you as I doe money, which buyes euerye thing, and thats but like a Puncke, for euery man has to doe with it. His name good Maister Spark? hee's very welcome. O signeur, these Gentlewomen haue not long vsed your companie, yet you see how Tobacco hath alreadie refined their spirits. Truly signeur if you should haue as much fauour as you haue complexion, you would bee highly fauoured. Cheape signeur, nowe by the light of this day, I cannot loue you. As the clapper of a Mill, my Lord. I my Lord, but I hope it sayes nothing. With whom my Lord? If a hundred men in a company, mee't doth not the worthiest man amongst them, first take his place, and sit downe? And I am sure my Lord, where ere the bodie comes the taile first takes his place, and sits downe, and therefore I hope tis the worthiest part of the body. Or the foote trot to maintaine the head? If a were of my minde, a would thinke no poyson too good for you. I Fleir, and thou hast a couple of vertuous Mistres ses, O they are a couple of damn'd peeces, that will plot or counsell the death of two such worthie Gentle men. Which is that? You haue tolde a wise tale, fayth fir wee carrie none. If we were, I hope thou wouldest not wrong vs. O Signior Petoune, what newes? Faith Signior I am very sorry for my Lord. Plot summary

Plot — The Fleer
Before the start of the action, Piso deposed Antifront, whose daughters thereupon fled from Florence. Antifront now travels in search of his missing children. He arrives in London under the alias Fleer, and meets some gentlemen. One of them, Spark, gets him a job with two of his countrywomen, a pair of Florentine courtesans working in London. Antifront is dismayed to discover that they are his missing daughters. Two sisters, Susan and Nan, are interested in Spark and his friend Ruffle, who are among the courtesans’ many clients. They dress up as boys and try to get the gentlemen to employ them as pages; Spark and Ruffle refuse, and the ‘pages’ end up working for two other clients, Sir John Havelittle and Piso junior, the son of the Florentine usurper. ‘Fleer’ wants to engineer a marriage for his daughters, selecting Havelittle and Piso as the eligible bachelors. The daughters prefer Spark and Ruffle, but are refused; taking offence, they ask Piso and Havelittle to kill the gentlemen, and suggest poison as the safest method. The plot is overheard by ‘Fleer’ and the ‘pages’. ‘Fleer’ gets himself another job looking after an apothecary’s shop, and sells Piso and Havelittle a poison which is really a sleeping potion. Spark and Ruffle dine with Piso and Havelittle and apparently die. The hosts are at once arrested on suspicion of poisoning and are committed for trial. Fleer is sent on an errand to the senior judge, who is unable to attend through illness, and sends word back for the other two judges to proceed without him. Given a ring to authenticate the message, Fleer instead uses it to get himself deputized as the third judge. He produces a letter which establishes that the guilt extends to the daughters as well as the poisoners, and all four are sentenced to death. News arrives that Piso’s father has died, making him the new Duke of Florence; but-he tells the Florentines to recall the deposed Antifront instead, and offers to make amends for his father’s treason by marrying Antifront’s elder daughter. Antifront reveals his identity and says Piso can do just 1606 that; his younger daughter will marry Havelittle. He produces the murder victims, still alive, and bestows them on Nan and Susan.
