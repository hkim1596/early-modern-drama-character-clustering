One worde sweet nimph. No good sweete Count pardon me. Nay by thy sweete fauour do not interrupt mee. … ut vp thy pistol tis a most dangerous humor in thee. Oh is that all why see tis vp agayne, now thou shalt lee Ile come to her in thy humor, sweete lady I loue swee … wordes, but sweete deedes are the noble sowndes of a noble spaniard, noble by countrie, noble by valour, noble by byrth, my very foote is nobler then the head of another man, vppon my life I loue, and vpon my loue … iue liue, and so sweete N … ph I kisse your hand, why loe heere we are both, I am in this hand, and hee is in that handy dandy p … ickly prandy, which hand will you haue. Pardon me sir, pardon me. By thy sweete let me speake one worde with thee, I do not like this humor in thee in pistoling men in this sort, it is a most dangerous and stigmaticall humor, for by thy fauour tis the most sinest thing of the world for a man to haue a most gentlemanlike car … iage of himselfe, for otherwise I doe hold thee for the most tall resolute and accomplisht gentleman on the face of the earth, harke yee weele meete at Corrucus and weele haue a pipe of Tobacco adew adew. It is base and ridiculous. I will, I will presto and I wil follow thee.

Plot — 146
Jehovah sends John the Baptist to proclaim the coming of Christ in accordance with ancient prophecy. Having baptised Christ, John turns his attention to King Herod. The Pharisees, who feel threatened by John’s preaching, instigate an inquiry; but Herod is satisfied that he is not an evil man, and consents to see him. However, the Pharisees have an ally in Herod’s wife, Herodias: John has spoken against their happy but illicit marriage, and she fears being sent back to her despised first husband. Using her sexual allure, she persuades Herod to imprison John, but Herod will not put him to death. From his prison cell, John sends his disciples to find out whether Jesus is the promised saviour; they report back that he is. Herodias visits him, but cannot persuade him to recant. Herod celebrates his birthday with a feast. Tryphera’s dancing pleases him so much that he swears to give her whatever she desires. Herodias puts her up to asking for John’s severed head on a charger. Herod obliges; nobody tries to dissuade him. Jehovah declares that good will come of John’s death. Both Herodias and Herod feel guilty; Herod gives John’s body to his disciples for burial.
