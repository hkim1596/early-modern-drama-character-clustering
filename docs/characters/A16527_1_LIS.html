His death of ſorrow makes my ſoule the prey, Though many thought that I for it had long'd: For if by thoſe that he reſts bound t'obey, One can be wrong'd, then I indeede was wrong'd. And yet what ſuretie can you haue of her? Can Lawes binde them that are aboue the Law? It's hard t'eſtabliſh concord twixt the two, Where th'one muſt hate, and th'other alwayes feare. What can hir freedome and your peace procure? And would you do ſuch euill to ſhed her blood? The Macedonians will abhorre this wrong. But who ſhal haue the realme amidſt thoſe broils? So to poſſeſſe the realme you haue no right. This ſtate doth to it ſelfe an heire afford. The people all will grudge againſt your ſtate. And in their hearts they will deteſt you too. What though Olympias in a little ſpace, May loſe her powre, together with her breath? Yet there remaines another of her race, That is by nature bound t'auenge her death. Ile be your friend, yet wiſh you would refraine, From doing this; but ere you be vndone, Since that I by your guiltineſſe may gaine, Ile ſuffer that which I would not haue done. You from a dangerous yoke haue vs relieu'd, Which I ſuſpect we had experienc'd ſoone: And why then ſhould we labour to ſeeme grieu'd At that thing done, which we wiſh not vndone? No, ſince that all for ſoueraignety doe ſtriue, And haue once taſted what it is to raigne: Theres none of vs but rather die, than liue T'embrace a ſubiects ſeruile ſtate againe. And though perchance with Alexanders ſonne, If heire both of his fathers worth and ſtate: We might haue moſt reſpected places wonne, As ſpeciall pillars of the Princes ſeate. Yet though more great than others, as before, It would haue grieu'd vs, leſſe then one to fall: The fall from firſt to ſecond grieues one more, Then from the ſecond to the laſt of all. Our old renowne to vs had ruine brought, And would haue made vs odious to remaine: It's dangerous for a ſubiect to be thought, One that deſires, or yet deſerues to raigne. When any tempeſt threatned had his throne, He would haue ſought aſſurance at our coſt: For when that iealouſie hath ſeizd on one, The greateſt vertues are ſuſpected moſt. Yea though we could to quite our ſtate conſent, Vs from ſuſpition nought but death could purge: Still greatneſſe muſt turmoile, or then torment, If borne, a burden, if laide downe, a ſcourge. Thus Time the truth of all things doth proclame, Man is a craftie creature, hard to know, That can a face for euery fortune frame, No truſt in mortalles, nor no faith below. Whiles as our owne particulars doe moue, We what we wiſh for moſt, ſeeme to miſlike: And oft of others doe the courſe diſproue, Whilſt we want nought but meanes to doe the like. Then whilſt Perdiccas did attempt before To make the reſt that were his equalls thrall, Who than Antigonus deteſted more, Th'ambitious minde of one that would haue all? But ſince Perdiccas and his faction fell, Whom he as traitours to the ſtate purſude: He in his place ſucceeding to rebell, Hath what he ſeemde t'vndo againe renude. And yet I many a time haue muſde of this, How from the world he did Eumenes ſend.

Plot summary

Plot — The Alexandraean Tragedy
Alexander the Great is dead, poisoned by Cassander. He leaves behind a pregnant widow but no definite heir. Unable to get into the underworld because as yet unburied, his ghost returns to earth and recounts his life story, a tale of escalating atrocity as power progressively divorced him from ordinary humanity. He decides to inspire his captains with ambition and bloodlust. The captains are unable to agree on how best to secure the Macedonian Empire, and various proposals are advanced: to await the birth of Alexander's posthumous child, and appoint a regent in the interim; to crown either Alexander’s bastard brother or his elder son, who is half-barbarian and so unacceptable to the people; to split up the empire between themselves. The only thing they can agree on is that the common foot-soldiers should have no say in the decision. Lysimachus and Seleucus decide to bide their time, let their fellow captains wear themselves out with wrangling, and then step in to take the empire for themselves. Meleager tries to have Perdiccas, his rival, murdered, but the plot fails and Perdiccas responds in kind with more success. When Alexander’s child is born, it is a son. Perdiccas and others become his joint regents, but Perdiccas schemes for personal supremacy. He removes his two colleagues in the regency, gives the other captains their own kingdoms in an effort to secure their loyalty, and plans to marry into the royal family. Two of his new subject kings, Antigonus and Ptolemy, ally themselves against him. He accepts the advice of Eumenes to deal with the rebels first before engaging with dynastic matters, but is murdered by his own soldiers during the campaign. Olympias, Alexander’s mother, plots against Cassander, but Cassander besieges her and, starving, she has to give herself up. She refuses his mercy and is executed. Cassander then procures the murder of Alexander’s remaining family. Antigonus secures the support of Eumenes, but then has him treacherously killed. The four remaining captains (Cassander, Ptolemy, Lysimachus, and Seleucus) agree to unite against Antigonus. Philastrus prophesies continuing strife: Cassander will die in disgrace, Seleucus will kil Lysimachus and then die himself, and Ptolemy’s line will ultimately end with a lustful queen who will see his empire fall into the hands of the enemy.
