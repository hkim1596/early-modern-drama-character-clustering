O Cloris, here haue thou and I full oft Sate and bene mery, in this shadie Groue. Here haue we sung full many a Rundelay, Told Riddles, and made Nosegayes, laught at loue, And others passions, whilst my selfe was free, From that Intollerable miserie, Whereto affection now inuassells me. Now Cloris I shall neuer more take ioy To see, or to be seene, with mortall eye, Now sorrow must be all my companie. I am vndone Cloris, let that suffice. O Cloris if thou be, as once, I was Free, from that miserable plague of loue, Keepe thee so still, let my affliction warne Thy youth, that neuer man haue power, to moue Thy heart to liking, for beleeue me this, They are the most vnfaithfull impious race Of creatures on the earth; neuer beleeue Their protestations, nor their vowes, nor teares, All is deceipt, none meanes the thing he sweares. Trust a mans faith? nay rather will I goe And giue my selfe a prey to Sauage beasts, For all they seeke, and all they labour for, Is but t'vndoe vs, and when that is done, They goe and triumph on the spoile the'haue wonne. Trust men, or take compassion when they grieue, O Cloris tis to chearish and relieue The frozen Snake, which with our heat once warmd, Will sting vs to the heart in recompence, And ô no maruaile tho the Satyre shund, To liue with man, when he perceiu'd he could, With one and the same breath blow heat and cold. Who would haue euer thought Palaemons othes Would haue prou'd false? who would haue iudgd the face That promisd so much faith, and honestie, Had bene the visor but of treacherie? 'Tis possible, Palaemon is vntrue. O Cloris if thou didst but know how long, And with what earnest suite, he sought my loue, What vows he vsd, what othes, what teares among What shewes he made, his constancie to prooue, You would admire: and then againe to see How I although I lou'd him with my heart Stood out, and would by no meanes vrged be, To shew the least affection of my part. For I had heard that, which (ô now too well) I finde, that men were cunning, and would not Regard the thing that easily was got. And therefore I would trie him, and not seeme His vowes, nor protestations to esteeme, At length one day, here in this selfe-same place, (Which I shall euer, and good cause I haue To thinke on whilst I liue) walking with me, After he had vrgd me most earnestly: O Siluia, said he, since nor othe, nor vow, Nor teares, nor prayers, haue the power to moue, Nor all that I can doe, can make thee know How true a heart, I offer to thy loue; I must trie some way else to shew the same, And make thy vndiscerning wilfull youth Know, though too late, (perhaps vnto thy shame) Thy wayward error, and my constant truth: When thou maist sigh, and say in griefe of minde, Palaemon lou'd, and Siluia was vnkinde. With that wringing my hand, he turnes away, And though his teares would hardly let him looke, Yet such a looke, did through his teares make way, As shew'd how sad a farewell there he tooke. And vp towards yonder craggie Rock he goes, His armes incross'd, his head downe on one side, With such a mournfull pace, as shewd his woes Way'd heauier then his passions could abide: Faine would I haue recald him back, but shame, And modestie could not bring forth his name: And faine would I haue followed, yet me thought It did not fit the honour of a maide To follow one, yet still I sent from me, T'attend his going, feare, and a carefull eye. Plot — The Queen's Arcadia (Arcadia Reformed)
Arcadia is no longer the place of pastoral simplicity it once was: old shepherds have grown litigious, young ones are falling in love unwisely and unrequitedly, and the nymphs, who seem less beautiful than before, have abandoned the modest simplicity of traditional Arcadian dress in favour of quirky new fashions. The problems arise from foreign influences on this previously sequestered land. Colax has travelled abroad, and brought back with him the city woman Techne, and they are selfishly interfering in the love- lives of the nymphs and shepherds: they have made Silvia and Palaemon jealous of one another with mendacious tales of infidelity; and Colax has persuaded Dorinda to jilt Myrtillus in his favour, making her the latest in a string of sexual conquests. Meanwhile Lincus, a lawyer, and Doctor Alcon have both come from town to set up practices in Arcadia. Lincus has found the local amity bad for business: the Arcadians do not covet one another’s property and so never go to law. For Alcon, however, the Arcadians’ fellow-feeling is profitable, because when one falls ill, others follow suit, and they all want the same medicine from him — which is just as well, because he has only one to offer, and that is a placebo. Both are keen to drum up trade, so Lincus has set Montanus and Acrisius against one another, and Alcon has introduced the Arcadians to an unhealthy habit: tobacco-smoking. Carinus (who has recently jilted Amaryllis) and Amyntas are rivals for the love of Cloris, and argue about which of them has the greater command of her affections; but in fact Cloris loves neither of them, and has sworn chastity until she finds a worthy man. Colax now wants a change of girlfriend, and asks Techne to make Cloris love him. Techne offers to give Cloris a new outfit, and arranges for them to meet at a cave so she can try it on; but it is Colax who keeps the appointment, and attempts to rape her. Techne herself has fallen for Amyntas, and arranges for him to see Cloris meeting Colax. Amyntas becomes suicidal, so Techne sends Cloris to stop him; but she arrives too late, and he has already taken poison.
