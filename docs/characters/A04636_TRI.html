I thinke him a prophane person, indeed. Good Brother, we must bend vnto all meanes, That may giue furtherance, to the holy cause. Not alwaies necessary. The Children of perdition are, oft times, Made instruments euen of the greatest workes. Beside, we should giue somewhat to mans nature, The place he liues in, still about the Fire, And fume of Mettalls, that intoxicate The brayne of Man, and make him prone to passion. Where haue you greater Atheists, then your Cookes? Or more prophane, or cholerick then your Glasse-men? More Antichristian then your Bell-founders? What makes the Diuell so diuelish, I would aske you, Sathan, our common enemy, but his being Perpetually about the fire, and boyling Br stone, and Arsnike? We must giue, I say, Vnto the motiues, and the stirrers vp Of humors in the blood. It may be so. When as the Worke is done, the Stone is made, This heate of his may turne into a zeale, And stand vp for the beauteous discipline, Against the menstruous cloth, and ragg of Rome. We must awayt his calling, and the comming Of the good Spirit. You did fault, t' vpbraid him With the Brethrens blessing of Heidelberg, waighing What neede we haue, to hasten on the Worke, For the restoring of the silenc'd Saints, Which ne'er will be, but by the Philosophers Stone. And, so a learned Elder, one of Scotland, Assur'd me; Aurum potabile being The onely med'cine, for the ciuill Magistr te, T'incline him to a feeling of the cause: And must be dayly vs'd, in the disease. Let vs call on him, then. Sir, be appeased, Hee is come to humble Himselfe in Spirit, and to aske your patience, If too much zeale hath carried him, aside, From the due path. The Brethren had no purpose, verely, To giue you the least greeuance: but are ready To lend their willing hands, to any proiect The Spirit, and you direct. And, for the Orphanes goods, let them be valew'd, Or what is needfull, else, to the holy Worke, It shall be numbred: Here, by me, the Saints Throw downe their purse before you. I, 'tis very praegnant. Verely, 'tis true. We may be temporall Lords, our selues, I take it. I pray you, Sir. Let me finde grace, Sir, in your eyes; The man He stands corrected: neither did his zeale (But as your selfe) allow a tune, some-where. Which, now, being to'ard the Stone, we shall not need. Minde him not, Sir. I doe command thee, Spirit (of zeale, but trouble) To peace within him. Pray you Sir, goe on. Truely, Sir, they are Wayes, that the Godly Brethren haue inuented, For propagation of the holy cause, As very notable meanes; and whereby, also, Themselues grow soone, and profitably famous. Peace. Ananias. It is an ignorant zeale, that haunts him, Sir. But truely, else, a very faithfull Brother; A Botcher: and a man, by reuelation, That hath a competent knowledge of the Truth. But how long time, Sir, must the Saints expect, yet? What will the Orphanes goods arise to, thinke you? How! Can you so? I, but stay This act of coyning, is it lawfull? Ha? you distinguish well. Casting of money may be lawfull. Truely, I take it so. I'll make a question of it, to the Brethren. The dores are shut against vs. You shall doe well. Profane as Bel, and the Dragon. Peace, Deacon Ananias. 'Tis well, the Saints shall not loose all yet. Goe, And get some Carts— Be patient Ananias.

Plot — The Alchemist
With his master absent to avoid the plague in London, Face has set up an occult consultancy in his house in association with the formerly itinerant rogues Subtle and Doll Common: Face fetches in the clients and Subtle poses as the alchemist. They quarrel over the relative merits of their contributions to the enterprise, but resolve to collaborate competitively in the day’s ===== PAGE 80 ===== business. They are visited by successive clients: Dapper, who wants a familiar to help him win at gambling; Drugger, who wants advice on the most auspicious layout for his shop; Sir Epicure Mammon, who seeks the philosopher’s stone to fulfil his fantasies of wealth, luxury, and voluptuousness, and who brings along with him the sceptic Surly; and the religious sectarians Ananias and Tribulation, who want both the stone and Subtle’s claimed expertise in counterfeiting foreign coins. Dapper is persuaded that he is the Fairy Queen’s nephew and heir, and is promised an introduction to her. Mammon brings various domestic articles for aurification, which Subtle then sells on to Ananias and Tribulation, saying they belong to orphans. Clients come thick and fast. Drugger introduces his neighbours, Kastril (who wants te learn how to quarrel) and the wealthy widow Pliant (who wants her fortune told and whom Face and Subtle both want to marry). They are sent away so that the tricksters can deal with Dapper, who in his turn is secreted in the privy while they attend to Mammon. The knight courts Doll, who is presented to him as a lord’s sister prone to religious mania, which he activates by ill-chosen words; but he is then told that his lust has caused a setback in the alchemical process, which demands moral purity. Meanwhile, Surly returns, disguised as a Spanish count seeking sexual favours; but, since Doll is busy with Mammon, he is foisted on Dame Pliant, who is tempted with the chance of becoming a Spanish countess. His attempt to tell her the truth is outmanoeuvred when Face and Subtle convince her that he is an impostor and that the real Count is on his way; Drugger is persuaded to impersonate him and sent off to borrow a Spanish suit for the role. Lovewit, the master of the house, returns to hear Tumours of strange goings-on. Face’s explanation is confuted by the cries of Dapper, forgotten in the privy, so he confesses everything.
