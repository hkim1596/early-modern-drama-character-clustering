Most excellent of Women, that you are worthy All that your hopes can lodge in you, I graunt: But Fortune and the Graces were at ods When you were gotten, else you had bin made As great as Dignitie can make a Woman. But yet you know (I pray forgiue my plainenesse) That in Estate you donnot equall me, If that were it I lookt at. Why you mistake me, if you thinke I weepe. Somthing has toucht my eye, that do's offend it. Not so good Shuttle-cocke, your pointed wit stabbes desperately at all times. Looke you, 'tis thus: If you can loue me, faire one, so; if not— Yes. I cry you mercy. How Sir? You spoke something like comfort, Sir. You are a welcome man, pray shew how. Prythy away. I will pledge you. Now Sir will you speake? You amaze me. Yes very true I find it: but for the cure of this? Wud thou wert gone. What does this fellow talke? O that it were so; by my Fathers Soule I wud be free as that is, and as happie, For I wud laugh this Woman from my heart, And she shud be no more to me, then her I know not, I wud be so much Man, and something more: For I wud wish to enioy her as a Man, Lose her in mind, and find her in my bloud, And I doe feele such turnings in my thoughts. See her, I and speak to her, and call her Woman, There were no Treason in't, were it? Good; then let vs goe: for what Ile doe till I come there, I know not, or if I did, I wud not vt ter it. Nay I wil haue speech with her, & tell her roundly How she ha's play'd the Murdresse with a man That would haue dy'd most willingly for her. No, but somewhat vehement. I donnot come to taske you to your word, But to behold the Riches I haue lost, That must confesse my selfe vnworthy of you. Hither I came resolu'd, to let you know That I durst looke vpon you, and could lose you Without sigh or teare: but hauing seene you, You haue afresh kindled the fire againe, And I must die a Martyr in your loue. If those had not bin witnesses sufficient, Why did you not enioyne me to a Taske, Harder then euer Crueltie did yet? You should ha' said; I dare thee Dorilus To die: or doe but say so yet, That I may be assured you can loue Dorilus dead, whom liuing you despis'd, And I will at your feet yeeld my lat'st breath. Let it be dangerous, and 'tis the better, And I shall easilier accomplish it. Pray speake it. A Garland of all Flowers? This is a Taske indeed. But it is nothing, when the Prize is thought of that it shall purchase. I beleeue you: Faire farwell. How doe you Sir? you appeare to me more troubled then my selfe. I must goe trauaile. To gather Flowers, Strawberries— I was a telling you: She has enioyn'd me to bring her a faire Garland, of all Flowers that can be nam'd, which must continue fresh as long as I remaine constant to her. This is her imposition. I thought you Sir of more humanitie, And better breeding, then to mocke miserie. All, and become in debt to pay that Man. Better then three hundred pound a yeere. Without those wise Considerations, You instantly shall haue it made to you, Not in words onely, but in deeds, If you performe what you propose to me. Most ioyfully, and with what speed you please. Dutie ha's taught me, though you were not her Fortune ha's made you: Euery thing delights To be commanded by you; vnder your wish Lyes all things vpon Earth, and growes for you, Prospers for your sake, striues to be louely, In emulation of your Excellence. Here is a Garland of those Flowers you spoke of, Which Nature put forth, and Art fetcht for you; Place it vpon your Head, and it shall dim The glorious splendor of your other Crowne: Lasting it is, as it; for it shall flourish According to your wishes, till I alter And change the course of my Affection. I shall. I did Sir, and a great one: nor left there, But added two or three to that; the least was, The blasting of this Beautie he ador'd. How many wayes doe you desire to kill me? You say the Duke's acquainted— Why doe you abuse me thus? What? to enioy you? He did it then to giue vs leaue to sinne, That he might punish. VVhat doe we then contriuing Lust together? That Rebell Bloud, which rageth against him, Had better bin bestow'd vpon rude Beasts, Wilder then VVolues or Tygres; we are worse: They that wud wrong this truly Royall Prince, This Prince of his owne Passions, as of Men, Deserue to see no day, to tast no foode, No cloathes to shield him from the rage of winter, But liue more wretched then the last of Beggers, Die without teares or prayers, and want a graue. Take your vowes backe againe, and place 'hem better, For here I make a generall release Of all debts twixt vs, be a free woman, And set vp anew, with caution, that you neuer Proue banker-out againe, deale not so largely, Nor trust so prodigally, lest you do meete VVith such as will take the full forfeiture; So Vertue guard you and your goodnesse Crowne, Your thoughts and actions with true chastitie. Yes, very gracious. No more. Heare me first. Because you may not spread a Calumnie, Which when y'are not your selfe, Wine may bring forth: Here I protest by all that's Righteous, Both in Earth and Heauen, though I had bought And payd for this Sinne as your selfe can witnesse, And might haue purchas'd it (but with her Curses) I gaue her Vowes backe freely to her selfe, Made her the same Duchesse that she was, And is still absolute. Freely: and had my Life bin in the Bargaine, It had gone, And Wife and Children, If I had bin worth 'hem—O you donnot know, And it is tedious to relate it to you. Pray stay Sir, and make me not so wretched As leaue that vgly sinne Ingratitude For my Companion: I may through your friendship Thinke this life happy, to score age vpon me, And die, with Stories of you to my children. Bernard my friend, and chamber-fellow. O, your zeale Sir hath bin euer showne, Why here man, I am here. Yes. Why? I see thee plaine as I did before, And euery thing else. Why sister I am well? next vnder Heauen I prayse this Gentleman. Here sister iust before you. Yes, in the very mouth of you, as a man wud say Trust me I'm glad of it, Ile talke the bolder and and the lowder, they shall heare me. Stay. Stay I say. Heare me ye ministers of Iustice, And thou Patron of it and Truth, That comes to you for succour, and for safety. The All preseruer that guards Innocence, And often lets it pine, but neuer perish, Can raise a voice from stones, or trees, or windes, To pleade the cause that neds no Eloquence. What hath this Lady done that here you bring her Vnto a Barre of shame? It ist for being vertuous, Because she hath bin constant to her Lord: But some haue cald her chastitie in question, That neuer had a sparke in their owne Breasts, And haue possest the Duke from his owne thoughts, That she must needs be bad because they are so. Has euery woman so much wit to hide That fault especially? and had not shee, Who is the mirror of of her sexe for that, More then for Beauty? But she told the Duke She had offended in an idle promise, And that's obiected to her for a crime; A peece of impudence vnparalleld. When had she meant to haue wrong'd her royall Lord, She wud haue lockt the secret in her heart, And set her face with smiles, beaten with kisses Suspition from his bosome; but she discouered The guist, the giuer, and the vow that bought it. Who ist not knowes, dishonestie consisteth In the deed done, not in the spoken word? And she imagining the deed in which She imployed the doer, neuer to be done, And so the wickednesse. Let me not be thought tedions Nor be offensiue, if I bring to minde The meeting of those two, how she reuealed, That you great Duke consented to the fact, Encourag'd her vnto't, and led her on, If she had bin a villaine to her death. But he looking on her, and seeing in her eyes, The image of faire Vertue how it wept, Gaue backe her vowes, and freed her from his heart, In that shape first she stood, but againe plac'd her As his most roy all mistresse, and your wife. It hath bin still a maxime, she's not chast That hath not bin attempted, but she who hath stood Temptation, and resisted. Gold is the purer For being tryde; and Vertue put in act, Appeares more glorious, when it hath wrought it selfe Out of those troubles which would stifle it. Heauen was assaulted by the Giants once, Which shewd Ioues powre the greater: the pensiue soule Fighting with sin, the diuell, and with death, After the victory, triumphs and sings Eternally amongst the blessed Angels, Crown'd with perpetuall Peace and happinesse: But she for being vertuous must die, For conquering her affections, for louing Sincerely, and effectually, her Lord; For hauing not the cunning to dissemble, But for being Simple, Chast, and Innocent, Iust, Noble, Beautious, Excellent in all, Saue what no common woman wud haue mist. For this, and for this onely she must die. Now talking with you, plucking off this gloue, It fell out of my hand. How? let it alone. Farewell my Lord. Bernard sneaks into his tutor Landoff’s study, reads his magic books, and attempts to conjure up the devil. The spirit who appears is in fact Landoff himself in disguise: he has been trying to. find out why his pupil is melancholy, and left his study door unlocked so that the prospect of magic might cause him to reveal the answer. It does: Bernard wants to go home because he has fallen for a local girl, Julia, but has not yet plucked up the courage to tel her. Dorilus tries to court the proud, ambitious Dorigen, but she has her sights set on becoming a duchess. She asks that he make her father an earl, and, once that is done, she points out that there is no impediment of rank preventing him from 1619 marrying her. He does so, but Dorilus persists in his attentions after the wedding. She promises to reciprocate if he fulfils an impossible task: to bring her a garland containing every species of flower, which will remain fresh so long as he is constant in his love. Landoff, still posing as a spirit, agrees to fetch him the blooms, and Dorilus gladly signs away his estate to Bernard by way of payment. Presented with the garland, Dorigen is disconcerted to realize that she will have to keep her side of the bargain. She confesses to the Duke, who seems unconcerned at the prospect of her adultery, but sets Raymond to spy on her. Dorilus refuses to press his claim on Dorigen, but Raymond reports that adultery has occurred: he has become infatuated with Julia and thinks his chances will be enhanced by the fall of Dorigen. Dorigen is imprisoned for treasonable adultery and her family expelled from the court; she is to be allowed no advocate to speak for her at her trial. Dorilus procures an invisibility ring from Landoff, supplied by a genuine spirit, and intervenes in the trial after Dorigen refuses to defend herself. The Duke accepts that his disembodied voice was sent by providence to exonerate an innocent woman, and Dorigen is freed. However, Raymond persuades him to change his mind, and Dorigen is taken back to prison.
