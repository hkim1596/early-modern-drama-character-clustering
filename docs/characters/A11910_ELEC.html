¶By treason that mi mother wrought my father lyeth slayne, And drinkyng of their fathers cup the chyldren do complayne. Aegist engroceth Castels got by fornication. ¶I thee request euen for the loue my father thou doest owe, And for the honour of the crowne, whose brute abrode doth growe In euery coast: and by the gods that dyuersly do eal, Take into thy tuicion, conuey away, and steal, Thys poore Orest : such kynd of theft is pietie indede. ¶He is escapt and gone, and with vnmesurable myght The Chariot horse with rayne at will do send out of my syght. Now free from peryll on my foes attendance will I make. And offer willingly my head the deadly wound to take. The cruell conqueress of her spouse is come, whose spotted wede With sprynkels (signe of slaughter) do beare record of her dede. Her gory handes new bathed in blood as yet they be not drye, Her rough & churlish rigorous lookes the facte do notyfye. Unto the temple will I trudge. Plot summary

Plot — Agamemnon (Seneca's Eighth Tragedy)
The ghost of Thyestes describes his crimes and foretells the death of Agamemnon. During her husband’s long absence at the Trojan war, Clytemnestra has had an affair with Aegisthus; but now Agamemnon is coming home. She tells Aegisthus her doubts about their liaison, but when he proposes suicide, she agrees to join him in finding another way to dispose of their problems. Agamemnon arrives after a stormy crossing from Troy, bringing Cassandra with him as a prisoner. She foretells his murder by Clytemnestra, which duly comes to pass. Clytemnestra wants to kill her children 1566 too, but Orestes has escaped, and Aegisthus is reluctant to behead Electra; instead she is imprisoned in a distant dungeon. Cassandra exults in the overthrow of Troy’s conqueror, and predicts Clytemnestra’s downfall. She is beheaded, but Orestes survives to avenge his father’s death.
