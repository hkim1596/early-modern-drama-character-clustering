Because I will not be drunke for any mans pleasure. Tis good English now, t'was Dutch, may bee you ha some conspiracy vpon mee. Not, not, good Whible if you vrge agen I shall sus pect. And beate you Whible if you be not satisfied. Dost tempt me? This whelpe has some plot vpon men, I smell pow der my young Mistris would haue blowne vp my braines this Peter-gunner should haue given fire, 'tis not the first time shee hath conspired so but two not doe, I was neuer yet couzned in my life, and if I pawne my braines for a bot tle of Sacke, or Claret, may my nose as a brand for my negli gence carry euerlasting Malmesey in it, and be studded with Rubies and Carbuncles, Mistris you must pardon my offici ousnesse, be as angry as a Tyger I must play the Dragon and watch your golden fleece, my Master has put me in trust and I am not so easily corrupted. I ha but two eyes Argus had a hundred, but hee must be a cunning Mercury must pipe them both a sleepe I can tell you. And now I talke of sleepe, my lodging is next to her chambers, it is a confidence in my Master to let his Liuery lye so neere her, Seruingmen haue e're now proved themselues no Eunuches, with their Masters Daughters, if I were so lusty as some of my owne tribe, it were no great labour to commit Burglary vpon a Mayden head, but all my nourishment runs vpward into braines and I am glad on't, a temperate blood is signe of a good Liver, I am past tilting, here shee is with the second part of her to the same tune, an other maide that has a grud ging of the greene sicknes. and wants a man to reco uer her. As you see forsooth. But I am in ſober sadnes I thanke my starres. As much wit as will keepe Breynes from melting this hot weather. Very good Sacke and Claret ith house. An other inclosed without direction happily ob serued. It is a shee Letter it seemes. And a bowling gamester too, for his byas was to wards my Mistres, but I may chance to cast a rub in his way to keepe him from kissing. O the cunning of these Gipses, how when they list they can talke in a distinguishable dialect, they call men foxes but they make, tame geese a some on vs, and yet like one a those in Rome I may prove so happy to p eserue your distressed Capitoll, what newes brings this Kickshaw. So, I can suffer this jeare. Exit. What doe all these Butterflies here, I doe not like it. SVre this is it, my Mistris and her maide are both fast still, I ha watched vnder the bed all night, to rob her pocket of this paper, and I ha don't, some fellow at this opportunity would haue wrigled himselfe into one of their flesh.

Plot summary

Plot — The Witty Fair One
PLOT After a dinner party at his brother Worthy’s house, Sir George Richly goes off to visit his prospective son-in-law Sir Nicholas in the country, leaving his daughter Violetta in Worthy’s care and under the watchful eye of his servant Brains: he is concerned that, as his heir, she should not be the object of unwelcome attentions from gallants. The other dinner guests take a walk in the garden. Aimwell has fallen for Violetta and asks Worthy what his chances are; he gets a frank answer, and resolves to give up his suit, but changes his mind when Violetta’s own words seem to imply that her father’s matrimonial plans for her may not have her full consent. Meanwhile Fowler woos Worthy’s daughter Penelope, but admits in private that he is not in earnest, only practising his skill. Sir George returns home with Sir Nicholas. He proves to be an arrant fool dependent on his opportunistic tutor, who can see that the objective of the marriage is one knight’s desire to control the other’s estate. Aimwell sends a letter to Violetta enclosed in one from Fowler to Penelope. Through a mix-up, Violetta keeps her reply and sends him back his original letter, which he takes as a rebuff. Brains steals the remaining letter from her and sends it to her father, who is outraged and plans to advance the date of the wedding. Violetta’s chambermaid Sensible, sacked by Sir George, tells Aimwell about the mistake with the letters; reassured that she loves him after all, he starts to lay plans. Importuned by Sir Nicholas’s tutor, Violetta agrees to elope with him rather than marry Sir Nicholas, on condition that he kills Brains. She gets permission to go out shopping, escorted by Brains, and the tutor picks a quarrel. While Brains’s attention is distracted, Violetta substitutes Sensible for herself. Brains discovers the ruse, but is arrested at the tutor’s suit before he can do anything about it. Brains writes asking Sir George to arrange for his release, and mentions the attempted elopement but not the substitution; so when Sir Nicholas confronts the tutor, it is Sensible, not Violetta, whom he takes back and marries. Meanwhile, Violetta and Aimwell are also married. Sir Nicholas and Sir George accept the fait accompli and forgive their servants. In the sub-plot, Fowler feigns a fever as a wooing technique; Penelope promises to marry him when he is better.
