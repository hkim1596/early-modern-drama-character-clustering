If you will conceale it, And I by ignorance omit to pay Those sacred duties that I ought, be pleas'd To pardon me. For if he be A father that begot this flesh, this clay, What's he to whom we owe our second birth Of soule and reason? Father, I must call you By that name, father. O Vrania, Here, here he stands, to whom I owe my selfe, And thou owest me: we reverence in our Temples Marble, and brasse, whose statues serve for nothing But to hang cobwebs on: oh! how much rather Should we adore this Deity, that bestowed Such happinesse upon us! Farewell father. Pray give mee entrance: I am not mad, (and yet I would I were) Am I not mad to wish so? Let me come And see him, sure you had your selfe a father. Did you not wish to see him ere he died? If he be dead: wee'l only pray a while, And weep; will tears pollute the hallowed Ompha? For we must shed them, yes, we cannot choose: Come sister, he will let us, for though Lalage Was our sad mother, yet the Gods will let us Weepe for her: come, come Amarillis, come. Why Pilumnus, Why? By the dread Ompha, spare this guilty blood, And I'le expound the Oracle. What fire has yet his bloud or quench't or kindled? Where? in what brest? No love in all Trinacria But under Cupids scepter faints and groanes More now then ever. Thy unfortunate Damon, And more unfortunate Amarillis stand A sad example; Thy Vrania (O sad sweet name!) may with her poore Amintas Witnesse his tyrannous reigne: here in Sicilia Turtles grow jealous, Doves are turn'd unchast, The very Pellica … s of Trinacria … woods Are found unnaturall, and thirst the bloud Of their young brood, (alas who can believe it?) Whom they were wont to suckle with their own. O wretched season! Bitter fruits of love! The very Storks with us are Parricides. Nay even the senselesse trees are sensible Of this imperious rage: the gentle Vine (The happy embleme once of happier Lovers) That with such amorous twines, and close imbraces Did cling about the loved-loving elme, With slacker branches now falls down and withers: If then to adde more fuell to the flame, To powre in oyle and sulphure be to quench it, The flame is quench'd. Nor are you hee, Pilumnus, That must expound the Oracle, 'Tis a witt Such as mine is neglected, that must hit The Goddesse meaning: you, the living Oracle Of Sicilie, the breathing Ompha of the Kingdome Will misconceive the Goddesse; you are wise Skil'd in the vertues of all herbs, and flowers, What makes our Ewes ean best, what keeps the ̄ sound; Can tell us all the mysteries of heaven, The number, height, and motion of the starres; Tis a mad brain, an intellect, you scorne That must unty this riddle. But his bloud Hath been already shed in Amarillis: Shee is his bloud, so is Vrania yours, And Damon is your bloud; That is the bloud The Goddesse aimes at, that must still her ire, For her bloud hath both quench't and kindled fire. Love, the fire That must be quench't and kindled. Damons love To his Laurinda in that bloud extinguish'd, Is by that powerfull bloud kindled anew To Amarillis, now grown his desire: Thus Claius bloud hath quench't and kindled fire. Spare these blasphemies, For Damon is acquitted & affoil'd Of any trespasse. Thus, Amarillis is the Sacrifice The Goddesse aim'd at: and the bloud of Sacrifice (As you all know) may lawfully be spilt Even in the Holy vale, and so it was; Besides your Damon is a Priest by birth, And therefore by that Title, he may spill The sacrifized Amarillis bloud. If this interpretation be not true, Speak you Sicilians, I'le be judg'd by you. Still my impossible Dowry for Vrania Leaves mee unfortunate in the mid'st of joy; Yet out of piety I will heere a while (Though blest I am not 'till she be my bride) In publique joyes lay private griefes aside. Ceres, I doe thank thee, That I am author of this publique joy: But is it justice (Goddesse) I alone Should have no share in't? Every one I see Is happy but my selfe that made 'em so, And my Vrania that should most be so. I thirst amidst the Bowles; when others sit Quaffing off Nectar, I but hold the cup; And stand a sadder Tantalus of love, Starving in all this plenty; Cere's Demand Feeds mee with gall; stretching my doubtfull thoughts On many thousand racks: I would my Dowry Was all the gold of Tagas, or the ore Of bright Pactolus channell:—But, Vrania, Tis hid, alas I know not what it is. How! in this habit! This me thinks befits not A Lover, my Vrania. O is it just, so faire a one as you Should vow Virginity? must the sacred womb Of my Vrania fit to have brought forth A fruitfull race of Gods, be ever barren? Never expect Lucina? shall this beauty Live but one age? how curs'd is our posterity That shall have no Vrania's! can one Tombe Contain all goodnesse? Ceres rather blast The corne thou gav'st us: let the earth grow barren; These trees, and flowers wither eternally; Let our Plowes toyle in vaine; and let there be No more a harvest: Every losse is small, Yea though the Phaenix selfe should burne to ashes And nere revive again! But let there be Some more Vrania's — But yet Vrania, I hope we may sometimes come pray together; 'Tis not prophane, and mid'st our sacred Orisons Change a chast kisse or two; or shall I too Turne Virgin with thee?—But I foole my selfe, The Gods intend to crosse us, and in vaine We strive ( Vrania ) to crosse them again. Why, will she answer mee before Vrania? Plot summary

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
