Palaemon no, she neuer made me vow, But I did euer hope she would be mine. For that I had deliuered vp my youth, My heart, my all, a tribute to her eyes, And had secur'd her of my constant truth, Vnder so many faithfull specialties, As that although she did not graunt againe, With any shew the acquittance of my loue Yet did she euer feeme to intertaine My affections, and my seruices t'approoue. Till now of late I know not by what meane, (Ill fare that meane) she grew to that dispight, As she not onely clowds her fauours cleane, But also scorn'd to haue me in her gsight. That now I am not for her loue thus, mou'd But onely that she will not be belou'd. How may that be, Palaemon pray thee tell? Why what is Siluia false, or is she gone? But good Palaemon, tell what proofes hast thou Of her disloyalty, that makes thee show These heauie passions, and to grieue so much? Ah good Palaemon cease these sad complaints, And moderate thy passions, thou shalt see She may returne, and these reports be found But idle fictions, on vncertaine ground. Adue Palaemon, and thy sad distresse, Shall make me wey Dorindas losse the lesse: For if I should be hers, and she proue so, Better to be mine owne, and let her go. Well met Carinus, I can tell you newes, Your riuall, poore Amyntas, hath vndone And spoild himselfe, and lyes in that weake case, As we thinke neuer more to see his face. But hauing there vs'd all the helpe we could, And all in vaine, and standing by with griefe, (As we might well, to see so sad a sight) (And such an worthy Shepheard in that plight) We might perceiue come running downe the hill, Cloris, and Techne, with what speed they could, But Cloris had got ground, and was before, And made more haste, as it concernd her more. And neerer as she came, she faster went, As if she did desire to haue bene there Before her feete, too flow for her swift feare. And comming to the place, she sodainely Stopt, startes, and shrikt, and hauing made such haste T'haue something done, now could she nothing doe. Perhaps our presence might perplexe her too, As being asham'd that any eye should see The new appearing of her naked heart, That neuer yet before was seene till now. For we perceiu'd how Loue and Modestie With seu'rall Ensignes, stroue within her cheekes Which should be Lord that day, and charged hard Vpon each other, with their fresh supplies Of different coulours, that still came, and went, And much disturb'd her but at length dissolu'd Into affection, downe she casts her selfe Vpon his senselesse body, where she saw The mercie she had brought was come too late: And to him calles; ô deare Amyntas speake, Looke on me, sweete Amyntas, it is I That calles thee, sit is, that holds thee here, Within those armes thou haste esteem'd so deare. And though that loue were yet so young in her As that it knew not how to speake, or what, And that she neuer had that passion prou'd, Being first a louer ere she knew she lou'd, Yet what she could not vtter, she supplide, With her poore busie hands that rubb'd his face, Chafd his pale temples, wrung his fingers ends, Held vp his head, and puld him by the hands, And neuer leaft her worke, nor euer ceast. But yet at length, as if those daintie hands, Had had a power to haue awakened death, We might perceiue him moue his heauie eyes, Which had stood fixt all the whole time before, And fastens them directly vpon her. Which when she saw, it strooke her with that force, As that it pierc'd through all the spirits she had, Made all the powers and parts of her shrinke vp, With that conuulsion of remorse and griefe, As out she shrik'd, ô deere, ô my deere heart, Then shrinkes againe, and then againe cryes out. For now that looke of his did shake her more, Then death or any thing had done before, That looke did read t'her new conceiuing heart, All the whole tragicke Lecture of his loue, All his sad suffrings, all his griefes, and feare, And now in th'end what he had done for her. And with that powerfull force of mouing too, As all a world of words could neuer doe. Ah what a silly messenger is Speach, To be imploi'd in that great Embassie Of our affections, in respect of th'eye? Ah 'tis the silent rhetorick of a looke, That worker the league betwixt the states of hearts, Not words I see, nor knowledge of the booke, Nor incantations made by hidden artes, For now this looke so melts her into teares, As that she powr'd them downe like thunder droppes, Or else did Nature taking pittie now Of her distresse, imploy them in that store, To serue as vailes, and to be interposde Betwixt her griefe and her, t'impeach her sight, From that full view of sorrow thus disclosde. And now with this came in Vrania there, With other women, to imploy their best To saue his life, if b'any meanes they can. And so we came our way, being sent for now About some conference for our hunting sportes, And with vs Techne comes, who is supposde, T'haue bene a speciall cause of much of this. Ah would to God Dorinda had bene there, T'haue seene but Cloris acte this wofull part; It may be, it might haue deterr'd her heart From cruelty, so long as she had liu'd. Ah Amarillis. I would that were true. But loe where come our chiefest heardsmen now, Of all Arcadia, we shall know more newes. Dorinda then I likewise haue my blisse And reckon all the sufferings I haue past Worthy of thee to haue this ioy at last.

Plot — The Queen's Arcadia (Arcadia Reformed)
Arcadia is no longer the place of pastoral simplicity it once was: old shepherds have grown litigious, young ones are falling in love unwisely and unrequitedly, and the nymphs, who seem less beautiful than before, have abandoned the modest simplicity of traditional Arcadian dress in favour of quirky new fashions. The problems arise from foreign influences on this previously sequestered land. Colax has travelled abroad, and brought back with him the city woman Techne, and they are selfishly interfering in the love- lives of the nymphs and shepherds: they have made Silvia and Palaemon jealous of one another with mendacious tales of infidelity; and Colax has persuaded Dorinda to jilt Myrtillus in his favour, making her the latest in a string of sexual conquests. Meanwhile Lincus, a lawyer, and Doctor Alcon have both come from town to set up practices in Arcadia. Lincus has found the local amity bad for business: the Arcadians do not covet one another’s property and so never go to law. For Alcon, however, the Arcadians’ fellow-feeling is profitable, because when one falls ill, others follow suit, and they all want the same medicine from him — which is just as well, because he has only one to offer, and that is a placebo. Both are keen to drum up trade, so Lincus has set Montanus and Acrisius against one another, and Alcon has introduced the Arcadians to an unhealthy habit: tobacco-smoking. Carinus (who has recently jilted Amaryllis) and Amyntas are rivals for the love of Cloris, and argue about which of them has the greater command of her affections; but in fact Cloris loves neither of them, and has sworn chastity until she finds a worthy man. Colax now wants a change of girlfriend, and asks Techne to make Cloris love him. Techne offers to give Cloris a new outfit, and arranges for them to meet at a cave so she can try it on; but it is Colax who keeps the appointment, and attempts to rape her. Techne herself has fallen for Amyntas, and arranges for him to see Cloris meeting Colax. Amyntas becomes suicidal, so Techne sends Cloris to stop him; but she arrives too late, and he has already taken poison.
