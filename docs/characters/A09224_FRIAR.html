Guenthian as I am true man, So will I doo the best I can: Guenthian as I am true Priest, So will I bee at thy behest: Guenthian as I am true Friar, So wil I be at thy desire. Heere sweare I by my shauen crowne, VVench if I giue thee a gay greene gowne, Ile take thee vp as I laid thee downe, And neuer bruze nor batter thee. Then my Guenthian to begin, Sith idlenes in loue is sinne, Boie to the towne I will thee hie, And so returne euen by and by, VVhen thou with cakes and muskadine, And other … unkets good and fine, Hast fild thy bottle and thy bagge. Wench to passe away the time in glee, Guenthian set thee downe by me, And let our lips and voices meete, In a merrie countrey songe. E … cum spiritu tuo. Friends haue you any thing els to say to the Friar? And you sir when yee eate: Ha … y … ny thing els to say to the Friar? 〈◊〉 … y bee s … I count it physicke, To feede but on one dish at a sitting: Sir would you any thing els with the Friar? Nay and that be the matter good enough, Is this all yee haue to say to the Friar? My maisters and frends, I am a poore Friar, a man of Gods making, and a good fellow as you are, legs, feete, face and hands, & hart from top to toe, of my word, … ght shape and Christendome: and I loue a wenche as a wench should be loued, and if you loue your selfe walke good friends I pray you, & let the Friar alone with his flesh. Brother, brother, and my good Countrimen. Then brother and my good friends, Hands off and if you loue your ease. Fellow be gone quicklie, or my pike staffe and I will set thee away with a vengeance. My Countrymen? nay marry sir shal you not be my countrimen, you sir, you, specially you sir that refuse the Friar, and renounce his countrie. Of honestie saiest thou? They are neither Gentlemen nor Welshmen, That will denie their countrie: Come hither wenche, Ile haue about with them once more, For denying of theyr Countrie. As much as you will with me sir, But not at any hand with my wench, I and Richard my man heere. For here, Contra omnes gentes. But is this Lluellen the great Camber Britaine? Tis pittie my Lorde, But you should haue more of this learning You profit so well by it. And Richard my man sir and you loue me, He that stands by me, and shrunke not at all weathers, And then you haue me in my colours. Knowledge my sonne, knowledge I warrantye, How saist thou Morgaine, art thou not a very prophet? This my Lord a meanes by you, O he is a prophet, a prophet. My Lord he comes to your time thats flat. As how Iack? By ladie my Lord you go neere the matter, But what saith Morgaine Pigote more? Farewell, be farre from the Spigote, Come boie we must buckle I see, The prince is of my profession right: Rather than he wil lose his wenche, He will fight Ab ouo vsque ad mala. Harke you sir, I am afeard you will not be so re solued, by that time you knowe so much as I can showe you, here be hote Dogges I can tell you, meanes to haue the baiting of you. Saw haw, mai … er; I haue found, I haue found. O Cupido quantus quantus. O Lord, I Sir full gladlie.
