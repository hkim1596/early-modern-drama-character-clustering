None. Sir! Sir, I beleeue it, but I neuer did Tie my affection vnto any colour. You rellish too much Courtier, sir. Why? you doe; And so doe all men when they women wooe. This is pretty Poetry, good fiction this: Sir, I must leaue you. What would you craue? You doe not know my bounty; Say what t'is. If I should giue you one, would you refraine, on that condition, ne'r to begge againe. Then't seemes you haue, Though you get nothing, a delight to craue, One will not hurt my lippe, which you may take, Not for your loue, but for your absence sake. So farewell sir. Thrice happy dayes they were, and too soone gone, When as the heart was coupled with the tongue, And no deceitfull flattery or guile: Hung on the Louers teare-commixed smile: Could women learne but that imperiousnesse, By which men vse to stint our happinesse, When they haue purchast vs for to be theirs, By customarie sighs and forced teares, To giue vs bittes of kindnesse lest we faint, But no aboundance, that we euer want, And still are begging; which too well they know Endeeres affection, and doth make it grow: Had we these sleights, how happy were we then, That we might glory ouer loue-sicke men? But Arts we know not; nor haue any skill, To faine a sowre looke to a pleasing will, Enter Ioyes. Nor cowch a secret loue in shew of hate: But if we like, must be compassionate; Yet I will striue to bridle and conceale, The hid affection which my heart doth feele. You talke strangely sister. Discouer, what will you discouer? Sir, I'm at your disposing, but my minde Stands not as yet towards marriage, Were you so pleasde I would a little longer Enioy the quiet of a single bed. What say you sister? A Cord! what to doe? Sister, know I loue thee, And I'le not thinke a thought thou shalt not know; I loue a Gentleman that answeres me, In all the rites of loue as faithfully, Has woo'd me oft with Sonets, and with teares, Yet I seeme still to slight him. Experience tells, The Iewell that's enioy'd is not esteem'd, Things hardly got, are alwayes highest deem'd. You shall. Why sister, what will you doe? Y'are the strangest creature, what would you haue me doe? Fie how you talke, 'tis meere immodestie, The common'st strumpet would not doe so much. Ile loose his company for euer first. Why sister, pray sister. Good sister hold your tongue, I will goe downe to him. If youle forbeare to call, I will goe downe. Sir, you professe loue vnto me, let me intreate you it may appeare but in some small request. But for this present to forbeare this place, Because my father is expected heere. Plot summary

Plot — Greene's Tu Quoque, or The City Gallant
The gallant Geraldine woos the mercer’s daughter Gertrude. She is attracted to him, but feigns lack of interest. Her father, Sir Lionel, is newly knighted at court. He plans to give up life as a tradesman and leaves Spendall to run his shop. Spendall is prodigal with the shop’s money: he sups with a whore and her associates, borrows money, gambles, and_ lives extravagantly. Staines is in the clutches of a usurer, his servant Bubble’s uncle, and plans to cut and run: he dismisses Bubble and is about to flee London when news arrives that the usurer is dead. Bubble inherits the estate, hires his former master as a servant, and plans, with his advice, to make himself a gallant. Staines intends to bilk his money back from Bubble and then use it buy back his lands once Bubble is forced by prodigality to sell them. Bubble sends him to buy new clothes, but Staines wears them himself and uses the money to gamble; Bubble himself joins the game as one of his opponents. Staines cheats with false dice, but is discovered by Spendall. They fight a duel; after both sustain slight wounds, they become friends. Sir Lionel plans to marry Gertrude to Bubble, hoping to get control of his inheritance. Her sister Joyce tries to get her to accept Geraldine, but she is _ steadfast. Joyce tells Geraldine that Gertrude does love | him, and he induces her to reveal her true feelings by feigning suicide. Bubble comes to Sir Lionel’s house to woo Gertrude, but Staines advises him that the best way to win the ladies’ admiration is to dispraise himself — so he makes a fool of himself. Even so, Sir Lionel continues with his plans — though now Bubble’s wife is to be Joyce, while Gertrude is destined for Scattergood, another gallant. Joyce herself is attracted to Staines, but she too is reluctant to act on it. Her brother Will gets Staines a fashionable suit in place of his servant’s blue coat and brings him to see Joyce, but at first she will not speak; when she does, it is to insult him at great length, making him wish he could shut her up again. Spendall is arrested for debt. He tries to get money from his whore with which to bribe the sergeants, but she will not admit to knowing him. He is taken to prison, where he sells all his clothes; but this fails to clear the debt. A widow hears about his case from Sir Lionel and charitably pays his debts; released, he takes her generosity as evidence of sexual interest, and plans to woo her.
