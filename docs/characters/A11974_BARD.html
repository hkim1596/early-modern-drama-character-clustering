Who keepes the gate here ho? where is the Earle? Tell thou the Earle, That the Lord Bardolfe doth attend him heere. Here comes the Earle. Noble Earle, I bring you certaine newes from Shrewsbury. As good as heart can wish: The King is almost wounded to the death, And in the fortune of my Lord your sonne, Prince Harry slaine outright, and both the Blunts Kild by the hand of Dowglas, yong prince Iohn, And Westmerland and Stafford fled the field, And Harry Monmouthes brawne the hulke sir Iohn, Is prisoner to your sonne: O such a day! So fought, so followed, and so fairely wonne, Came not till now to dignifie the times Since Caesars fortunes. My lord, Ile tell you what, If my yong Lord your sonne, haue not the day, Vpon mine honor for a silken point, Ile giue my Barony, neuer talke of it. Who he? He was some hilding fellow that had stolne The horse he rode on, and vpon my life Spoke at a venter. Looke, here comes more news. enter Mor ton. I cannot thinke, my Lord, your sonne is dead ▪ Sweet earle, diuorce not wisedom from your honor, We all that are ingaged to this losse, Knew that we ventured on such dangerous seas, That if we wrought out life, twas ten to one, And yet we venturd for the gaine proposde, Choakt the respect of likely perill fear'd, And since we are oreset, venture againe: Come, we will al put forth body and goods. The question then Lord Hastings standeth thus, Whether our present fiue and twentie thousand, May hold vp head without Northumberland. Yea mary, theres the point, But if without him we be thought too feeble, My iudgement is we should not step too far. It was my Lord, who lined himselfe with hope, Eating the ayre, and promise of supplie, Flattring himselfe in proiect of a power, Much smaller then the smallest of his thoughts, And so with great imagination, Proper to mad-men, led his powers to death, And winking, leapt into destruction. We fortifie in paper and in figures, Vsing the names of men in steed of men, Like on that drawes the model of an house, Beyond his power to build it, who (halfe thorough) Giues ore, and leaues his part-created cost, A naked subiect to the weeping clowdes, And waste for churlish winters tyrannie. What, is the King but iue and twenty thousand? God saue your grace. Away you horson vpright rabble, away. And you do not make him hangd among you, the gal lowes shall haue wrong. Well my Lord, he heard of your graces comming to towne, theres a letter for you. In bodily health sir. Yea my Lord. At the old place, my lord, in Eastcheape. Pray thee go downe good Ancient. Be gone good Ancient, this will grow to a brawle anon. Come, get you downe staires. Mistris Tere-sheete. Bid mistris Tere-sheete come to my maister. Plot — 2 Henry the Fourth
After the battle of Shrewsbury, there are contradictory rumours abroad as to the outcome: some say that the King and his son have vanquished Lord Hotspur of Lancaster, whereas in fact they won. Now Lord John of Lancaster is travelling north with a power, including Sir John Falstaff, to crush the regrouping remnants of the Percy rebellion. Mistress Quickly tries to have Falstaff arrested for debt, while threatens to prevent him setting out for London, but he talks his way out of it. Back from a military expedition in Wales, Prince Henry and Poins disguise themselves as drawers and eavesdrop on Falstaff’s conversation: he says uncomplimentary things about them, but Falstaff excuses himself inventively when confronted. Northumberland’s family persuade him to flee to Scotland rather than join the other northern rebels. Falstaff musters troops in Gloucestershire, but the best men bribe their way out, leaving him with the least suitable soldiers. Prince John sees the rebels and agrees to redress their grievances if they disband their army; but when they do so, he has them arrested for treason. Falstaff arrives in the north late, but meets the rebel Sir John Colville, who promptly surrenders without a fight, having heard Falstaff’s reputation. Prince John returns to court, but Falstaff goes via Gloucestershire to visit Justice Shallow. The sickly King prepares for death, and worries about the kingdom’s future in the hands of his eldest son. News arrives of the defeat of all the rebels. Prince Henry finds his father asleep with the crown beside him, and, thinking him dead, tries it on. The King wakes to find his crown gone. Brought back, the Prince excuses himself, and the King gives deathbed advice: he should pursue an aggressive foreign policy to distract from internal dissent. He dies in the Jerusalem chamber, fulfilling a prophecy that he will die in Jerusalem. Henry V is not the King everyone expected the Prince to be. He confirms in office the Lord Chief Justice, who had imprisoned him for his misdemeanours. Pistol brings to Gloucestershire news of his accession, and Falstaff anticipates power for himself and his associates. He is disappointed: when he accosts the King at his coronation, Henry refuses to acknowledge him, and banishes him — though with a pension to ensure that poverty does not lead him back into crime.
