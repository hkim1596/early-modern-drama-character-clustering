Full speech

Altered Trachinus, I say no more, and shame that any should know so much. Trachinus, what would you more, all true. A labour intollerable for Pandion. Because it is harder to shape a life to dissem ble, then to goe forward with the libertie of trueth. Doe you knowe in court any that meane to liue? Reporte hath not alwaies a blister on her tongue. Yet hath Salurus blasts, and water boughes, wormes and Caterpillers. Nor the excellencie of Sapho the occasion: but the iniquitie of flatterers, who alwaies whisper in Prin ces eares suspition and sowrenesse. Yea, & with the iudgement of the world, that she is without comparison. I would I might returne streight. But not still. Mine eie drinketh neither the colour of wine nor women. When I behold bewty before the sunne, his beams dimme bewtie: when by candle, bewty obscures toarch light: so as no time I can iudge, because at anie time I cannot discerne being in the sunne a brightnesse to shadow bewtie, and in bewtie a glistering to extin guish light. I follow. And you sir boy goe to Syracusa about by land, where you shall meete my stuffe, pay for the cariage, and conuey it to my lodging. Yea. A straunge nature of colde, to driue one into such an heate. Indeede Lady, I haue no more Phisick then wil purge choller, and that if it please you, I will practise vp pon you. It is good for women that be waspish. It is true, and are not you a female content. Of men we learne to speake, of Gods to holde our peace. Silence shall disgeste what follye hath swallo wed, and wisdome weane what fancie hath noursed. If it were, what then? Why, in courtes there is nothing more com mon. And as to be bald among the Micanyans it was ac counted no shame, because they were all balde: so to be in loue among courtiers it is no discredit: for that they are al in loue. As of the Seres wooll, which beeing whitest & softest, fretteth soonestand deepest. I will goe with you: but send our pages away.

Plot — Sappho and Phao

She makes Phao beautiful; and, unknown to her, @iipi@ makes him irresistibly attractive to women, even though he is not interested in them. Sibyllaj whom the gods have given long life but not eternal youth, counsels Phao not to waste his prime years. He falls in love with Sappho, and Sibylla advises him on the technique of wooing. Sappho is taken ill; Phao, who has some pharmaceutical knowledge, is summoned to minister to her. In fact, her malady is love for Phao, whose base origins make him unsuitable for her. Venus too falls for Phao, and procures from Wailea new arrows for Cupid, with which he can break her rival Sappho’s infatuation and turn Phao’s affections her way. Cupid carries out the first part of his mission, but also tells Sappho what he has done; so she persuades him to use another of the new arrows to make Phao hate Venus. Cupid decides to be Sappho’s son instead of Venus’, and gives her his arrows: now she, not Venus, will be the lady of love. Venus swears to get even, and, convinced of love’s pointlessness, Phao leaves Sicily.
