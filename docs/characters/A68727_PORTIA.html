By my troth Nerriſſa, my little body is awearie of this great world. Good ſentences, and well pronounc'd. If to do were as eaſie as to know what were good to do, Chappels had beene Churches, and poore mens cottages Princes Pallaces, it is a good diuine that followes his owne inſtructions, I can eaſier teach twentie what were good to be done, then to be one of the twentie to follow mine owne teaching: the braine may de uiſe lawes for the blood, but a hote temper leapes ore a colde de cree, ſuch a hare is madnes the youth, to skippe ore the meſhes of good counſaile the cripple; but this reaſoning is not in the faſhion to chooſe mee a husband, ô mee the word chooſe, I may neyther chooſe who I would, nor refuſe who I diſlike, ſo is the will of a ly uing daughter curbd by the will of a deade father: is it not harde Nerriſſa, that I cannot chooſe one, nor refuſe none. That hee hath a neyghbourlie charitie in him, for hee borrowed a boxe of the eare of the Engliſhman, and ſwore hee would pay him againe when he was able: I think the Frenchman became his ſuretie, and ſeald vnder for another. Therefore for feare of the worſt, I pray thee ſet a deepe glaſſe of Reyniſhe vvine on the contrarie Casket, for if the deuill be within, and that temptation without, I knowe hee will chooſe it. I will doe any thing Nerriſſa ere I will be married to a ſpunge. Yes, yes, it was Baſſanio, as I thinke ſo was he calld. I remember him well, and I remember him worthie of thy prayſe. How nowe, vvhat newes? In termes of choyſe I am not ſoly led By nice direction of a maydens eyes: Beſides, the lottrie of my deſtenie Barrs me the right of voluntary chooſing: But if my Father had not ſcanted me, And hedgd me by his wit to yeeld my ſelfe His wife, who winnes me by that meanes I told you, Your ſelfe (renowned Prince) than ſtoode as faire As any commer I haue look'd on yet For my affection. You muſt take your chaunce, And eyther not attempt to chooſe at all, Or ſweare before you chooſe, if you chooſe wrong Neuer to ſpeake to Lady afterward In way of marriage, therefore be aduiſ'd. Firſt forward to the temple, after dinner Your hazard ſhall be made. Too long a pauſe for that which you finde there. To offend and iudge are diſtinct offices, And of oppoſed natures. Thus hath the candle ſingd the moath: O theſe deliberate fooles when they doe chooſe, They haue the wiſedome by their wit to looſe. Come draw the curtaine Nerriſſa. Heere, what would my Lord? No more I pray thee, I am halfe a-feard Thou wilt ſay anone he is ſome kin to thee, Thou ſpendſt ſuch high day wit in prayſing him: Come come Nerryſſa, for I long to ſee Quick Cupids Poſt that comes ſo mannerly. I pray you tarry, pauſe a day or two Before you hazard, for in chooſing wrong I looſe your companie; therefore forbeare a while, Theres ſomething tells me (but it is not loue) I would not looſe you, and you know your ſelfe, Hate counſailes not in ſuch a quallity; But leaſt you ſhould not vnderſtand me well, And yet a mayden hath no tongue, but thought, I would detaine you heere ſome moneth or two before you venture for me. I could teach you how to chooſe right, but then I am forſworne, So will I neuer be, ſo may you miſſe me, But if you doe, youle make me wiſh a ſinne, That I had beene forſworne: Beſhrow your eyes, They haue ore-lookt me and deuided me, One halfe of me is yours, the other halfe yours, Mine owne I would ſay: but if mine then yours, And ſo all yours; ô theſe naughty times puts barres betweene the ovvners and their rights, And ſo though yours, not yours, (proue it ſo) Let Fortune goe to hell for it, not I. I ſpeake too long, but tis to peize the time, To ech it, and to draw it out in length, To ſtay you from election. Well then, confeſſe and liue. Away then, I am locktin one of them, If you doe loue me, you will finde me out. Nerryſſa and the reſt, ſtand all aloofe, Let muſique ſound while he doth make his choyſe, Then if he looſe he makes a Swan-like end, Fading in muſique. That the compariſon may ſtand more proper, my eye ſhall be the ſtreame and watry death-bed for him: he may win, And what is muſique than? Than muſique is euen as the flouriſh, when true ſubiects bowe to a new crowned Monarch: Such it is, As are thoſe dulcet ſounds in breake of day, That creepe into the dreaming bride-groomes eare, And ſummon him to marriage. Now he goes vvith no leſſe preſence, but with much more loue Then young Alcides, when he did redeeme The virgine tribute, payed by howling Troy To the Sea-monſter: I ſtand for ſacrifice, The reſt alo are the Dardanian vviues: With bleared viſages come forth to view The iſſue of th'exploit: Goe Hercules, Liue thou, I liue with much much more diſmay, I view the fight, then thou that mak'ſt the fray. Tell me where is fancie bred, Or in the hart, or in the head, How beget, how nouriſhed? Replie, replie. It is engendred in the eye, With gazing fed, and Fancie dies: In the cradle where it lies I et vs all ring Fancies knell. Ile begin it. Ding, dong, bell. Come on Nerriſſa, I haue worke in hand That you yet know not of; weele ſee our husbands before they thinke of vs? They ſhall Nerriſſa: but in ſuch a habite, that they ſhall thinke we are accompliſhed vvith that we lacke; Ile hold thee any wager vvhen we are both accoutered like young men, ile proue the prettier fellow of the two, and weare my dagger with the brauer grace, and ſpeake betweene the change of man and boy, vvi'h a reede voyce, and turne two minſing ſteps into a manly ſtride; and ſpeake of frayes like a fine bragging youth: and tell quaint lyes how honorable Ladies ſought my loue, vvhich I denying, they fell ſicke and dyed. I could not doe withall: then ile repent, and wiſh for all that, that I had not killd them; And twenty of theſe punie lies ile tell, that men ſhall ſweare I haue diſcontinued ſchoole aboue a twelue-moneth: I haue within my minde a thouſand raw tricks of theſe bragging lacks, vvhich I will practiſe. Fie, what a queſtion's that, if thou wert nere a lewd interpreter: But come, ile tell thee all my my whole deuice vvhen I am in my coach, which ſtayes for vs at the Parke gate; and therefore haſt away, for we muſt meaſure twenty miles to day I did my Lord.

Plot — The Merchant of Venice (The Jew of Venice)
Bassanio plans to woo the heiress Portia, hoping that, if successful, her wealth will get him out of debt. His friend and principal creditor, the melancholy merchant Antonio, agrees to finance the venture, but all his money is tied up in commercial activity. He seeks a loan from the Jew Shylock, who himself raises the money by borrowing from his co-religionist Tubal. ===== PAGE 342 ===== 342 |. 1047. The Merchant of Venice Shylock is resentful of Antonio’s attacks on him for taking interest on loans, but frames a ‘merry bond’ which will entitle him only to a pound of Antonio’s flesh should the money not be repaid on the due date. Lancelot Gobbo leaves Shylock’s service and is hired by Bassanio, who is planning a going-away feast. Lancelot brings a letter from Shylock’s daughter Jessica to Lorenzo, whom she wishes to marry. Shylock is invited to the supper, and, while he is away, Lorenzo helps Jessica to escape from her father’s house; afterwards, it is unclear whether Shylock is more distressed by the loss of his daughter or the money she took with her. Bassanio leaves for Belmont, accompanied by Gratiano. Portia’s marital prospects are regulated by the will of her dead father: her suitors must choose which of three caskets contains her picture, and she may only wed the suitor who gets it right, while suitors who get it wrong must remain unmarried ever after. The Prince of Morocco chooses the golden casket, signifying a much-desired object, but gets only a death’s head. The Prince of Aragon chooses silver, signifying the suitor’s desert, and gets a fool’s head. Bassanio chooses lead, signifying that the suitor must risk everything, and gets Portia. She gives him a ring, which he promises never to remove. oo Rumours circulate in Venice that Antonio is ruined by the loss of all his ships. Unable to pay his debt to the vindictive Shylock, he writes to Belmont for Bassanio’s help. Lorenzo also comes to Belmont with Jessica, and, leaving her household in their care, Portia. secretly follows her new husband to Venice. Having acquired a disguise and a testimonial from a lawyer kinsman, she takes part in the hearing of Antonio’s case before the Duke. Shylock refuses either to accept the repayment of his money or to be merciful; but Portia points out that the bond entitles him to take no blood with his pound of flesh.
