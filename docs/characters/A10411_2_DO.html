Hare or Deere my Lord? Ha? would you so? Nay say your worst I care not, Did I goe ever? Yet you kiss'd her With admirable courtship. Pal'd round about with Pick-teeth. A Ginger hall. Kitchins all of Christall. The spits are Spanish needles. Of Amber. That bear as well in winter as in summer. Of Nectar: will this please you? every grove Stor'd with delighfull birds. Yes. What pretty things are these both to be borne To Lands and Livings, we poore witty knaves, Have no inheritance but Braines:—who's this? —One of my Mistresse beagles. In what, Alexis? So, and did not you Compare the stag to my fair mistresse? ha! Pursued by you and Damon, caught by neither? And to that end his sister Keepes them about him; but in vaine, his Melancholy Has took so deepe impression. Excellent application! To see the wit of love! O lamentable! who would be in love? O pittifull! Pittifully sweet! Nere feare your father, Mistresse, kisse securely, I'le be your Mercury, and charme a sleepe Old Argus. But if he chance to spy You and your sweet-hearts here, I know not of it. Nay you know if I had seene them, I should have told him. Poore Dorylas is blind, he sees not here Damon, no nor Alexis. Alack I am innocent: if the belly swell I did not fetch the poyson. Why took she mine, but meaning to requite? You talke of Love and Cupid, I have been plagu'd with a whole swarme of Cupids. I know not, but I am sure I have a thousand naturall rapiers Stick in my flesh! In plain tearmes I have been driving One of your swarmes of Bees, gentle Laurinda; Now will the hony and the wax fall together by th'eares. Well argu'd for the waxe, now for the hony. The hony is the sweeter argument. The waxe is very forward to the bargain; He would be sealing of her. Delicious reason, my mouth waters at it ▪ Two excellent twin-arguments borne at a birth. I knew that before! More Cupids, more bees, more stinging yet! And shee too Should dreame of Damon. — So, I am sure Still Cupids factor: well ere long I see There will be many an heire the more for mee. Another lover, Enamour'd of the Ground! Slid; I thinke he takes mee For Oberon already. Who, the madman? Every evening He walkes abroad into the vallie here With Thestylis. Farewell old walking Ivibush. Bold mortall, hold thy hand. Did we out of love And our intire affection, of all Orchards Chuse your's to make it happy by our dances, Light ayry measures, and fantastique rings! And you ingratefull mortall thus requites us. All for one Apple! You know, Iocastus, Our Grace have Orchards of our owne more precious Then mortals can have any: And we sent you A Present of them t'other day. I must confesse Their outside look'd something like yours indeed; But then the tast more relish'd of eternitie, The same with Nectar. They cannot speake this language, but in ours They thank you, and they say they will have none, They doe request you now To grant them leave to dance a Fayry ring About your servant, and for his offence Pinch him: doe you the while command the traitour Not dareto stirre, not once presume to mutter. Ti ti tatie. Our noble freind permits, Tititatie: Doe you not sir? Ti ti ta tie. And rise up Sir Iocastus, our deare Knight. Now hang the hallowed bell about his neck, We call it a mellisonant Tingle Tangle, (Indeed a sheep-bell stolne from's own fat wether.) The ensigne of his knighthood. Sir Iocastus, Wee call to mind we promis'd you long since The President of our Dances place; we are now Pleas'd to confirme it on you: give him there His Staffe of Dignity. Now begone. O good Mopsus! Good Mopsus but conceale us, And I will promise by to morrow night To get thee Thestylis. And I will. Well: We do … . Nay be not so discourteous; Stay and take An apple first: you Iocalo give him one, And you another, and our Grace a third. Harke, Thestylis, the birds doe bid you joy. A beauteous Lady, bright, and rare, Queen Mab her selfe is not so faire. Yes beauteous virgin; Thy each part Has shot an arrow through my heart; Thy blazing eye, thy lip so thinne, Thy azure cheek, & christall chinne, Thy rainbow brow, with many a rose; Thy saphyre eares, and ruby nose, All wound my soule, O gentle be Or Lady you will ruin mee. How's this? are you no woman then? Can such bright beauty live with men? Indeed I thought no man but hee Could of such perfect beauty bee. I have an hearb, they Moly call, Can change thy shape (my sweet) and shall. To tast this Moly but agree, And thou shalt perfect woman bee. My Queene Iocasta thou shalt bee: Halfe your estate then give to mee, Else, you being gone, there none will be, Whose Orchard I dare here frequent. The other halfe let Mopsus take. Come let us walke, majestique Queene, Of Fairy mortalls to be seene. In chaires of Pearle thou plac't shalt bee, And Empresses shall envy thee, When they behold upon our throne Iocasta with her— Dorilas.

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
