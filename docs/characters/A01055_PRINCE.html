Why all this Company? I beleeue it. Of me? my subiects talke of me? Ile borrow patience A little time to listen to these wrongs, And from the few of you which are here present, Conceiue the generall voyce. By all your loues I charge ye, without feare Or flattery, to let me know your thoughts, And how I am interpreted: Speake boldly. So—Tutor, your conceit? You are a Courtier. You haue not spoken yet. You are somewhat long a thinking. Am I not worthy of your thought? Pitty? Is it a misery to be a Prince? Your Soueraigne whiles he liu'd. But what of him? I charge thee by the duty that thou ow'st vs, be plaine in what thou meanest to speake: there's some thing that we must know: be free, our eares are open. This is meere trifling—Ha? are all stollen hence? We are alone: Thou hast an honest looke, Thou hast a tongue, I hope, that is not oyld With flattery. Be open, though tis true, That in my younger dayes I oft haue heard Agenors name, my Father, more traduc'd, Then I could then obserue; yet I protest, I neuer had a friend, a certaine friend, That would informe me throughly of such errors, As oftentimes are incident to Princes. I see then I must court thee. Take the word Of a iust Prince for any thing thou speakest. I haue more then a Pardon, thankes and loue. She was a louely beauty: Prethee forward. Good call it backe agen into thy memory, Else losing the remainder, I am lost too. But what became of faire Eroclea? No hope liues then Of euer, euer seeing her againe. I am prepar'd to heare, say what you please. My eare is open to thee. In habit of a man? If I did loue her, Rhetias : yes I did. Giue me thy hand: As thou didst serue Meleander, And art still true to these, henceforth serue me. Forget the sadder story of my Father, And onely Rhetias, learne to reade me well, For I must euer thanke thee; th'ast vnlockt A tongue was vow'd to silence, for requitall Open my bosome, Rhetias. To tye thee to an oath of secrecy— Vnloose the buttons, man, thou dost it faintly, What findst thou there? Looke well vpon't. Whose! Hers that was once Eroclea : for her sake Haue I aduanst Sophronos to the Helme Of gouernment; for her sake will restore Meleanders Honours to him; will for her sake Beg friendship from thee, Rhetias. O be faithfull, And let no politicke Lord worke from thy bosome My griefes: I know thou wert put on to sift me: But be not too secure. Continue still thy discontented fashion: Humour the Lords, as they would humour me; Ile not liue in thy debt.—We are discouer'd. It is our duty To loue the vertuous. Your commendations, Sweet Cousin, speakes him Nobly. What fortune led him to Cyprus! Some other time. How is a cald? Parthenophill? Wee shall sort time to take more notice of him. We will obserue it. The name of this conceit. Here I finde it. Hydrophobia terme you this? I not dislike the course. True. You name this here, Hypocondriacall. Tis very strange: but Heau'n is full of miracles. We are thy debtor ( Corax ) for the gift Of this inuention: but the plot deceiues vs; What meanes this empty space. Hold, let no man henceforth name the word agen Wait you my pleasure, Youth; tis late, to rest. Yee haue consented all to worke vpon The softnesse of my nature; but take heede: Though I can sleepe in silence, and looke on The mockery yee make of my dull patience; Yet'ee shall know, the best of yee, that in mee There is a masculin, a stirring spirit; Which prouokt, shall like a bearded Comet Set yee at gaze, and threaten horrour. Flye then to the desart, Where thou didst first encounter this Fantasticke, This airie apparition; come no more In sight: Get yee all from me; he that stayes, Is not my friend. Some angry power, cheates with rare delusions, My credulous sense: the very soule of Reason Is troubled in me—the Physician Presented a strange Maske, the view of it Puzzl'd my vnderstanding: but the Boy— Rhetias, thou art acquainted with my griefes, Parthenophill is lost, and I would see him; For he is like to some thing I remember A great while since, a long, long time agoe. How Rhetias! The young man in disguise vpon my life, To steale out of the Land. Doe, doe my Rhetias. As there is by nature In euery thing created contrarietie: So likewise is there vnity and league Betweene them in their kind; but Man, the abstract Of all perfection, which the workmanship Of Heauen hath model'd, in himselfe containes Passions of seuerall qualitie, the musicke Of mans faire composition best accords, When tis in consort, not in single straines. My heart has been vntun'd these many moneths, Wanting her presence, in whose equall loue True harmony consisted; liuing here We are Heau'ns bounty all, but Fortunes exercise. What Eccho yeelds a voyce to my complaints? Can I be no where priuate? Stand vp; tis not the figure stampt vpon thy cheekes, The coozenage of thy beauty, grace, or tongue, Can draw from me a secret, that hath been The onely lewell of my speechlesse thoughts. Cunning Impostor, Vntruth hath made thee subtle in thy trade: If any neighbouring Greatnesse hath seduc'd A free-borne resolution, to attempt Some bolder act of treachery, by cutting My weary dayes off. Wherefore ( Cruell-mercy ) Hast thou assum'd a shape, that would make treason A piety, guilt pardonable, blood-shed As holy as the sacrifice of peace? Ioyne not too fast Thy penance, with the story of my suffrings. So dwelt simplicity with virgin truth ; So Martyrdome and holinesse are twins, As innocence and sweetnesse on thy tongue. But let me by degrees collect my senses, I may abuse my trust. Tell me, what ayre Hast thou perfum'd, since Tyranny first rauisht The contract of our hearts? Buried! Right, as I In Cyprus. —Come to triall, if thou beest Eroclea, in my bosome I can finde thee. We are but Fooles To trifle in disputes, or vainely struggle With that eternall mercy which protects vs. Come home, home to my heart, thou banisht-peace, My extasie of ioyes would speake in passion, But that I would not lose that part of man, Which is reseru'd to intertaine content. Eroclea, I am thine; O let me seize thee As my inheritance. Hymen shall now Set all his Torches burning, to giue light Throughout this Land, new settled in thy welcome. Enough, in stead of Musicke, euery night To make our sleepes delightfull, thou shalt cloze Our weary eyes with some part of thy story. Feare not: to behold Eroclea safe, will make him young againe; It shall be our first taske. Blush sensuall follies, A Cast-away, a poore despised Maid, Onely for me to hope was almost sinne, Yet troth I neuer tempted him. Thus Princes should be circled with a guard Of truly noble friends, and watchfull subiects. O Rhetias, thou art iust; the Youth thou told'st me. That liu'd at Athens, is returnd at last To her owne fortunes, and contracted I oue. We must part: The sudden meeting of these two faire Riuolets With th' Iland of our armes, Cleophila, The custome of thy piety hath built Euen to thy younger yeeres a Monument Of memorable Fame; some great reward Must wait on thy desert. Away then. Fly hence, shadowes, that doe keep Watchfull sorrowes, charm'd in sleepe; Though the Eyes be ouertaken, Yet the Heart doth euer waken Thoughts, chain'd vp in busie snares Of continuall woes and cares: Loue and griefes are so exprest, As they rather sigh then rest. Ye shall not kneele to vs; rise all, I charge ye: Father, you wrong your age, henceforth my armes And heart shall be your guard; we haue o're-heard All passages of your vnited loues. Be young againe, Meleander, liue to number An happy generation, and dye old In comforts as in yeeres. The Offices And Honours which I late on thee conferr'd, Are not fantasticke bounties, but thy merit; Enioy them liberally. Good man, wee both haue shar'd enough of sadnes: Though thine ha's tasted deeper of th'extreme; Let vs forget it henceforth. Where's the picture I sent yee? Keepe it, tis a counterfeit, And in exchange of that, I ceaze on this, The reall substance: with this other hand I giue away before her Fathers face His younger ioy, Cleophila, to thee Cousin Amethus : take her, and be to her More then a Father, a deseruing husband. Thus rob'd of both thy children in a minute, Thy cares are taken off. Eroclea was you know, contracted mine; Cleophila, my Cousins by consent Of both their hearts: We both now claime our owne; It onely rests in you to giue a blessing For confirmation. Leaue the rest to time. Cousin Thamasta, I must giue you too: She's thy wife, Menaphon. Rhetias, for thee And Corax, I haue more then common thanks. On, to the Temple; there all solemne Rites Perform'd, a generall Feast shall be proclaim'd. The Louers Melancholy hath found cure; Sorrowes are chang'd to Bride-songs. So they thriue, Whom Fate in spite of stormes hath kept aliue. TO be too confident, is as vniust In any Worke, as too much to distrust; Who from the lawes of study haue not sweru'd, Know, beg'd applauses neuer were deseru'd. We must submit to Censure: so doth He, Whose houres begot this issue; yet being free For his part, if He haue not pleas'd you, then In this kinde, hee'le not trouble you agen.

Plot summary

Plot — 420
A group of mounted knights arrive at a wedding, and one of their number explains their presence. They worship Diana for as long as they remain unmarried, but also sacrifice to Hymenaeus, god of marriage. When they were last doing so, the god spoke, telling them one of Diana’s nymphs had got married to an earl favoured by Minerva. Diana is particularly pleased to know that the couple have agreed to wait a year before consummating the marriage. The two goddesses have asked Hymenaeus to arrange for the marriage- feast to be honoured, so he sends the knights with gifts: Minerva sends the groom a garland, Diana sends the bride a veil, and Hymenaeus sends them respectively a picture of a dove and a coronet. The knights present their shields and dance with ladies from the audience.
