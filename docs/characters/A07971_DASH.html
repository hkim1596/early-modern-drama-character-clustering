Gentlemen my selfe, and all that depends on my selfe, or on any thing that hath dependance on my selfe, is at your service. Sweet young Gentleman, you are the Epitome of a faire body, and shall command the Commander of my selfe and family. I will but present a glasse of Greeke Sacke to the hands of a noble Lord, and returne to serve you. Now Gentlemen dispose of your seruant. How I naught I who drew it? name but your drawer; he is punish't whilst you pronounce it. I'le not keepe an offensive mouse that eats the crums under my ta ble, but shall pay his life to doe you service. I am bound to serve you Gentlemen, and I wish my roofe were worthier, and my disordered houshould order'd to your content. Gentlemen, your servant will send his servants to wait upon you presently. What Gentlemen, none to attend you? (whooh) An unlook'd for happines that my unworthy selfe is prefer'd. I am the servant of my servant that shall serve you. And unlesse hee serve you he is not my servant. I'le tunre them all away presently for this neglect of your worthy Persons. I can assure you Gentlemen, the Grape from whence this Sacke was prest— I would have fetcht it farther Gentlemen to doe you service. A voyage to the Indies should bee no more then a descending into my cellar, and up agen. Gentlemen; I intended a paire of Scales with a glasse of Wine in one Ballance, and a piece of gold in the other, or a jewell. Sweet Gentlemen, I am the servant of your noble wits. I must kisse the a re hand of an honourable Lord, who is now departing: I will then returne to be dispos'd by you. The best orders that can be kept in my hous or any office of my house shall bee at your Worships ser vice. Worthy Sir, I have now made ready a roome to entertaine your worthy person. My house was so cramd with Lords; and this especially with most choyce gentle men, some of the admired wits, to which very name I owe respect and service. Questionlesse, Sir, there are of the wits some mercenary ones, whom your money may command to be your servants. But these are of a nobler straine. Howsoe ver, I will upon the next occasion shew my desire to doe you service. Sweet Mris. Tongall, my best tongue, and all is at your service. I kisse the hand of your servant. Noble sir, I presume to present my desires to doe you service. I retribute all due thanks for your kindnesse, that you would doe me so great an honour. What will that Gentleman with his table-booke! (Whooh) be nimble sirrah; and bring of my kingdome (that's my word for good wine) that it may wait on these Gentlemen. Sure this Gentleman write's what I speake. I hope 'twas not treason to say my Kingdome. I would I knew what he were. Kind and worthy Sir, my life is your servant for this noble care. Sir, I owe your goodnesse all that ever I have beene, am, or shall be. He writes againe. To undoe me. Sir, I desire you will command all that is to bee commanded in my house to doe you ser vice. Yes, yes; he writes againe. Alas Sir, Mris Tongall, 'twas not neighbourly done of you to bring an informer into my hous . I believe it, to undoe a poore Vintener, that cannot complement a Gentleman into a ten pounds ex pence; but his necke must be in danger. Sir, if I were a King I would be your servant. He writes againe. Alas, Sir, I, am undone then. Yes, yes; to apprehend me. I feare I shall be prov'd both. Mr. Constable, I am your servant, I hope you suspect no Traitors in my house. If you doe, they shall wait upon you into the Cellar; and there commit what treason you will against as good Sack as is in the King of Spaines Dominions. The Gentleman writes still. I am ut terly lost. I feare I must wait upon the Gallowes. Yes, yes; a meere plot to goe along, that hee may witnesse against me. Most gladly Sir, and bee your servant. But how shall I be sure of it? I am satisfied: and will be your servant in any thing but treason. Let mee bee your servant Mr. Constable, and light you downe. I hope my house will receive no scan dall by this.
