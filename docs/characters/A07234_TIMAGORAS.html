Full speech

'Tis well, prouide my Horse. 'Twas a deed Deseruing rather Trophees, then reproofe. I am so farre from enuie, I am proud You haue outstrip'd me in the race of honour. O 'twas a glorious day, and brauely wonne! Your bold performance gaue such lustre to Timoleons wise directions, as the Armie Rests doubtfull, to whom they stand most ingag'de For their so great successe. You abuse your fortune, To entertaine her choyce, and gratious fauours, With a contracted browe; Plum'd victorie Is truly painted with a cheerefull looke, Equally distant from proud insolence, And base deiection. Tush, these feares are needlesse, Shee cannot, must not, shall not be so cruell. A free confession of a fault winnes pardon; But being seconded by desert, commands it. The Generall is your owne, and sure; my Father Repents his harshnesse: for my selfe, I am Euer your creature, one day shall be happy In your triumph, and your Mariage. Euer touching On that harsh string? she is your owne, and you Without disturbance seaze on what's your due. I long since Expected, that the virgins, and the Matrons, The old men striuing with their age, the Priests Carrying the Images of their gods before 'em Should haue met vs with Procession: Ha! the gates Are shut against vs! The misterie, Rascalls? Open the ports, and play not with an anger, That will consume you. Can we indure this? Scorn'd too! are you turn'd stone? Hold parley with our Bond-men? force our entrance, Then Villaines, expect. Hell, and Furies! O, the gods! Vnheard of insolence! Let the Citie sinke first. Make a firme stand: The slaues not satisfied, they haue beat vs off, Prepare to sally forth. The same wise thing; 'Slight, what a beast they haue made thee! Affricke neuer Produc'd the like. 'Tis a ghest In wisdome neuer to be entertain'd On triuiall probabilities; but when Hee does appeare in pregnant proofes, not fashion'd By idle doubts and feares, to be receiu'd, They make their owne hornes, that are too secure, As well as such as giue them grouth, and being From meere imagination. Though I prize Cleora's honour equall with mine owne; And know what large additions of power This match brings to our family; I preferre Our friendship, and your peace of minde so farre Aboue my owne respects, or hers, that if Shee hold not her true value in the test, 'Tis farre from my ambition for her cure, That you should wound your selfe. 'Tis vnfit then, That I should presse it further. What would Timandra? Collect thy selfe, and speake. If you belieue so, Follow the chase still: And in open court Plead your owne interest; we shall finde the Iudge Our friend I feare not. Are you stupid? 'Slight leape into his armes, and there aske pardon. O, you expect your slaues reply, no doubt We shall haue a fine oration; I will teach My Spaniell to howle in sweeter language, And keepe a better method. 'Tis the bold Theban! I will studie In my future seruice to descerue your fauour And good opinion.

Plot summary

Plot — The Bondman (The Noble Bondman)
Carthage has territorial designs on Sicily, so Corinth sends its general, Timoleon, to take charge of the defence of Syracuse. On arrival, he criticizes the Sicilians for appointing unworthy and inexperienced senators and for general corruption. He decrees that all private wealth should be sequestered to pay for the war. It is an unpopular measure until Cleora public- spiritedly gives up her jewels, forcing the others to follow her example. Cleora has several suitors, including Pisander of Thebes; but her brother Timagoras, keen for her to marry his friend Leosthenes, slandered Pisander to induce their father to send him away. Now that the young men are going to war, Leosthenes is jealous about what she may get up to in his absence, so she promises literally not to see or say anything until his return. The slaves of Syracuse take advantage of the absence of the army and rebel, enslaving their former masters. The ringleader is Cleora’s slave Marullo, who claims to be motivated by unrequited passion for his mistress, but, when he gains access to her, disclaims any intention of raping her and receives in return a wordless acknowledgement of her regard for him. He encourages the former slaves to treat their former masters well, since their positions may again be reversed. News arrives of a victory over the Carthaginians, with particular military distinction achieved by Leosthenes. The rebellious slaves man the city’s walls and gates to keep out the returning army; from the walls, Marullo declares their grievances and demands their freedom. Timoleon realizes they will have to fight their way into the city, but the slaves put up a valiant resistance until cowed by the sight of their masters’ whips. Marullo flees to Cleora’s house, where he is captured. Cleora tells Leosthenes what happened, and asks him to use his influence to have Marullo spared, but this only makes Leosthenes jealous again. Cleora’s father gives her freedom to choose her own husband; he respects Marullo for his restraint in not ransacking the house during the revolt. Cleora realizes she must marry either Leosthenes or Marullo: both are worthy of her, but she is bound to disappoint one. On hearing that Marullo has been ill-treated by his captors, she rushes off to visit him in prison. Leosthenes and Timagoras follow, alerted by her maid, and find them alone together.
