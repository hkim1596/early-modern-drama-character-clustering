From forth the depth of my imprisoned soule, Steale you my sighes, testifie my paine, Conuey on wings of mine immortall tone, My zealous praiers, vnto the starrie throne: Ah mercifull and iust thou dreadfull God, Where is thine arme to laie reuengefull stroakes Upon the heads of our rebellious race? Loe Israell once that flourisht like the vine, Is barraine laide, the beautifull encrease Is wholly blent, and irreligious zeale Incampeth there where vertue was inthroan'd Ah-lasse the while, the widow wants reliefe, The fatherlesse is wrongd by naked néed, Deuotion sléepes in sinders of Contempt, Hypocrisie infects the holie Priest, Aye me for this, woe me for these misdéeds, Alone I walke to thinke vpon the world, And sigh to sée thy Prophets so contem'd: Ah-lasse contem'd by cursed Israell. Yet Ionas rest content, tis Israels sinne That causeth this, then muse no more thereon, But pray amends, and mend thy owne amisse. Plot summary

Plot — A Looking Glass for London and England
Triumphant over the King of Jerusalem, the Assyrian king Rasni declares himself divine and marries his sister, Remilia; a viceroy who objects is banished and the flatterer Radagon given his place. Inordinately proud of her beauty, Remilia is struck by lightning. Rasni is unable to revive her, and buries her in state; but is encouraged by Radagon to think of the living rather than the dead, and to take Alvida, wife of the King of Paphlagonia. When her husband objects to the adulterous liaison, she poisons him. Alvida continues to be unfaithful: her ambition attracts her to Rasni, but her sexual appetite favours the Viceroy of Cilicia, so she takes both. Thrasybulus and Alcon are both cheated by a usurer to whom they are trying to repay money: he keeps them talking beyond the due hour and then secures possession of Thrasybulus’ farm and Alcon’s cow as a penalty for non-payment. They attempt to sue him, but he bribes both the judge and their lawyer, and they lose their cases. Alcon appeals to Radagon, his son, to help his starving family, but Radagon is too proud to acknowledge his parent, and Rasni has him ejected from the court. Radagon’s mother curses him, and: he is swallowed by subterranean fire. The smith catches them canoodling, but when he attempts to take reprisals, Adam argues that he is the one who should be beaten for the sin of jealousy. An angel orders Jonah to preach against Nineveh’s wickedness. He is reluctant, and takes a ship to Tarshish instead. The ship encounters a storm, which Jonah identifies as a sign of divine displeasure at his disobedience. He has himself thrown overboard, and the storm abates. A whale swallows him, then belks him up on shore. He goes to Nineveh as ordered. These events are watched and commented on by the prophet Hosea, brought from Jerusalem by an angel.
