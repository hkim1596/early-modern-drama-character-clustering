Full speech

Sir, the respects I beare you, and the obedience I owe my husband that commanded it, brought me over willingly to offer my service to so noble a freind. A rot on the best linings of your three pil'd durable; your everlasting almanack of high dayes, feastes and sessions: was it my desire? Thou lyest thou wrong side of a lawyer turn'd outwards: I had better businesse at home. I could have seen if mother Whirl had spun the last pound of flax I sent her, or called at Knocks the weavers for my new Napkins. I have no maides to cudgell their tasks out of. Indeed I can hardly keepe any for such a goat as thou art. May shee make him a happy wife; and prove obedient from my example. I cannot in obedience but allow of his wisdom. Thou changling nurst with the milke of an asse whence thou derivest thy great empty noddle: must you hinder a good motion? Are there not cuckolds enough already? And Mistresse Bride! send you joy. You are complaind of by these— Is she not your wife? Truly Mistresse Bride I should be glad to apprehend you in bed together: 'twas expected all had been cock sure by this. 'Twas but a plot of your old foster father to pretend mar rying her himselfe when he meant her onely for you. To expresse it more fully, and how deere you are to him, he hath conserred his whole estate upon you. My Worship full husbands hand is to the writing. Is it so sir? as if my word were not sufficient! Command both me and mine husband. And welcome with my husbands good liking. To your house? as if the enterteinment of constables on a sharing day were not chargeable enough, but you must complement a way wine and sweet meats. 'Tis enough that we wish them well; let others do them good. The motion's good: it should have been mine sir. You shall see the feathers of a Phenix; the beake of a Pelican, and the skins of … irds, beasts and fishes, stufft with hay, enough to bring down the marker. For coynes and meddalls he hath those with inscriptions that speake their date 500 yeeres before the use of letters. He hath the fingers and toes of a Colossus, and three hayres of a giants upper lip, each of them as big as a bull rush. Then he hath the the members of a pignie, that cannot be discern'd but through a multiplying glas. Yet it is thought he would gladly part with all he hath for the Phylosophers stone; I or the Unicornes horne at Windsor. My husband will still be before me, to prevent the a men of a shrew. Fer. and Hort. whisper. These gentlemen are witnesses to the old mans deeds, as well as my worshipfull husband Are you making motions now, before I can recover breath to begin my exordium as my sonne at the University taught me to say. Neighbour Horten 'tis thus. My selfe and my husband have this day been eyewitnessesses of strange accidents; chance upon chance, and fortune upon fortune: one disaster hath been the cause and cure of another. Now we desire the fa vour that we may see your trinkets, knaks and knaveries (par don me neighbour I meane no hurt) to delight this melancho lick gentlewoman that hath found and lost, and lost and found a husband yet no husband— And shall we see all? The great Sea horse what you call that cures women of the crampe. What justice sir? My husbands name is Ferret: what's your businesse sir? With what tro? a reproofe or a jeer out of your ta ble book notes, enlarg'd with a peece of a charge; then with a half bribe and single fees they are released to boast how cheap they have scaped. Come come you shall back to this house, and be se vere, and wise and say little, let mine be the speaking part, yours the doing: I'le make them come off and on roundly. Plot summary

Plot — 576
Wit is charged by his father to woo and marry Wisdom, or lose his inheritance, Ignorance sends her son Idleness to mislead him. Posing as Honest Recreation, he introduces Wit to Wantonness, who gives him a fool's headdress and steals his money. Good Nurture restores him to sense and introduces him to the true Honest Recreation, whom Wit finds boring. Two sailors blindfold Idleness and rob him of the money he stole from Wit. Wit rescues Idleness and fights Irksomeness, who leaves him for dead. Wisdom revives him and promises to be his lady if he will kill Irksomeness. She gives him a sword, Perseverance, which he uses to behead the monster. Idleness is proclaimed an outlaw, and is arrested after he steals Mother Bee’s porridge pot while Doll and Lob are kissing in the barn. Wit falls into Fancy’s clutches and is imprisoned. Good Nurture releases him and takes him to be married to Wisdom. Idleness also intends to go to the wedding, disguised as a priest.
