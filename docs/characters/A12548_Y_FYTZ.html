Full speech

Since I was cast vpon this fatall Rocke, And saw my Loue disseuered by the waues, And my kinde Steward in the Ocean drownd, Here I haue liu'd, fed onely with raw Fish, Such as the Sea yeelds: and each Shippe I see, (As dayly there are some furrow this way) I call vnto for ayde, but nere the neere. Once ask't me, What I was? I answer'd him, An Englishman. Quoth he, Stay there and starue. To the next that past, I sayd I was a French-borne. Ile ayde no French quoth he. Vnto a third, That I a Spaniard was. He bad me hang : So that I know not what I ought to say, Nor whom to speake to: but in happy time, From this high Rocke, I see a tall Shippe come, Furnisht with all his Sayles; and as it ploughes The Ocean vp, it rayses hills of snow, That fly on both sides as they did giue way, To make a valley for the Shippe to passe. Their Captaine as I thinke lookes vpon me, And has tooke notice of my wauing hand. Now the Ship turnesand this way ploughes amaine, As if it meant to runne it selfe aground: In happy time, now I shall be relieu'd. Your answere there? In the honourable entercourse of men I should doe so, and were you in my case, You would inforce your owne necessitie. I would be as thou art, proud of nothing. As it ankers here It beares a goodly show; but launcht againe, And a stormerise, it may be cast aland As I haue beene: nay worse, it may may be sunke, And then what is't, but a fayre something, nothing? What is, and now is not; mans life, or a dreame, Now swimming, and then swallowed in the streame. And sword but that the Sea deuour'd it. Wilt thou turne honest? I would change your minde From this erronious and ill boding thought, Because of late you freed me from the rocke, But if it be so hurtfull to your sight Be your own death, Ile not reueale my minde. According to your Maiesties commaund, I come to know your pleasure for the Letter I should deliuer to the English King, With that base strumpet that has Iniur'd you. And you the prisoner to be safe lockt vp, For your incontinence and wanton life. So confident, her innocence is great, That can doe this sincerely without trickes: But if you be the same that you would seeme, How comes that your reputations growne, Into such scandall, and your name the theame, Of euery idle fellow in the Court? That Groomes report, faire Infortunate is: The French Kings loue; Nay worse his concubine. The voyce of men is held the voyce of God: And where an euill is so farre proclaimde, The generality approues the guilt, And shees vnworthy to surviue a minute, To be the separation of two hearts, Made one by Marriage. But you did kisse the King. Rise, rise, hereafter the discourse Ile tell, Meane time Fytzwaters welcomes Floramell. Runs the stream this way, is the wind turnd thus? In sight of Heauen I vowe Shee is as chaste for any lust from mee, As vnborne Infants, and I vse her loue, But to acuance my soueraig e and his Realme. No other case by honour I p o e t, In signe whereof I oppose n innocent breast Against the sword: if you beleeue not, kill, But neuer man died for a lesser ill. To their great seruice, greater I oppose, And doe beseech your Highnes wrong me not. Fathers both shee shall not. All lets are now remou'd, I am truly happy.

Plot summary

Plot — The Hector of Germany, or The Palsgrave, Prime Elector
. Defeated in his attempt to seize power in Spain, Henry the Bastard attempts to get himself elected Holy Roman Emperor; he secures the support of three of the seven Electors, promising that Saxony will be the power behind his throne. But the other Electors, including the sickly Palsgrave, prefer the Duke of Savoy’s candidature, so the Bastard uses force: he defeats the opposing Electors in battle, imprisons two of them, and has himself crowned, then breaks his word and excludes Saxony from power. News of the ===== PAGE 311 ===== 1613 usurpation cures the Palsgrave, who prepares to mobilize an army. Spain is the theatre of war for their first confrontation. The Palsgrave is victorious, and the Bastard is captured, but rescued by Saxony. The warring potentates seek alliances against one another: the Palsgrave goes to England, the Bastard to France. The Palsgrave competes in an English tournament and is installed in the Order of the Garter. King John of France is reluctant to fight the English again (they have too good a track record of victory in France), and hopes to use diplomacy to secure Edward III’s neutrality. Refused the chance to conduct the mission, Saxony goes to England in disguise, but is identified by the Palsgrave, who dishonours him by pulling off a lock of his hair. Diplomacy fails, and Edward and the Palsgrave invade France, dispatching troops to free the imprisoned Electors. The Palsgrave defeats and kills Saxony in single combat. King John withdraws his support from the Bastard, and Savoy is formally crowned Emperor. In the sub-plot, Lord Fitzwaters is betrothed to Floramell, but discovers that she loves his son. He witnesses a secret assignation between the lovers in the garden of Lord Clinton, Floramell’s father. Clinton orders Young Fitzwaters off his land; Lord Fitzwaters goes further and banishes him from England. Floramell goes with him, leaving in her place a cross-dressed page, whom Lord Fitzwaters ‘marries’. The lovers are separated in a shipwreck: Floramell is washed up on the French coast and becomes a maid of honour at court; Young Fitzwaters is marooned on a barren rock and subsists on a fish diet until he is rescued by Saxony. King John is attracted to Floramell and offers to make way for her by having the Queen murdered. Floramell will not consent to adultery or murder, but agrees to give him a single kiss.
