Excellent, His Iewell for her Iewell, well put in Duke. That's better she must weare his Iewell lower. No the diuell was in your dreame. Excellent Diuell. Shee hath taught him in a dreame To make away his Dutchesse and her husband. What fury rais'd thee vp? away, away I pray will you go to bed then, Least you be blasted. How? come to Rome, So, haue you done? Are you out of your wits, my Lord Ile fetch her backe againe? Now, you that stand so much vpon your honour, Is this a fitting time a night thinke you, To send a Duke home without ere a man: I would faine know where lies the masse of wealth Which you haue whoorded for my maintenance, That I may beare my beard out of the leuell Of my Lords Stirop. Pray what meanes haue you To keepe me from the gallies, or the gallowes? My father prou'd himselfe a Gentleman, Sold al's land, and like a fortunate fellow, Died ere the money was spent. You brought me vp, At Padua I confesse, where I protest For want of meanes, the Vniuersity iudge me, I haue bene faine to heele my Tutors stockings At least seuen yeares: Conspiring with a beard Made me a Graduate, then to this Dukes seruice, I visited the Court, whence I return'd: More courteous, more letcherous by farre, But not a suite the richer, and shall I, Hauing a path so open and so free To my preferment, still retaine your milke In my pale forehead, no this face of mine I'le arme and fortefie with lusty wine, Gainst shame and blushing. So would I. I would the common'st Courtezan in Rome, Had bene my mother rather then thy selfe. Nature is very pittfull to whoores To giue them but few children, yet those children Plurality of fathers, they are sure They shall not want. Go, go, Complaine vnto my great Lord Cardinall, Yet may be he will iustifie the act. Lycurgus wondred much men would prouide Good stalions for their Mares, and yet would suffer Their faire wiues to be barren, The Dutchesse come to Court, I like not that, Wee are ingag'd to mischiefe and must on. As Riuers to finde out the Ocean Flow with crooke bendings beneath forced bankes, Or as wee see to aspire some mountaines top, The way ascends not straight, but Imitates The suttle fouldings of a Winters snake, So who knowes policy and her true aspect, Shall finde her waies winding and indirect. My Lord do you marke their whispering, I will com pound a medicine out of their two heads, stronger then garlick, deadlier then stibium, the Cantarides which are scarce seene to sticke vpon the flesh when they work to the heart, shall not do it with more silence or inuisible cunning. They are sending him to Naples, but I'le send him to Candy, her's another property to. He will shoot pils into a mans guts, shall make them haue more ventages then a cornet or a lamprey, hee will poyson a kisse, and was once minded, for his Master-peece, because Ire land breeds no poyson, to haue prepared a deadly vapour in a Spaniards fart that should haue poison'd all Dublin. O thou cursed antipathy to nature, looke his eyes bloud-shed like a needle a Chirurgeon stitcheth a wound with, let me embrace thee toad, & loue thee ô thou abhominable loth some gargarisme, that will fetch vp lungs, lights, heart, and liuer by scruples. He dies this night by such a polliticke straine, Men shall suppose him by's owne engine slaine. But for your Dutchesse death. Remember this you slaue, when knaues come to pre ferment they rise as gallouses are raised i'th low countries, one vpon another shoulders. Or cocoulds, for your cocould is your most terrible tickler of letchery: whoore-maisters would serue, for none are iudges at tilting, but those that haue bene old Tilters. You are a dull asse, 'tis threatned they haue bene very publicke. What then? A Cardinall I hope will not catch conyes. True, her vpper part by that rule, if you will win her nether part to, you know what followes. I do put on this feigned Garbe of mirth, To gull suspition. I made a kind of path To her & mine owne preferment. Hum! thou art a souldier, Followest the great Duke, feedest his victories, As witches do their seruiceable spirits, Euen with thy prodigall bloud, what hast got? But like the wealth of Captaines, a poore handfull, Which in thy palme thou bear'st, as men hold water Seeking to gripe it fast, the fraile reward Steales through thy fingers. Thou hast scarce maintenance To keepe thee in fresh shamoyes. Heare me, And thus when we haue euen powred ourselues, Into great fights, for their ambition Or idle spleene, how shall we find reward, But as we seldome find the mistle-towe Sacred to physicke: Or the builder Oke, Without a Mandrake by it, so in our quest of gaine. Alas the poorest of their forc'd dislikes At a limbe proffers, but at heart it strikes: This is lamented doctrine. When age shall turne thee, White as a blooming hauthorne. I saw him at last Tilting, he shewed like a peuter can dlesticke fashioned like a man in armour, houlding a Tilting staffe in his hand, little bigger then a candle of twelue i'th pound. A lame one in his lofty trickes, hee sleepes a horse backe like a poulter, He carries his face in's ruffe, as I haue seene a seruing man carry glasses in a cipres hat-band, monstrous steddy for feare of breaking, He lookes like the claw of a blacke-bird, first salted and then broyled in a candle. Why do you laugh? There was no such condition in our couenant. You shall not seeme a happier man then I, You know our vow sir, if you will be merry, Do it i'th like posture, as if some great man Sate while his enemy were executed: Though it be very letchery vnto thee, Doo't with a crabbed Polititians face. Ha? Dost euer thinke to speake againe? I do not greatly wonder you do breake: Your Lordship learn't long since. But Ile tell you, This laughter scuruily becomes your face, If you will not be melancholy, be angry. See now I laugh too. No, my Lord; You sister are the crocodile: you are blemisht in your fame, My Lord cures it. And though the comparison hold not in euery particle; yet obserue, remember, what good the bird with the pricke i'th head hath done you; and scorne ingratitude. It may appeare to some ridiculous Thus to talke knaue and madman; and sometimes Come in with a dried sentence, stuft with sage. But this allowes my varying of shapes, Knaues do grow great by being great mens apes. Their sattin cannot saue them. I am confident They haue a certaine spice of the disease, For they that sleep with dogs; shall rise with fleas. Are you cholericke? I'le purg't with Rubarbe. I cannot coniure; but if praiers or oathes VVill get to th'speech of him: though forty deuils VVaight on him in his liuery of flames, I'le speake to him, and shake him by the hand, Though I bee blasted. So, the wolfe and the rauen are very pretty fools when they are yong. Is it your office, sir, to keepe me out?

Florence and Monticelso plan to tempt Bracciano into adultery by getting Camillo out of Rome on public business, but Bracciano plots murder: Isabella is killed when she kisses a poisoned picture of Bracciano, and Flaminio breaks Camillo’s neck. Vittoria is arrested and arraigned before an ecclesiastical court; unable to prove her complicity in Camillo’s murder, it convicts her of adultery and commits her to a house of convertites. Flaminio feigns madness to evade interrogation. Lodovico witnesses Isabella’s murder. He returns to Rome illegally to bring Florence the news, but his pardon is signed after the event. He and Flaminio meet and quarrel. Florence borrows from Monticelso a list of villains, in order to select an agent of vengeance. By writing a letter feigning love for Vittoria, he induces Bracciano to contrive her escape from the house of convertites, disguised as a page. Monticelso is elected Pope; his first act is to excommunicate Bracciano and Vittoria. Florence sends Lodovico to Padua to assassinate Bracciano, and by trickery makes it appear that Monticelso supports the conspiracy. Bracciano marries Vittoria and accepts into his court Mulinassar, a Moorish soldier, who is actually Florence in disguise.
