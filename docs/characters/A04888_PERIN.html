No sir, you were not best. As how sir, by art, or by some foolish gift God hath giuen you, You are some Uissitian, or skild in Uisognomy, or in palmes ry For I am sure, you can neuer do it by Astronomie, Because there are no starres to knowe a knaue. Base Uillain, were it not hat the Kinges presence doth priuilege thy presu ̄ ptio ̄ , I wold teach you to iest with your fellows ▪ Why (base companion) meane you me? My gratious Lord, if I might not offend, I would intreat a fauour at your hand, Tis so, I heard of late, my gratious Lord, That my kinde father lay at poynt of death, And if (my Lord) I should not visite him, The world (I feare) would fynd great fault with me. My gratious Lord, I will not stay there long. Only but see my father and returne againe. Till when my grations Lord, I take my leaue. Brethren you haue spoken wel, I must needs say, But now giue eare to me, to me that keeps the court. Father, I liue as Aristipus did, & vse my wits to flatter with the king. If any in priuate conference name the king, I straight informe his Grace they enuie him: Did Sinon liue with all his subtiltie? He could not tell a flattering tale more cunninglie: Some tyme I moue the King to be effeminate, And spend his tyme with some coy Curtizan: Thus with the King I currie fa our still, Though with my heart I wish him any ill: And sometime I can counterfeit his hand and seale, And borrow money of the communalty: And thus I liue and flaunt it with the best, And dice and carde inferiour vnto none: And none dares speake against me in the court, Because they know the King doth fauour me. But where was the money tendered? Wel, if thou be wel aduised, take thy oath, But yet remember before whome thou swearest, The God of trueth and perfect equitie, Which will reuenge wrong to the innocent, with thousand plagues and tortors worse than death. Very wel, and what say you maister Squire. A plaine fellow, and his name is Honesty. I thank you sir, but I must dine with my honest friend here, els I would not refuse your gentle offer.

Plot summary

Plot — A Knack to Know a Knave
. . Concerned for the good order of his kingdom, King Edgar commissions Honesty to seek out and expose knaves. The dying Bailiff of Hexham advises his four sons to live by fraud and deceit in their various: professions, and to enrich themselves at the expense of the commonweal and the poor..He dies and is carried off by the devil. While Edgar deals with the case of 1592 Philarchus, a courtier who has abused his father, Honesty observes the corrupt dealings of the Bailiff’s sons: Cuthbert, a cony-catcher, who bears false witness in an attempt to take possession of a farm; Walter, a farmer, who oppresses his neighbours and exports corn to feed the enemy; John, a priest, who refuses to give to charity; and Perin, a courtier, who misuses the royal seal and sells Walter an export licence for his corn under pretext of raising revenues for the crown. Meanwhile honest members of the state, from knight to ploughman, complain of their economic decline. Edgar sends Ethenwald to woo Alfrida by proxy, but Ethenwald falls in love with her himself and marries her. The King forgives him and visits the couple (receiving a petition from the mad men of Gotham on the way), but the jealous Ethenwald is convinced that Edgar plans to cuckold him, and has Alfrida and the kitchen maid swap identities during the royal visit. An outraged Edgar sees through the subterfuge and plans to take revenge, but Bishop Dunstan, with the help of the devil, persuades him to pardon Ethenwald instead. Honesty exposes the various knaves he has observed, and, with royal permission, sentences them all to gruesome and unusual punishments.
