Then ide with me, Ile bring you to my sister Somerfield. That's true indeed. And what of that. And what of this Sir Oliuer, be plaine, Somer-fields heire, when should this be, Tut It cannot be, Some ten miles hence I saw the maid last night. Stand close Knight, close, And marke this Captaines humor, his name is Puffe, A dreames as a walkes, and thinkes no woman Sees him but is in loue with him. Nay good Sir Puffe, We haue to many mad men already, We will on Sir Ol uer, We will on, let me aloue to touchim, I wounder how my spirit did forbeare, To strike him on the face: had this beene spoke, Within my Liberties, had dyed for it. I there, there, that he will neuer mistrust. Downe with the slaue. A sirra, Ex: Face I knew my hands no longer could forbeare him, Why did you not strike the Knaue, sir Oliuer ? But then it was too late. Which I wil do. You say very right sir Oliuer, very right, I haue't in my noddle ifaith, That's all the fault Old Iustices haue, when they are at feasts, They wil bib hard, they wilbe fine: Sun-burnt Sufficient, foxt, or Columberd now and than, Now could I sit in my charyre at home and nod A drunkard to the stocks, by vertue of, The last statute arely. I am indeed. I doe, A light supper quoth you, pray God it be, Pray god I carry it cleanly, I am shure it lies, As heauy in my belly as moult lead, Yet lle goe see my Sister Sommerfield, I euen so late, Night is the mother of wit, as you may see, By Poets or rather Cunstables In their examinations at midnight, Weele lie together without marrying, Saue the Curats fees, and the parish a labour, Tis a thriuing course. Thats true, they fly indeed like wild-geese, In flocks, one in the breech of another. But the best is a small matter stayes them, And so farwell. I say as you say sister, but for the lawes, There are so many that men do stand in awe, Of none at all; take heed they steale not you. Who woes a widow with a faire full Moone Shall surely speed, beware of full Moones widdow, Will Smalshankes has your daughter no word but mum, My warrant you shall haue when time shall come. I my warrant widdow. My warrant can stretch far; no more but so, Twill serue to ketch a knaue, or fetch a Doe. Then he's a gentleman, for tis a true note of a gentleman, to make much of other mens wiues, bring him vp, a sirra, makes a much of your Choach mans wife, this geere will runne a wheeles then shortly, A man may make much more of a nother mans wife, then a can do of's owne. How's this? made her yours sir? per quam regulam, Nay we are letter'd sir, as well as you, Redde rationem per quam regulam. Sirra what are you? So am I. Agreed, take each his place, and heere the case Argued betwixt them two. Now Throte or neuer, stretch your selfe.

Plot summary

Plot — Ram Alley, or Merry Tricks
The rich heiress Constantia Somerfield disguises herself as a page and enters the employ of her beloved, Boucher, intending to keep an eye on his fidelity. The rich widow Taffeta drops a handkerchief to get Boucher’s attention, and invites him into her house. Despite a prophecy that a widow will endanger his life, soul, lands, and reputation, Boucher is attracted to her. He has several rivals, including the old knight Sir Oliver Smallshank and Captain Puff, who claims marital rights in Taffeta and threatens to attack anyone who shows any sexual interest in her. Sir Oliver comes to woo Taffeta, but hides under her farthingale when Puff arrives. Puff assaults her, and Sir Oliver is forced out of his hiding place. Seeing the advantages of being a courtier’s wife, Taffeta agrees to marry him. Sir Oliver’s younger son, Will, is in debt, and his lands are mortgaged to Throat the lawyer. He pretends to have eloped with Constantia, but the woman he introduces to his family is actually his whore, Frances. He leaves her with Throat while he goes to arrange the wedding, asking Throat to confirm his cover story: that he has expectations of becoming a lord. Throat tells her the truth, that Will is a penniless fortune-hunter, and woos the lady for himself. When Will takes her, ostensibly to a nocturnal marriage ceremony, Throat has them attacked in the darkened streets. They are separated from Frances, whom Throat then marries himself. Will arranges to have himself arrested for debt when he comes looking for his putative fiancée; Throat is initially unwilling to help him, but, encouraged by Frances, agrees to pay the debt and return Will’s mortgage if he will sign a legal document renouncing all prior claims to Constantia Somerfield. Will agrees, and gets his lands back and some of his debts paid. Constantia’s mother, Lady Somerfield, comes to town, and is told that Will has abducted her daughter. Throat introduces himself as her son-in-law, and is arrested. Meanwhile Frances steals away with her new husband’s jewels. She is arrested at a tavern, and thinks it a ruse to get her away from Throat’s man; when the debt turns out to be real, she tries to sweet-talk the sergeant into releasing her. She is redeemed by her accomplice, Beard, only to be ‘rescued’ from him by Will's elder brother, who still thinks her Constantia and intends to marry her himself.
