Yes perdie, as my Coach, and my fan, and a man or two that 〈…〉 turne, and other things which Ide bee loath euery one should see, because they shal not be common, I am in manner of a Lady in one point. I am fayre enough to bee a Widdow, Sir Quin tilian. One I must haue sir Vaughan. One I must haue, for now euery one seekes to crow ouer me. I muse why sir Adam Prickshaft flyes so farre from vs. I thinke (sauing your reuerence) Sir Adam a puppy, for a dog is the most louing creature to a christian that is, vnles it be a childe. Perdie sir Vaughan I cannot dance. O Sir Adam Prickshaft, you are a the bow hand wide, a long, yard I assure you: and as for Suitors, truelie they all goe downe with me, they haue all one flat answere. If I be you shall not ride me. Nay, eene as you please Captaine, it shal be at your choice. Discouer me? discouer what thou canst of me. Lay me open to the world? I? dam thee filthie Captaine, dam thy selfe. Wod I might once come to that damming. So thou wilt I warrant, in thy abhominable sinnes; Lord, Lord, howe many filthy wordes hast thou to answere for. VVhy dost call mee such horrible vngodlie names then? Wod thou shouldst wel know, I am no Maggot, but a meere Gentlewoman borne. I scorne thee not, not I. To mee? why Master Captaine, you know that I haue my choise of three or foure payre of Knights, and there fore haue small reason to flye out I know not how in a man of war. You know I am offered that by halfe a dozen. I am witnes young Gentlemen to that. Why doe you aske Madam? I thinke he's within an Inch of a foole. Troth I shall neuer bee enameld of a bare-headed man for this, what shift so euer I make. Good Master Tucca lets ha no coniuring heere. O Iesu it I know where abouts in London Babilon stands. If they draw sweet hearts, let vs shift for our elues. I pray you Master Tucca, will you send me the fiue pound you borrowed on me; O you cannot heare now, but Ile make you heare me and feele me too in another place, to your shame I warrant you, thou shalt not conny-catch mee for fiue pounds; he tooke it vp Sir Vaughan in your name, hee swore you sent for it to Mum withall, twas fiue pound in gold, as white as my kercher. I, to Mum withall, but hee playes mum-budget with me. I promise you has almost conuerted me, I pray bring forward your bald reasons M. Poet. By your leaue (Master Poet) but that Bush-naturall, is one a the trimmest, and most intanglingst beautie in a wo man. By my troth sweet Ladies, it's Cake and pudding to me, to see his face make faces, when hee reades his Songs and Sonnets.

Plot summary

Plot — Satiromastix, or The Untrussing of the Humorous Poet
Celestine and Sir Walter Terrill are married. At the wedding dinner, the King becomes enamoured of Celestine and induces the bridegroom to swear to give up his first night of sex with her: he must deliver her to court that evening, still a virgin. Her father, Sir Quintilian, devises a scheme to save both her honour and Sir Walter’s: he gives her a cup of poison, and she drinks it. She keeps her marriage vows, and Sir Walter delivers her body to the King as part of a masque. The King is abashed to discover that she is a chaste, faithful wife. Then she wakes up: it was only a sleeping draught, and Sir Quintilian has proved both the bride’s constancy and the bridegroom’s love. In the sub-plot, Sir Quintilian and two other knights all become interested in marrying the widow Mistress Miniver, and each independently bribes Tucca to advance his suit. Miniver seems most interested in the bald Sir Adam, so Sir Vaughan engages Horace to write a satire against baldness. He invites the ladies, including Miniver, to a banquet of plums, and there Horace recites his poem on the glories of hair. The poem has the desired effect on Miniver; but Sir Adam hires Horace’s rival, Crispinus, to write a defence of baldness. It all comes to naught, however, for Tucca ends up marrying Miniver himself. In the third plot, Horace’s bitter epigrams against Tucca get into circulation among the gallants. Tucca swears vengeance, and stabs Horace, but with an edgeless sword; Horace’s response is shown to derive from fear rather than physical pain. Horace is accused of giving up a useful trade, bricklaying, and of unlawfully arraigning his rivals on the stage. He is brought to court dressed as a satyr, and the King appoints Crispinus to judge him. Crowned with a nettle wreath, Horace is exposed as an impostor (the real Horace was a better poet), and is made to forswear his inclination to arrogance and personal satire.
