My noble Younker: Welcome Gentlemen, I want ſuch gueſts, heeres beere was neuer drunke, Freſh ſet a broach: puts downe the Diamont For liuely ſparkling: of tranſparent view, More cleare then amber, or faire orient pearle, Fetcht from the fartheſt Inde, In me? you know me well, Plaine Flutterkin, a downe, right dealing man, I haue no guardes to ſet me brauely out; But what I thinke I ſpeake, and freely too, Without deceit or ſimulation. It ſhall be done with much celerity. Heeres to that hand; That bleſt you ſo with croſſes of that kind. Then giue me leaue I will begin this round; This ſwelling cuppe I will drinke liuely out, Not one word more, before I ſeet about. I thanke you ſir, you ſhall not go vnpledgde: Here Paſquill, Hans, you two ſhall haue your ſhares: Your Worſhips welcome, you do grace mine houſe. Yea twenty, if you will, heeres Wine enough. The towne is full, good lyquor wais it round, What newes ſir? did you aske? Here is ſmall newes: our Church-men diſagree About opinions, which nere troubles me: I am a man, I hope, beleeues the right, Theres but one God, one true religion; One way to heauen, two or three to hell, If they teach right, according to Gods word. I will beleeue them, otherwiſe ile chuſe. No ſir, he does not, needs not, will not ſir; I will not miſſe you, if it ſhall pleaſe God Nothing ſhall ſtay me, nought ſhall keepe me backe. Mocke on my Gallants, ſee what will come on't, A ſhame on Lurchers, you haue kilde me vp. I ranne ſo faſt, ere I could oretake you. Then Ile be hang'd, good ſir, how can that runne? That doth but dade, can hardly goe or ſtand. Wheres Paſquill is he gone? Would I were with him in ſuch aequipage; Or he were here, for I am almoſt choak't. Not the leaſt droppe. Were you a thouſand Merchants; Serieantes, drinke againe. Or Younkers not a droppe, Thinke you Ile dye For want of wholeſome drinke? al's out, ſee there: So now I am well, can walke a mile or two, As luſtique as a Boore, and neere complaine. So nigh already; Oh, I ſee the houſe; I ſmell the Kitchen, ſee the Chimneis ſmoake. Come Serieant; put the better legge before; You ſhall ſpeake firſt, if well, Ile ſecond you. Out from the horrour of infernall deepes; Paſſe forwards on; for I muſt ſtay behind: Some ſmall occaſion biddes me ſtand aſide. I heare you Serieant, I can beare your mocks: You neuer knew fat men but honeſt yet; A good companion, full of mirth and wit; Leane iades caſt off, lye ſtaruing in a ditch; When plumper ſteeds are ſteemde among the rich. O happy you, that ſo did ſpend your time, In dangers great abroad, by ſea and land, While lazie Lurdaines lay and ſlept at home, You rayzde your fortunes, got a braue eſtate, And after all, now leade a Country life, Amongſt your neighbours with a vertuous wife.

Plot summary

Plot — Hans Beer-Pot (See Me and See Me Not)
Hans is sent to town to sell corn for his master, Cornelius; his mistress, Hanneke, gives him money to give to her younger son, a student, anda shilling on top for beer-money. Cornelius suspects that Hans is cheating him, and follows him to town. There he finds his son and son-in-law drinking with Hans at a tavern; they have been losing bets with Sergeant Goodfellow about his linguistic ability, and discussing the relative merits of infantry and cavalry (with extensive reference to Turkish history). Hans sneaks away, thinking he has not been spotted; but in fact he has. The town characters go to the country as Cornelius’ guests at Christmas dinner. There they discuss the decline in public respect for soldiers, making extensive reference to the great military men of the Elizabethan era, including some with whom Cornelius served in his younger days.
