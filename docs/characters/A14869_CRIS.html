Nor doe I looke for it sir. Signior Contilupo, the Court holds it fit, You leaue this stale declaiming 'gainst the person, And come to the matter. It showes a poore malicious eloquence, And it is strange, men of your grauitie Will not forgoe it: verely, I presume, If you but heard your selfe speaking with my eares, Your phrase would be more modest. Well then, to your proofes, and be not tedious. No? no contention about parting your goods? No flaw, no vnkindnesse? Bethink your selfe, this cannot chuse but sauour Of a womans malice deeply; and I feare, Y'are practiz'd vpon most deuillishly. How hapt Gentlewoman, you reueal'd this no sooner? I should rather aske you, why you reueale it now? Your penitence? Might not your penitence haue beene as hartie, Though it had neuer summon'd to the Court Such a conflux of people. Most iust. Stay, heere's an Accusation, But here's no proofe; what was the Spanyards name You accuse of adultery? I knew that Spanyard well: if you be a Bastard, Such a man being your father, I dare vouch you A Gentleman, and in that Signiour Contilupo, Your Oratory went a little too farre. When doe wee name Don Iohn of Austria, The Emperours sonne, but with reuerence: And I haue knowne in diuers Families, The Bastards the greater spirits; but to'th purpose, What time was this Gentleman begot? And be sure you lay your time right. No? what then, Pumpes? Are you certaine of that? That's well he neuer lay with her, But in anno seuenty one, let that be remembred. Stand you aside a while. Mistris, the truth is, I knew this Crispiano, liued in Naples At the same time, and loued the Gentleman As my bosome friend; and as I doe remember, The Gentleman did leaue his Picture with you, If age or neglect haue not in so long time ruin'd it. I pray let me see't, let me see the face I then loued so much to looke on. No, no, Gentlewoman, I haue other businesse for you. I commend you Lady, There was a maine matter of Conscience, How many ills spring from Adultery! First, the supreame Law that is violated, Nobilitie oft stain'd with bastardy, Inheritance of Land falsly possest, The husband! corn'd, wife sham'd, and babes vnblest, So, hang it vp i'th Court; you haue heard, What has been vrged gainst Romelio. Now my definitiue sentence in this cause, Is, I will giue no sentence at all. No, I cannot, for I am made a partie. Sir, I am of your Counsell, for the cause in hand Was begun at such a time, fore you could speake; You had need therefore haue one speake for you. Behold, I am the shadow of this shadow, Age has made me so; take from me fortie yeares, And I was such a Summer fruit as this, At least the Paynter sayned so: for indeed, Painting and Epitaphs are both alike, They slatter vs, and say we haue been thus: But I am the partie here, that stands accused, For Adultery with this woman, in the yeare Seuentie one: now I call you my Lord to witnesse, Foure yeares before that time, I went to'th Indies, And till this month, did neuer set my foot since In Europe; and for any former incontinence, She has vowed there was neuer any: what remaines then, But this is a meere practise 'gainst her sonne, And I beseech the Court it may be sifted, And most seuerely punisht.

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
