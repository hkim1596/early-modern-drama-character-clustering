Hath a short finger aud a naked chinne; A skipping eye, dare lay my iudgement (faith) His loue is glibbery; there's no hold ont, wench: Giue me a husband whose aspect is firme, A full cheekt gallant, with a bouncing thigh: Oh, he is the Paradizo dell madonne contento. For fancies passion, spit vpon him; figh: His face is varnisht: in the name of loue, VVhat country bred that creature? Matzagent? now by my pleasures hope, He is made like a tilting staffe; and lookes For all the world like an ore-rosted pigge: A great Tobacco taker too, thats flat. For his eyes looke as if they had bene hung In the smoake of his nose. Auoid him: for he hath a dwindled legge, A lowe forehead, and a thinne cole-black beard, And will be iealous too, beleeue it sweete: For his chin sweats, and hath a gander neck, A thinne lippe, and a little monkish eye: Pretious, what a slender waste he hath! He lookes like a May-pole, or a notched stick: Heele snap in two at euery little straine. Giue me a husband that will fill mine armes, Of steddie iudgement, quicke and nimble sense: Fooles relish not a Ladies excellence. But what? pree the goe downe, lets see her face: God send that neither wit nor beauty wants Those tempting sweets, affections Adamants. Nay faith, sweete creature, weele not vaile our names. It pleas'd the Font to dip me Rossaline. That Ladie beares the name of Mellida, The duke of Venice daughter. Nothing but little humours: good sweet, on. Pleasures bodie, what makes my Lady weepe? Faith all the nobler, tis more generous. Sauourly put off. I vouchsafe it; be my slaue. Signior Balurdo, wilt thou be my seruant too? Ile spit in thy mouth, and thou wilt, to grace thee. Flauia, attend me to attire me. Belike the falling sicknesse. What seruant, nere a word, and I here man? I would shoot some speach forth, to strike the time With pleasing touch of amorous complement. Say sweete, what keepes thy minde, what think'st thou Pish, pish; I fixed in vnmooued scorne? Why, Ile loue thee to night. Faith, as the toy puts me in the head. And walke? And eate apples? Bodie a mee, the Duke: away the glasse. By the sweete of loue, I should feare extreame ly that he were an Eunuch. Spark spirit, how like you his voice? So helpe me, youth, thy voice squeakes like a dry cork shoe: come, come; lets heare the next. Come, kneele, kneele. Oh, when I see one were a perewig, I dreade his haire; another wallowe in a greate sloppe, I mistrust the proportion of his thigh; and wears a ruffled boot, I feare the fashion of his legge. Thus, something in each thing, one tricke in euery thing makes me mistrust im perfection in all parts; and there's the full point of my addiction. Or rather, your glowe worme represents your wit, which only seems to haue fire in it, though indeed tis but an ignis fatuus, and shines onely in the darke deade night of fooles admiration. Faith sir, your wits spurs haue but walking ro wels; dull, blunt, they will not drawe blood: the gen tlemen vshers may admit them the Presence, for anie wrong they can doe to Ladies. Troth vncle, when my sweet fac't cuz hath tolde me how she likes the thing, call'd wedlock; may be Ile take a suruey of the checkroll of my seruants; & he that hath the best parts of, Ile pricke him downe for my husband.

Plot — 184
Mistress Missa is under attack from Protestants: they want to replace the Mass with a simple Lord’s Supper involving none of her magical powers. Knowledge persuades Freemouth that she is guilty of heresy and blasphemy, and he cites her before Justice. Initially Justice tries to have Freemouth himself arrested, but Knowledge informs him of a recent change in the crown’s religious policy: Protestants are no longer to be persecuted. Justice agrees to allow Freemouth to proceed against Missa, and he accuses her in Palemon’s court. She admits everything except claiming to be more powerful than God. Porphyry and Philargury undertake Missa’s defence. Porphyry denies the right of Knowledge and Freemouth, lay persons, to question ecclesiastical matters, and attempts to have the charges thrown out, or at least referred to a spiritual court; but Palemon insists that he is competent to try the case even though he is not a priest. Missa’s defence turns on the claim that the mass was ordained by Christ at the Last Supper; but Knowledge demonstrates that the scriptural passages cited contain no mention of it, and that it is not coextensive with the Lord’s Supper, which requires no vestments, can be administered by anyone rather than only a priest, and claims no power to control the weather, work magic, or save souls. 1548 Porphyry tries to argue on the principle of obedience of authority, but Knowledge retorts that authority cannot command things contrary to scripture (such as the mass). Transubstantiation is invoked and rebutted, and Knowledge argues that all sacrifices are superseded by the atonement at the crucifixion; thus the mass is unnecessary. Palemon finds against Mistress Missa. Though she deserves death, he commutes the penalty to banishment, and she goes home to her father, the Pope.
