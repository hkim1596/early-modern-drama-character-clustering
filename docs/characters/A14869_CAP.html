Full speech

For pities sake, you that haue teares to shed, Sigh a soft Requiem, and let fall a Bead, For two vnfortunate Nobles, whose sad fate Leaues them both dead, and excommunicate: No Churchmans prayer to comfort their last groanes, No sacred seed of earth to hide their bones; But as their fury wrought them out of breath, The Canon speakes them guiltie of their owne death. The Lord Ercole, and the noble Contarino, Both of them slaine in single combat. Looke vp sir, you are preserued beyond naturall reason, you were brought dead out a'th field, the Surgeons ready to haue embalmed you. You are diuinely informed sir. What ayme you at by this rumour of your death? These are crimes that either must make worke For speedy repentance, or for the Deuill. This is a priuate way which I command, As her Confessor. I would not haue you seene yet, Till I prepare her. Peace to you Lady. You are wel imployd, I hope; the best pillow i'th. World for this your contemplation, is the earth, And the best obiect heauen. And I am come To bring you tidings of a friend not dead, Reserued to life againe. One whom I dare presume, next to your children, You tendred aboue life. For hee should haue been Your sonne in Law, miraculously saued, When Surgery gaue him ore. Withall and you be wise, Remember what the mother has reueal'd Of Romelio's treachery. Hauing from Leonoras Wayting-woman, Deliuer'd a most strange Intelligence Of Contarino's recouery, I am come To sound Romelio's penitence; that perform'd, To end these errours by discouering, What shee related to me. Peace to you sir, Pray Gentlemen, let the freedome of this Roome Be mine a little. Nay sir, you may stay. Will you pray with me? Shall I pray for you? O you haue a dangerous voyage to take. Pray tell me, do not you meditate of death? These things you know, are out of my practice. Were I in your case, I should present to my selfe strange shaddowes. I would make you a good Christian. Did you murder Contarino? Why sir? As one that faine would iustle the deuill Out of your way. But to giue him the foyle, goes not by strength. Here's food for you. Can you feed, and apprehend death? This confidence, If it be grounded vpon truth, tis well. O, I tremble for you: For I doe know you haue a storme within you, More terrible then a Sea-fight, and your soule Being heretofore drown'd in securitie, You know not how to liue, nor how to dye: But I haue an obiect that shall startle you, And make you know whither you are going. I am glad you so receiue it. Why this is well: And now that I haue made you fit for death, And brought you euen as low as is the graue, I will raise you vp agen speake comforts to you Beyond your hopes, turne this intended Duell To a triumph. Yes Madam, he is liuing, and Ercoles Second. Some euill Angell Makes him deafe to his owne safetie, we are shut Into a Turret, the most desolate prison Of all the Castle, and his obstinacy, Madnesse, or secret fate, has thus preuented, The sauing of his life. To little purpose. Oh looke vpwards rather, Their deliuerance must come thence: to see how heauen, Can inuert mans firmest purpose: his intent Of murthering Contarino, was a meane To worke his safety, and my comming hither To saue him, is his ruine: wretches turne The tide of their good fortune, and being drencht In some presumptuous and hidden sinnes, While they aspire to doe themselues most right, The deuil that rules ith ay re, hangs in their light. Madam, I shall. We haue been prisoners too long: Oh sir, what meane you? Contarino's liuing. Behold him liuing.

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
