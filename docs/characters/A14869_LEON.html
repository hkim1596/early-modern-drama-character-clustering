Sir, you are nobly welcome, and presume You are in a place that's wholly dedicated To your seruice. Sir, your fame renders you most worthy of it. You haue bin strange a long time, you are weary Of our vnseasonable time of feeding: Indeed th' Exchange Bell makes vs dine so late; I thinke the Ladies of the Court from vs Learne to lye so long a bed. I would not haue you value it the lesse, If I say, Tis granted already. Oh sir, shaddowes, are coueted in Summer, And with me, tis Fall o'th Leafe. Indeed Sir, I dare tell you, My Looking-glasse is a true one, and as yet It does not terrifie me. Must you haue my Picture? You will enioyne me to a strange punishment: With what a compeld face a woman sits While shee is drawing? I haue noted diuers, Either to faine smiles, or sucke in the lippes, To haue a little mouth; ruffle the cheekes, To haue the dimple seene, and so disorder The face with affectation, at next sitting It has not been the same; I haue knowne others Haue lost the intire fashion of their face, In halfe an houres sitting. In hote weather, The painting on their face has been so mellow, They haue left the poore man harder worke by halfe, To mend the Copie h wrought by ▪ but indeed, If euer I would haue mine drawen to'th life, I would haue a Paynter steale it, at such a time, I were deuoutly kneeling at my prayers, There is then a heauenly beautie in't, the Soule Moones in the Superficies. Indeed Sir, I am a Widdow, And want the addition to make it so: For mans Experience has still been held Womans best eyesight. I pray sir tell mee, You are about to sell a piece of Land To my sonne, I heare. Now I could rather wish, That Noble men would euer liue ith Countrey, Rather then make their visit's vp to'th Citie About such businesse: Oh Sir, Noble Houses Haue no such goodly Prospects any way, As into their owne Land: the decay of that, Next to their begging Churchland, is a ruine Worth all mens pitie. Sir, I haue forty thousand crownes Sleepe in my Chest, shall waken when you please, And slie to your commands, Will you stay supper? I would not haue you come hither sir, to sell, But to settle your Estate. I hope you vnderstand Wherefore I make this proffer: so I leaue you. Contarino Were you talking of? he lost last night at Diee Fiue thousand Duckets; and when that was gone, Set at one throw a Lordship, that twice trebled The former losse. And most carefully Carried the Gentleman in his Carroch To a Lawyers Chamber, there most Legally To put him in possession: was this wisedome? Youle be aduis'd I hope: Know for your sakes I married, that I might haue children; And for your sakes, if youle be rul'd by me, I will neuer marry agen. Here's a Gentleman Is noble, rich, well featur'd, but 'boue all, He loues you intirely; his intents are aymed For an Expedition 'gainst the Turke, Which makes the Contract cannot be delayed. Heare me, if thou dost marry Contarino, All the misfortune that did euer dwell. In a parents curse, light on thee. Shee is yours. To be contracted in teares, is but fashionable. Virgins must seeme vnwilling. Great persons doe not euer come together. And truely I haue heard say, To be a little strange to one another, Will keepe your longing fresh. This is the happiest houre that I euer arriued at. What a dismall noyse yon bell makes, Sure some great person's dead. Why doe they ring before my gate thus? Let them into'th Court, I cannot vnderstand What they say. What Noble men I pray sir? Yet I would haue him liue To satisfie publique Iustice for the death Of Ercole: oh goe visit him for heauens sake. I haue within my Closet a choyce Relicke, Preseruatiue 'gainst swounding, and some earth, Brought from the Holy Land, right soueraigne To staunch bloud: has he skilfull Surgeons, thinke you? I am twentie yeares elder since you last opened your lips. You haue giuen him the wound you speake of, Quite thorow your mothers heart. Oh, may you liue To winne many soules to heauen, worthy sir, That your crowne may be the greater. Why my sonne made me beleeue he stole into his chamber, And ended that which Ercole began By a deadly stabb in's heart. A most noble fellow in his loyaltie. I read what worthy comforts I haue lost In my deare Contarino, and all addes To my dispayre.—Within there. Fetch the picture hangs in my inner closet. I remember, I let a word slip of Romelio's practise At the Surgeons: no matter I can salue it, I haue deeper vengeance that's preparing for him, To let him liue and kill him that's reuenge I meditate vpon. You trouble him. Sure the old man's franticke. It is the fruit of a most heartie penitence. To my shame I speake it, neuer. Indeed I might haue confest it, Priuately toth Church, I grant; but you know repentance Is nothing without satisfaction. Contarino liuing? Hold, hold, for heauens sake hold. Oh here's one betweene, Claimes to be neerer. Cease here all further scruteny, this paper Shall giue vnto the Court each circumstance, Of all these passages.

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
