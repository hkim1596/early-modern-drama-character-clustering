Though Calchas nothing sayde Yet Hector telles it vs himſelfe, of whoſe ſeede are we frayde. The worthy bloud of noble men oft tymes we ſe it playne, Doth after in their heires ſuccede and quickly ſpringes agayne. For so the hornles youngling yet, of high and ſturdy b ſte, With lofty necke and braunched brow, doth ſhortly rule the reſt. The tender twig that of the lopped ſtocke doth yet remayne, To match the tree that bare the bough, in time startes vp again With equall top to former wood the roume it doth ſupply, And ſpreads on ſoyle alow the ſhade, to heauen his braunches hye. Thus of one ſparke by chaunce yet left it hapneth ſo ful oft. The fyre hath quickly caught his force and flamth agayn aloft. So ſcare we yet leaſt Hectors bloud might riſe er it be long, Feare caſtes in all thextremity and oft interprets wrong. If ye reſpect our caſe ye may not blame theſe old ſoldiars Though after years and monthes twice flue, they feare again the wars. And other trauails dreadyng Troy, not yet to be wel wonne, A great thing doth the Grecyans moue, the feare of Hectors ſon. Rid vs of feare, this ſtayeth our fleete, and pluckes vs backe agayne, And in the hauen our nauy ſtickes, til Hectors bloud be ſlayne. Count me not feerce for that by fates I Hectors ſonne require, For I as wel if chaunce it would Oreſtes ſhould deſyre. But ſince that needes it muſt be ſo, beare it with pacient hart: And Suffer that which Agamemnon ſuffred in good part. Who moſt deſtres to die, would fayneſt liue when death drawth on, Theſe noble wordes with preſent feare of death woulde ſoone be gone. It is but vayne to hyde the thinge that ſtrayght ye wil deteckt No feares may moue the mothers hart, ſhe doth them al neglect. This tender loue ye beare your child, wherin ye ſtand ſo ſtoute, So much more circumſpectly warnth, the Greekes to looke about. Leaſt after ten yeares tract of tyme and battell borne ſo farre, Some one ſhould liue that on our children might renew the warre, As for my ſelfe, what Calchas ſayth, I would not feare at all But on Telemachus I dread, the ſmart of warres would fall Thy grieuous ſorrowes to redreſſe, would God it lay in mee, But at thy wil to take delay of tyme I graunt it thee. Now take thy laſt leaue of thy Sonne, and fil thy ſelfe with teares. Oft tymes the weeping of the eyes, the inward griefe out weakes.

Plot summary

Plot — Troas (Seneca's Sixth Tragedy)
After a ten-year war, Troy has fallen: King Priam had been murdered, the city burned, the women taken into slavery; but Queen Hecuba’s age means that none of the Greek conquerors are interested in her. Achilles’ ghost returns from the dead, demanding the blood of Hecuba’s daughter Polyxena in revenge for his own treacherous murder by Paris. Until she is sacrificed on his tomb, he will not allow the Greek fleet to sail home. Agamemnon is doubtful about this, but Pyrrhus reminds him that he was willing to sacrifice his own daughter to get the fleet to Troy, and Calchas confirms that the price of returning to Greece is the same as the price of leaving it: a virgin’s death. The ghost of Hector appears to Andromache in a dream and warns her to flee from Troy with their son: Calchas has demanded his blood as well as Polyxena’s. She hides the boy in Hector’s tomb, and tells Ulysses he is dead; but Ulysses insists on removing Hector’s ashes if the Greeks cannot have their sacrifice. Andromache produces the child, and Ulysses has him thrown to his death from a tower. Helen is sent to prepare Polyxena to marry Pyrrhus, but she tells Hecuba and Andromache that the Greeks intend a sacrifice, not a wedding. Pyrrhus leads Polyxena away and kills her. A messenger describes the two deaths. Not knowing whom she should grieve for most — husband, daughter, or nephew — Hecuba wishes for death. The women, Hecuba included, are taken away to live in slavery in Greece.
