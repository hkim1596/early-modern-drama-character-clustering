Gallants, Know old Iohn Harcop keepes a Wineseller, Has Traueld, bin at Court, knowne Fashions, And vnto all beares habit like your selues, The shapes of Gentlemen and men of sort. I haue a health to giue them ere they part. My healths are welcome: welcome Gentlemen. Welcome infayth Sir. In youth I swild my fill at Venus cup, In sted of full draughts now I am faine to sup. Now maister Scarborrow. And both agreed so. Then long may you liue together, haue store of sons. But sonne here is a man of yours is come from London. And you being Ward to him son Scarborrow, And know him great, it fits that you obay him. It dus it dus, for by an antient law, We are borne free heires, but kept like slaues in awe, Who are for London Gallants? Brothers to him ere long shall be my sonne, By wedding this young girle: You are welcome both, Nay kisse her, kisse, tho that she shall Be your Brothers wife, to kisse the cheeke is free. Good, good y faith, this kissings good y faith, I lou'd to smacke it too when I was young, But Mum: they haue felt thy cheek Clare, let them hear thy tung. I gesse to see this girle, shal be your sister. What? Do you borrow of your kindred Sir? you are a merrie Gentleman. His absence shall not make our harts lesse merrie Then if we had his presence. A daie ere long, Will bring him backe, when one the other meets, At nooneith Church, at night betweene the sheets. Weele wash this chat with wine. Some wine: fill vp, The sharpner of the wit, is a full cup. And so to you Sir. Nay, nay, nay, Women are weake and we must beare with them, Your frolicke healths, are onely fit for men, Gods precious, for his mercy, wheres this wench? Must all my friends and guests attend on you? Where are you Minion? That sad voyce was not hers I hope: Whose this, my daughter? Patience good teares, and let my words haue way Clare, my daughter, helpe my seruants there: Lift vp thine eyes, and looke vpon thy father, They were not borne to loose their light so soone, I did beget thee for my comforter, And not to be the Author of my care. Why speakst thou not? Some helpe my Seruants there: What hand hath made thee pale? Or if thine owne, What cause hadst thou that wert thy fathers Ioy, The Treasure of his age, the Cradle of his sleepe, His all in all? I prethee speake to me? Thou art not ripe for death, come backe againe, Clare, my Clare, If death must needs haue one, I am the fittest, prethee let me go, Thou dying whilst I liue, I am dead with woe. Thou wert not wont to be so sullen childe, But kind and louing to thy aged father: Awake, awake, Ift be thy lasting sleepe, Would I had not sence for griefe, nor eies to weepe. Was that the cause that thou hast soyld thy selfe, With these red spots, these blemishers of beauty? My child, my childe, wast periury in him, Made thee so fayre, act now so foule a finne, That he deceiued thee in a Mothers hopes, Posterity, the blisse of marriage? Thou hast no tung to answere no, or I, But in red Letters writes: For him I die. Curse on his Traiterous tung, his youth, his blood, His pleasures, Children, and possessions, Be all his dayes like winter, comfortlesse: Restles his nights, his wants Remorcelesse, And may his Corps be the Phisitians stage, Which plaid vpon, stands not to honored Age, Or with diseases may he lie and pine, Till greefe wasts blood, his eies, as greefe doth mine. Plot summary

Plot — The Miseries of Enforced Marriage
The ward William Scarborow betroths himself to Clare Harcop, but on his return to London he is forced to marry Katherine, the niece of his guardian, Lord Falconbridge. He is rewarded with control of his own’ and his younger brothers’ property. He writes to tell Clare the news of his unwelcome marriage; upon receiving the letter, she dies (possibly by suicide). ===== PAGE 337 ===== 1606 Scarborow takes her death badly: he blames his wife, and becomes prodigal. Ilford fakes his own arrest for debt in order to induce Scarborow to guarantee the supposed loan, thereby defrauding him of his money. This still leaves him with the patrimony of his younger brothers, Thomas and John; but he spends it, and drunkenly assaults his friends and kinsmen. The brothers and the honest family servant Butler are forced into theft to keep themselves from starvation. They steal £300 from Harcop, Clare’s father, which they use to make their sister appear to be a rich woman. Butler tricks Ilford into marrying her, but on learning that she is penniless her new husband throws her out; Butler offers to support her out of his meagre wages. Thomas confronts and fights with his elder brother, but saves his life when they are attacked by Ilford. Now penitent, Thomas declares his intention to confess to Harcop that he and John stole the money, even though it will mean hanging for them both. John falls out with him over this, but they are reconciled by Butler. Dr Baxter, who married Scarborow to Katherine, talks him into repentance. On his deathbed, Lord Falconbridge also repents and leaves the Scarborow brothers double their original wealth in restitution, along with a dowry for their sister.
