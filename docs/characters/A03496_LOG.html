Full speech

Nay, and you once fall to Set ſpeeches, I am gone; I perceiue you are not for common talke; I wonder, now I thinke on't, in what Praedicament a womans tongue is; let's ſee: yet, what if I make it a Tranſcendent? and yet it can't be ſo, for 'tis neither vnum, nor verum, nor honum: 'faith, and't bee in any Praedicament, it ſhall bee in Quantitate Continua, and that's oppoſite to Diſcreta; or rather, ſince 'tis ſo irregu lar, and therefore can hardly bee admitted into any Order, I will count it that Monſter in Nature, and Contradiction of Philoſophie, Infinitum in acta. Hey day! this is tick-tack: Here's another ſhorter tricke: well, I perceiue there's no other courſe — which is your way? Doe you ſpeake firſt. VVhy, mine lies this way. I, But I muſt go this way to doe a little buſineſſe firſt. But I muſt walke here alone a little to thinke on't firſt. Why, then fare you well; I can thinke on my buſines by the way. Why, you woun't follow me? I am going to a Feaſt. I am going to Ethicus. O you gods! which of you will come to deliuer me? Well, if wee muſt together, and if you will ſticke ſo cloſe vn to me; yet, good Miſtres Tongue, do not cleaue to the roofe of my Mouth. How now, Sir! What doe you whiſpering with my Lawyer? With mine? I, mine, I'm ſure I gaue him a fee. And't pleaſe you, he abuſes me before your face; hee bribes my Lawyer. Thine? he's none of thine. He's mine. Cauſidicus, are not you my Lawyer? And't pleaſe you, Grammaticus was ſoundly beaten by this fellow Poeta, and, I forſooth, by his man a clogge-headed Rogue; but that riming Raſcall ſet him on. He's a Raſcall to Me I'am ſure. And't pleaſe you Polites, euery one counts him but a diſſolute Raſcall, and ſo hee has in all times beene held but for my facultie, what age euer flouriſht in which that flouriſht not? Grammaticus what thinkeſt thou of this departure? is it not prettie? Thou talke of integritie? goe, goe, thou art a crackt Pitcher, a broken Piſſe-pot, Polites talkes againſt Logicians; when as your Logicians are the onely Schollers in the world: but the beſt is he does but talke againſt them. Sirrah, Dunces? How you Slaue? Come, come; I'm ſure our caſe is in a fine Predica ment: I thinke we haue beene put off long enough: i'ſaith all Law-caſes ſhall hereafter be no more put in the Predicament of Action; but of Quando, of Quando; a plague of theſe Lawyers. Exeunt Log, & Grammat. I care not for marrying; I ſee no good Foundation, for any ſuch Relation.

Plot summary

Plot — Technogamia, or The Marriages of the Arts
Astronomia has several suitors: Geographus (whom her mother dislikes), Geometres (whom her friends favour), and Poeta (who has recently jilted Historia to pursue her). Against her kinsman Polites’ advice, Historia still wants to marry Poeta, consummating the long-standing association between history and the poetic imagination. Poeta quarrels with Grammaticus, and Logicus becomes involved in the ensuing affray. Unsure whether to fight a duel with Poeta or sue him for assault, Logicus sets both courses in train. Hearing that Poeta has been hurt in the fight, Historia sends to Medicus for help, but Magus, who is helping Geometres, sees an opportunity to dispose of one of his client’s rivals, and persuades Medicus to send poison instead. Medicus’ man, Sanguis, delivers the poison, but spots that it isn’t medicine. Poeta infers Historia's treachery, but persuades himself out of absolute misogyny by thinking of Astronomia. To reconcile Poeta with Logicus and Grammaticus, Ethicus arranges a banquet. Several of the wooers use magical help in their suits. Poeta meets two gipsies, who pick his- pocket and foretell that he will marry Astronomia. Using sieve- divination, Magus predicts that Geometres will marry Astronomia, and will achieve this mainly through hypnotism, with a little pharmaceutical help. (Geometres finds the third option, conjuration, too frightening to try.) However, when Astronomia drinks the supposed love philtre at Ethicus’ feast, it poisons her. Meanwhile, Poeta seems reconciled to his former adversaries, but gets drunk and tries to pick fights with Geographus and Grammaticus; Magus charms him to sleep. Causidicus accepts fees from both Logicus and Poeta, so when the case comes to trial, Polites dismisses him and forces the parties to speak for themselves. The trial is adjourned when news arrives that Astronomia, burning up with the poison, has fallen into a coma. Medicus attends her, and cures her by administering an enema. Meanwhile the gipsies and servants indulge themselves in country pastimes: morris dancing, barley-break, and riddles. Polites takes up Geographus’ and Historia’s love- suits: he persuades Astronomia’s mother that Geographus will be a worthy son-in-law, so long as he gets rid of his servant Phantastes, and the couple are married.
