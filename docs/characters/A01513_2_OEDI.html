Full speech

Daughter, I must commend thy noble heart. O yes deare daughter, leaue thou me alone Amid my plagues: be mery while thou maist. I will endure, as fatall lot me driues, Resting these crooked sory sides of mine Where so the heauens shall lend me harborough. And in exchange of riche and stately toures, The woodes, the wildernesse, the darkesome dennes Shalbe the bowre of mine vnhappy bones. "One happy day did raise me to renoune, "One haplesse day hath throwne mine honor downe. That sitteth not amid thy pleasant yeares. Where is thy mother? let me touche hir face, That with these hands I may yet féele the harme That these blind eyes forbid me to beholde. O wife, O mother, O both wofull names, O wofull mother, and O wofull wyfe, O woulde to God, alas, O woulde to God Thou nere had bene my mother, nor my wyfe. But where lye nowe the paled bodies two, Of myne vnluckie sonnes, Oh where be they? O bodies deare, O bodies dearely boughte Unto your father, bought with high missehap. Nowe commes Apollos oracle of passe, That I in Athens towne should end my dayes: And since thou doest, O daughter myne, desire In this exile to be my wofull mate, Lende mée thy hande, and let vs goe togither. Unto the wretched, be a wretched guyde. And where shall I sette foorth my trembling féete? O reache mée yet some surer staffe, to staye My staggryng pace amidde these wayes vnknowne. Nowe can I blame none other for my harmes But secrete spight of foredecréed fate, Thou arte the cause, the crooked, olde and blynde, I am exilde farre from my countrey soyle, And suffer dole that I myghte not endure. O carefull caytife, howe am I nowe chang'd From that I was? I am that Oedipus, That whylome had triumphant victorie, And was bothe dread and honored eke in Thebes: But nowe (so pleaseth you my frowarde starres) Downe headlong hurlde in depth of myserie, So that remaynes of Oedipus no more As nowe in mée, but euen the naked name, And lo, this image, that resembles more Shadowes of death, than shape of Oedipus. O onely comforte of my cruell happe. This thy desire, that is both good and iuste, Imparte to some that be thy trustie frendes, Who moude with pitie, maye procure the same. And of those fewe, yet one of those am I: Wherefore, goe we nowe daughter, leade the waye Into the stonie rockes and highest hilles, Where fewest trackes our steppings may be spyde. "Who once hath sit in chaire of dignitie, "May shame to shewe him selfe in miserie.

Plot summary

Plot — Jocasta
Oedipus unknowingly murdered his father and married his mother Jocasta; on learning the truth, he blinded himself. His sons imprisoned him and agreed to alternate annually as kings of Thebes: Polynices went into exile while Eteocles took the first turn on the throne; but Eteocles refused to give it up at the end of his year in office, and Polynices has besieged Thebes in association with the King of Argos. Jocasta intervenes as their mother, and persuades Polynices to enter Thebes and meet Eteocles under truce. Eteocles will not hand over power, but offers to accept his brother back as a private citizen. Jocasta is unable to reconcile her sons, and Polynices’ forces prepare for battle. Eteocles names Creon as his successor, and asks him to marry Antigone to his son Haemon. Creon consults Tiresias, and learns that Thebes is doomed to destruction in divine revenge for Jocasta’s incest; only the death of his other son, Menoeceus, can save the city. Keen to die for his country, Menoeceus refuses to go into exile as his father wishes, reports the prophecy to Eteocles, and kills himself. Meanwhile, the battle is indecisive, and Eteocles and Polynices agree to settle their dispute in single combat. Jocasta and Antigone are too late to stop them killing each other: arriving to find them both dying, Jocasta stabs herself with Polynices’ dagger. The besieging army is massacred by the Thebans. Antigone brings the three bodies back to Thebes and tells her father what has happened. Creon declares himself King of Thebes and gives order for the funerals of Jocasta and Eteocles, but demands that Polynices, as an enemy of the city, should remain unburied. Antigone protests, pleads, and refuses to marry Haemon, threatening to kill him and then herself should Creon try to force her. Creon banishes both her and Oedipus. She helps her blind father to touch the dead bodies, and he asks her to see that Polynices is buried.
