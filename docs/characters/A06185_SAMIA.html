Ah litle boy how glad thy mother would Supply thy wants but naked néed denies: Thy fathers slender portion in this world, By vsury and false deceit is lost, No charitie within this Citie bides: All for themselues, and none to helpe the poore. Is this thy slender care to helpe our childe? Hath nature armde thée to no more remorse? Ah cruell man, vnkind, and pittilesse, Come Clesiphon my boy, ile beg for thée. Ah silly man I know thy want is great, And foolish I to craue where nothing is. Haste Alcon haste, make haste vnto our sonne, Who since he is in fauour of the King, May helpe this haplesse Gentleman and vs. For to regaine our goods from tyrants hands. Nay Traitor, I wil haunt thée to the death, Ungratious sonne, vntoward and peruerse, Ile fill the heauens with ecchoes of thy pride, And ring in euery eare thy small regard, That doest despise thy parents in their wants, And breathing forth my soule before thy féete, My curses still shall haunt thy hatefull head, And being dead, my ghost shall thée pursue. Iustice O King, iustice against my sonne. This cursed Radagon. Oh polliticke in sinne and wickednesse, Too impudent for to delude thy Prince. Oh Rasni this same wombe first brought him foorth. This is his father, worne with care and age, This is his brother, poore vnhaphie lad, And I his mother, though contemn'd by him, With tedious toyle we got our litle good, And brought him vp to schoole with mickle charge: Lord how we ioy'd to sée his towardnesse, And to our selues we oft in silence said, This youth when we are old may succour vs. But now preferd and lifted vp by thée, UUe quite destroyed by cursed vsurie, He scorneth me, his father, and this childe. Oh all you heauens, and you eternall powers, That sway the sword of iustice in your hands, (If mothers curses for her sonnes contempt, May fill the ballance of your furie full) Powre downe the tempest of your direfull plagues, Upon the head of cursed Radagon. Plot summary

Plot — A Looking Glass for London and England
Triumphant over the King of Jerusalem, the Assyrian king Rasni declares himself divine and marries his sister, Remilia; a viceroy who objects is banished and the flatterer Radagon given his place. Inordinately proud of her beauty, Remilia is struck by lightning. Rasni is unable to revive her, and buries her in state; but is encouraged by Radagon to think of the living rather than the dead, and to take Alvida, wife of the King of Paphlagonia. When her husband objects to the adulterous liaison, she poisons him. Alvida continues to be unfaithful: her ambition attracts her to Rasni, but her sexual appetite favours the Viceroy of Cilicia, so she takes both. Thrasybulus and Alcon are both cheated by a usurer to whom they are trying to repay money: he keeps them talking beyond the due hour and then secures possession of Thrasybulus’ farm and Alcon’s cow as a penalty for non-payment. They attempt to sue him, but he bribes both the judge and their lawyer, and they lose their cases. Alcon appeals to Radagon, his son, to help his starving family, but Radagon is too proud to acknowledge his parent, and Rasni has him ejected from the court. Radagon’s mother curses him, and: he is swallowed by subterranean fire.
