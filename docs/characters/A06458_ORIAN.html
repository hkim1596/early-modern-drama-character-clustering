Full speech

Y'are merry my Lord. Your Lordship hath a good wit. Come my Lord, I haue heard before of your Lordships merry vaine in jesting against our Sexe, which I beeing desirous to heare, made mee rather choose your Lordships house, then any other, but I know I am wel come. Beleeue me, this your Lordships plainnes makes me thinke my selfe more welcom, then if you had sworne by all the prettie Court oathes that are, I had beene wel comer then your soule to your bodie. Before I would not vnderstand him, but now he speakes riddles to me indeed. Now Venus be my speed, inspire me with all the seuerall subtill temptations, that thou hast already giuen, or hast in store hereafter to bestowe vpon our sexe: grant that I may apply that Phisicke that is most apt to worke vpon him: whether he will soonest bee moou'd with wan tonnesse, singing, dauncing, or beeing passionate, with scorne, or with sad and serious lookes, cunningly mingled with sighes, with smiling, lisping, kissing the hand, and making short cursies; or with whatsoeuer other nimble power, he may bee caught: doe thou infuse into mee, and when I haue him, I will sacrifice him vp to thee. My Lord, I am a woman, and such a one— Why my vnpatient Lord? Can ye be so vneasie, can ye freeze, and Such a summers heat so ready To dissolue, nay gentle Lord, turne not away in scorne, Nor hold me lesse faire then I am: looke on these checks, They haue yet enough of nature, true complexion, If to be red and white, a forehead hie, An easie melting lip, a speaking eie, And such a tongue, whose language takes the eare Of strict religion, and men most austere: If these may hope to please, looke here. Y'are yet to harsh, to dissonant. Ther's no true musicke in your words, my Lord. Mine own seruants shall attend me. Looke you be true. Hast thou prouided one to beare my letter to my brother? What doest thou doubt? Sir, what may be the currant of your busines, that thus you single out your time and place? Heauen, and the powers diuine, guard well the innocent. What's my offence? what haue these yeares com mitted, That may be dangerous to the Duke or State? Haue I conspir'd by poyson? haue I giu'n vp My honour to some loose vnsetl'd blood That may giue action to my plots? Deare sir, let me not die ignorant of my faults? Who is my accuser? Arrigo, take these wordes, and beare them to the Duke, It is the last petition I shall aske thee: Tell him the child, this present houre brought forth To see the world, ha's not a soule more pure, more white, More virgin then I haue. Tell him Lord Gondarinoes Plot, I suffer for, and willingly: tel him it had bin a greater honour, to haue sau'd then kil'd: but I haue done: strike, I am arm'd for heauen. VVhy stay you? is there any hope? Haue you the power to saue? You will not venture that? Ye dare not be so kind? If I should slight my life, I were too blame. I shall beleeue it, if you saue my life. I dare not buy my life so. Then no; nay looke not ruggedly vpon me, I am made vp too strong, to feare such lookes. Come, doe your Butchers part: before I would win life, with the deare losse of honour, I dare finde meanes to free my selfe. Villaine, I will not; murderer doe thy worst, thy base vnnoble thoughts dare prompt thee to; I am aboue thee slaue. No nor by— Ile guard the one, let heauens guard the tother. My Lord, I am your subiect, you may command me, prouided still, your thoughts be faire and good. Lord Gondarino, you haue wroug'd me highly, yet since it sprung from no peculiar hate to me, but from a generall dislike vnto all women, you shall thus suffer for it; Arrigo, call in some Ladies to assist vs: will your Grace take your State?

Plot summary

Plot — The Woman Hater (The Hungry Courtier)
Oriana is on her way to visit the Duke’s court when she is caught in a hail-storm, and takes refuge in the house of Gondarino. Gondarino is a confirmed misogynist, and makes her duly unwelcome. The Duke, on his way to visit Oriana at home, is also caught in the storm and he too shelters in Gondarino’s house. Gondarino suspects that he is unwittingly being made the host of an illicit assignation (and indeed the Duke is attracted to Oriana). Oriana takes umbrage at Gondarino’s attitude to women, and pretends to pursue him amorously in an effort to torment him out of it. He sends her to stay in a bawdy-house, and tells the Duke that she is unchaste. Her brother Valore disputes this, and Gondarino is suspected of slander. To defend himself, he shows her to the Duke in her new lodgings, but his story is discredited when it emerges that he sent her there himself. The Duke sends the courtier Arrigo to kill Oriana, but he offers to spare her in return for sex. She refuses, and thereby proves her chastity: the Duke was testing her, and she now accepts his proposal
