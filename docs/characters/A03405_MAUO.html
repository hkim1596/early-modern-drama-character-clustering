Wee doe obay: And I choose Musick first. But if (by Art) as all our Artist say, There is no reall truth to be attain'd, Why should wee labour in their loues bestow? The wisest said: I know I nothing know, But all this prooues not wee may know a truth. What dullards thus, would dote in rusty Arte? Plod ding vpon a booke to dull the sence, And see the world become a treasure-house, Where Angells swarme like Bees in Plenties streets, And euery Peasant surfets on their sweetes? Gallants let vs inuent some pleasing sportes, To fit the Plentuous humor of the Time, I cannot feed my appetite with Ayre, I must pursue my pleasures royally, That spung'd in sweat, I may returne from sport, Mount mee on horse-back, keepe the Hounds and Haukes, And leaue this Idle contemplation, To rugged Stoicall Morosophists. Faire Ladies, could these times affoord you cates, You should be feasted in Apolloes hall; But (Lords) the chaps of wide-pancht gluttonie, Haue wasted all the dainties of the land. Seruant Philarchus, what, no maske too night? What? insolent with glib prosperity? Faith Gentlemen no Players will appeare: Gallants, to your Maske. Well, though the penny raisd them to the pound, Iuſt Enuie, causelesse Pride doth still confound. I will confirme thy pledge, and meete thee too. O pallid Enuie how thou suck'st my bloud, And wastes my vitall spirits: I could raue, Runne madde with anguish, for my slight respect, O wher's the honour to my high borne bloud! When euery peasant, each Plebeian, Sits in the throne of vndeseru'd repute, When euery Pedlers-French is term'd Monsignuer, When broad-cloathd trades-man, and what lack you sir. Is wrapt in riche habiliments of silke, Whilst vrgent need makes Princes bend their knee, As seruile as the ignobilitie, To crouch for coyne, whilst flaues tye fast our Lands, In Statute Staple, or these Marchants bands. Peace prating Scholler: bid the Sea be still, When powerfull windes doc tosse the raging waues, Or stay the winged lightning in his course; When thou doost this, thy words shall charme me too. Till then preserue thy breath. How? dare Philarchus? yes, I dare doe more; In bloud or fire; or where thou darst not come; In the numme fingers of cold death I dare. What are you playing with my feather too? A pox vpon this linguist, take him hence; Philarchus, I defie thee, and in scorne, Spit on thy bozome: vowing heere by heauen, If either sword, or fire, or strength of men, Or any other steeled violence, Can bring to swift confusion what is thine, Vpon this gratefull soyle; it shall be done. My lands? My soule is bigge in trauaile with reuenge, And I could rip her wombe vp with a stabbe, To free th' imprisoned issue of my thought. The broyles of warre wherein I gloried more Then Priams Hector, who by burning walls, Was traild along (dread victories deepe fall) So from these gates my selfe in meane disgrace Am banisht forth, pinch't through with pouerty. Who tels vs all 'tis true that shee hath sed, Poore flyes will tickle Lyons being dead. Is that Philarchus that complaines? 'tis so, Time was, I could haue din'd amongst my friends, Now stands at euery doore a lack and Apes And tels me 'tis too late, his Lord hath din'd. See poore Philarchus powring out his plaints, To vnrelenting walls, relentlesse men. Heere come our wiues, how wretchedly they looke. Could I but learne (with Craesus ) to endure The falling sicknesse of sad Pouerty, Who lost a rich commanding Emperie Patience would prooue a tutor to my grieues.

Plot — Histriomastix, or The Player Whipped
Under the benevolent rule of Peace, various orders of society turn to learning in their different ways: Mavortius and his fellow noblemen and gentlemen become students of the liberal arts, professional people appoint the scholar Chrisoganus as their lecturer, and some artisans organize themselves as a company of players, Sir Oliver Owlet’s Men. However, the new reign of Plenty tempts many of them away from their studies, leaving only Chrisoganus loyal. While the lawyers and merchants take advantage of the leisure of the vacation to go hunting, Mavortius entertains his guests with a play given by Owlet’s Men. Plenty is succeeded by Pride, who encourages a dedication to fashion and a neglect of proper decorum: the lawyers and merchants enrich themselves unjustly, Mavortius dismisses many of his servants to release more money to spend on pleasure, and both Chrisoganus (who has turned playwright) and Owlet's Men price themselves out of the market. Next, Envy comes to power, causing class resentment in the realm: the citizenry’s adoption of the latest fashions causes resentment among the nobility, envious behaviour disrupts the players’ rehearsal, and Mavortius becomes belligerent. The next ruler is War, under whom the players are conscripted as soldiers, Mavortius fights with his friends, the common people rise in rebellion against their social superiors, and the Spanish invade the realm. Out of this chaos comes Poverty, leading aristocrats, merchants. and peasants alike to debt, bankruptcy, and famine. Owlet’s men, unable to pay their taxes, are banished from the realm. Finally Poverty and her associates are expelled by the return of Peace and Plenty. Queen Elizabeth also appears, in the guise of Astraea, and the play ends with the five lesser rulers resigning their sceptres to Peace.
