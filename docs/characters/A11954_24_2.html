O, God ſaue ye: Eu'n to the Hall, to heare what ſhall become Of the great Duke of Buckingham. Were you there? Pray ſpeake what ha's happen'd. Is he found guilty? I am ſorry fort. But pray how paſt it? That was hee That fed him with his Prophecies. After all this, how did he beare himſelfe? When he was brought agen to th' Bar, to heare His Knell rung out, his Iudgement, he was ſtir'd With ſuch an Agony, he ſweat extreamly, And ſomthing ſpoke in choller, ill, and haſty: But he fell to himſelfe againe, and ſweetly, In all the reſt ſhew'd a moſt Noble patience, I doe not thinke he feares death. Certainly, The Cardinall is the end of this. That tricke of State Was a deepe enuious one, All the Commons Hate him perniciouſly, and o' my Conſcience Wiſh him ten faddom deepe: This Duke as much They loue and doate on: call him bounteous Buckingham, The Mirror of all courteſie. Let's ſtand cloſe and behold him. If the Duke be guiltleſſe, 'Tis full of woe: yet I can giue you inckling Of an enſuing euill, if it fall, Greater then this. This Secret is ſo weighty, 'twill require A ſtrong faith to conceale it. I am confident; You ſhall Sir: Did you not of late dayes heare A buzzing of a Separation Betweene the King and Katherine ? But that ſlander Sir, Is found a truth now: for it growes agen Freſher then e're it was; and held for certaine The King will venture at it. Either the Cardinall, Or ſome about him neere, haue out of malice To the good Queene ▪ poſſeſt him with a ſcruple That will vndoe her: To confirme this too, Cardinall Campeius is arriu'd, and lately, As all thinke for this buſines. I thinke You haue hit the marke; but is't not cruell, That ſhe ſhould feele the ſmart of this: the Cardinall Will haue his will, and ſhe muſt fall. So are you. 'Tis all my buſineſſe. At our laſt encounter, The Duke of Buckingham came from his Triall. 'Tis well: The Citizens I am ſure haue ſhewne at full their Royall minds, As let 'em haue their rights, they are euer forward In Celebration of this day with Shewes, Pageants, and Sights of Honor. May I be bold to aske what that containes, That Paper in your hand. Alas good Lady. The Trumpets ſound: Stand cloſe, The Queene is comming. Then, two Iudges. A Royall Traine beleeue me: Theſe I know: Who's that that beares the Scepter? A bold braue Gentleman. That ſhould bee The Duke of Suffolke. And that my Lord of Norfolke? Heauen bleſſe thee, Thou haſt the ſweeteſt face I euer look'd on. Sir, as I haue a Soule, ſhe is an Angell; Our King has all the Indies in his Armes, And more, and richer, when he ſtraines that Lady, I cannot blame his Conſcience. Thoſe men are happy, And ſo are all, are neere her. I take it, ſhe that carries vp the Traine, Is that old Noble Lady, Dutcheſſe of Norfolke. Their Coronets ſay ſo. Theſe are Starres indeed, And ſometimes falling ones. No more of that. You ſaw the Ceremony? Good Sir, ſpeake it to vs? But what follow'd? What two Reuerend Byſhops Were thoſe that went on each ſide of the Queene? He of Wincheſter Is held no great good louer of the Archbiſhops, The vertuous Cranmer. Who may that be, I pray you. He will deſerue more.

Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
