Full speech

Make hast with that shield, see … hammer'd well, For when 'tis done I'le giue't my father Ioue, 'Tis of the purest mettall Lemnos yeelds. Pyragmon yes, that masse must be wrought well And soundly temper'd, bid your fellow Cyclops Worke lustily, it must be soone dispatcht. No matter when, the Huswiffe's too fine finger'd, And saith, the very smoake my Fordge doth cast Choakes her, the very aire of Lemnos (man) Blasts her white cheekes, she scarce will let me kisse her, But shee makes vergisse faces, saith my visadge Smug'd thus with cole-dust, doth infect her beauty, And makes her weare a beard, shee's, sure, in Paphos, Cypresse, or Candy, shee's all for play Whilst we Ioues thunders hammer hard all day. I'le tell thee man, I was when I was borne A pretty smug knaue, and my father Ioue Delighted much to dance me in his lap. Vpon a time as hee was toying with mee In his high house aboue, that Phaeton Had at that instant set the world a fire, My father when he saw heauens bases smoake, Th'earth burne, and Neptunes broth to seeth with heate; But startles vp to thunder-strike the lad, And lets me fall: downe tumbled I towards the earth: I fell through all the Planets by degrees, From Saturne first, so by the Moone at last: And from the Moone downe into Lemnos Isle Where I still liue, and halt vpon my fall, No maruell if't lam'd mee, for, Pyragmon. How high I tumbled, who can gesse aright, Falling a Summers day from morne to night? Had I not bene deriu'd from God-like seed, Trust me Pyragmon I had don't indeed. But to the Forge, for I Appollo spie, Hee that sees all things with the daies bright eye. Good morrow Phoebus, what's the newes abroad … For thou seest all things in the world are done, Men act by day-light, or the sight of Sunne. Thrice happy Phoebus, That whilst poore Vulcan is confin'd to Lemnos Hast euery da … these pleasures. What newes else. Could not god Phoebus bring mee to see this pa stime. There's simple doing. God Mars. A bed; with whom? some pretty wench I warrant. Tell me good Phoebus, That when I meete him … I may floute God Mars, Tell mee, but tell me truely on thy life. Out on her whore, out on him Cuckold-maker, Phoebus I'le be reuendge on great God Mars, Who, whilst I hammer here his swords and shields, Hammers vpon my head, I will complaine To Ioue, and all the Gods, and tell them flat I am a Cuckold. Enough, I haue deuis'd a secret snare, A draw-net, which I'le place vpon the Couch Where they still vse to bed, a wire so temper'd, And of such finenesse to deceiue the eie. So catch them when they are at it, and by this I may presume, and be sure I am Cuckold. If I can catch them, all the Gods I'le call To see my wrongs, there sports I'le neere to marre, And venge me on that letcherous God of warre. By her baud Charis, this I know the place, Which with adulterate pastimes they pollute. Here will I set my pit-fall for these birds, And catch them in the closure of this wire, So, so, al's fit, my snare in order plac't, Happy the time, that I this Charis trac't. Gramercy my kind wife. Out of her Whoore.

Plot — The Brazen Age (The Age of Brass)
Hercules and the shape-changing Achelous fight for the hand of Dejanira; Achelous transforms himself into a series of monsters, but Hercules retains the upper hand, and Achelous concedes defeat after one of his bull’s horns is pulled off (later to become the cornucopia). Hercules takes Dejanira home, and en route they meet Nessus, the only survivor of Hercules’ massacre of the centaurs. Feigning friendship, the vindictive Nessus offers to carry Dejanira across the river on his back; but when he gets to the other side, he attempts to abduct her. Hercules shoots him and, dying, he tells Dejanira that his blood is a love-charm, which she should use on Hercules if he ever seems interested in another woman; in fact, it is poisonous. Hercules travels to Egypt, where King Busiris sacrifices foreigners, and kills him on his own altar, but refuses to accept his crown. During his absence, Diana, offended that King Oeneus has sacrificed to all the gods but her, sends a boar to ravage the Calydonian countryside. The lords of Greece gather to hunt the boar. One of them is Adonis, and Venus falls in love with him; he insists on hunting, the boar kills him, and she transforms him into a flower. Atalanta joins the hunt, and Meleager falls in love with her; when the boar is killed, he is awarded its head, but he offends his companions by presenting it to her. In the ensuing fight, Meleager kills his uncles. His mother Althea is torn between her duty to her brothers and to her son. Meleager’s life is in her power, for, when he was born, the Fates visited her, pointed out a brand in the fire, and foretold that the child would live until the brand was burnt up; she rescued it and has kept it safe ever since. Now she burns the brand, and he dies; in remorse, she kills herself with his sword. Hercules joins Jason and the Argonauts on their quest to retrieve the golden fleece from Colchis.
