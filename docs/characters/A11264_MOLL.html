Not Marry: forsweare Marriage? why all women know 'tis as honorable a thing as to lye with a man; and I to spight my Sisters vowe the more, haue entertainde a suter al ready ▪ a fine gallant Knight of the last Fether, hee sayes he will Coach mee too, and well appoint mee, allow mee money to Dice with-all, and many such pleasing protestations hee sticks vpon my lips; indeed his short-winded Father ith' Countrie is wondrous wealthy, a most abhominable Farmer, and therefore hee may doo e in time: troth Ile venture vpon him; women are not without wayes now to helpe them-selues ▪ if he proue wise and good as his word, why I shall loue him, and vse him kindly: and if hee prooue an Asse, why in a quarter of an houres warning I can transforme him into an Oxe;—there comes in my Reliefe agen. How now? what's the newes? Sir Iohn Penny-Dub? where? where? Ha's my Mother seene him yet. Direct him hether softly, good Frailtie, —Ile meete him halfe way. 'Tis happinesse my Mother saw him not: O we come good Sir Ioha. Nay the Fates forsend that I should anger the fashion? Trust me, very pritty, and mouing, y'are worthy on't sir,— O my Mother, my Mother, now shee's here, Weele steale into the Gallery. How knowes hee that, what, h'as some Deuill told him? A double torment. And I vowde neuer to be such an Asse, but to marry. what a crosse Fortune's this? I am glad, I haue the vse of my tongue yet: tho of no thing else, I shall finde the way to marry too, I hope shortly. Pray Sir Iohn Pennydub, let it be deferd awhile, I haue as bigge a heart to marry as you can haue; but as the Fortune teler tolld me. And then hee told mee more-ouer Sir Iohn, that the breach of it, kept my Father in Purgatorie. Thy Fathers death? is the old Farmer dead? And you'le keepe your word with mee now, Sir Iohn. that I shall haue my Coach and my Coach-man? And two white Horses with black Fethers to draw it? A guarded Lackey to run befor't, and pyed liueries to come trashing after't. And to let me haue money in my purse to go whether I will. Then come, what so ere comes on't, weele bee made sure together before the Maides a'the Kitchin. Do you follow him, Ile not be long after. there meetes them a Noble man, Sir Oliuer Muckil, d Sir Andrew Tip-staffe. And I Sir Iohn with soule, heart, lights and all.

Lady Plus has just been widowed, but her son Edmond. does not grieve appropriately at the funeral. She mourns a good, liberal husband, who strove to make his family rich by fair means and foul; she swears not to remarry, and her elder daughter Frances renounces marriage too. Not so her younger daughter, Moll, who entertains a suitor. The other two Plus women are also being pestered by unwanted suitors, one of whom has bribed Lady Plus’s brother-in-law, Sir Godfrey, to recommend him. Captain Idle is taken to prison for highway robbery; there he is visited by his puritan kinsman Nicholas, one of. the Plus household’s servants, and also by Skirmish and Pieboard, two tricksters who plan to get him out. To that end, they persuade Nicholas to steal Sir Godfrey’s chain. Pieboard visits Lady Plus posing as a fortune teller, and tells her that her husband is in purgatory, from which he can be released only if both she and her daughters reverse their marital intentions (which he has overheard): she and Frances must marry, and Moll must not. As a puritan, Lady Plus doubts the theology of this, so he establishes his authenticity by predicting that, before the day is out, there will be bloodshed outside her door and Sir Godfrey will suffer a loss, with worse to follow for the women of the household.
