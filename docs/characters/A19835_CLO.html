Why Siluia, whence, should all this griefe arise? Tell me, sweete Siluia, how comes that to passe? Is't possible Palemon should b'vntrue? If it be so, deare Siluia, I thinke then That thou saist truth, there is no trust in men, For I protest I neuer saw a face That promisd better of a heart then his, And if he faile, whose faith then consta t is? Siluia, indeed and I haue heard so too. Well Siluia, I haue heard so sad a tale, As that I grieue to be a woman borne, And that by nature we must be exposd Vnto the mercie of vnconstant men. But what saide then Palaemon in the ende? But what assurance haue you of his fraud: It may be you suspect him without cause? Pray who was that? tell mee good Siluia, tell. Siluia adieu, the Gods relieue thy woes, Since men thus faile, and loue no pittie showes, Loue? nay, I'me taught for louing whilst I liue, Siluia, thy councell hath lockt vp my heart So fast from loue, as let them sigh, and grieue, And pine, and waile who will, I for my part Will pittie none of all this race of men. I see what showes so euer they pretend, Their loue is neuer deadly, none of these That languish thus, haue dide of this disease That euer I could heare, I see all do Recouer soone, that happen thereinto. And if they did not, there were no great hurt, They may indure, they are of stronger powers, Better their hearts should ake, then they breake ours. Well had I not bene thus forewarnd to day, Out of all question, I had shortly fal e, Into the melting humour of compassion too; That tender pittie that betrayes vs thus. But thus had I bene seru'd, had I reueald The least regarde of common courtesie To such as these: but I doe thanke the Gods I haue reseru'd me, from that vanitie: For euer I suspected this to be The vaine of men, and this now settles me. And for Carinus, let him vaunt what good He did for me, he can but haue againe My hearty thankes, the paiment for his paine, And that he shall and ought in woman-hood. And as for loue, let him go looke on her That sits, and grieues, and languishes for him, Poore Amarillis, who affects him deare, And sought his loue with many an wofull teare. And well deserues a better man then be, Though he be rich Lupinus sonne, and stands Much on his wealth, and his abilitie, She'is wittie, faire and full of modestie. And were she of my minde, she rather would Pull out her eyes, then that she would be seene, To offer vp so deare a sacrifice To his wilde youth, that scornes her in that wise. What is this creature then you praise a man? Nought else, it is enough he be a man. What hath this man you thus commend fa name? Techne, me thinkes, the praises that you giue Shewes your owne loue, and if he be that man You say, 'twere good you kept him for your selfe. For me? alas Techne you moue too late. Yes sure, my promise is already past. No that I may not when it is once past. The tongue is but the Agent of the heart. And now I doe remember too, I, had A foolish idle dreame, and this it was: Me thought the fayrest of Montanus lambs, And one he lou'd the best of all his flock, Was singled out, and chac'd b'a cruell curre, And in his hote pursuit makes towards me, (Me thought) for succour, and about mee ran, As if it begd my ayde to saue his life, Which I long time deferrd, and still lookt on, And would not res ue it, vntill at lenght I saw it euen quite wourtied out of breath, And panting at my feete and could no more: And then me thought, I tooke it vp from death, And cherisht it with mee, and brought it back. In troth 'tis no good luck to dreame of Snakes, One shall be sure t'heare anger after it. Indeed and I haue heard it neuer failes. Plot summary

Plot — The Queen's Arcadia (Arcadia Reformed)
Arcadia is no longer the place of pastoral simplicity it once was: old shepherds have grown litigious, young ones are falling in love unwisely and unrequitedly, and the nymphs, who seem less beautiful than before, have abandoned the modest simplicity of traditional Arcadian dress in favour of quirky new fashions. The problems arise from foreign influences on this previously sequestered land. Colax has travelled abroad, and brought back with him the city woman Techne, and they are selfishly interfering in the love- lives of the nymphs and shepherds: they have made Silvia and Palaemon jealous of one another with mendacious tales of infidelity; and Colax has persuaded Dorinda to jilt Myrtillus in his favour, making her the latest in a string of sexual conquests. Meanwhile Lincus, a lawyer, and Doctor Alcon have both come from town to set up practices in Arcadia. Lincus has found the local amity bad for business: the Arcadians do not covet one another’s property and so never go to law. For Alcon, however, the Arcadians’ fellow-feeling is profitable, because when one falls ill, others follow suit, and they all want the same medicine from him — which is just as well, because he has only one to offer, and that is a placebo. Both are keen to drum up trade, so Lincus has set Montanus and Acrisius against one another, and Alcon has introduced the Arcadians to an unhealthy habit: tobacco-smoking. Carinus (who has recently jilted Amaryllis) and Amyntas are rivals for the love of Cloris, and argue about which of them has the greater command of her affections; but in fact Cloris loves neither of them, and has sworn chastity until she finds a worthy man. Colax now wants a change of girlfriend, and asks Techne to make Cloris love him. Techne offers to give Cloris a new outfit, and arranges for them to meet at a cave so she can try it on; but it is Colax who keeps the appointment, and attempts to rape her. Techne herself has fallen for Amyntas, and arranges for him to see Cloris meeting Colax. Amyntas becomes suicidal, so Techne sends Cloris to stop him; but she arrives too late, and he has already taken poison.
