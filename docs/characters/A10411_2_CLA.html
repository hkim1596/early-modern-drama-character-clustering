Thrice Sacred Valley, I kisse thy hallowed Earth! I see the smoake steame from the Cottage tops, The fearfull hus wife rakes the embers up. All hush to bed. Sure no man will disturbe mee. O blessed vally! I the wretched Claius Salute thy happy soyle, I that have liv'd Pelted with angry curses in a place As horrid as my griefes, the Lylibaean mountaines, These sixteene frozen winters, there have I Beene with rude out-lawes, living by such sinnes As runne o'th' score with justice 'gainst my prayers & wishes. And when I would have tumbled down a rock, Some secret powre restrain'd me: There I lately heard By a disconsolate Pilgrim that sought death, That my Amyntas wits (ah me!) were marr'd. Twas not a time to thinke to save my selfe When my poore boy was lost. Lost said I?—O Phoebus, If there be soveraigne power in juice of hearbs, And that the teeming earth yeeld medicinall flowres To cure all maladies, I have sought the skill; No leafe no root hath scap'd mee: I may boast it, I have been natures diligent Apothecary. Be lucky my emplaister! I have temper'd The surest Recipe the worlds garden yeelds; 'Twould put Orestes in his wits again. I know I step upon my death: the Oracle Desires my blood for sacrifice, and Pilumnus For his old hate still seekes it: make long stay I dare not, only I desire t'apply My medicine and be gone. Who's this I spy? —I doe remember now that countenance; It is my sister Thestylis, I'le stand close T'observe their actions. What breed is hee? Yes, I had almost Forgot it, I would faine have seene her too. So you may Vrania, If you will grant me one request. I would intreat you presently to vow Virginity to Ceres, that Amyntas No more may toyle his brain in thinking what To give you for a Dowry. Remember it Vrania. Tis he, I heare his name, good mole away. How no feare Can make me loose the father! Death or danger Threat what you can; I have no heart to goe Back to the mountaines, 'till my eyes have seen My Amaryllis! Ha! I heard the sound Of Amarillis; where's that blessed creature, That owes the name? are you the Virgin? O speak, what hand, What barbarous Tigers issue, what cursed whelpe Of Beares or Lyon, had the marble heart To wound so sweet a Nymph? I durst presume upon the power of art, Did I but know the murtherer. It is not so, For had it been by her own hand, my skill Could have preserv'd her life. Who's that? Conceale him not! in plain, I am thy father Thy father, Amarillis, that commands thee By these gray haires to tell mee. I am Claius. I, doe, I feare not death. Let every Virgins hand when I am slaine Ring me a knell of Plaudits: let my Dirges Be amorous Ditties, and in stead of weeping Dance at my funerall! Tis no griefe for mee To dye to make my countrymen some sport. Here's one in whom I only wish to live Another age. The Murtherer Is in this place, the issue of her blood Is stop'd oth' sudden. Cruell man, 'tis thou Hast done this bloudy act, that will disgrace The story of our nation, and imprint So deepe a blemish in the age we live in For savage Barbarisme, that eternity Shall nere weare out: Pilumnus, on my knees I beg the justice of Sicilian lawes Against this monster. Speak Amaryllis I'st not this wolfe? Still I am confident 'Twas hee. Yes, this unmercifull man (If he be man that can doe such a crime) Has wounded her.

Plot summary

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
