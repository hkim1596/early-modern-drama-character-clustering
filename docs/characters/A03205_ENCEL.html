That must be left to great Enceladus, The pride and glory of the Tytans hoast. I that haue curb'd the billowes with a frowne, And with a smile haue made the Ocean calme, Spurn'd downe huge mountains with my armed foot, And with my shoulders lift the vallies high, Wil in the wrinkles of my stormy brow, Bury the glories of the Cretan King, And on his slaughtered bulke braine all his sonnes. Do thou stand still, Whil'st I the foes of Tytan pash and kill. Am I not eldest from great Tytans loynes, The Saturnists hereditarie scurdge? Leaue all these deeds of horror to my hand, I like a Trophy ore their spoyles will stand. Come arme your sinowy limbes, With rage and fury fright pale pitty hence, And drowne him in the sweate your bodies still. With hostile industry tosse flaming brands About your fleecy lockes, to threat their Cities With death and desolation, let your steele Glistring against the sunne, daze their bright eyes, That with the dread of our astonishment They may be sunke in Lethe, and their graue May be the darke vawlt, cal'd obliuious Caue, Set on them, waste their confines as we march, And let them tast the rage of sword and fire, Th'Alarm's giuen, and hath by this arriú'd Euen at the wals of Creet, the cittadell Where the Cathedrall Saturne is enthron'd. This Gigomantichia be eternis'd For our affright and terror: If they flye, Tosse rockkes, and toppes of Mountaines after them To stumble them, or else entombe them quicke. 'Tis sunke into the deep Abysme of hell. Teare from his head the golden wreath of Creet. Tread on his captiue bulke, and with thy weight Great Tytan, sinke him to the infernall shades, So low, that with his trunke, his memory May be extinct in Lethe. By these Gods. We neither feare nor value, but contend To equall in our actions: both shall dye. There shall no proud Saturnian liue, to braue The meanest of the high-borne Tytanoyes. Conquest is ours. Maugre diuine, or base terrestriall powers. There pause, that word disturbs all thy claime, And proues that Tytan seates him in his owne. Destruction is our vassaile, and attends Vpon the threatning of our stormy browes. We trifle howers. Arme all your fronts with horror, Your hearts with fury, and your hands with death. Thunder meet thunder, tempests stormes defie, Saturne and all his issue this day dye. Two royall armies then on both sides stand, To view this strange and dreadfull Monomachy. Thy fall, Saturnian, addes to my renowne: For by thy death I gaine the Cretan Crowne.

Plot summary

Plot — The Golden Age, or The Lives of Jupiter and Saturn
King Uranus of Crete is dead. Titan is the eldest son, but his younger brother Saturn is the popular choice to ascend the throne. Titan agrees to make way for him provided that he agrees to settle the succession on Titan’s issue and kill his own sons as soon as they are born. Saturn is declared a god, and under his benevolent rule, the people learn agriculture, ship- building, and architecture, and begin to make weapons for hunting. His wife, Sibylla, becomes pregnant, but Saturn keeps his promise to Titan and kills the baby. During a second pregnancy, an oracle foretells that the child will depose Saturn. This time Sibylla demands the privilege of killing her own child; but neither she nor her women can bring themselves to do it, so the baby is sent to a foster mother, the Princess of Epirus, and Saturn is deceived with the blood of a goat. Jupiter grows up in the court of King Melisseus of Epirus, and is sent as an envoy to conclude a peace with King Lycaon of Pelasgia; but Lycaon has murdered the Epirote hostage and offers his limbs to Jupiter as a cannibalistic banquet. Jupiter deposes Lycaon and the people elect him their King. During the conflict, he encounters Callisto, Lycaon’s daughter, and is attracted to her. She asks him to grant her a boon to prove his love; he promises, but her request is to live a chaste life as a nymph of Diana. She joins Diana, but the nymphs are already paired off, so she must await the next recruit to have a bedfellow. This turns out to be Jupiter in female disguise. During a stag hunt, he diverts her, reveals his identity and rapes her. Months later, the nymphs undress to bathe together; Callisto is reluctant, and is forcibly stripped.
