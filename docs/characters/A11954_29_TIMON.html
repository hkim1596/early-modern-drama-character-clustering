Full speech

Oh no doubt my good Friends, but the Gods themſelues haue prouided that I ſhall haue much helpe from you: how had you beene my Friends elſe. Why haue you that charitable title from thouſands? Did not you chiefely belong to my heart? I haue told more of you to my ſelfe, then you can with modeſtie ſpeake in your owne behalfe. And thus farre I confirme you. Oh you Gods (thinke I,) what need we haue any Friends; if we ſhould nere haue need of' em? They were the moſt needleſſe Creatures liuing; ſhould we nere haue vſe for 'em? And would moſt reſemble ſweete Inſtruments hung vp in Caſes, that keepes there ſounds to them ſelues. Why I haue often wiſht my ſelfe poorer, that I might come neerer to you: we are borne to do bene fits. And what better or properer can we call our owne, then the richer of our Friends? Oh what a pretious com fort 'tis, to haue ſo many like Brothers commanding one anothers Fortunes. Oh ioyes, e'ne made away er't can be borne: mine eies cannot hold out wate me thinks ▪ to forget their Faults. I drinke to you. They haue e'ene put my breath from mee the ſlaues. Creditors? Diuels. May you a better Feaſt neuer behold You knot of Mouth-Friends: Smoke, & lukewarm water Is your perfection. This is Timons laſt, Who ſtucke and ſpangled you with Flatteries, Waſhes it off, and ſprinkles in your faces Your reeking villany. Liue loath'd, and long Moſt ſmiling, ſmooth, deteſted Paraſites, Curteous Deſtroyers, affable Wolues, meeke Beares: You Fooles of Fortune, Trencher-friends, Times Flyes, Cap and knee-Slaues, vapours, and Minute Iackes. Of Man and Beaſt, the infinite Maladie Cruſt you quite o're. What do'ſt thou go? Soft, take thy Phyſicke firſt; thou too, and thou: Stay I will lend thee money, borrow none. What? All in Motion? Henceforth be no Feaſt, Whereat a Villaine's not a welcome Gueſt. Burne houſe, ſinke Athens, henceforth hated be Of Timon Man, and all Humanity. Excellent Workeman, Thou canſt not paint a man ſo badde As is thy ſelfe. Muſt thou needes Stand for a Villaine in thine owne Worke? Wilt thou whip thine owne faults in other men? Do ſo, I haue Gold for thee. Haue I once liu'd To ſee two honeſt men? Let it go, Naked men may ſee't the better: You that are honeſt, by being what you are, Make them beſt ſeene, and knowne. I, you are honeſt man. Moſt honeſt men: Why how ſhall I requite you? Can you eate Roots, and drinke cold water, no? Good honeſt men: Thou draw'ſt a counterfet Beſt in all Athens, th' art indeed the beſt, Thou counterfet'ſt moſt liuely. Will you indeed? Looke you, I loue you well, Ile giue you Gold Rid me theſe Villaines from your companies; Hang them, or ſtab them, drowne them in a draught, Confound them by ſome courſe, and come to me, Ile giue you Gold enough.

Plot summary

Plot — Timon of Athens
; Timon gives freely of his riches to his acquaintances: every little gift will trigger disproportionate largesse by way of requital. He intervenes financially to secure Ventidius’ release from a debtors’ prison, and settles money on his servant Lucilius to enable him to marry the girl he loves, a wealthy man’s daughter. He entertains guests at a lavish feast at which a masque of Amazons is performed. But his creditors. are alarmed by his uncontrolled spending, and call in his debts, which amount to more than the value of his entire estate. He imagines that he can still call on the former recipients of his liberality, but they make their excuses and leave him in difficulties; he is failed even by Ventidius, now a rich man after his father’s death. Timon lays on a second feast, and invites his unreliable friends; they think normal service has been resumed, but are discomfited to find that the menu consists of water alone. Timon drives them off with abuse and leaves Athens in misanthropic fury. Alcibiades attempts to save a friend from execution for murder, but the Senate dismisses the appeal; when he persists, they banish him. In the forest, Timon digs for roots to eat, but finds only buried gold. He meets Alcibiades, marching against Athens with his soldiers, and gives him gold to ensure the city’s destruction; he also pays the army’s whores to spread venereal disease. He encourages a band of thieves to prey upon mankind, but his words persuade them to rethink their way of life. His faithful steward seeks him out and offers to share his own savings; Timon rewards him for his honesty but does not change his low opinion of the rest of humanity. News gets round that Timon still has gold, and hangers-on arrive, hoping this means his bankruptcy was only a bluff to test his friends; he drives them violently away. The Senate asks Timon to return to Athens as its tuler, to defend it against Alcibiades, but Timon, who is busy writing his own epitaph, wishes annihilation on them, and recommends suicide as their only means of escape. Alcibiades’ messenger to Timon finds only his tomb. Athens surrenders, and Timon’s epitaph is read.
