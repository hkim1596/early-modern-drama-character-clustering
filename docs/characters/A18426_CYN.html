What are you? Thou seem'st an honest Souldier; pray thee then Be as thou seem'st; betake thee to thy charge And leaue this place; adde not affliction To the afflicted. How sweet a breath the calmnesse of the night in spires the aire withall? So Maide, I feare me thy lips haue gone so oft to the bottle, that thy tongue-strings are come broken home. So Damzell. You say I must. How excellent ill this humour suites our habite? Nay pray thee take it to thee. HEarke I heare some footing neare vs. Your bottle has poisond this wench sir. Come y'are too rude. Ile pledge you Sir. If the world should see this. The praise I haue had, I would continue. Not yet sweet loue. A little stay will not endanger them. The daies proclaimer has not yet giuen warning. The Cock yet has not beate his third alarme. Thou shalt, thou shalt; though my loue to thee Hath prou'd thus sodaine and for hast lept ouer The complement of wooing, Yet only for the worlds opinion. I must maintaine a forme in parting hence. True; but those complements might haue their time for fashion sake. Well goe and see; Returne, weele goe home. Thou shalt not rest before I be resolu'd What happy winde hath driuen thee back to harbour? Was it my loue? Yet say so (sweet) that with the thought thereof I may enioy all that I wish in earth. Die? All the Gods forbid; O this affright torments me ten parts more then the sad losse of my deare husband. Yet heare a womans wit. Take counsaile of Necessitie and it. I haue a bodie here which once I lou'd And honour'd aboue all; but that time's past. That shall supply at so extrem a need the vacant Gibbet. What hurt is't, being dead it saue the liuing? Vexe not thy selfe deare loue, nor vse delay. Tempt not this danger, set thy handes to worke. The Gods forbid. O thou hast told me newes that cleaues my heart; Would I had neuer seene thee, or heard sooner This bloudie storie; yet see, note my truth Yet I must loue thee. I can not doo't. Loue must salue any murther: Ile be iudge Of thee deare loue, and these shall be thy paines In steede of yron, to suffer these soft chaines. Arise I say, thou sauer of my life. Doe not with vaine-affrighting conscience Betray a life, that is not thine but mine: Rise and preserue it. It is my Loue. So you may doe indeede, Or if your owne strength will not serue, wee'le aide Our handes to yours, and beare him to the place. For heauens loue come, the night goes off apace. Doe loue, be speedie. Whose there? Here was no souldier. O Ero! we are vndone. Thou did'st, thou did'st. Nay I resolue to sit out one brunt more; to trie to what aime heele enforce his proiect: were he some other man, vn knowne to me, his violence might awe me; but knowing him as I doe, I feare him not. Do thou but second me, thy strength and mine shall master his best force, if he should proue outra gious. Despaire they say makes cowardes turne couragious. Shut vp the Tomb. Shut the Tomb. I, you shall finde me constant. I, for that was alwaies heauier then's whole bodie besides So does not mine, Loue's resolute; and stands not to consult With pettie terrour; but in full carrier Runnes blind-fold through an Armie of misdoubts, And interposing feares; perhaps Ile weepe Or so, make a forc't face and laugh againe. All this I know. But yet my loue to thee Swallowes all this; or whatsoeuer doubts Can come against it. Shame's but a feather ballanc't with thy loue. No, I'de mall it down againe with this. She snatches vp the crow. Nay, then Ile assay my strength; a Souldier and a raid of a dead man? A soft-r'ode milk-sop? come Ile doot my selfe. No, Ile not lose the glorie ant. This hand, &c. Content your selfe, tis in a fitter hand. Ill-destin'd wife of a transform'd monster; Who to assure him selfe of what he knew, Hath lost the shape of man. Poore Souldiers case; doe not we know you Sir? But I haue giuen thee what thou cam'st to seeke. Goe Satyre, runne affrighted with the noise Of that harsh sounding horne thy selfe hast blowne, Farewell; I leaue thee there my Husbands Corps, Make much of that. Exit. cum Er.

Plot summary

Plot — The Widow's Tears
Renouncing Fortune and devoting himself instead to the goddess Confidence, Tharsalio intends to woo the widowed Countess Eudora. His wooing technique is grossly sexual, and he also quarrels with a rival, Rebus. Rebuffed and mocked by his family, he seeks out Arsace and bribes her to advise Eudora not to marry him, but when he returns to her and throws out Rebus, he is rejected a second time. Finally, however, she accepts him, and their wedding is marked by a masque. ===== PAGE 180 ===== Lysander is concerned about his wife Cynthia’s constancy, and plans to test it by faking his own death: with the help of Lycus he undertakes a journey and sends back a report that he has been murdered by mountain bandits. His body is interred in the family monument; Cynthia and her waiting-woman go to live in the tomb in order to be near him. Lysander returns incognito, disguised as a soldier assigned as the sentinel of the cemetery. He successfully seduces Cynthia, but she insists that their liaison be conducted privately to avoid compromising her reputation. Tharsalio sees them kissing inside the tomb and takes revenge on the ‘sentinel’ by stealing one of the crucified bodies he is supposed to be watching. When this is discovered, Cynthia suggests disinterring Lysander’s body and substituting it for the stolen one. Lysander goes to fetch a crowbar, removes his disguise, and meets Tharsalio, who deduces what is happening and informs Cynthia. She is thus able to pretend that she knew who the ‘sentinel’ was all along. Lycus is arrested for Lysander’s murder, and the tomb is opened to test whether his presence will make the corpse’s wounds bleed; but the coffin proves to be empty, and the ‘sentinel’ is found hiding in the tomb. Both men are sent for trial by the Governor, recently appointed with a hard-line law-and-order policy. Eudora intervenes to explain the truth, and Lycus is released; after Tharsalio replaces the stolen body, so is Lysander. Tharsalio allows him to believe that Cynthia was never tempted to infidelity.
