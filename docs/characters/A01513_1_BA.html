Well you iest faire, but I would aduise you take héede, I haue bidden you a thousande times beware, you will be spied one day talking with Dulippo. I haue giuen you a wherfore for this why many times, but go too, followe your owne aduise till you ouer whelme vs all with soden mishappe. Well, looke well about you: a man would thinke it were inough for you secretly to reioyce, that by my helpe you haue passed so many pleasant nightes togither, and yet by my trouth I do it more than halfe agaynst my will, for I would rather you had setled your fansie in some noble fa milie, yea and it is no small griefe vnto me, that reiecting the suites of so many nobles and gentlemen, you haue cho sen for your darling a poore seruaunt of your fathers, by whome shame and infamie is the best dower you can looke for to attayne. I can not denie, but at the beginning I did recom mende him vnto you (as in déede I may say that for my selfe I haue a pitiful heart) séeing the depth of his vnbridled affection, and that continually he neuer ceassed to fill mine eares with lamentable complaynts. Well you may iudge of Nourse as you liste: In déede I haue thought it alwayes a déede of charitie to helpe the miserable yong men, whose tender youth consumeth with the furious flames of loue: but be you sure if I had thought you would haue passed to the termes you nowe stande in, pitie nor pencion, peny nor pater noster shoulde euer haue made Nurse once to ope ̄ hir mouth in the cause. And haue I these thanks for my good wil? why then I sée wel I shall be counted the cause of all mishappe. Then I am glad you haue changed your minde yet. Then I vnderstande you not, how sayde you? I can not tell, you loue to lye with Dulipo very well: this geare is Gréeke to me, either it hangs not well togither, or I am very dull of vnder standing, speake plaine I pray you. Howe? make you so deintie to tell it Nourse, least she shoulde reueale it? you haue trusted me as farre as may be, I may shewe to you, in things that touche your honor if they were knowne: and make you strange to tell me this? I am sure it is but a trifle in comparison of those things wherof heretofore you haue made me priuie. I promise you of my honestie, say on. How Erostrato? is it not our neighbour, whiche? Are you sure of this? Are there no other Sicylians héere: nor none that passe this way, which may discouer them? This hath béen a straunge aduenture, but I pray you howe hang these thinges togither? that the studente whome you say to be the seruant, and not the maister, is become an earnest suter to you, and requireth you of your father in mariage? Daughter you haue reason, but let vs go in before he come any néerer. WEre there dames héere, or did mine eyes dazil? He muste haue better eyesight that shoulde marry your Polynesta, or else he may chaunce to ouersée the best poynt in his tables sometimes.

Plot summary

Plot — Supposes
Polynesta loves Dulippo, whose suit has been promoted by her nurse, and who is supposed a poor servant. In fact he is not Dulippo at all but Erostrato, who two years ago gained access to Polynesta by swapping identities with his servant and entering her father Damon’s household. Meanwhile Cleander seeks her hand, and the supposed Erostrato (the true Dulippo) becomes his rival for trust with him. Cleander appears to offer the best dowry, but Damon gives ‘Erostrato’ a fortnight’s grace pending his father Philogano’s arrival to underwrite his counter-offer. ‘Erostrato’ duly arranges to produce a false Philogano, recruiting a Sienese gentleman for the role by persuading him that a diplomatic incident has made Ferrara an unsafe place for a Sienese visitor. Overhearing an argument between two women servants, Damon discovers that ‘Dulippo’ and Polynesta have been sleeping together. ‘Dulippo’ is apprehended and imprisoned in the dungeon of his master’s house, and therefore unable to warn his servant of the arrival of the true Philogano: he misses his son and has come to take him back to Sicily. By chance, Philogano meets the true Dulippo, who refuses to acknowledge him. After the locals confirm that they know the young man as Erostrato, Philogano suspects that he has murdered his master and stolen his identity, and seeks legal assistance from Cleander. However, Dulippo turns out to be Cleander’s son, whom he lost as a child when evacuating Otranto during the Turkish conquest; he was later captured by Sicilians and sold to Philogano. The exchange of identities is revealed, allaying Damon’s worries about his daughter’s reputation. Cleander withdraws his suit: he no longer needs to marry and beget an heir now that his lost son has been restored to him. The true Erostrato is confirmed as Polynesta’s future husband.
