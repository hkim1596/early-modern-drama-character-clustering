There is no leisure; thinke when thou hast imbrac'd her Can she imagine thou didst ever honour her, Ever beleeve thy oathes, that tamely sufferst An old dry ham of horse flesh to enjoy her Enjoy her Maiden head; take but that from her That we may tell posterity a man had it, A handsome man, a gentleman, a young man, To save the honour of our house, the credit, Tis no great matter I desire. Free us both from the feare of breeding foules And ophs, got by this shaddow: we talke too long. Such a dainty Doe, to be taken By one that knowes not necke beefe from a Phesant, Nor cannot rellish Braggat from Ambrofia. Is it not conscience. A meritorious thing. No wringings in your minde now as you love me. Doe, doe, and dreame of Dotrells, get you to bed quickly And let us ha no more stirre, come no crying Tis too late now, carry your selfe discreetely, The old theefe loves thee deerely, thats the benefit. For the rest you must make your owne play, Nay not that way, Theile pull yee all to peeces, for your whim whams, Your garters and your gloves, goe modestly And privately steale to bed, tis very late Mall, For if you goe by them such a new larum. This way This through the Cloisters: and so steale to bed, When you are there once, all will separate And give yee rest, I came out of my pitty To shew you this. Here's the keyes Goe presently and locke the doores fast after yee, That none shall follow. Good-night sweete Cosen, A good, and sweete night, or Ile curse thee Franke. Move faster, faster, youle come too late else. Ile stay behind and pray for ye, I had rather she were dishonest, Than thou shouldst have her. Why doe you raile thus? Cannot a scurvy accident fall out, But I must be at one end on't? Cannot young sullen wenches play the sooles, And marry, and die, but I must be the agent? All that I did (and if that be an injury, Let the world judge it) was but to perswade her (And as I take it) I was bound to it too, To make the reverend coxecombe her husband Cuckold: What else could I advise her, was there harme i'this? You are of yeares, and have runne through experience, Would you be content if you were young agen, To have a continuall cough grow to your pillow, A rottennesse, that vaults are perfumes to Hang in your roofe, and like a fogge infect you; Anointed hammes to keepe his hinges turning Reeke ever in your nose, and twenty nightcaps, With twenty severall sweats. I know not, I am no Conjurer, you may looke the body, I was like to be stolne away my selfe, the Spirit Had like to ha surpris'd me in the shape of a woman, Of a young woman, and you know those are dangerous. Farewell good Aunt, I thanke you, Adiew honest Nicke, the divell if he have power, Will persecute your old bones, for this marriage, Fare well Mistresse Win. Tis a fine time a night too, but we must part Nick. No, no, farewell Toby, Farewell spinie Nicholas, no such thing, There be wayes i'the world, if you see me A day or two hence, may be wee'le cracke a quart yet, And pull a bell, commend to the houshold; Nay, cry not Toby 'twill make thy head giddy. No more Toby go, the times may alter — But where's the coarse of my dead cosen, (If she be dead) I hop'd 'thad but dissembled That sits heavy here: Toby, honest Toby, Lend me thy Lanthorne, I forgot 'twas darke, I had neede look to my wayes now. No, good night good neighhour Toby, I will wander, I scorne to submit my selfe, ere I have rambled, But whether, or with what, thats more materiall; No matter, and the worst come it is but stealing, And my Aunt won'ot see me hang'd for her owne credit, And farewell in a halter costs me nothing. Ha, who's that, and thou beest a man speake? Plot summary

Plot — The Night Walker, or The Little Thief
The usurer Justice Algripe has seized the wastrel Lurcher’s lands to settle a mortgage; he was also amorously interested in Lurcher’s sister, who has since disappeared. The destitute Lurcher is left to steal for a living; invited to join him, his friend Wildbrain prefers womanizing, on the grounds that it will not end on the gallows. Lurcher is approached by a boy, Snap, and, after ascertaining his skills at deception, takes him into his service. Algripe marries Wildbrain’s cousin Maria, who consented out of filial obedience rather than love: she loves Hartlove. Her mother’s household opposes the match, suspecting that old Algripe is impotent, and Wildbrain arranges for Hartlove to meet her on her way to bed and cuckold Algripe on his wedding night. She refuses his advances and faints when they are found together; she is taken for dead and prepared for burial. Her mother throws Wildbrain out for his part in the incident. Lurcher burgles the house that night, frightening the servants by pretending to be a ghost; but what he supposes to be a chest of jewels is actually Maria’s coffin. Upon discovering what their swag really is, he and his accomplices attempt to bury her in secret, but have a chance encounter with Algripe in the churchyard. Snap frightens him away by impersonating Maria’s ghost, but the thieves are scared off themselves when Maria wakes up in her coffin. Wildbrain meets Hartlove in the dark; Hartlove bears him a grudge and fights him. Maria hears the commotion and pretends to be her own ghost, enjoining them not to fight. Wildbrain is already wounded, and takes refuge in the next house he finds, which belongs to Lurcher’s mistress and partner in crime. They fall for one another and he shows off her gifts to Lurcher, who recognizes them as stolen 1615 property — his own booty. Maria’s mother accuses Algripe of stealing the body; when he refuses to repay the marriage portion, she begins litigation. Lurcher cons his way into Algripe’s house posing as a bookseller, ties him up, and steals his valuables and papers, including his own mortgage. Maria returns to her mother’s household posing as her nurse’s Welsh niece. Struck by the resemblance to dead Maria, Hartlove falls in love with her. Lurcher impersonates a constable and takes Algripe to a church where, he says, the villains who robbed him are hiding out; he then drugs Algripe and stows him in the vault.
