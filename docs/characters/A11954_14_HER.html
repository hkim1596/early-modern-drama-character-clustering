I had thought (Sir) to haue held my peace, vntill You had drawne Oathes from him, not to ſtay: you (Sir) Charge him too coldly. Tell him, you are ſure All in Bohemia's well: this ſatisfaction, The by-gone-day proclaym'd, ſay this to him, He's beat from his beſt ward. To tell, he longs to ſee his Sonne, were ſtrong: But let him ſay ſo then, and let him goe; But let him ſweare ſo, and he ſhall not ſtay, Wee'l thwack him hence with Diſtaffes. Yet of your Royall preſence, Ile aduenture The borrow of a Weeke. When at Bohemia You take my Lord, Ile giue him my Commiſſion, To let him there a Moneth, behind the Geſt Prefix'd for's parting: yet (good-deed) Leontes, I loue thee not a Iarre o' th' Clock, behind What Lady ſhe her Lord. You'le ſtay? Nay, but you will? Verely? You put me off with limber Vowes: but I, Though you would ſeek t' vnſphere the Stars with Oaths, Should yet ſay, Sir, no going: Verely You ſhall not goe; a Ladyes Verely 'is As potent as a Lords. Will you goe yet? Force me to keepe you as a Priſoner, Not like a Gueſt: ſo you ſhall pay your Fees When you depart, and ſaue your Thanks. How ſay you? My Priſoner? or my Gueſt? by your dread Verely, One of them you ſhall be. Not your Gaoler then, But your kind Hoſteſſe. Come, Ile queſtion you Of my Lords Tricks, and yours, when you were Boyes: You were pretty Lordings then? Was not my Lord The verver Wag o' th' two? By this we gather You haue tript ſince. My ſecond Ioy, And firſt Fruits of my body, from his preſence I am bar'd, like one infectious. My third comfort (Star'd moſt vnluckily) is from my breaſt (The innocent milke in it moſt innocent mouth) Hal'd out to murther. My ſelfe on euery Poſt Proclaym'd a Strumpet: With immodeſt hatred The Child-bed priuiledge deny'd, which longs To Women of all faſhion. Laſtly, horried Here, to this place, i' th' open ayre, before I haue got ſtrength of limit. Now (my Liege) Tell me what bleſſings I haue here aliue, That I ſhould feare to die? Plot summary

Plot — The Winter's Tale
Polixenes of Bohemia is coming to the end of a nine- month stay at the Sicilian court of Leontes, his childhood playmate. Leontes cannot persuade him to extend his stay, but the pregnant queen, Hermione, has more success. Leontes conceives a suspicion that Polixenes and Hermione have committed adultery, and that her unborn child is a bastard. He commands Camillo to poison Polixenes, but instead Camillo warns the intended victim and escapes with him to Bohemia. Leontes arrests Hermione and sends to the oracle of Apollo for confirmation of her adultery. She gives birth to a daughter in prison, and Paulina hopes to bring Leontes to his senses by showing him the baby; instead, he orders her husband, Antigonus, to take it out of his dominions and abandon it. Hermione is tried for adultery and treason. The oracle, when read, exonerates her and foretells that, unless the lost be found, Leontes will have no heir. Leontes refuses to believe it, whereupon his son Mamillius, who fell sick when his mother was arrested, dies. Hermione collapses in court and is later reported dead. Antigonus abandons the baby on the coast of Bohemia, and is eaten by a bear. The child, Perdita, is found and adopted by an old shepherd. Sixteen years pass. The penitent Leontes, still mourning Hermione, attempts to recall Camillo to Sicily, but Polixenes will not let him go. Worried by rumours that Prince Florizel is courting a shepherd’s daughter, Polixenes attends the sheep-shearing feast in disguise. When it becomes clear that Florizel intends to marry Perdita without consulting his father, Polixenes intervenes and threatens awful reprisals. Camillo helps Florizel and Perdita to abscond to Sicily, knowing that Polixenes will follow them, enabling him to return home at last. Leontes welcomes the newcomers, who include not only the Bohemian royal family but also the old shepherd, who produces the objects found with baby Perdita; these establish her identity as Leontes’ long-lost daughter. The two kings are reconciled, and Polixenes withdraws his objection to his son’s marriage. The court visits a newly sculpted statue of Hermione in Paulina’s chapel. It turns out to be Hermione herself, still alive and awaiting the return of her daughter.
