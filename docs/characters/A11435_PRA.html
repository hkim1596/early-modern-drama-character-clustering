Lord, lord, what pretty impes you are in your majorities! No in-sooth gossip Mag-py it is one of us, heavens blesse thee babie, and a well appointed impe it is. No, no, Mistris Barren, an Infant smileing, and a Lambes bleateing is a signe of fertility it is so in Artimedorus; you frown'd when you were borne, and thats the reason you are so sterill; Artimedorus saith so in his fourth booke. None nose. Had it bene man-child, their had bene three evident signes of an whoremaster; a Roman Nose, Cherry Lip, and a bald Pare, for so Artimedorus in his Problems. I surely Gossip Mag-py, and it is a great signe of frugality if the Starrs, and Planets be concordant, for saith Artime dorus; if it be borne under Venus, it will be faire as you are, if under Sol, Rich as you are, and if under Mer curie. I surely Gossip, and stop their wrinckles with too, and saith Artimedorus, in his third booke of his Modernes, if borne under Castor, and Pollux, store of children. You speake broad Gossip, 'tis Pollux. Insooth Gossip, she has given you a veny; Good lacke mistris Vrsula, where have you negotiated Your selfe; you should have bene present, and have Negotiated your selfe about the Maxims, and principles Of child-bearing; what? you had a Mother? No matter for the father; we talke of the surer side, you may be sure to know your mother, when your mother hardly knowes your father; 'tis a very facetious point, as Artime dorus in his booke of dreames sets it downe. Off with your hat sir, you come not here without re verence, see if the little infidell smile not on him, busse, busse, it. Blesse the baby, it has sufficient if it live to be of the sages. Blesse the baby—it has—ey marry has it! Blesse the baby, it has a purse, and no money in't yet, but it may have, and it please the destinies. Looke how it smiles. Blesse us daughter say not so! I hope you will not part in a trance, nor steale away in a qualme; come, come what should be your reason? An't be a dreame, let me come too it; was it a sor rowfull dreame? Artimedorus saith there be divers kinde of meates engender dreames; as Beanes, long Peason-Lentills, Cole-worts, Garlicke, Onions, and the like; Leekes, Ches-Nuts, and other opening Rootes, as Rad-dish, Carrets, Skirrets, Parsenips; now there is some flesh is provoca tive too; as the Hart, the Bore, the ould Hare, and Beefe; and then of fowles, as the Crane, Ducke, Drake, Goose, and Bustard; if you tasted any of these they will engender dreames. But a very true one; looke you Artimedorus in his third booke of his Moderns saith to dreame of Flowers is very good to a woman in child-bed; it argues she shall soone enjoy her husband; to walke on the Seas specifies to a man, delight, but to a woman a dissolute life, for the Sea is like a harlot, a glicery face, and a broken heart. Come, come, doe you sleepe? wee'le watch; by this good drinke; Gossip Mag-py, I was almost dry. So, so, she sleepes, come sit round, and lets have a Carrouse to the litle infidell. By this good liquor, it is so. Twinns daughter that rule most the signe being in Virgo, looke you Gossip Barren, could you once dreame of sore eies you should be sure of children? By this dyet-bread Artimedorus saith so; marke Mistris Vrsula, to dreame to have Lyce, eyther in head or body, in some quantity signifies a proper man well appointed; and by this drinke I dream'd my husband when he came first a woing; came i'th liknes of a Kentish twindle Pippen; that is just, as if two stones grew together, no sooner was I married, but I had two sonnes presently just as Artimedorus saith by this diet-bread. You begin to be sleepy; I can prescribe you a medi cine of Poppy, Mandragora, and other drowsy Sirrops; heida all a sleepe? if my charge sleepes, let me rest, for by this drinke i'me heavy too— Heigho; what's the matter Mistris Vrsula! Dispatch every one severall waies some to th'feilds some to'th water-side; las 'tis but a fit, twill be over presently—away away severally. We trac'd her through the Snow, step, by step, Vntill we came unto the River side, Where like a cunning Hare she had indented To cozen her persuers, and cozen'd her selfe For dround we found her on the River side Nigh Collicke Ferry.

Plot — The Vow Breaker, or The Fair Maid of Clifton
Though Young Bateman is betrothed to Anne Boot and loved by her, her father reneges on the ===== PAGE 347 ===== 1628 arrangement. Meanwhile, war looms: England enters an alliance with a federation of Scottish lords hostile to the French Queen Regent of Scotland, and troops are being recruited to march north. Bateman joins up, hoping that his absence will make Old Boot’s heart grow fonder, and Anne vows to love only him; but her father puts pressure on her to marry German, and, seduced by his wealth, she agrees. The French are vanquished, and Bateman distinguishes himself in battle; but he is troubled by dreams about Anne, and returns home on the day of her wedding, just missing the ceremony. German goes away on a long business trip, leaving Anne in her father’s house. Old Bateman sues Boot for breach of the pre-contract; Young Bateman hangs himself outside Anne’s door, and his ghost haunts Anne, invisible to the rest of her family. Anne gives birth to a baby girl. While the women around her sleep, she sees the ghost and runs out of the house; following her footsteps in the snow, the household find her drowned in the river. The two bereaved fathers, Boot and Bateman, commiserate together. In Scotland, Sir Gervase Clifton insults the Queen Regent by drawing an unfavourable comparison with Queen Elizabeth, and captures Martigues in a single combat, but releases him with honour. Joshua the Puritan intends to hang his cat for Sabbath-breaking - she killed a mouse on Sunday - but the cat is saved by the arrival of a French ambassador. The English attack Leith, and Clifton captures a French colonel’s armour. Hostilities are ended by diplomatic means, and Scotland is to be demilitarized. The English and French lords feast together, and Joshua gets drunk. The English troops return south to meet the Queen at Nottingham; the common soldiers plan to put on a play for her, but decide on a morris dance instead. She meets the civic authorities and rewards her military commanders.
