Full speech

Sir, though it be unusuall in the Court, (Since 'tis the Courtiers garbe) I bend my knee, And doe expect what followes. Your Lordships vow'd adorer: What a thing this brother is! yet Ile vouchsafe him The new Italian shrug—How clownishly The book-worme does returne it. Pray you be happy in the knowledge of This paire of accomplish't Monsieurs. They are Gallants that have seene both Tropicks. Which wee'l make use of; Let's warme our braines with halfe a dozen healths, And then hang cold discourse, for wee'll speake fire-works. Sir. My duty shall Sir, Take any forme you please, and in your motion To have me married, you cut off all dangers The violent heats of youth might beare me to. Nor shall you my Lord, Nor your faire Daughter ever finde just cause To mourne your choice of me; the name of husband Nor the authority it carries in it Shall ever teach me to forget to be As I am now her servant, and your Lordships, And but that modesty forbids, that I Should sound the Trumpe of my owne deserts, I could say my choice manners have beene such, As render me lov'd and remarkable To th' Princes of the blood. Then for my hopes. They stand As faire as any mans, What can there fall In compasse of her wishes which she shall not Be suddenly possess'd of? Loves she titles? By th' grace and favour of my princely friends, I am what she would have me. Full increase of honour Wait ever on your Lordship. Shee's mine Sir, feare it not: In all my travailes, I nere met a Virgin That could resist my Courtship. If this take now, W'are made for ever, and will revell it. Ile assure ye Gentlemen, my Tailor has travail'd, And speakes as lofty language in his bills too, The cover of an old Booke would not shew thus. Fye, fie, what things these Academicks are, These book-wormes, how they looke! I thanke'm for it, Long may he study to give me his state. Saw you my Mistresse? Songs she May have, and reade a little unbak'd Poetry, Such as the Dablers of our time contrive, That has no weight nor wheele to move the minde, Nor indeed nothing but an empty sound; She shall have cloathes but not made by Geometrie, Horses and Coach but of no immortall race; I will not have a Scholar in mine house Above a gentle Reader, They corrupt The foolish women with their subtle problems: Ile have my house call'd Ignorance, to fright Prating Philosophers from enterteinment. To morrow, I thinke, we must have a Masque Boyes, And of our owne making. What, Nymphs with beards? Let's goe, let's goe, I must goe visite, Gentlemen, And marke what sweet lips I must kisse to morrow. And how do's my Master? It seemes your anger is at me, deare Uncle. These are but harsh words Uncle. I would be loth to give you. Doe, speake apaco, for we beleeve exactly: Doe not we stay long Mistris? See, my mad Uncle Is courting my faire Mistresse. My father's here, my brother too! that's a wonder, Broke like a spirit from his Cell. Will you write brother? You but vexe your selfe brother, And vexe your studie too. Yes, would he were buried, I feare hee'l make an affe of me a younger. Tis done already, All, all, is broken off, I am undone friend, My brother's wife againe, and has spoil'd all, Will not release the land, has wonne the Wench too. What would ye have me doe, Sir? The reverence that I ever bare to you Sir, Then to my Uncle, with whom 't had been but sawcinesse T' have been so rough— Yes marry would it, That are prime Courtiers, and must know no angers, But give thankes for our injuries, if we purpose To hold our places. Wee'll to councell, And what may be done by mans wit or valour Wee'll put in execution, I must entreat her, Shee's parcell of my goods. Nay, then you must goe, I must claime mine owne. O, is your heart come downe? what are your tearmes, Sir? Put up, put up. Pray hold, Sir. Are you so unnaturall? Pray Sir, Take my submission, and I disclaime for ever. Yes with all our hearts. In truth Sir, I came but to doe my duty. My Coach and horses made The ransome of our cowardize. And does the Court that should be the example And Oracle of the Kingdome, reade to us No other doctrine. Piety then, And valour, nor to doe nor suffer wrong, Are there no vertues. And all this You seriously beleeve. Now my eyes are open, And I behold a strong necessity That keepes me knave and coward. Nor can I change my Copy, if I purpose To be of your society. My sword forc'd from me too, and still detein'd, You thinke's no blemish. And yet you weare a sword. Wil't not fight of it selfe? Ile borrow this, I like it well. And now I have it, leave me, y'are infectious, The plague and leprosie of your basenesse spreading On all that doe come neere you, such as you Render the Throne of Majesty, the Court Suspected and contemptible, you are Scarabee's That batten in her dung, and have no pallats To taste her curious viands, and like Owles Can onely see her night deformities, But with the glorious splendor of her beauties You are strucke blinde as Moles, that undermine The sumptuous building that allow'd you shelter, You sticke like running ulcers on her face, And taint the purenesse of her native candor, And being bad servants cause your masters goodnesse To be disputed of; make you the Court That is the abstract of all Academies, To teach and practice noble undertakings (Where courage sits triumphant crown'd with Lawrell, And wisedome loaded with the weight of honour) A Schoole of vices. A heavenly one that raising me from sloth and ignorance, (In which your coversation long hath charm'd me) Carries me up into the aire of action, And knowledge of my selfe; even now I feele But pleading onely in the Courts-defence, (Though farre short of her merits and bright lustre) A happy alteration, and full strength To stand her Champion against all the world, That throw aspersions on her. Recant What you have said, ye Mungrils, and licke up The vomit you have cast upon the Court, Where you unworthily have had warmth and breeding, And sweare that you like Spiders, have made poyson Of that which was a saving antidote. Except you two, You Rascals! And that untill You are againe reform'd and growne new men, You nere presume to name the Court, or presse Into the Porters Lodge but for a penance, To be disciplin'd for your roguery, and this done With true contrition. You againe, May eat scraps and be thankfull. Keepe your oathes, And without grumbling vanish. May all the poorenesse of my spirit goe with you, The fetters of my thraldome are fil'd off: And I at liberty to right my selfe, And though my hope in Angellina's little, My honour (unto which compar'd shee's nothing) Shall like the Sunne disperse those lowring Clouds, That yet obscure and dimme it; not the name Of brother shall divert me, but from him, That in the worlds opinion ruin'd me, I will seeke reparation, and call him Unto a strict accompt. Ha! 'tis neere day, And if the Muses friend rose-cheek'd Aurora, Invite him to this solitary grove, As I much hope she will, he seldome missing To pay his vowes here to her, I shall hazard To hinder his devotions—The doore opens.— Tis he most certaine, and by's side my sword, Blest opportunity. Now Sir, Treate of no other Theme, Ile keepe you to it, And see y'expound it well. The same Sir, Your younger brother, who as duty bindes him, Hath all this night (turn'd out of doores) attended, To bid good morrow t'ye. O much, Sir, here I end not, but beginne; I must speake to you in another straine, Than yet I ever us'd, and if the language Appeare in the delivery rough and harsh, You (being my Tutor) must condemne your selfe, From whom I learn'd it. I come not To lay claime to your birthright, 'tis your owne, And 'tis fit you enjoy it, nor aske I from you Your learning and deepe knowledge; (though I am not A Scholar as you are) I know them Diamonds By your sole industry, patience and labour Forc'd from steepe rockes and with much toile attended, And but to few, that prize their value granted, And therefore without rivall freely weare them. I require then, (As from an enemy and not a brother) The reputation of a man, the honour, Not by a faire warre wonne when I was waking, But in my sleepe of folly ravish'd from me, With these, the restitution of my sword, With large acknowledgement of satisfaction, My Coach, my Horses, I will part with life, Ere lose one haire of them, and what concludes all, My Mistresse Angellina, as she was Before the musicall Magicke of thy tongue Inchanted and seduc'd her. These perform'd, And with submission, and done publiquely At my Fathers and my Uncles intercession, (That I put in too) I perhaps may listen To tearmes of reconcilement; but if these In every circumstance are not subscrib'd to, To th' last gaspe I defie thee. My rest is up, Nor will I give lesse. This discourse Is from the subject. You shall fight then. None. She detein'd, I'me deafe To all perswasion. Second him, or call in more helpe, Come not betweene us, Ile not know nor spare you; D'ye fight by th' booke? Never Charles, Till thine, and in thy death, be doubled in me. Nor I from any, this shall decide my interest, Though I am lost to all deserving men, To all that men call good, for suffering tamely Insufferable wrongs, and justly sleighted, By yeelding to a minute of delay In my revenge, and from that made a stranger Unto my fathers house and favour, one wholm'd With all disgraces, yet I will mount upward, And force my selfe a fortune, though my birth And breeding doe deny it. All this is lip-salve. Ile take leave A little to consider. What of her? I would doe much Sir, But still my reputation! And if I follow not, a Cowards name Be branded on my forehead. I dare not doubt it, Sir. He that advances But one step forward dies. Force will not do't nor threats, accept this service From your despair'd of Eustace.

Plot summary

Plot — The Elder Brother
Louis wishes his lazy daughter Angelina would take more exercise, but her woman Sylvia has a better idea: she should get married. Unfortunately Angelina has high standards: when Louis proposes one or other of Brisac’s sons, Charles the scholar and Eustace the courtier, she objects that neither will have the money to keep her as she would wish. Louis pursues the idea anyway, but the bookish Charles, who has only ever seen one woman, his mother, has been put off marriage by the bad examples of antiquity. Louis is willing to accept Eustace, the younger brother, so long as Brisac agrees to disinherit Charles. Much to the disgust of Charles’s uncle, Miramont, a legal instrument is drawn up for the purpose. Charles seems unconcerned by his father’s plans, until he sees Angelina, whereupon he changes his mind about women, refuses to sign away his birthright, and woos the girl himself. She finds him more attractive than Eustace, and the wedding is aborted. Brisac throws him out; Angelina goes with him, and they live together in unmarried chastity. Spurred on by his father, Eustace visits them at night and tries to seize Angelina, but Charles puts up a fight and takes Eustace’s sword from him. Brisac has arranged for Charles’s servant Andrew to marry Lily, and given the couple a farm; but in reality Lily is his mistress and, on the same night as the brothers fight, he visits her for sex. Andrew brings Miramont to the farm to see the liaison for himself, and intervenes as Brisac is undressing for bed. Brisac tries to buy Andrew’s silence with a larger grant of land before realizing that there is a second witness. Eustace borrows a sword and confronts Charles, but Charles will not give up Angelina, and they fight. The bout is interrupted by the arrival of news that Brisac has been arrested by Louis, who insists on the return of Angelina (in the same condition as before). The brothers join forces to rescue their father. Miramont points out that, if he pursues the case, Louis will both ruin Angelina’s reputation and put himself in the control of lawyers; so Louis drops the matter. Miramont pays off the officers and insists that, as a penance, Brisac should abide by his offer to enlarge Andrew’s farm.
