Full speech

Yes, mistris, he will come: but pray resolve Me one thing for my long service; What Businesse have you with the Churchman? Is it to make your Will, or to get you a new Husband? Then I would desire you to remember me, Mistris, I have serv'd you long, and that's the best Service to a woman: make a good Will if you Meane to dye, that it may not be said, Though Most women be long liv'd, yet they all dye With an ill-will. Why then remember your selfe mistris; Take heede how you give away the head; It stands yet upon the shoulders of Your widdowhood; the loving embracing Ivie Has yet the upper place in the house; If you give it to the Holly, take heede, There's pricks in Holly; or if you feare not The pricks, take heede of the wands, you Cannot have the pricks without the wands; You give away the sword, and must defend Your selfe with the scabbard; these are pretty Instructions of a friend; I would be loth to See you cast downe, and not well taken up. I will first give him a caveat, to use you As kindely as he can. If you finde my mistris Have a minde to this coupling at barly-breake, Let her not be the last couple to be left in hell. If she have a minde to a fresh husband, or So, use her as well as you can; let her enter Into as easie bands as may be. O mistris, where are you? I thinke you are the fortunat'st Woman, that ever breath'd of two shoes: the thiefe is Found. Bring him away Iug; nay, you shall see the strangest Piece of felony discover'd that ever you saw, Or your great grandmothers Grandam before, or after, A pirate, a water thiefe. Bring him away Iug; yet the villaine would not Confesse a word till it was found about him. Did you not lose your wedding Ring the other day? No; well, thanke him that brings it Home then; and will aske nothing for his paines. You see this Sammon? It cost but six pence: but had the Fisher knowne The worth of it, 'twould have cost you forty shillings. Is not this your Ring? Your maid Ioane examining this Sammon, that shee Bought in the Market, found that he had swallowed This Gudgeon. Alas, blame not the poore fish, mistris, hee being a fleg, matique Creature, tooke Gold for Restorative. He tooke it faire, And he that gets Gold, let him eate Gold. Now doe I see the old proverbe come to passe; Give a woman lucke, and cast her into th'sea: There's many a man would wish his wife good Lucke, on that condition he might throw her Away so. But mistris, there's one within would Speake with you, that vexeth as fast against Crosses, as you doe against good lucke. Ile tell her so for my owne credits sake. I had rather helpe you to a close, stoole, And't please you. There was some pleasure ere you came to that. That could be but little griefe. But it may be he has not that should please her. We have hunted well, mistris; doe you not see The hare's in sight? Nay, I'l be sworne upon a booke of Callico for that.

Plot — A New Wonder, A Woman Never Vexed
Old Forster has recently made his fortune by marrying a rich widow, but they are both dissatisfied with the financial irresponsibility of his brother and son, whom ===== PAGE 17 ===== 1624 they are on the point of disowning. Robert, the son, has purloined some of his stepmother’s money to buy his uncle, Stephen Forster, out of debtors’ prison; he also gives Stephen money to buy new clothes. Stephen uses the money for gambling, and is cheated. Robert is disappointed to find him in the gaming-house, and he himself likewise gets into trouble with his stepmother for going there, even though he only went in to look for Stephen. Mistress Forster has an uncommonly even- tempered friend, another rich widow, who enjoys miraculous good fortune. When she loses her wedding ring in the river, it turns up inside a salmon bought by her maid. She meets Stephen at the gaming-house and likes him. She suggests that he should marry for money, and offers herself. He accepts, and they perform a hand-fasting. Jane is being pursued by two undesirable suitors, an old knight and a young fool. Her father, Alderman Brune, suggests Robert Forster as a third option, if he can be reconciled with his father. Brune is Old Forster’s partner in a mercantile venture, but accepts Forster’s offer to buy him out; he takes payment in cloth rather than cash, which clears Forster’s warehouses for the new merchandise due in any day. However, Forster continues to look askance at Robert. News arrives that the ships have sunk in the Thames: Forster is bankrupt, and voluntarily commits himself to prison in order to avoid his creditors. Stephen takes charge of his new wife’s property, and proves, against expectations, to be provident rather than spendthrift. He asks Robert to help him manage the estate, and adopts him as his heir, insisting only that he does nothing to help his father. However, Stephen himself pays the prison-keeper to ensure that his brother remains comfortable. When Jane’s two other suitors complain of the favour shown to Robert, Stephen produces evidence that they are in debt to the widow’s first husband (and therefore now to him), and the threat of arrest gets rid of them. The widow generously accepts their payment of £200 to discharge a debt of ten times that amount. Unable to resist his sense of filial obligation, Robert purloins the money and gives it to his father. Stephen pretends to be angry, but secretly pays off his brother’s creditors and thereby secures his release.…
