Well wench, wee haue crosse fates: our Husbands such inueterate foes, and we such entire friends, but the best is we are neighbours, and our backe Arbors may afford visitation freely: prethee let vs maintaine our familiaritie still whatsoeuer thy hus band doe vnto thee, as I am afraid he will crosse it i'the nicke. Well said wench, and the Pricke-song wee vse shall be our husbands. Wilt'ou little Iew? And I to kisse thy husband Vpon the least belly-ake. This will mad'em. No, my Lord, nor doe we take your toy, as they say. This is a childes birth, that must not be deliuered before a man, Though your Lordship might be a Mid-wife for your chinne. Why, dost thinke Ile make my Husband a Cuckold? O here they come. Well sir, we will not sticke with you. What's here? a Ship sailing nigh her hauen? With good ware belike: 'tis well ballast. You see no likelihood of that: would it not faine be in the hauen? The word, Vt tangerem Portum. Marry, for ought I know, God grant it. What's there? You graspe my hand too hard ifaith, faire sir, Holding her by the hand. Well sir, your vizor giues you colour for what you say. I marry: I like this wooer well: Hee'll win's pleasure out o'the stones. The second change. Isabella fals in loue with Rogero when the changers speak. You dare put it into my hands. Then thus, to morrow (you'll be secret, seruant.) He shall not be long out, but you shall put in, I warrant you. Haue a care that you stand iust i'the nicke about sixe a clocke in the euening; my Maide shall conduct you vp, to saue mine honor you must come vp darkling, and to auoid suspition. But if you faile to doo't. Tye this ring fast, you may be sure to know. You'll brag of this, now you haue brought me to the bay. Thais, you're an earely riser. I haue that to shew will make your hayre stand an-end. How, how, what would I say? nay, by this light, what would I not doe? If euer Amazon fought better, or more at the face then Ile doe, let me neuer be thought a new married vvife. Come vnmasque her: 'tis some admirable creature, vvhose beau tie you neede not paint. I warrant you, 'tis done to your hand. 'Tis my Husbands hand, and a Loue-Letter: But for the contents I finde none in it. Has the lustfull monster, All backe and belly-steru'd me thus? What defect does he see in mee? Ile be sworne wench, I am of as pliant and yeelding body to him, e'en vvhich way hee vvill, hee may turne mee as hee list himselfe. What? and dedicate to thee: I marry, here's a stile so high, as a man cannot helpe a Dog o'er it. He was wont to write to me in the Citie phrase, My good Abigall : here's Astonishment of nature, vnparaleld excelency, and most vnequal raritie of creation: Three such wordes will turne any honest woman in the world whore: for a woman is neuer wonne till shee know not vvhat to answere; and beshrew me if I vnderstand any of these: you are the partie I perceiue, and here's a white sheete, that your hus band has promist me to do penance in: you must not thinke to dance the shaking of the sheetes alone though there be not such rare phrases in't, tis more to the matter; a legible hand, but for the dash, or the (hee) and (as): short bawdy Parenthesis as euer you saw, to the purpose: hee has not left out a pricke I warrant you, wherein he has promist to doe me any good, but the Law's in mine owne hand. To be short sweete hart, Ile be true to thee, though a lyer to my Husband: I haue signed your Husbands bill like a Wood cocke as hee is held, perswaded him (since nought but my loue can asswage his violent passions) hee should enioy, like a priuate friend, the pleasures of my bed: I tolde him my Hus band was to goe to Mawrano to day, to renew a Farme hee has, and in the meane time hee might be tenant at will, to vse mine: this false fire has so tooke with him, that he's rauisht afore hee come. I haue had stones on him all red: dost know this: I meane thus: the same occasion will serue him too, they are birds of a feather, and vvill flye together, I vvarrant thee wench, appoint him to come: say that thy Husband's gone for Mawrano, and tell mee anone if thou mad'st not his heart-bloud spring, for ioy, in his face. They say mine has compiled an vngodly volume of Satyres against women, and cals his booke The Snarle. Make much of him, for all our wanton prize, Follow the Prouerbe, Merry be and wise. Exeunt. This is a rule I still will keepe in brest, Loue well thy husband wench, but thy selfe best. I feare not to come late. Exeunt. Oh husband, I little thought to see you in this taking. So you may still husband. I by my faith, and so I am husband, Belike you know I am with child. O mee vnfortunate! Husband: I'le naile me to the earth, but I'le Winne your pardon. My Iewels, iointure, all I haue shall flye: Apparell, bedding, I'le not leaue a Rugge; So you may come off fairely. Plot summary

Plot — The Insatiate Countess
Suitors visit Isabella, who is in open mourning for her dead husband, but secretly glad to be free of his jealousy and restrictions. She accepts Count Roberto’s suit and they are married the following day; but when she dances with Count Rogero in the wedding masque, her affections turn. She woos Rogero by letter and elopes with him to Pavia; Roberto enters holy orders. At Pavia, Rogero is visited by his friend Gniaca; Isabella falls in love with the newcomer, and feigns illness to give herself an opportunity to seduce him. After bedding Gniaca, she then feigns penitence to free herself from Rogero. The spurned lover takes his revenge by circulating bitter satires on Isabella’s promiscuity, so she sets on Gniaca to kill him. In the attempt, Gniaca is reconciled with his former friend, so Isabella seduces Don Sago, colonel in the Spanish power occupying Pavia, to murder both Rogero and Gniaca. Sago kills Rogero, and is arrested, but pardoned by the Duke of Medina. Isabella’s attempt to bribe the Duke fails to secure her freedom, and she is beheaded after a scaffold reconciliation with Roberto. In the sub-plot, the enemies Claridiana and Mizaldus are both married on the same day. They quarrel when they meet, and Mizaldus’s wife Abigail and Lady Lentulus, who are close friends, compare notes and swap places: each receives her own husband in the other’s bedroom. Meanwhile their friends Mendosa and Lady Lentulus, attracted to one another, try to circumvent the latter’s vow not to admit a man through her front door: he climbs up to her window by rope ladder, but falls into the street, where the watch find him injured and send him to a surgeon. Claridiana and Mizaldus are arrested in each other’s house; appalled at the thought of being cuckolded, they both confess to murdering Mendosa. Mendosa in turn tries to save Lady Lentulus’s honour with a false confession that he was attempting to burgle her house. In court, the Duke establishes that Claridiana and Mizaldus are telling incompatible stories about the ‘murder’, and they are sentenced to death in jest; in Mendosa’s case, however, the sentence is genuine. Claridiana and Mizaldus are determined to die until confronted with evidence of their wives’ chastity; with reconciliation comes reprieve.
