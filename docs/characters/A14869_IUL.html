He died in perfect memory I hope, And made me his heyre. He's gone the right way then without question: Friend, in time of mourning, we must not vse any action, That is but accessary to the making men merry, I doe therefore giue you nothing for your good tidings. Honest fellow, giue me thy hand, I doe not thinke but thou hast carried New yeares gifts to'th Court in thy dayes, and learndst there to be so free of thy paynes taking. Doe you know him? And what's his businesse? And what's your counsell? He comes hotly vpon me at first: whoring? When did you euer heare, that a Cockesparrow Had the French poxe? A very fine Naturallist, a Phisician, I a e you by your round slop; for tis iust of the bignes, and no more, of the case for a Vrinall: tis concluded, you are a Phisician. What doe you meane sir, youl take cold. Pray preserue your veluet from the dust. I was neuer so abused with the hat in the hand In my life. Taylors? Besides my fathers allowance? Why Gentleman, doe you thinke an Auditor begat me? Would you haue me make euen at yeares end? How I am bayted? I thinke this fellow is a witch I those are Hell hounds. Hee's a mad fellow. Where are these Gallants, the braue Ercole, And noble Contarino? Met you the Lord Ercole? Why, what's the matter? To fight? Let's take seuerall wayes then, And if't be possible for womens sakes, For they are proper men, vse our endeauours, That the pricke doe not spoyle them. You shall not: Take him vp gently: so, and bow his body, For feare of bleeding inward. Well, these are perfect louers. It has been euer my opinion, That there are none loue perfectly indeed, But those that hang or drowne themselues for loue: Now these haue chose a death next to Beheading, They haue cut one anothers throats, Braue valiant Lads. This is my father, how the Iudges haue bleated him. And I from you sir. I could neuer away with after reckonings. A pox ont, I haue vndertaken the challenge very foolishly: what if I doe not appeare to answer it? I doe not like that so well. I see then I must fight whether I will or no. Very certaine; and now you talke of fencing, Doe not you remember the Welsh Gentleman, That was trauailing to Rome vpon returne? There was a strange experiment of a Fencer. Marry it taught him an ill grace in's play, It made him gape still, gape as he put in for't, As I haue seene some hungry Vsher. Not vnlikely. Who can tell me, If we may breath in the Duell? Nor drinke? That's scuruy, anger will make me very dry. In rainy weather. Then twas wet indeed sir. Now I am right in the Bandileere for th' gallows. What a scuruy fashion tis, to hang ones coffin in a scarfe? Yes, if he had had an houre-glasse by him, I would haue wisht him he would haue ioggd it a little. But your mother, your mother's lockt in to. Harke, he knocks to be let out and he were mad. Ha, what sayes he? Contarino liuing? I am sory for one thing. That I made not mine owne Ballad: I doe feare I shall be roguishly abused in Meeter, If I miscarry. Well, if the young Capuchin Doe not talke a'th flesh as fast now to your mother, As he did to vs a'th spirit; if he doe, Tis not the first time that the prison royall Has been guiltie of close committing. I thanke you, I haue an humour now to goe to Sea Against the Pyrats; and my onely ambition, Is to haue my Ship furnisht with a rare consort Of Musicke; and when I am pleased to be mad, They shall play me Orlando.

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
