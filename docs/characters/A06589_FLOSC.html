Full speech

Madame, if you woulde compare the state of Cynthia with your owne, and the height of Endimion his thoughts, with the meanenesse of your fortune, you would rather yeeld then contende, being betweene you and her no comparison, and rather wonder then rage at the greatnes of his minde, beeing affected with a thing more then mortall. But knowe you not fayre Ladie, that Cynthia gouerneth all things? Your grapes woulde be but drie huskes, your Corne but chaffe, and all your vertues vaine, were it not Cynthia that preserueth the one in the bud, and nourisheth the other in the blade, and by her influence both comforteth all things, and by her autho ritie commaundeth all creatures; Suffer then Endimion to followe his affections, though to obtaine her be im possible, and let him flatter himselfe in his owne ima ginations, because they are immortall. A strange practise, if it be possible. A reuenge incredible, and if it may be, vnna turall. When thys is done what then, shall it conti nue tyll hys death, or shall he doate for euer in this de light? Why if this be not the end, all the rest is to no ende. Affection that is bred by enchauntment, is like a flower that is wrought in silke, in colour and forme most like, but nothing at all in substance or sauour. Well, vse your owne wyll, but you shal finde that loue gotten with witch-craft, is as vnpleasant, as fish, taken with medicines vnwholsome. Then about it. Poore Endimion, what traps are layde for thee, because thou honourest one that all the world wondreth at. And what plots are cast to make thee vnfortunate, that studiest of all men to be the faith fullest. Vse your discretion, I will in this case, neither giue counsell nor consent, for there cannot bee a thing more monstrous, then to force affection by sorcery, nei ther doe I imagin anie thing more impossible. Ah Endimion, none so ioyfull as Floscula, of thy restoring. Endimion, I reioyce to see thee in thy former estate.
