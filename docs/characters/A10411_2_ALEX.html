Laurinda, (The name which but to speak I would not wish For life or breath.) Let not thy powerfull beauty Torment us longer: Tell us which of us You value most. Or if he cannot so, as sure he cannot, Yet he will rather chuse to dye then live Once to oppose your liking. How, me Laurinda? That word doth relish immortalitie. Still more perplexed: what doe you think I am? Which I? Thus you scorne my love. Damon, desist thy suit or loose thy life; Thou heard'st Laurinda wish she were my wife. I am her head: That word doth seeme t'impart She meanes my marriage. But, Damon, I next to her heart doe stand: I am her hope, in that you plainly see The end of her intents doth aime at me. I am her flame. The flames the hotter, therefore her desire Most aimes at mee. She promis'd now I should injoy her Bed. I see she still conceales it, and with speeches Perplext and doubfull masks her secret thoughts. I goe that way a hunting, And will call for her. And would she might preuaile! All happinesse to Damon be Except Laurinda. Thus we in love and courtesie contend. I much wonder, Dorylas, Amyntas can be sad, having such follies To provoke mirth. But I am ne're at home; Thou and I, Damon, Are absent from our selves. That may end One of our miseries, and the others life! Now you are like cruell To both, and play the tyrant equally, On him you hate as much as him you love. O those words to me Are Poyson. And what am I? Most blessed I! this Garland once did twine About her head that now imbraces mine. But me she gave That which was hers. I think 'tis greater kindnesse to receive. I will dispute no more. And determine of our feares. Come Damon, by this argument let us prove, Which tis of us Laurinda best doth love. How early, Damon, doe lovers rise? He that of us shall have Laurinda, Damon, Will not be up so soone: ha! would you Damon? I much wonder the Sunne so soone can rise! No summer, Damon. And he doth rise From her to gaze on faire Laurinda's eyes. On whom depends our only happinesse. Yes, the first we meet. Devotion's not so early up as love. That modest rose He grafted there. The virgins now come from the Temple. That name breaths life & soul to poore Alexis. As many joyes crown mine With happinesse. No other blessing hath preserved mee. I'le instruct you, Take here the paper, pen and inke. You are to passe your censure, Being the first Nymph that we have met this morning, Which of us two must have the faire Laurinda. Write your award; our mutuall oathes doe bind us Not to deny't. Damon you wrong us both. How can it? you know, Damon, I have not beene one minute from your presence. Neither, Nor I nor you could sleepe one winke this night, The expectation of this morning tryall Did keepe us both awake. To lovers they are none, we break those bonds As easily as threds of silke: A bracelet Made of your maidens haire's a stronger chaine Then twenty cobweb oathes, which while we break Venus but laughs: it must be your perswasion That works him to it. And when we left 'em He was incens'd. Now I expect to hear my blessed doome. O, I am ruin'd in the height of hope. How like the hearb Solstitiall is a lover, Now borne, now dead again, he buds, sprouts forth, Flourishes, ripens, withers in a minute. Now my Laurinda, now (o happy now!) All lets that stood between my joy and mee Are gone and fled. Season? why, can love Be ever out of season? Lau. And such shall our affection still be seene. Loves, harvest shall; (Lovers you know) his harvest ought to bee All the yeare long. A while in lov's an age. 'Tis but cold love that's temperate in desire. And I, but how unwilling Cupid knowes!

Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
