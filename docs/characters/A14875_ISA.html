Full speech

And aboue health To see my Lord well, Deuotion my Lord. 'Tis burdened with too many, and I thinke The oftner that we cast our reckonings vp, Our sleepes will be the sounder. Nay my deere Lord I will not haue you angry, Doth not my absence from you two moneths, Merit one kisse? O my loued Lord, I do not come to chide; my iealousy, I am to learne what that Italian meanes, You are as welcome to these longing armes, As I to you a Virgine. You haue oft for these two lippes Neglected Cassia or the naturall sweetes Of the Spring-violet, they are not yet much whithered, My Lord I should be merry, these your frownes Shew in a Helmet, louely but on me, In such a peacefull enterueiw me thinkes They are to too roughly knit. Neuer my deere Lord. I pray sir burst my heart, and in my death Turne to your antient pitty, though not loue. O to too far you haue curst. Forbid it the sweet vnion Of all things blessed; why the Saints in heauen Will knit their browes at that. O my winding sheet, Now shall I need thee shortly, deere my Lord, Let me heare once more, what I would not heare, Neuer. O my vnkind Lord may your sins find mercy, As I vpon a woefull widowed bed, Shall pray for you, if not to turne your eyes, Vpon your wretched wife, and hopefull sonne, Yet that in time you'le fix them vpon heauen. No my deere Lord, you shall haue present witnesse, How I'le worke peace betweene you, I will make My selfe the author of your cursed vow I haue some cause to do it, you haue none, Conceale it I beseech you, for the weale Of both your Dukedomes, that you wrought the meanes Of such a separation, let the fault Remaine with my supposed iealousy, And thinke with what a pitteous and rent hear, I shall performe this sad insuing part. Sir you shall not, No though Uittoria vpon that condition Would become honest. By my life sir no, I sweare by that I do not care to loose. Are all these ruines of my former beauty, Laid out for a whores triumph? O that I were a man, or that I had power To execute my apprehended wishes, I would whip some with scorpions. To dig the strumpets eyes out, let her lye Some twenty monethes a dying, to cut off Her nose and lippes, pull out her rotten teeth, Preserue her flesh like Mummia, for trophies Of my iust anger: Hell to my affliction Is meere snow-water, by your fauour sir, Brother draw neere, and my Lord Cardinall, Sir let me borrow of you but one kisse, Hence-forth I'le neuer lye with you, by this, This wedding ring. And this diuorce shall be as truly kept, As if in thronged Court, a thousand eares Had heard it, and a thousand Lawyers hands, Seal'd to the separation. Let not my former dotage, Make thee an vnbeleuer, this my vow Shall neuer on my soule be satisfied With my repentance, manet alta mente repositum. No sir I'le presently to Padua, I will not stay a minute. "Vnkindnesse do thy office, poore heart breake, "Those are the killing greifes which dare not speake.

In this, Bracciano is abetted by Vittoria’s brother Flaminio, who persuades her husband Camillo that their sleeping apart will make her more amorous. Vittoria tells Bracciano of a dream which seems to suggest the murder of Camillo and the Duchess Isabella; but the liaison is forestalled by the intervention of Vittoria’s mother, Cornelia. Flaminio inveighs against Cornelia for compromising his chances of preferment. Isabella comes to Rome to effect a reconciliation with her husband. Cardinal Monticelso and the Great Duke of Florence also put pressure on him to fulfil his responsibilities, both marital and political. In a private interview with Isabella, Bracciano vows never again to sleep with her, but she tries to limit the political damage by making the same vow herself in public. Florence and Monticelso plan to tempt Bracciano into adultery by getting Camillo out of Rome on public business, but Bracciano plots murder: Isabella is killed when she kisses a poisoned picture of Bracciano, and Flaminio breaks Camillo’s neck. Vittoria is arrested and arraigned before an ecclesiastical court; unable to prove her complicity in Camillo’s murder, it convicts her of adultery and commits her to a house of convertites. Flaminio feigns madness to evade interrogation. Lodovico witnesses Isabella’s murder. He returns to Rome illegally to bring Florence the news, but his pardon is signed after the event. He and Flaminio meet and quarrel. Florence borrows from Monticelso a list of villains, in order to select an agent of vengeance. By writing a letter feigning love for Vittoria, he induces Bracciano to contrive her escape from the house of convertites, disguised as a page. Monticelso is elected Pope; his first act is to excommunicate Bracciano and Vittoria. Florence sends Lodovico to Padua to assassinate Bracciano, and by trickery makes it appear that Monticelso supports the conspiracy. Bracciano marries Vittoria and accepts into his court Mulinassar, a Moorish soldier, who is actually Florence in disguise.
