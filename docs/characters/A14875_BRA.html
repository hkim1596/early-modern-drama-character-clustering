Your best of rest. Flamineo. Quite lost Flamineo. Are wee so happy. Wee are happie aboue thought, because boue merrit. O but her iealous husband. O should she faile to come, Giue credit: I could wish time would stand still And neuer end this enteruew this hower, But all delight doth it selfe soon'st dcuour. Let me into your bosome happy Ladie, Powre out in stead of eloquence my vowes, Loose me not Madam, for if you forcgo me I am lost eternallie. You are a sweet Phisition. Excellent Creature. Wee call the cruell fayre, what name for you That are so mercifull? What valew is this Iewell In sooth ile haue it; nay I will but change My Iewell for your Iewell. Yes. Your hand boy growing to souldier. O dissemblance. Do you bandy factions gainst me? haue you learn't, The trick of impudent basenes to complaine Vnto your kindred? Because your brother is the corpulent Duke, That is the great Duke, S'death I shall not shortly Rackit away fiue hundreth Crownes at Tenis, But it shall rest vpon record: I scorne him Like a shau'd Pollake, all his reuerent wit Lies in his wardrope, hee's a discret fellow When hee's made vp in his roabes of state, Your brother the great Duke, because h'as gallies, And now and then ransackes a Turkish flye-boate, (Now all the hellish furies take his soule,) First made this match, accursed be the Priest That sang the wedding Masse, and euen my Issue. Your hand I'le kisse, This is the latest ceremony of my loue, Hence-forth I'le neuer lye with thee, by this, This wedding-ring: I'le ne'remore lye with thee. And this diuorce shall be as truely kept, As if the Iudge had doom'd it: fare you well, Our sleeps are seuer'd. Let not thy loue, Make thee an vnbeleeuer, this my vow, Shall neuer on my soule bee satisfied With my repentance: let thy brother rage Beyond a horred tempest or sea-fight, My vow is fixed. Neuer? You see 'tis not my seeking. May it thriue with you. Forbeare your kindnesse, an vnbidden guest Should trauaile as dutch-women go to Church: Beare their stooles with them. That question Inforceth me breake silence, I was there. Cowardly dogs barke loudest. Sirrah Priest, Ile talke with you hereafter,—Do you heare? The sword you frame of such an excellent temper, I'le sheath in your owne bowels: There are a number of thy coate resemble Your common post-boyes. Now you and I are friends sir, wee'le shake hands, In a friends graue, together, a fit place, Being the embleme of soft peace t'attone our hatred. In this, Bracciano is abetted by Vittoria’s brother Flaminio, who persuades her husband Camillo that their sleeping apart will make her more amorous. Vittoria tells Bracciano of a dream which seems to suggest the murder of Camillo and the Duchess Isabella; but the liaison is forestalled by the intervention of Vittoria’s mother, Cornelia. Flaminio inveighs against Cornelia for compromising his chances of preferment. Isabella comes to Rome to effect a reconciliation with her husband. Cardinal Monticelso and the Great Duke of Florence also put pressure on him to fulfil his responsibilities, both marital and political. In a private interview with Isabella, Bracciano vows never again to sleep with her, but she tries to limit the political damage by making the same vow herself in public. Florence and Monticelso plan to tempt Bracciano into adultery by getting Camillo out of Rome on public business, but Bracciano plots murder: Isabella is killed when she kisses a poisoned picture of Bracciano, and Flaminio breaks Camillo’s neck. Vittoria is arrested and arraigned before an ecclesiastical court; unable to prove her complicity in Camillo’s murder, it convicts her of adultery and commits her to a house of convertites. Flaminio feigns madness to evade interrogation. Lodovico witnesses Isabella’s murder. He returns to Rome illegally to bring Florence the news, but his pardon is signed after the event. He and Flaminio meet and quarrel. Florence borrows from Monticelso a list of villains, in order to select an agent of vengeance. By writing a letter feigning love for Vittoria, he induces Bracciano to contrive her escape from the house of convertites, disguised as a page. Monticelso is elected Pope; his first act is to excommunicate Bracciano and Vittoria. Florence sends Lodovico to Padua to assassinate Bracciano, and by trickery makes it appear that Monticelso supports the conspiracy. Bracciano marries Vittoria and accepts into his court Mulinassar, a Moorish soldier, who is actually Florence in disguise.
