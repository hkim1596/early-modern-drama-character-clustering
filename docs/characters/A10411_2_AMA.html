For what? deere brother speake! Borrow there? How this greefe Racks my tormented soule? but the neglect Of Damon more afflicts mee: the whole Senate Of heaven decrees my ruine. Would to Ceres That I were dead! Then had not I been wretched! Alas what strange distraction, Tosse his distemperd braine! Mine is that restlesse toile. Seeme to be What he conceives you. I will not. What a sad paire are wee? And he that I Doe love, loves not; or, if he love, not mee. And my Damon Has undone me. But his unkindnesse, me; unhappy me! But does not: thy Amyntas Returnes thee mutuall love. Mine has reason, but no love. O mee! Come Vrania: Let's sit together like two marble monuments Of ever weeping misery— If so, my Damon, How many times hath thy unkindnesse ruin'd Sad Amaryllis? every frowne is mortall. Be not so cruell to mee! Gentle Damon, —Accept this witnesse of my love, it is The story of poore Ech … , tha … for love Of her Narcissus pin'd into a voice. Damon, suppose I should, And then the Gods for thy contempt of mee Should plague thee like Narcissus. —. Prithee love accept it, 'Twas wrought by mine own hand O, I am sick to death! My griefe o're weighs me! Like a Taper Allmost burnt out: sometimes all a darknesse, And now and then a flash or two of comfort, But soone blown out againe. Ah Thestylis, I cannot long subsist. For thee vain's labour; Away! I hate thee cause my Damon does, And for that reason too I hate my selfe, And every thing but him! Doe Vrania, or I dye. Stay me not Vrania! Dishevel'd haire, poore ornament of the head I'le teare you from my crowne! what dost thou here? Weake chaines! my pride presum'd you had a powre To fetter Heroes! and in amorous Gives Lead any sheapheard captive! But Damon breakes thee like a spiders loome! And thou poore face that wer't so oft beli'de For fair and beauteous, by my flattering glasse; I'le teare those crimson roses from my cheekes, That but my selfe nere yet inchanted any. My will is fixt! Since Damon hates my life I'le goe and see If I can please him in my death: if hee'le but deigne To kisse me, and accept my latest breath, I shall salute the Gods a happy soule. —This dart I'le give him; and upon my knees ▪ Beg till I have obtain'd to dye by him: Death from that hand is welcome. I shall wrong you, Laurinda! No injoy him, The treasure of the Earth: my latest words Shall be praiers for you: mild Vrania, Sister in blood to Damon, not in affection, Nymph take this whistle, 'twas a Tritons once, With which I call my Lamb-kins when they stray; 'Tis Amaryllis last bequeathment to you. Laurinda, my great legacy is yours, Gentle-ungentle Damon. Innocence Let me not understand you. How, to Damon? To him she loves so deerely? Why yet sir I know no more. What was it he said last?—Thou hast indeed A time wherein thou maist revenge my scorne. —With love, no otherwise: and there thou shalt not Prevent mee, Damon. I will write—This inke Deserves not to record the name of Damon, Tis black and ugly; thou thy selfe hast furnisht mee With that of better colour. 'Tis my blood That's truly Cupids inke: love ought to write Only with that;—. This paper is too course; O that I had my heart, to write it there! But so it is already. Would I had A Parchment made of my own skin, in that To write the truth of my affection, A wonder to posterity!—Hand make hast As my bloud does, or I shall faint I feare Ere I have done my story.— My blood congeales Within my quill, and I can write no more. I will fold it up And send it; who's that's here? my eyes Are dimme, ha, Dorylas! Dorylas, carry this letter for mee. Good Dorylas be gone, whilest yet my breath Will give me leave to say it was not you. Yes, That fatall name is mine. I shall anon Be nothing but the name. I'le rather dye Then name him, though it be a name I use Oft to repeat, and every repetition Is a new soule unto mee: 'tis a name I have taught the birds to caroll, every Laurell and Cedar beares it registred Vpon his tender barke; it is a name In which is all the life I yet have left; A name I long to speake; yet I had rather Dye all the severall sorts of death twice over Then speake it once. Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
