Such woefull plaints cannot repaire your ſtate. what Stoicke ſtrange, who moſt preciſe appeares, Could that youths death with teareleſſe eyes behold, In all perfections ripe, though greene in yeares, A hoarie judgement under lockes of gold? No, no man lives, but muſt lament to ſee The worlds chiefe hope even in the bloſſome choak'd, "But men cannot controll the Heavens decree, "And what is done, can never be revok'd. Let not this loſſe with griefe torment you more; Of which a part with you your Country beares: If wailing could your ruin'd ſtate reſtore, Soules charg'd with griefe ſhould ſaile in Seas of teares; Leſt all our comfort daſh againſt one ſhelfe, And his untimely death but haſten yours. Have pitty of your people, ſpare your ſelfe, If not to your own uſe, yet unto ours. This benefit muſt binde me with the reſt To ſerve your Majeſtie, and hold you deere, And I'le be free with you, yet I proteſt, That what I friendly ſpeake, you freely heare. Oft men of Iudges thence have parties gone, "Where both their eares were patent but to one. A deadly rancor reconcil'd againe, "With conſanguinity would ſeal'd remaine. Though natures law you car'd not to tranſgreſſe, "Nor this your wrong'd ally would not repaire; "Yet the regard to Monarchs in diſtreſſe, "Should move the mighty with a mutuall care; "Thoſe terrours too which thunder in your eare, "I thinke the Lydians will not well allow: "For when the Cedar falles, the Oake may feare, "That which o'rethrowes the Medes may trouble you. "And when a neighbours houſe they burning view, "Then their owne dangers men may apprehend; "It better is with others to purſue, "Then be when but alone, forc'd to defend. Plot summary

Plot — Croesus
Solon visits Croesus, hoping to persuade him to give up his immoderate love of gold and think about less transitory things. He explains that all mortality is subject to change, and tells Croesus stories about the importance of posthumous fame, but Croesus considers them irrelevant to his situation as a king. Solon realizes that Croesus wili change only by losing the power which has made him arrogant. Adrastus is banished from Phrygia after accidentally killing his brother. (His intended victim was the man who poisoned his girlfriend.) Croesus has a nightmare about the death of his son Atys, and becomes excessively protective: he makes Atys marry in order to secure the succession, appoints him a bodyguard, and bans the type of sharp implement which caused his death in the dream. Mysia is ravaged by a wild boar, and its people appeal to Croesus for help. He is happy to hear that they don’t want his money, but distressed that they ask instead that he send Atys to hunt the boar. Atys protests at his father’s reluctance, so Croesus accedes, assigning Adrastus to look after him during the mission. Atys is killed, and Adrastus kills himself in remorse. In the past, Astyages, King of Media and Croesus’ brother-in-law, was perturbed by a prophecy that he would be deposed by his grandson. When the child was born, he tried to have it killed; but the herdsman who was given the job spared its life and adopted it as a replacement for his own stillborn baby. During his youth, Cyrus’ princely behaviour led to his identification. Astyages served Harpagus a banquet of his own son’s flesh, so Harpagus contacted Cyrus and raised a rebellion. Now Cyrus has led the Persian army to depose Astyages. Against his counsellor’s advice, Croesus plans to intervene and conquer Persia, but Cyrus anticipates him and attacks pre-emptively, ordering that Croesus be taken alive. Croesus is nearly killed by a Persian soldier, but saved when his dumb son cries out and identifies him. Cyrus intends to burn him at the stake, but is impressed by Croesus’ acknowledgement that Solon was right to warn him not to trust in worldly things. Cyrus frees him, and orders restraint in his soldiers’ looting.
