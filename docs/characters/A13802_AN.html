I vnderstand you not. Hands off. I dare, and must To all the world, 'long as Antonio liues. To me these tearmes! Tick, tock. O my faire daughter Flauia ! Let all the Starres Powre downe full blessings on thee. Ope the dores. Know'st not thy father, old Antonio ? Is all the world growne frantick? Thy louing father, Flauia. That dead and drown'd Am I. Th'art much abus'd, I liue: come downe, and know mee. Let mee come in. Quickly, or else — I'le beate the dores and windowe's About your eares. Impudent whoore! If I be chang'd beyond thy knowledge, sonne, Consider that th'excesse of heate in Barbarie, The feare of shipwrack, and long tedious iournies, Haue tan'd my skin, and shrunk my eyes and cheekes; Yet still this face, though alter'd, may be knowne. This skarre beares witnesse, 'twas the wound thou cur'dst With thine owne hands. I haue no other markes Or reasons to perswade thee; me thinkes, this word I am thy father, were argument sufficient To bend my knees, and creepe to my embracements. WRetched Antonio, hast beene preseru'd so strangely From forraine miseries to be wrong'd at home? Bar'd from thy house by th'scornes of thine owne children? 'May be my sonne hath sold it in my absence, Thinking me dead. How long has't cal'd you master? Which Antonio? Alas My weaknesse growne by age, and paines of trauell, Dissarmes my courage to defend my selfe; I haue no strength but patience. Peter, and Thomas, William, what you please. Sir, heat of wine. But good sir, if I be not I, who am I? Strange negligence To loose my selfe! me thinkes I liue and moue, Remember. Could the fearefull apprehension Of th'vgly feare of drowning so transforme me? Or did I dye, and by Pythagoras rule, My soule's prouided of another lodging? Dangers at sea Are pleasures, weigh'd with these home-iniuries. Was euer man thus skar'd beside himselfe? O most vnfortunate Antonio! At sea thou suffredst shipwrack of thy goods, At land of thine owne selfe. Antonio ? Or what name else they please? fly, fly to Barbarie, And rather there endure the forraigne crueltie Of fetters, whippes, and Moores, then here at home Be wrong'd and baffled by thy friends and children. These wrongs recall my strength ▪ I am resolu'd. Better dye once, then suffer alwayes. Draw. This some Asse! As I remember, fourescore and fifteene pound In Barbarie gold. Had Lucio kept his word I had carried iust a hundred. How an Astrologer? Then hence it came they cald me Trincalo, And talk't of an Astrologer; which names Almost inrag'd me past my selfe and senses. 'Tis true I promis'd, but haue oft repented it. And much more since he goes about to cheate me. He must not haue her, sir. Sonne, at your pleasure Dispose of Flauia, with my full consent. I do; and euer had desire to match Into that family; and now I finde my selfe Old, weake, vnfit for marriage, you shall enioy her, If I can worke Pandolfo by intreatie: Cricca, to thy direction We yeeld our selues, manage vs at thy pleasure. SIgnior Pandolfo! welcome. Thanks, deerest friend. To speake my dangers past, were to discourse Of dead men at a feast. Such sad relations Become not marriages. Sir, I am here Return'd to doe you seruice: where's your sonne? Thankes Eugenio. How thinke you gentlemen? were it amisse To call downe Flauia and Sulpitia, That what we do, may with a full consent Be entertain'd by all? Lelio, call my daughter. Was't not right? and spoken Like old Antonio ? Safe. EVgenio, Flauia, Lelio, and Sulpitia ▪ Marriages once confirm'd, and consummate, Admit of no repentance. Therefore 'tis fitting All parties with full freedome speake their pleasure, Before it be too late. And you Pandolfo ? First for preparatiue, Or slight Praeludium to the greater matches; I must intreate you that my Armellina Be match't with Trincalo. Two hundred crownes I giue her for her portion. Signior Pandolfo, y'are wise, and vnderstand How ill hot appetites of vnbridled youth Become gray haires. How graue and honourable Wer't for your age to be inamored With the faire shape of vertue, and the glory Of your fore-fathers! Then would you blush to thinke How by this dotage, and vnequall loue, You staine their honour, and your owne. Awake, Banish those wild affections; and by my example Turne t'your reposed selfe. Sir, Conformitie of yeares, likenesse of manners, Are Gordian knots that bind vp matrimony. Now betwixt seuenty Winters, and sixteene, There's no proportion, nor least hope of loue. Fye, that a gentleman of your discretion, Crown'd with such reputation in your youth, Should in your Westerne dayes, loose th'good opinion Of all your friends; and run to th'open danger Of closing the weake remnant of your dayes With discontentment vnrecouerable. These things consider'd, I bestow my daughter Vpon your sonne Eugenio ; whose constant loue With his so modest carriage, hath deseru'd her. And, that you freeze not for a bed-fellow, I marry you with Patience. Signior Pandolfo, When you recouer your selfe, lost desperately In disproportion'd dotage, then you'l thanke me For this great fauour: be not obstinate: Disquiet not your selfe.

Plot summary

Plot — Albumazar
The neighbours Pandolfo and Antonio, both widowers, have arranged to marry each other’s daughters; but before they can do so, Antonio is lost and presumed dead on an overseas voyage, and the plan goes into abeyance because his son Lelio disapproves of Pandolfo as a potential brother-in-law. Albumazar’s accomplice Furbo has gained access to Pandolfo’s household, posing as his daughter’s tutor, and has learned of his .- amorous aspirations, and also his credulity about. magic and astrology. Albumazar plans to trick him, and sets about demonstrating his occult power. Ronca shows Pandolfo some of the astrologer’s amazing inventions: a telescope and an ‘otacousticon’ (actually a pair of ass’s ears) which allow him to see and hear things at a distance. Albumazar then predicts Pandolfo’s marriage, and also a stabbing which his associates then enact in a brawl. Pandolfo asks the astrologer to raise Antonio’s ghost, but instead Albumazar undertakes to transform the tenant farmer Trincalo into Antonio’s likeness for one day, so that he can give Flavia in marriage to Pandolfo. Trincalo agrees because this will give him access to Antonio’s household, and therefore to the maid Armellina, whom he loves. Pandolfo is required to prepare a room for the metamorphosis, laid out with valuable possessions. When he begins to have second thoughts about paying Albumazar, his servant Cricca suggests that he should pretend to have been robbed of his valuables; but when they are indeed stolen, Albumazar pretends in turn that the transformed Trincalo has just locked them away for safe keeping. After undergoing the fake metamorphosis, Trincalo is told he may not look at himself in a mirror for fear of breaking the spell.
