Commend me to the Bells of S. Maries, and tell'em my Chops water to chime all in? Downe with the bonny Bels? How now Hyena; why camst thou hyther? Com'st thou againe to gybe my miseries? Has thy maligneing harted father sent thee To scoffe my sorowes? keepe of I charge thee, Thou did'st bewitch my poore boy with a Kisse, Thy breath is sure infectious, and I feare Their's something in thee smells of sorcery. Stand at distance. Teares? 'tis impossible! Marble will drop, and melt against the raine, And from the cragy Rocks, Fountainous Flouds Oft get inforced issues; but to gaine Relenting teares from thy obdurate harte 'Tis impossible, as to force Fire from snow Water from flint, say the Sun shall not shine, As well upon the begger as the King, That is alike indifferent to all. Pray listen; Would not that Physitian be well hang'd That for his practise sake Kills his patient, And after pleades a sorrow to his freinds? She weepes, an evidence of a harty sorrow, My boy would not have seene her weepe thus long, But hee'd have minister'd comfort I my teares Playes the theife with mine eies too. Whom converses she withall? What should I see? Is this thy sorow, com'st thou to mocke me? Because I hang his Picture ne're my bed, Com'st thou to laugh me! out out, fond-ling noe! See thus I gaze on it; stroke his snowy hands, And prune the curled tresses of his locks, Which the Arts-man neatly has dishevell'd. Certes shee's madd. Hee'rs but one Picture! I could tell thee, I nail'd him to the Earth Riveted a stake quite through his bosome, And bid thee goe seeke him; but I love not To mocke miseries; i'le take this Picture hence, It troubles your sight. 'Tis thy forc'd fancies, and thy guilt together persuades thee so; pray thee be a woman, Whom thou cam'st to comfort, comforts thee; Though I intended to have hurl'd at thee, Stings of dishonour, ignominies, reproaches, And all the stocke of calumnies, and scorne, Which thou art guilty off; now my pity Converts them into sorrow for thy sorrowes; Hartily I doe— Distemper not your selfe at fancies; Your time hastens to maturity, Y'are very big, and may endanger your fruite, If you give way to passions. Come goe with me. I'le give thee comfortable cordialls That shall remove these objects from thine eies, Expelling all disastrous accidents, And plaine thy thoughts as smooth as innocence, Which when thou hear'st, then in rapture boast, Thou dread'st no visions, fury, feind nor Ghost. Oh my poore boy! How happy had I beene if he had liv'd? I come to see how sorrow does become thee Doo'st thou remember that? Monster, behold my poore boyes Picture, Thou would'st not shed a teare, nor lend a sigh, Poore emblem of a penitentiall heart, When in these armes I hug'd my dead boyes corpes, Now monster, who i'st will weepe or sigh, for thine? Murderer I will. See what the fruites of wealth have brought thee now, An everlasting scandall to thy name. A conscience full of horror, and black deedes; Natures externall superfluities Her white, and red Earth, rubbidg, drosse, and oare, VVhich she but lent thee to keepe Marts withall, Thou hast converted to most grosse abuses, Thou wouldst not else have scorn'd my poore boys love, To match with wealthy German; see thy fruits, Thy bazes, and foundations now are suncke, And looke there lyes the ruines of thy workes. Noe, I will make thee sensible of thy ils, First thou art causer of thy daughters death, For thou enforc'd her to the breach of faith; Next my sonns ruin, whom parac'd like, Thou laugd'st at in his fatall tragedy; VVhom but a villaine that abjures all lawes, That breakes all precepts, both of heave'ns, and mans, And natures too could have done this; should I Like one that dares affront divinity Laugh at thy daughters fall. I cannot chuse. But beare a burden in calamities; Our angers have like tapers spent themselves, And onely lighted others, and not us. Striving like great men for supremacy! VVe have confounded one anothers goodnes, Come we will be freinds, i'le dig a soleme cell, VVhich shall be hung with sables round about, VVhere we will sit, and write the tragedy Of our poore children; i'le ha'it so set downe As not one eye that vewes it, but shall weepe Nor any eare but sadly shall relent, For never was a story of more ruth, Then this of him, and her, yet nought but trnth.

Plot — The Vow Breaker, or The Fair Maid of Clifton
Though Young Bateman is betrothed to Anne Boot and loved by her, her father reneges on the ===== PAGE 347 ===== 1628 arrangement. Meanwhile, war looms: England enters an alliance with a federation of Scottish lords hostile to the French Queen Regent of Scotland, and troops are being recruited to march north. Bateman joins up, hoping that his absence will make Old Boot’s heart grow fonder, and Anne vows to love only him; but her father puts pressure on her to marry German, and, seduced by his wealth, she agrees. The French are vanquished, and Bateman distinguishes himself in battle; but he is troubled by dreams about Anne, and returns home on the day of her wedding, just missing the ceremony. German goes away on a long business trip, leaving Anne in her father’s house. Old Bateman sues Boot for breach of the pre-contract; Young Bateman hangs himself outside Anne’s door, and his ghost haunts Anne, invisible to the rest of her family. Anne gives birth to a baby girl. While the women around her sleep, she sees the ghost and runs out of the house; following her footsteps in the snow, the household find her drowned in the river. The two bereaved fathers, Boot and Bateman, commiserate together. In Scotland, Sir Gervase Clifton insults the Queen Regent by drawing an unfavourable comparison with Queen Elizabeth, and captures Martigues in a single combat, but releases him with honour. Joshua the Puritan intends to hang his cat for Sabbath-breaking - she killed a mouse on Sunday - but the cat is saved by the arrival of a French ambassador. The English attack Leith, and Clifton captures a French colonel’s armour. Hostilities are ended by diplomatic means, and Scotland is to be demilitarized. The English and French lords feast together, and Joshua gets drunk. The English troops return south to meet the Queen at Nottingham; the common soldiers plan to put on a play for her, but decide on a morris dance instead. She meets the civic authorities and rewards her military commanders.
