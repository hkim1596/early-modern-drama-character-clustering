My feares are falne vpon me, oh my heart! My sonne the pandar: now I find our house Sinking to ruine. Earth-quakes leaue behind, Where they haue tyrannised, iron, or lead, or stone, But woe to ruine violent lust leaues none Woe to light hearts they still forerun our fall. What make you heare my Lord this dead of night? Neuer dropt meldew on a flower here, tell now. O that this faire garden, Had all poysoned hearbes of Thessaly, At first bene planted, made a nursery For witch-craft; rather a buriall plot, For both your Honours. O thou dost make my brow bend to the earth, Sooner then nature, see the curse of children In life they keepe vs freqeuently in teares, And in the cold graue leaues vs in pale feares. Where is thy Dutchesse now adulterous Duke? Thou little dreamd'st this night shee is come to Rome. The liues of Princes should like dyals moue, Whose regular example is so strong, They make the times by them go right or wrong. Vnfortunate Camillo. I will ioyne with thee, To the most wofull end ere mother kneel'd, If thou dishonour thus thy husbands bed, Bee thy life short as are the funerall teares In great mens. Bee thy act Iudas-like betray in kissing, Maiest thou be enuied during his short breath, And pittied like a wretch after this death. What? because we are poore, Shall we be vitious? O that I ne're had borne thee, Misery of miseries. Is this your pearch, you haggard? flye to'th stewes. I heare a whispering all about the Court, Your are to fight, who is your opposite? What is the quarrell? Will you dissemble? sure you do not well To fright me thus, you neuer look thus pale, But when you are most angry. I do charge you Vpon my blessing; nay I'le call the Duke, And he shall schoole you. Yes. Yes: but 'tis mended. Ha, O my horrour! Helpe, oh he's murdered. O my perpetuall sorrow! Alas he is not dead: hee's in a trance. Why here's no body shall get any thing by his death. Let me call him againe for Gods sake. O you abuse mee, you abuse me, you abuse me. How many haue gone away thus for lacke of tendance; reare vp's head, reare vp's head; His bleeding inward will kill him. Let mee come to him; giue mee him as hee is, if hee bee turn'd to earth; let mee but giue him one heartie kisse, and you shall put vs both into one coffin: fetch a looking glasse, see if his breath will not staine it; or pull out some feathers from my pillow, and lay them to his lippes, will you loose him for a little paines taking? Alas! I would not pray for him yet. Hee may liue to lay mee ith' ground, and pray for mee, if you'l let mee come to him. Hee lies, hee lies, hee did not kill him: these haue kill'd him, that would not let him bee better look't to. O you scritch-owle. Let mee goe, let mee goe. The God of heauen forgiue thee. Do'st not wonder I pray for thee? Ile tell thee what's the reason, I haue scarce breath to number twentie minutes; Ide not spend that in cursing. Fare thee well Halfe of thy selfe lies there: and maist thou liue To fill an howre-glasse with his mouldred ashes, To tell how thou shouldst spend the time to come In blest repentance. Indeed my yonger boy presum'd too much Vpon his manhood; gaue him bitter wordes; Drew his sword first; and so I know not how, For I was out of my wits, hee fell with's head Iust in my bosome. I pray thee peace. One arrow's graz'd allready; it were vaine T'lose this: for that will nere bee found againe. This rosemarie is wither'd, pray get fresh; I would haue these herbes grow vp in his graue When I am dead and rotten. Reach the bayes, Ile tye a garland heere about his head: 'Twill keepe my boy from lightning. This sheet I haue kept this twentie yere, and euerie daie Hallow'd it with my praiers, I did not thinke Hee should haue wore it. O reach mee the flowers. You're very wellcome. There's Rosemarie for you, and Rue for you, Hearts-ease for you. I pray make much of it. I haue left more for my selfe. You are, I take it, the graue-maker. Will you make mee such a foole? heere's a white hand: Can bloud so soone bee washt out? Let mee see, When scritch-howles croke vpon the chimney tops, And the strange Cricketith ouen singes and hoppes, When yellow spots doe on your handes appeare, Bee certaine then you of a Course shall heare. Out vpon't, how 'tis speckled! h'as handled a toad sure. Couslep-water is good for the memorie: pray buy mee 3. oun ces of't. Do you heere, Ile giue you a saying which my grandmother (sir? Was wont, when she heard the bell tolle, to sing ore vnto her lute Call for the Robin-Red-brest and the wren, Since ore shadie groues they houer, And with leaues and flowres doe couer The friendlesse bodies of vnburied men. Call vnto his funerall Dole The Ante, the field-mouse, and the mole To reare him hillockes, that shall keepe him warme, And (when gay tombes are rob'd) sustaine no harme, But keepe the wolfe far thence: that's foe to men, For with his nailes hee'ldig them vp agen. They would not bury him' cause hee died in a quarrell But I haue an answere for them. Let holie Church receiue him duly Since hee payd the Church tithes truly. Plot — The White Devil (Vittoria Corombona)

In this, Bracciano is abetted by Vittoria’s brother Flaminio, who persuades her husband Camillo that their sleeping apart will make her more amorous. Vittoria tells Bracciano of a dream which seems to suggest the murder of Camillo and the Duchess Isabella; but the liaison is forestalled by the intervention of Vittoria’s mother, Cornelia. Flaminio inveighs against Cornelia for compromising his chances of preferment. Isabella comes to Rome to effect a reconciliation with her husband. Cardinal Monticelso and the Great Duke of Florence also put pressure on him to fulfil his responsibilities, both marital and political. In a private interview with Isabella, Bracciano vows never again to sleep with her, but she tries to limit the political damage by making the same vow herself in public. Florence and Monticelso plan to tempt Bracciano into adultery by getting Camillo out of Rome on public business, but Bracciano plots murder: Isabella is killed when she kisses a poisoned picture of Bracciano, and Flaminio breaks Camillo’s neck. Vittoria is arrested and arraigned before an ecclesiastical court; unable to prove her complicity in Camillo’s murder, it convicts her of adultery and commits her to a house of convertites. Flaminio feigns madness to evade interrogation. Lodovico witnesses Isabella’s murder. He returns to Rome illegally to bring Florence the news, but his pardon is signed after the event. He and Flaminio meet and quarrel. Florence borrows from Monticelso a list of villains, in order to select an agent of vengeance. By writing a letter feigning love for Vittoria, he induces Bracciano to contrive her escape from the house of convertites, disguised as a page. Monticelso is elected Pope; his first act is to excommunicate Bracciano and Vittoria. Florence sends Lodovico to Padua to assassinate Bracciano, and by trickery makes it appear that Monticelso supports the conspiracy. Bracciano marries Vittoria and accepts into his court Mulinassar, a Moorish soldier, who is actually Florence in disguise.
