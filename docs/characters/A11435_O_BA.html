Boote, boote, boote, thou art malapert, false, proud, A wretched miscreant, and dissembler, H' shall enjoy her, shees his lawfull wife Thy hand enstated hers, though falsely now Thou plaist the counterfet. Tell me Boote. Does not his birth, and breeding equall hers, Are not my revenues correspondent To equall thine; his purity of bloud Runs in as sweete a streame, and naturall heate As thine, or hers; his exteriour parts May parralell hers, or any others In a true harmony of lawfull love. Wast not thine owne motion, didst not give way, And entercourse to their privacies? Didst thou not make me draw conveighances Did not th'assurance of thy Lands seeme proball, Boote, Boote thou shall not carry it thus I'le make thee know theirs justice to be had If thou denyst it. hang thee hang thee miser! Tis thy base thoughts forges these false conceits, And but for thy daughter, I'de, i'de, i'de. Art thou so built on her fidelity Take heede boy; women by kinde are fickle, Absence in lovers brings strange events Lovers that hourely kisse finde due regard But those that absent are oft lose reward. I doubt not of her firmenes, but tis common An absent lover thrives not with a woman. Tis good counsell boy, and worth observance But thou darst trust her. Goe on then in thy entended purpose Noble sir Jarvis whose man thou art, I know will furnish thee. Well Boote time may make us friends. Hees at your service. I've miss'd my boy out of his bed to night Heavens grant that he be well, for in his eyes Sad discontentment sits! till yesterday I never saw him so propense to sorrow. Nor deepely touch'd with distemperature, When I began to tell him of his mistris Which I in violence of wordes branded With damned perjury; as Heaven knowes She has consum'd her goodnes; then would he Sit by, and sigh, and with salt teares trilling Downe his cheekes, entreat me not to name her, Curse her I must not I then would he steale to bed, As full of mournfull sorrowes as a sinner. Tis almost morne and I suspect him here Hovering about this house! oft would he say He woo'd her underneath a Plume-Tree, And underneath that Tree he vow'd to sit, And tell his sorrowes to the gummy boughes Though she disdaind to here them? protect me! Good Angells guard me, what heavy sight is this That like a sullen sadnes reaves my sense, Prove false mine eies that this may prove untrue? Better you never had seene then to see this. Leave your slimy cesternes, and drop out; Tis he, tis he, would I could tell a ly The falsest one that e're was tould by man That this might prove untrue; but tis in vaine To darke the Sunne, or wrastle 'gainst the truth Murtherers looke out, i'le rowze the thunderer, To rowze you from your sleepes! false feinds come out, And see a deede, the day wilbe asham'd of Caus'd by your perjuries. Looke this way monster see thou adultresse Behold the miserablest Map of woe That ever father mourn'd for; my poore boy Hard-harted fate that brought thee to this end, Hated Vipers they that were the causers, Curs'd be thy ground, and curs'd be all trees That brings forth such a bortive fruit as this. Laughst thou Crocadile? Are miseries lamented with contempts? The bookes of fate are not so closely shut, But they may open, and record the scornes Dwelling in every Region of thy face? A fixt decree may be set downe for thine, And thou maist Swan-like sing a Funerall O'de, Who then shall laugh at thee? Such dire becomings maist thou never want, Thou that wert once the Jewell of these eies, Looke here, and see the ruines of pale death. How soone a Gorgeous Pallace is suncke downe; Though he has surfetted upon this peece He has not tane the colour of his cheeke, Nature contests with death, and will out-doe him; Canst not thou spare one teare to balme him in, Nor lend a sigh as sorry for his fall? If not to day i'le come againe to morrow, So thou wilt shed two teares, and one poore sigh, Then gentle Charon will assigne him wafftage; Thy greifes are violent, and worke within Tis a fowle signe of an unpersant hart When as the eyes cannot impart a teare. Since none of you will weepe, i'le weepe alone Till Niobe like my teares convert to stone. Worse then the worst that ever could be nam'd. Pigmalion doated on the peece he made, So doe not I upon thy pourtraiture. I doe but hang thy faire resemblance here To tell me of my immortality. How sensible young Cedars are o'th winde, When as the aged Oake affronts all stormes 'Tis death, and natures fault, for the Diamond, Of blooming youth, despise decaying age. He might have tane thee el'ce, and left thee boy.

Plot — The Vow Breaker, or The Fair Maid of Clifton
Though Young Bateman is betrothed to Anne Boot and loved by her, her father reneges on the ===== PAGE 347 ===== 1628 arrangement. Meanwhile, war looms: England enters an alliance with a federation of Scottish lords hostile to the French Queen Regent of Scotland, and troops are being recruited to march north. Bateman joins up, hoping that his absence will make Old Boot’s heart grow fonder, and Anne vows to love only him; but her father puts pressure on her to marry German, and, seduced by his wealth, she agrees. The French are vanquished, and Bateman distinguishes himself in battle; but he is troubled by dreams about Anne, and returns home on the day of her wedding, just missing the ceremony. German goes away on a long business trip, leaving Anne in her father’s house. Old Bateman sues Boot for breach of the pre-contract; Young Bateman hangs himself outside Anne’s door, and his ghost haunts Anne, invisible to the rest of her family. Anne gives birth to a baby girl. While the women around her sleep, she sees the ghost and runs out of the house; following her footsteps in the snow, the household find her drowned in the river. The two bereaved fathers, Boot and Bateman, commiserate together. In Scotland, Sir Gervase Clifton insults the Queen Regent by drawing an unfavourable comparison with Queen Elizabeth, and captures Martigues in a single combat, but releases him with honour. Joshua the Puritan intends to hang his cat for Sabbath-breaking - she killed a mouse on Sunday - but the cat is saved by the arrival of a French ambassador. The English attack Leith, and Clifton captures a French colonel’s armour. Hostilities are ended by diplomatic means, and Scotland is to be demilitarized. The English and French lords feast together, and Joshua gets drunk. The English troops return south to meet the Queen at Nottingham; the common soldiers plan to put on a play for her, but decide on a morris dance instead. She meets the civic authorities and rewards her military commanders.
