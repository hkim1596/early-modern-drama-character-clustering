Master Nightingale, come hither, leaue your mart a little. All the purses, and purchase, I giue you to day by con ueyance, bring hither to Vrsla's presently. This they whisper, that Ouerdoo heares it not. Heere we will meet at night in her odge, and share. Looke you choose good places, for your standing i'the Fayre, when you sing, Nightingale. And i'your singing, you must vse your hawks eye nimbly, and flye the purse to a marke, still, where 'tis worne, and o'which side; that you may gi'me the signe with your beake, or hang your head that way i'the tune. And shall we ha' smockes Vrsla, and good whimsies, ha? Halfe I haue, Master Dan. Knockhum, is alwaies at your seruice, Not a whit, these fellowes were too fine to carry mo ney. Nightingale, stay a little. Indeede I'le heare some o' this! S'light, hee will call company, you shall see, and put vs into doings presently. Yes, Sir. Nor you feele. In, to Vrsla, Nightingale, and carry her comfort: see it told. This fellow was sent to vs by fortune, for our first fairing. Good Gentlemen, doe not vndoe me; I am a ciuill young man, and but a beginner, indeed. The little old Gouernour, Sir? Would you ha' the boxe and all, Sir? or onely that, that is in't? I, and 'twill be the longer a missing, to draw on the sport. Sir, if euer I breake my word, with a Gentleman, may I neuer read word at my need. Where shall I find you? Warrant, for what, Sir? What meanes hee? Beshrew him, he startled me: I thought he had knowne of our plot. Guilt's a terrible thing! ha' you prepar'd the Costard monger? I, I know; I should ha' follow'd his Protector-ship for a feat I am to doe vpon him: But this offer'd it selfe, so i'the way, I could not let it scape: heere he comes, whistle, Nightin gale whistles be this sport call'd Dorring the Dottrell. A delicate great boy! me thinks, he out-scrambles 'hem all. I cannot perswade my selfe, but he goes to grammer-schole yet; and playes the trewant, to day. Purse? a man might cut out his kidneys, I thinke; and he neuer feele 'hem, he is so earnest at the sport. Away, Nightingale: that way. Away, Costermonger, come to vs to Vrsla's. 'heart, if hee haue any more then a thing giuen him in stead of salt, onely to keepe him from stinking, I'le be hang'd afore my time, presently, where should it be trow? in his blood; hee has not so much to'ard it in his whole body, as will maintaine a good Flea; And if hee take this course, he will not ha' so much land left, as to reare a Calfe within this twelue month. Plot summary

Plot — Bartholomew Fair
Bartholomew Cokes has two objectives in life: to visit the fair which shares his name, and to marry Grace Wellborn, who, as a ward, faces a financial penalty should she refuse. He sends his servant Wasp to Littlewit the proctor to obtain a marriage licence, but then, impatiently, comes himself. He brings Grace with him, and she attracts the interest of Winwife, present with his friend Quarlous as a suitor to Littlewit’s mother-in-law, Dame Purecraft. Littlewit is more interested in Cokes’s talk of the fair, having secretly written one of the puppet plays to be performed there. The only snag is that the puritanical Dame Purecraft will disapprove of their visit, thinking fairs profane. Busy, an elder of Purecraft’s church, advises the family that fairs may lawfully be visited for good reason, and comes along with them to partake of the pork. After some unfortunate experiences involving mistaken identity, Justice Overdo no longer trusts informers and has decided to do his spying personally. He visits the fair in disguise to seek out ‘enormities’ perpetrated by the fairground folk. He is impressed by one young man, Edgeworth, and decides to rescue him from the clutches of the cheaters; but he does not know that Edgeworth is a cutpurse. While Overdo is busy telling him about the wickedness of tobacco, Edgeworth steals Cokes’s purse; Overdo gets the blame. When it happens a second time, Overdo is arrested and put in the stocks. There he encounters the madman Trouble-All, who persistently demands Overdo’s warrant for any action; it transpires that he was a court official driven out of his mind by Overdo’s own actions. The watchmen, concerned to get better authority, release Overdo from the stocks for the time being. Meanwhile, the Littlewits arrive at the fair, but Busy becomes over-excited in his condemnation, attacks the fairground booths, and is arrested; Dame Purecraft goes after him, leaving Littlewit and Win free to enjoy themselves. Edgeworth tricks Wasp into making affidavit so that, at Quarlous’s instigation, he can steal Cokes’s marriage licence. Wasp too is arrested and put in the stocks, but escapes by a ruse when Overdo and Busy are put in beside him. The women visitors to the fair are separated from the men. Littlewit leaves Win in the care of two fairground bawds while he goes off to check the arrangements for his puppet show.
