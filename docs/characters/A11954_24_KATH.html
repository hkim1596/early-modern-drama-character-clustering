Full speech

O Griffith, ſicke to death: My Legges like loaden Branches bow to' th' Earth, Willing to leaue their burthen: Reach a Chaire, So now (me thinkes) I feele a little eaſe. Did'ſt thou not tell me Griffith, as thou lead'ſt mee, That the great Childe of Honor, Cardinall Wolſey Was dead? Pre'thee good Griffith, tell me how he dy'de ▪ If well, he ſtept before me happily For my example. Alas poore man ▪ So may he reſt, His Faults lye gently on him: Yet thus farre Griffith, giue me leaue to ſpeake him, And yet with Charity. He was a man Of an vnbounded ſtomacke, euer ranking Himſelfe with Princes. One that by ſuggeſtion Ty'de all the Kingdome. Symonie, was faire play, His owne Opinion was his Law. I' th' preſence He would ſay vntruths, and be euer double Both in his words, and meaning. He was neuer (But where he meant to Ruine) pittifull. His Promiſes, were as he then was, Mighty: But his performance, as he is now, Nothing: Of his owne body he was ill, and gaue The Clergy ill example. Yes good Griffith, I were malicious elſe. After my death, I wiſh no other Herald, No other ſpeaker of my liuing Actions, To keepe mine Honor, from Corruption, But ſuch an honeſt Chronicler as Griffith. Whom I moſt hated Liuing, thou haſt made mee With thy Religious Truth, and Modeſtie, (Now in his Aſhes) Honor: Peace be with him. Patience, be neere me ſtill, and ſet me lower. I haue not long to trouble thee. Good Griffith, Cauſe the Muſitians play me that ſad note I nam'd my Knell; whil'ſt I ſit meditating On that Coeleſtiall Harmony I go too. Spirits of peace, where are ye? Are ye all gone? And leaue me heere in wretchedneſſe, behinde ye? It is not you I call for, Saw ye none enter ſince I ſlept? No? Saw you not euen now a bleſſed Troope Inuite me to a Banquet, whoſe bright faces Caſt thouſand beames vpon me, like the Sun? They promis'd me eternall Happineſſe, And brought me Garlands (Griffith) which I feele I am not worthy yet to weare: I ſhall aſſuredly. Bid the Muſicke leaue, They are harſh and heauy to me. You are a ſawcy Fellow, Deſerue we no more Reuerence? Admit him entrance Griffith. But this Fellow Let me ne're ſee againe. If my ſight faile not, You ſhould be Lord Ambaſſador from the Emperor, My Royall Nephew, and your name Capuchius. O my Lord, The Times and Titles now are alter'd ſtrangely With me, ſince firſt you knew me. But I pray you, What is your pleaſure with me? O my good Lord, that comfort comes too late, 'Tis like a Pardon after Execution; That gentle Phyſicke giuen in time, had cur'd me: But now I am paſt all Comforts heere, but Prayers. How does his Highneſſe? So may he euer do, and euer flouriſh, When I ſhall dwell with Wormes, and my poore name Baniſh'd the Kingdome. Patience, is that Letter I caus'd you write, yet ſent away? Sir, I moſt humbly pray you to deliuer This to my Lord the King. In which I haue commended to his goodneſſe The Modell of our chaſte loues: his yong daughter, The dewes of Heauen fall thicke in Bleſſings on her, Beſeeching him to giue her vertuous breeding. She is yong, and of a Noble modeſt Nature, I hope ſhe will deſerue well; and a little To loue her for her Mothers ſake, that lou'd him, Heauen knowes how deerely. My next poore Petition, Is, that his Noble Grace would haue ſome pittie Vpon my wretched women, that ſo long Haue follow'd both my Fortunes, faithfully, Of which there is not one, I dare auow (And now I ſhould not lye) but will deſerue For Vertue, and true Beautie of the Soule, For honeſtie, and decent Carriage A right good Husband (let him be a Noble) And ſure thoſe men are happy that ſhall haue 'em. The laſt is for my men, they are the pooreſt, (But pouerty could neuer draw 'em from me) That they may haue their wages, duly paid 'em, And ſomething ouer to remember me by. If Heauen had pleas'd to haue giuen me longer life And able meanes, we had not parted thus. Theſe are the whole Contents, and good my Lord, By that you loue the deereſt in this world, As you wiſh Chriſtian peace to ſoules departed, Stand theſe poore peoples Friend, and vrge the King To do me this laſt right. I thanke you honeſt Lord. Remember me In all humilitie vnto his Highneſſe: Say his long trouble now is paſſing Out of this world. Tell him in death I bleſt him (For ſo I will) mine eyes grow dimme. Farewell My Lord. Griffith farewell. Nay Patience, Vou muſt not leaue me yet. I muſt to bed, Call in more women. When I am dead, good Wench, Let me be vs'd with Honor; ſtrew me ouer With Maiden Flowers, that all the world may know I was a chaſte Wife, to my Graue: Embalme me, Then lay me forth (although vnqueen'd) yet like A Queene, and Daughter to a King enterre me. I can no more.

Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
