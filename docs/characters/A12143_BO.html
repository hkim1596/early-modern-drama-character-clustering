Is not that your daughter? Has she found another father? Let them tell the markes that lost it and take it agen By my consent Sebastian's a forehorse-but would I were to be the Dukes taster, Howes that? the Duke does love her, I hope not Honestly, she was not borne to he a Dutchesse, I have it Heaven forgive us, the Duke meanes to make her owne father the Pander, tis so, he does not use to give such offices for nothing, well go thy wayes for a princely Ferret, she cannot hold out upon these termes Tw'ere better you were both pickeld, I want patience to see any man kisse A hansome Gentlewoman, and when my owne lips Cannot use their owne priviledge, I prophesie what Will Become on her, for all her modesty, but dare stay No longer the sight of so much temptation. So please your highnesse hee's not ith Court Vnlesse he be in some o'the lobbyes, I could Not examine all: ha? tis come about, and the Father Ile lay my life is laying the law to her, Why was not I a Duke, I have as many titil lations, though I be the elder huntsman. Hayda a Privy Counselor too. We are like to have fine smock-age ont, virgins will be virgins If the Duke hold this humour and at such price forestall the market; A widow will be excellent meat againe; hee's Melancholy, Would you were as right for your owne sake: If you have a mind to quarrell drinke. Ile beare you company My Lord— Ile lead you sir. Ever while you live drinke wine at the fountaine Let it be rich and sparckling, my precious varlet, and how And how go things at Court? I would Hippolito were here, hee's a good fellow, and takes After his father, the Duke makes much on him Let it come, I am glad to see you sociable, come to the City and leave purchasing, du ty acres And it were as deepe as an vsurers conscience, My boy should ha't Shall have any? Be gon then. Yet agen, will you have your occupation broke about Your head? Ile make you cry and tune your voyce to the lamentation Of oh my fiddle, if you remove not presently. Come whom shal we now remember? heres to your Mistresse Till threescore y'are allowd, I never wore more favours at one and twenty, this Riband came from a Countesse, this locke I weare for a young Ladies sake, this touch was the fall of a Gentlewomans fanne that is new come to Court And she be, there is hope the Courtiers may convert her, here's To her first Enter Iugler. How many is there of you? Prethee Hocus Pocus, keepe thy grannams huckle bone. And leave us. Come our blood cooles; here's to your faire daughter I do not flatter you, but you may be proud, I say no more Your daughter, shee's a hansome Gentlewoman, the're be Worse faces at Court; Honesty where is it? Take heed what you say, least you be made to justifie it Honesty every where? Heres to you, come. If all were of your mind who should thrive in this world? Pledge me How shall Christians behave themselves in great offices? Or under-clarkes purchase honesty? but one terme were enough to undoe the City, the Court were but in ill case if great men should stand upon't, for the Countrey, tis bought and sold every market day. Come begin to me To the Duke. How? He loves your daughter— Let it come, me thinkes he is a very fine Gentleman, And one that loves a wench as well— As ill as I, let it be so, I were no good subiect to deny it To his Highnesse. Are you not so still? Tis welcome, shall we double our files? Charge me home then. Right, if a man dares not be honest he is a Coward But he that dares be dishonest, A pretty fellow heres to you agen; shalls have a wench? now am I addicted to embrace any thing in the likenesse of a woman, oh for a Chamber-maide to wrestle withall; send for a brace of Basaliskes, thou hast no spirit no Masculine vertue, now could I o're runne the whole Countrey of the Amazons. Heres to a Penthesilia beare up my valiant Mirmidon and we will do such feates shall make the Troians wonder at our backes and bring Dame Hellen to us. How not generous take heed what you say Then drinke your drinke, Now Troy burnesblew, wheras Hecuba? Vpon a condition I will drinke to thee No, no, thou wot not doe so much, and a man should die for a lift a'the leg: the Duke has a great minde to thy daughter, he is but mortall flesh and bloud, there be subjects that have as sound bodies no dispraise to his Excelency. Feare? would any durst send to me such A virgin Pinnace, rigd and gay with all flags. Nay nay, thou art so waspish, if a friend desired a curtesie, that is in fashion; because the Duke— Boy Ile pay the reckoning; Be honest and see what will Come on't Ile seeke out my sonne Hippolito. Hee'e be ruld by me, here's a coile about a tassell Gentle If please your grace let it be my imployment Your highnesse humble servant ▪ I am sorry my sonne should be absent, when your grace has service for him, but Ile find him out, I am acquainted with two or three of his haunts I know a Taverne is next doore to a— It has a course name To a baudyhouse To find him there, I cannot helpe it? Tis a tricke he learnd in France sir, where your nobility practise, he will leave it, when Capring and Kissing are out of fashion with Gentlemen And wilder too I should be sorry else I had ne're broke my wives heart else, with supping abroad and midnight revells ▪ I should ha beene troubled with her till this time Not much for the crosse point, But with your highnesse licence, Ile find out Hippolito To attend your pleasure. Save you Signior is my sonne here? You do not answer as you were wont, I aske for Hippolito, your friend, How is it with your beauteous Clariana? Pray commend my service to her Nothing but my service to your Lady Looke? I guest so at first sight, may I presume to aske it? That all? Yes a friend was made a Cuckold by a friend He did his wife and him a curtesie. I am not of your mind, and I had don't my selfe, I should Never had so much despaire as to hang my selfe, why tis as Common as shifting a trencher What dee see in my forehead you should thinke me so miserable? I can tell you, what they should be, They should be honest and love their husbands, and for their Sakes their bastards, which if they understand they are bound, to keepe, because their ill conditions drive us a broad to get 'em. Whither? a musical lip. I should be pleasd with such another,—a light wench And a yare, Ile attend you Lady. Exit. How came this tragedy? Howes this? She is too neere this griefe, this punishment Should ha beene mine long since, I was his father In sinne as well as yeeres, she is dead already Thy glasse had many sands till it was broken Then those few minutes that are left of mine, Ile number with my prayers.
