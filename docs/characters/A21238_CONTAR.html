Plot — The Queen's Entertainment at Woodstock
[The start of the entertainment is lost. The knights Loricus and Contarenus fight on horseback, watched by Lady Gaudina. They are interrupted by Hemetes the hermit.] He conducts them to the Queen’s bower, where he recounts the various characters’ histories. Less than seven years earlier, in the land of Cambia, Gaudina’s father objected to her love for Contarenus because of his low rank, and paid an enchantress to ===== PAGE 140 ===== send the knight to another country by magic. Learning of his actions, Gaudina left Cambia and met Loricus, who also left his own country after a disappointment in love; since then they have travelled together. Hemetes himself, meanwhile, was a knight blinded at the temple of Venus for the division of allegiance between love and learning, but compensated by Apollo with the gift of prophecy in matters of love. Three separate predictions by the unnamed enchantress, Sibylla, and Apollo mean that all four characters have anticipated achieving satisfaction (and, in Hemetes’ case, the restoration of his sight) in circumstances which have just come to pass: in an ideal state, England, two worthy knights have fought, two lovers met, and the events have been watched by the worthiest lady in the world, Queen Elizabeth. Hemetes asks the Queen to entertain Gaudina and Contarenus at court pending their return home to Cambia. The Queen accompanies the hermit back to his house, where she is served a banquet. The fairy queen pays a visit, and the Queen and her maids of honour receive gifts. Afterwards the Queen returns home in her coach and hears a song from inside an oak. Wanting to make her peace with her father, Gaudina consults the fairy queen for advice on the readiest way to Cambia. Occanon, however, is no longer there: having wandered the world in search of his lost daughter, he is now in England. Their reunion is marked by a resumption of their disagreement about her love-life: he urges her to give up Contarenus for the good of her country, but she holds the line that her lover’s virtue makes up for any deficiency in his rank. Occanon has more success persuading Contarenus to withdraw, but the knight refuses his compensatory offer of a noble life in Cambia: he prefers not to suffer the sight of his love with another man, but would rather join with Loricus in lamenting the crosses of fortune.
