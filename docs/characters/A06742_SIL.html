Wel met fair Nymph, or Goddeſſe if ye bee: Tis ſtraunge me thinkes, that one of your degree Should walke theſe ſolitary groues alone. My habit telles you that, a Forreſter: That hauing loſt a heard of skittiſh Deere, Was of good hope, I ſhould a found them heere. Nay ſtay: and further of your fortunes tell: I am not one that meanes you any harme. Shepheard not I, what meanſt to ſeeke him here? It is indeede, and as it ſeemes, diſtreſt, Whoſe griefe to know, I humbly made requeſt: But ſhe as yet will not reueale the ſame. Nay feare not to diſcouer what you are: It may be we may remedie your care. Now truſt me virgin, they were much vnkind, To ſeeke to match you ſo againſt your minde. Why go with me, my Lodge is not far off, Where you ſhall haue ſuch hoſpitalitie As ſhall be for your health and ſafetie. Ile bring her to a bower beſet with greene. Her dyet ſhalbe Venſon at my boord. And nothing elſe? Theſe walkes are mine, amongſt the ſhadie trees. The Nightingale is my continuall clocke. A hunts vp, I can tune her with my hounds. Within theſe woods are many pleaſant ſprings. I maruell that a ruſticke ſhepheard dare With woodmen then audaciouſly compare? Why, hunting is a pleaſure for a King, And Gods themſelues ſometime frequent the thing. Diana with her bowe and arrowes keene, Did often vſe the Chace, in Forreſts greene. And ſo alas, the good Athenian knight, And ſwift Acteon herein tooke delight: And Atalanta the Arcadian dame, Conceiu'd ſuch wondrous pleaſure in the game: That with her traine of Nymphs attending on, She came to hunt the Bore of Calydon. But we take choice of many a pleaſant walke And marke the Deare how they begin to ſtalke. When each according to his age and time, Pricks vp his head, and beares a Princely minde: The luſtie Stag conductor of the traine, Leads all the heard in order downe the plaine: The baſer raſcalls ſcatter here and there, As not preſuming to approach ſo neere. Shall I be thus out-faced by a ſwaine? Ile haue a guard to wayt vpon her traine, Of gallant woodmen, clad in comely greene: The like whereof, hath ſildome yet bene ſeene. Thinks Gemulo to beare the bell away? By ſinging of a ſimple Rundelay? No, I haue fellowes, whoſe melodious throates Shall euen as far exceed thoſe homely notes As doth the Nightingale in muſicke paſſe, The moſt melodious bird that euer was. And for an inſtance, here they are at hand, When they haue done, let our deſerts be ſcand. I am agreed, and for the loue I beare Ile boaſt, I haue a Tenant is ſo faire. The ̄ for a ſign that no man here hath wrong From hence lets all conduct her with a ſong. Is it a bargaine Gemulo, or not? Nor I breake mine, and here her cottage is: Ile call her forth. Neuer ſhall we betwixt our ſelues conclude Our controuerſie, for we ouer weene. So 'twas I thought, becauſe ſhe tends thy ſheepe Thou thinkſt in loue of thee ſhe taketh keepe: That is as towniſh damzels ſend the hand, But ſend the heart to him a looſe doth ſtand. So deales Eurymine with Siluto. That ſhall we quickly trye. Eurymine. How ſhuld I cal her Swain, but by her name? Agreed: but whether ſhall begin his note. Content, the longeſt ſhall begin. Sing loude, for ſhe is farre within. Repeat our bargain, ere we ſing our Song, Leaſt after wrangling, ſhould our miſtreſſe wrong. If me ſhe chuſe, thou muſt be well content: If thee ſhe chuſe, I giue the like conſent. As from the Oaken leaues the honie glides, Where Nightingales record vpon the thorne. So all my ſences cheere. Plot summary

Plot — The Maid's Metamorphosis
Considering Eurymine too low-born to be a suitable match for his son Ascanio, Duke Telemachus orders two of his gentlemen to escort her from the court and murder her. In the event, however, Phylander persuades his colleague that they have no obligation to follow unlawful orders. They plan to present the Duke with an animal’s heart, claiming it to be hers, and Eurymine agrees to live away from the court in country exile. She meets a forester and a shepherd, each of whom wants her to live with him; when they become competitive, she agrees to accept a cottage from the forester and a flock of sheep from the shepherd. Ascanio goes into the woods looking for Eurymine. Juno finds him asleep and, eager to prove her superiority to Venus, arranges for him to dream of where to find his love. Meanwhile, Eurymine meets Apollo, who is also attracted to her. To save herself from rape, she claims to disbelieve his claims of godhead, and asks him to prove himself by working a miracle: specifically, she asks him to change her sex. He does, but with an associated curse: as a man, she will fall hopelessly in love with another man, and long to be a woman again. Ascanio learns from an echo that Eurymine is nearby but inaccessible, and the hermit Aramanthus, who is also a dispossessed prince, explains her metamorphosis. This disappoints not only Ascanio but also her country admirers, and Aramanthus recommends that she should ask the Graces to intercede with Apollo on her behalf. Apollo eventually agrees and restores her original gender. Telemachus has a change of heart and sends Phylander to bring Eurymine back from the forest. Apollo reveals that she is in fact Aramanthus’ long-lost daughter, and so of royal blood: her status is after all no obstacle to her marriage with Ascanio.
