I charge you by your dueties to the State, And loue to gentrie, sheath your weapons. Gentlemen— Now in the name of manhood I coniure yee, Appeare in your true shapes; Italians, You kill your honours more in this reuenge, Than in his murder: Stay, stand, heer's the house. Sheath all your weapons worthy Gentlemen, And by my life I sweare, if Fontinell Haue stain'd the honour of your Sisters bed, The fact being death, Ile pay you his proude head, This honest Officer— Shall fetch him foorth: goe sirra, in our name Attach the French Lord. O what a scandall were it to a State, To haue a stranger, (and a prisoner) Murdred by such a troope? Besides, through Uenice Are numbers of his Country-men dispearst; Whose rage (meeting with yours) none can preuent The mischiefe of a bloudie consequent. Be satisfi'd my noble Countrymen, Ile trust you with his life, so you will pawne The faithes of Gentlemen, no desperate hand Shall rob him of it; otherwise, he runnes Vpon this daungerous point, that dares appose His rage gainst our authoritie: French Lord, Yeeld vp this strength, our word shal be your Guard. My honest fellow, with a good defence, Enter againe, fetch out the Curtizan, And all that are within. The beautie you adore so, is prophane, The breach of wedlocke (by our law) is death. With all seueritie. Make way, she comes. Now fetch Curuetto, and the Spaniard heather, Their punishments shall lye vnder one doome, What is she maskt? This combat shall not neede; for see, asham'd, Of their rash vowes, these Gentlemen heere breake, This storme; and doe with hands, what tongues should speake, Oh heer's our other spirits that walke i'th night, Signior Curuetto, by complaint from her, And by your writing heere, I reach the depth Of your offence; they charge your climbing vp To be to rob her: if so, then by law You are to dye vnlesse she marrie you. Well Signior, for we rightly vnderstand, From your accusers, how you stood her guest, We pardon you and passe it as a iest: And for the Spaniard sped so hardlie too, Discharge him Blurt, Signior we pardon you. Sirra, his debt, our selfe will satisfie. And since this heate of furie is all spent, And Tragicke shapes meete Comicall euent: Let this bright morning, merrily be crown'd With daunces, banquets, and choyce Musickes sound.

Plot — Blurt, Master Constable, or The Spaniard's Night Walk
Camillo and Hippolito return home to Venice after a war with France. Camillo brings the French lord Fontinelle as a prisoner, and presents him to his love Violetta. She falls for the Frenchman, so Camillo sends him to prison. Hippolito takes charge of the situation. He makes the courtesan Imperia dote on Fontinelle by sending her his picture; but Fontinelle refuses to accept her love and is taken back to his dungeon. Meanwhile Camillo’s attempts to woo Violetta back are met with rejection. Fontinelle escapes by changing clothes with Frisco, and, using Violetta’s page as a go-between, makes an assignation at a chapel, where they are secretly married. Afterwards, though, he takes up with Imperia. Camillo swears revenge on all three and leads a party of armed men to kill them and hang out their dead bodies. Violetta also goes to find her husband at Imperia’s, and is invited to join him in bed. Camillo’s men, the watch, and the Duke all arrive simultaneously at the brothel. The Duke prevents murder being done, but has Fontinelle arrested for adultery, which is a capital crime in Venice. Violetta denies that infidelity has occurred: it was all a plot laid by her and Fontinelle. In the sub-plot, Lazarillo also arrives in Venice from the wars and gets lodgings with Blurt the constable. He goes to Imperia’s house to lecture the ladies on Spanish marital conduct, and makes a nocturnal assignation with Imperia. Simperina makes another assignation with old Curvetto, but when he tries to attract her attention he is doused with water. Lazarillo is equally unlucky in his date: he falls through a trapdoor. Curvetto returns and is climbing up a rope ladder let down by Frisco when his foot slips; Frisco accuses him of trying to burgle the house, and he is arrested by Blurt. Lazarillo climbs out and he too is doused, with urine, and then arrested by Blurt. Curvetto refuses to 1601 marry Imperia to escape the death penalty, but the Duke pardons him and Lazarillo anyway, and agrees to pay the money the latter owes Blurt.
