Sir, Ile tell you, The greatest part of it I meane to imploy In payment of my Debts, and the remainder Is like to bring me into greater bonds, as I ayme it. I intend it for the charge of my Wedding. Yes Sir; and I must now intreat your pardon, That I haue concealed from you a businesse, Wherein you had at first been call'd to Counsell, But that I thought it a lesse fault in Friendship, To ingage my selfe thus farre without your knowledge, Then to doe it against your will: another reason Was, that I would not publish to the world, Nor haue it whispered scarce, what wealthy Voyage I went about, till I had got the Myne In mine owne possession. Tis my hope sir. I doe obserue how this Romelio, Has very worthy parts, were they not blasted By insolent vaine glory: there rests now The mothers approbation to the match, Who is a woman of that State and bearing, Tho shee be Citie-borne, both in her language, Her Garments, and her Table, shee excels Our Ladies of the Court: shee goes not gawdy, Yet haue I seene her weare one Diamond, Would haue bought twenty gay ones out of their clothes, And some of them, without the greater grace, Out of their honesties. Shee comes, I will trie How she stands affected to me, without relating My Contract with her Daughter. It could neuer haue got a sweeter ayre to fly in, Then your breath. They haue a kind, of Exchange among them too, Marry vnlesse, it be to heare of newes, I take it, Theirs, is like the New Burse, thinly furnisht With Tyers and new Fashions. I haue a suite to you. You are all Bounty, tis to bestow your Picture on me. You enioy the best of Time; This latter Spring of yours, shewes in my eye, More fruitfull and more temperate withall, Then that whose date is onely limitted By the musicke of the Cuckow. So please you Lady, and I shall preserue it As a most choyce Obiect. How? Excellent Lady, Now you teach Beautie a preseruatiue, More then 'gainst fading Colours; and your iudgement Is perfect in all things. Tis truth. I cannot, worthy Lady. What a Treasury haue I pearch'd. I hope you vnderstand wherefore I make this proffer. Shee has got some intelligence, how I intend to marry Her daughter, and ingenuously perceiued, That by her Picture, which I begged of her, I meant the faire Iolenta: here's a Letter, Which giues expresse charge, not to visit her Till midnight: faile not to come, for tis a businesse That concernes both our honors. Yours in danger to be lost, Iolenta. Tis a strange In unction; what should be the businesse? She is not chang'd I hope. Ile thither straight: For womens Resolutions in such deeds, Like Bees, light oft on flowers, and oft on weeds. How now sweet Mistris? You haue made sorrow looke louely of late, You haue wept. Ile make his brauery fitter for a graue, Then for a wedding. If he beare himselfe so nobly, The manliest office I can doe for him, Is to affoord him my pitie, since h e's like To faile of so deare a purchase: for your mother, Your goodnesse quits her ill; for your brother, He that vowes friendship to a man, and prooues A traytor deserues rather to be hang d, Then he that counterfets money; yet for your sake I must signe his pardon too. Why doe you tremble? Be safe, you are now free from him. Why hee's gone to sea. Signior Ercol , The wind has stood my friend sir, to preuent Your putting to Sea. Onely loue sir, That I might take my leaue sir, and withall Intreat from you a priuate recommends To a friend in Malta, 'twould be deliuered To your bosome, for I had no time to write. You deale equally. Oh. Ist possible Romelio's perswaded, You are gone to the East Indies. Now I shall my Lord. Good my Lord, giue vs leaue in a few words, To expound the Riddle, and to make it plaine, Without the least of scruple: for I take it, There cannot be more lawfull proofe i'th world, Then the oath of the mother. Mercy vpon me! oh, that thou art liuing Is mercy indeed! Yes but you haue forgot me, you and I haue sweat In the Breach together at Malta. In a Letter? O most incestious villaine. No more. Has the Armorer brought the weapons? I will no more thinke of her. And to you deare Lady, I haue entirely vowed my life.

Plot summary

Plot — The Devil's Law Case (When Women Go to Law the Devil Is Full of Business)
Contarino, who has been wooing Jolenta, asks her family’s permission to marry her, but her widowed mother Leonora misunderstands his oblique request and thinks she is herself the object of his interest. Meanwhile Jolenta’s brother, Romelio, would prefer her to marry Ercole; but she refuses him and Ercole takes offence. Contarino fights a duel with Ercole, and they wound one another. The bodies are sent to a 1618 monastery. Ercole recovers and decides to forgo his interest in Jolenta to Contarino, should he too survive. Concerned that he should not, Romelio visits the monastery disguised as a Jewish physician, and stabs Contarino’s body. He is seen by two surgeons, who plan to blackmail him; but ironically the stab clears Contarino’s wound and facilitates his recovery. Romelio’s financial situation is perilous: he has lost his mercantile carracks of spice. Contarino has named Jolenta his heir; Romelio plans to make her Ercole’s too, by fabricating the story that she is pregnant by him. Romelio himself will supply the eventual baby: he has got a nun, Angiolella, with child, and wants both to cover this up and ensure that his issue will inherit both suitors’ property. Jolenta, however, claims that she really is pregnant, by Contarino; so Romelio proposes that she should produce twins. Knowing of Leonora’s interest in Contarino, he fosters jealousy between mother and daughter. The Spanish judge Crispiano comes to Naples in disguise with three objectives: to observe his son’s lifestyle, to investigate reports of illegal gold-mining by Romelio, and to appoint the lawyer Ariosto as a judge. Blaming her son for Contarino’s death, Leonora brings a false law-case that will deprive him of his patrimony by establishing his illegitimacy: he was conceived, she claims, during his father’s absence. Crispiano sits as judge, and attributes the action to malice, but stands down when he becomes a party in the case: Leonora names him as Romelio’s natural father. Ariosto takes his place on the bench, and Crispiano demonstrates that he was not even in Europe at the time in question. To evade punishment, Leonora claims to have become a nun. Ercole, present in disguise, is arrested for killing Contarino, but accuses Romelio of the crime. In the absence of a witness, this is to be settled in a trial by combat.
