And who is he that séekes to haue my bloude, And shall not shed his owne as fast as myne? And this shall eke mainteine my rightfull cause. Ah wretch, thou art both vile and cowardlike, Thy high estate estéemes thy life too deare. For well I wist, that cancred heart of thine Coulde sefely kepe thy heade within these walles, And flée the fielde when combate should be callde. Not one small drop of my bloude shalt thou spill, But buy it deare against thy cancred will. Yelde villein, yelde my right which thou with-holds ▪ O aultars of my countrie soyle. O Gods, giue eare vnto my honest cause. O holy temples of the heauenly Gods. Out of my kingdome am I driuen by force. Punish O Gods this wicked tyrant here. No sauage beast so cruell nor vniust. Since from my right I am with wrong depriued. O father heare what iniuries I take. And you mother. O deare Citie. I must depart, and parting must I prays Oh deare mother the depth of your good will. I can not chuse but must thy will obey, Yet graunt me once my father for to sée. Where be my swéete sisters. Rest you in peace, O worthy mother myne. Hence forth n'am I your ioy ne yet your sonne. Lo here the cause that stirreth me by wrong. Well, speake, darest thou come armed to the fielde? For néedes or thou must ende this life of minde Or quenche my thirst with pouring out thy bloud. O royall race of Thebes now take thine ende. Deare Citizens, and you eternall Gods, Beare witnesse with me here before the worlde, How this my fierce and cruell enimie, Whom causelesse now my brother I do call, With threats of death my lingring steps doth driue Both from my right and from my countrey soyle, Not as beséemes the sonne of Oedipus, But as a slaue, an abiect, or a wretche: And since you be both pitifull and iuste, Uouchsafe O Gods, that as I parte with griefe, So may I yet returne with ioyfull spoyle Of this accursed tyraunt, and he slayne I may recouer quietly mine owne.

Plot summary

Plot — Jocasta
Oedipus unknowingly murdered his father and married his mother Jocasta; on learning the truth, he blinded himself. His sons imprisoned him and agreed to alternate annually as kings of Thebes: Polynices went into exile while Eteocles took the first turn on the throne; but Eteocles refused to give it up at the end of his year in office, and Polynices has besieged Thebes in association with the King of Argos. Jocasta intervenes as their mother, and persuades Polynices to enter Thebes and meet Eteocles under truce. Eteocles will not hand over power, but offers to accept his brother back as a private citizen. Jocasta is unable to reconcile her sons, and Polynices’ forces prepare for battle. Eteocles names Creon as his successor, and asks him to marry Antigone to his son Haemon. Creon consults Tiresias, and learns that Thebes is doomed to destruction in divine revenge for Jocasta’s incest; only the death of his other son, Menoeceus, can save the city. Keen to die for his country, Menoeceus refuses to go into exile as his father wishes, reports the prophecy to Eteocles, and kills himself. Meanwhile, the battle is indecisive, and Eteocles and Polynices agree to settle their dispute in single combat. Jocasta and Antigone are too late to stop them killing each other: arriving to find them both dying, Jocasta stabs herself with Polynices’ dagger. The besieging army is massacred by the Thebans. Antigone brings the three bodies back to Thebes and tells her father what has happened. Creon declares himself King of Thebes and gives order for the funerals of Jocasta and Eteocles, but demands that Polynices, as an enemy of the city, should remain unburied. Antigone protests, pleads, and refuses to marry Haemon, threatening to kill him and then herself should Creon try to force her. Creon banishes both her and Oedipus. She helps her blind father to touch the dead bodies, and he asks her to see that Polynices is buried.
