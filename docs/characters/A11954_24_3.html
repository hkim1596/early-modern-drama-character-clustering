Lord Chancellor, with Purſe and Mace before him. Among the crow'd i' th' Abbey, where a finger Could not be wedg'd in more: I am ſtifled With the meere rankneſſe of their ioy. That I did. Well worth the ſeeing. As well as I am able. The rich ſtreame Of Lords, and Ladies, hauing brought the Queene To a prepar'd place in the Qui e, fell off A diſtance from her; while her Grace ſate downe To reſt a while, ſome halfe an houre, or ſo, In a rich Chaire of State, oppoſing freely The Beauty of her Perſon to the People. Beleeue me Sir, ſhe is the goodlieſt Woman That euer lay by man: which when the people Had the full view of, ſuch a noyſe aroſe, As the ſhrowdes make at Sea, in a ſtiffe Tempeſt, As lowd, and to as many Tunes. Hats ▪ Cloakes, (Doublets, I thinke) flew vp, and had their Faces Bin looſe, this day they had beene loſt. Such ioy I neuer ſaw before. Great belly'd women, That had not halfe a weeke to go, like Rammes In the old time of Warre, would ſhake the preaſe And make 'em reele before 'em. No man liuing Could ſay this is my wife there, all were wouen So ſtrangely in one peece. At length, her Grace roſe, and with modeſt paces Came to the Altar, where ſhe kneel'd, and Saint-like Caſt her faire eyes to Heauen, and pray'd deuoutly. Then roſe againe, and bow'd her to the people: When by the Arch-byſhop of Canterbury, She had all the Royall makings of a Queene; As holy Oyle, Edward Confeſſors Crowne, The Rod, and Bird of Peace, and all ſuch Emblemes Laid Nobly on her: which perform'd, the Quire With all the choyſeſt Muſicke of the Kingdome, Together ſung Te Deum. So ſhe parted, And with the ſame full State pac'd backe againe To Yorke-Place, where the Feaſt is held. I know it: But 'tis ſo lately alter'd, that the old name Is freſh about me. Stokeley and Gardiner, the one of Wincheſter, Newly preferr'd from the Kings Secretary: The other London. All the Land knowes that: How euer, yet there is no great breach, when it comes Cranmer will finde a Friend will not ſhrinke from him. Thomas Cromwell, A man in much eſteeme with th' King, and truly A worthy Friend. The King ha's made him Maſter o' th' Iewell Houſe, And one already of the Priuy Councell. Yes without all doubt. Come Gentlemen ▪ ye ſhall go my way Which is to' th Court, and there ye ſhall be my Gueſts: Something I can command. As I walke thither, Ile tell ye more.

Plot summary

Plot — Henry the Eighth (All Is True)
The Kings of England and of France meet for a magnificent summit conference organized by Cardinal Wolsey. The English nobles resent Wolsey’s influence with the King, but Wolsey strikes first against the ringleader, Buckingham: evidence from his former surveyor, who bears him a grudge, secures his arrest; conviction for treason and execution follow. Queen Katherine persuades the King to repeal taxation which is causing hardship; Wolsey has it put about that his intercession secured this. King Henry attends a banquet at Wolsey’s house, performs in a masque of shepherds, and meets Anne Boleyn. Rumours circulate that he and the Queen are at odds: he claims to be troubled by the fact that she was originally married to his brother, but others notice that he is keen to marry Anne. Wolsey counsels divorce. A legatine court is summoned to judge the case, but Katherine refuses to co-operate and the court breaks up without reaching a decision. The King secretly marries Anne. The nobles conspire to overthrow Wolsey. Documents proving his corruption and treachery accidentally fall into the King’s hands, and Buckingham’s surviving allies serve him with articles of impeachment. He falls sick and dies. Anne is crowned Queen. Katherine too falls sick. Before her death she is visited by benevolent spirits, and by Caputius, the Imperial Ambassador, by whom she sends the King a request to love their daughter and provide for her former servants. Anne goes into labour and produces a daughter. A cabal of councillors attempts to overthrow Archbishop Cranmer, whom they consider a heretic. Before the hearing, Henry gives Cranmer his ring; the Archbishop produces it at a crucial moment, ensuring that the case is referred to the personal judgement of the King, who rebukes the conspirators. The vindicated Cranmer christens Henry’s new daughter, Elizabeth, and prophesies a golden future for England under her and her eventual successor.
