Tis true, the Bishop is in the tower, And goods and body giuen to Gaueston. I, and besides, lord Chamberlaine of the realme, And secretary to, and lord of Man. Thus leaning on the shoulder of the king. He nods, and scornes, and smiles at those that passe. Here comes my lord of Canterburies grace. And war must be the meanes, or heele stay stil. But say my lord, where shall this meeting bee? But I long more to see him banisht hence. Ignoble vassaile that like Phaeton, Aspir'st vnto the guidance of the sunne. VVe know our duties, let him know his peeres. Think you that we can brooke this vpstart pride? And to behold so sweete a sight as that, Theres none here, but would run his horse to death. Then speake not for him, let the pesant go. No, doe but marke how earnestly she pleads. She smiles, now for my life his mind is changd. All that he speakes, is nothing, we are resolu'd. Marke you but that my lord of Lancaster. And so will Penbrooke and I. Slay me my lord, when I offend your grace. Welcome Lord gouernour of the Ile of man. Looke to your owne crowne, if you back him thus. Lets to our castels, for the king is mooude. Ile haue his bloud, or die in seeking it. Meane time my lord of Penbrooke and my selfe, Will to Newcastell heere, and gather head. I warrant you. And ile follow thee. Forslowe no time, sweet Lancaster lets march. Vpon him souldiers, take away his weapons. Lancaster, why talkst thou to the slaue? Go souldiers take him hence, For by my sword, his head shall off: Gaueston, short warning shall serue thy turne: It is our countries cause, That here seuerelie we will execute, Vpon thy person: hang him at a bough: Souldiers, haue him away: But for thou wert the fauorit of a King, Thou shalt haue so much honor at our hands. Arundell, say your message. No, it needeth not, Arundell, we will gratifie the king In other matters, he must pardon vs in this, Souldiers away with him. When can you tell? Arundell no, we wot, He that the care of realme remits, And driues his nobles to these exigents For Gaueston, will if he zease him once, Violate any promise to possesse him. Penbrooke, what wilt thou do? Cause yet more bloudshed: is it not enough That we haue taken him, but must we now Leaue him on had-I wist, and let him go? Nay, do your pleasures, I know how twill prooue. My lord of Penbrookes men, Striue you no longer, I will haue that Gaueston.
