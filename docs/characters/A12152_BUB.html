If I were a woman, now could I fall in loue with my selfe: euery body tels me I am the proprest Gentleman in the Towne, and I put it vp, for the truth is, I dare not giue any one the lye, a pox a fighting, I can looke as big as another, but shall I be such an Asse, to venture my selfe with beasts? for they say, your swords most commonly are Foxes, and haue notable mettall in'em; let me see, I am now at Rufaldoes my father in Law that must be, here he is. Master Rufaldo, if you had beene my owne father, as you are but like to be my father in Law, I should haue prou'd my selfe a foole, by this hand I should not haue knowne you, why, how briske and neate, and youthfull he is! By this flesh that shines, a man would not take you to be aboue fiue or sixe and thirty at most, how came this? No, mine was but raked vp in the embers. Pray, how does my Sweet-heart Mistris Hillaria. No matter, sir, for that, I would not haue her easi ly, I would sweat for her, Ile warrant, Ile make her loue me. Itroth the Ditty is as pretty an ayer as ere I saw, 'tis diuided into three regions too, I warrant you, can make Ballads easily. But for all that, I haue read good stuffe sometimes, especially in your fighting Ballads, When Cannons are roa ring, and bullets are flying, &c. As sweet an aire as a man would wish to liue in, but 'tis some-what backeward. I should haue learn'd Musicke once too, but my Master had so many crochets, I could ne'r away with it: but where is your daughter, sir? there is no Musicke with out her, she is the best Instrument to play vpon. I had as liefe be betwixt hers, for all that. I doe not see what fault shee'can finde with me, and if I had some good word to come ouer her: but I must helpe it out and neede be, with swearing; but here she is. Let mee alone to enter my man, now the point is open: hum, stay, there's a man in her Tables more then I looked for: foot, he kisses her, Ile call Rufaldo backe, hees out of sight, it were but a cowardly tricke, for me to run away. He talkes of suet, I doe melt already. I will set a good face whatsoeuer come. Hilaria, how does my Loue, come kisse: why so: this comes of valour, we fall to, and he falls off, he's some coward, I hope, and how doest Hilaria? 'tis an age since I saw thee: what Springals is that? ha. My acquaintace, who is he? none but Knights, and Knights fellowes, are of my acquaintance, I scorne Gentlemen. Thou shalt doe much with me, sir, I am content for her sake whom I loue, to bee acquainted with you, wo't borrow any money? for so doe all that begin their acquaintance with me, 'tis the fashion, he is a coward, is he not? here: nay, and you scorne my money, I scorne your acquaintance. Why, he will borrow no money of mee, I had neuer such a tricke put vpon me, since I knew what ac quaintance was: Ime sure there haue beene forty acquain ted with me, since I came to Towne, and not one had not so little breeding, to let me aske 'em the question, wee could no sooner shake hands, but the other was in my poc ket: It may be 'tis his modesty. Sir, this is my Loue Hi laria, and if you will not borrow money of me, by this hand, there lyes my sword, he is a coward. Hilaria is my Mistresse, and if any man dare be so venturous, as to blast her reputation with a foule breath, he shall breathe his last. Or if you shall offer in my presence to defile her lip, or touch her hand, or kisse but the nether part of her vesture, you had better kisse her in another place; nay doe but blow on her. Or winke at her, or speake to her, or make signes, or thinke on her to my face, and you had better keepe your thoughts to your selfe: now to conclude, and if you bee agrieu'd, my name is Bubulcus, and you lye. Oh, that I could run thee thorow body and soule, I will challenge thee, a pox on your toes, would I had the paring of your nayles, were you dumbe so long for this? Who look't for you? when will you be here agen? looke for a challenge, the time may come, when I will beate thee. This is the Complement-Schoole. Troth, sir, I doe not know how to conster what you say, although I know it be Latine, sir: the cause of my comming to you, is to let you vnderstand, that here is a Complement-Schoole, and I haue great desire to be taught some of your figaries, and braue words, I doe meane to pay for't soundly too, sir: I thanke my starres, as they say, I haue ready money about me. A Gentleman Vsher at least. What saies he pray, in English? Why looke you, sir, I would haue two kindes of complements: for, sir, I am in loue, and I am in hate. Yes, I am in loue with a Wench, and would haue a delicate speech for her, and I am in hate with a Gentleman, a yong Animall, and I would kill him now without danger of the Law: to tell you true, hee did abuse me in the pre sence of my sweet-heart, and did (sauing this good com pany) kisse my backside. But it was with his foote, sir: now in regard I haue not the heart to kill him with my sword, I would cut him in pieces, and murder him with mouth-guns: looke you, sir, here's money, please your selfe: but I pray you giue me a powdring speech, for I would blow him vp: I beseech you, if euer you put gall into your Inke, make it a bitter speech. O, heauenly Farmers daughter! Sir, I desire to sucke below yor waste. Hee's one of the head forme, I warrant. Who? that's my father in Law. I am a Horse-courser. I had not the grace to mend my pace, I was an ho nest Horse-courser, and suffered euerie foole to ride me, I knew not what belonged to horse-play, let the world kicke at me, I neuer winch'd, all that I am damnd for, is, that de siring to thrine in the world, and to haue good-lucke to horse-flesh, I ambled to the bed of a Parsons wife that was coltish once, and gaue her husband a Horse for't in good fashion, he nener gaue me godamercie for't, indeed it prou'd afterwards to haue the Yellowes. Antonio is gone, no newes of him: I am glad of that, I hope he will come no more. Nay, nothing, nothing, I doe but—a— Bee not afraid, Gentlewomen, for I doe but, you see. Whet my long knife, somebody shall smart for't, but— Nay, no body, I doe not meane to fight, if I liue. Nay, nothing, sweet Ladies, be not troubled, I doe but sharpen my sword. I was eating Oysters tother day, and I had neuer a knife, and so— Welll, you are my friends, if you chance to heare of any mans death shortly, then say Bubulens. No, no, sweet-heart, doe not tremble, I will but— Lose my honour? Ile be caru'd first. No, no, 'tis but in vaine to perswade me, I'm re solu'd, if you loue me, doe not vse any arguments: The Cupidinaean fires burne in my brest, And like the Ouen Etna I am full Of squibs and crackers. We threw our doublets off, to shew wee had no Coate of maile, or priuie shirt vpon vs, against the lawes of duelling, in fine, I bid him say his prayers. Plot summary

Plot — The School of Compliment (Love Tricks)
Selina and Hilaria have each attracted the attention of several suitors. Selina’s brother Antonio is one of those interested in Hilaria; and Hilaria’s recently widowed father Rufaldo, who disapproves of Antonio and would rather she married the wealthy Bubulcus, would nevertheless like Selina for his second wife. Antonio has his friend and servant distract Rufaldo while he sneaks in to visit Hilaria; they do it by persuading Rufaldo that he has been miraculously rejuvenated. Selina’s father is happy to let her have her own way in ===== PAGE 140 ===== her love-life, because her sister Felice absconded after he interfered in hers. He proposes that she should be cautious about marrying Rufaldo, but she declares enthusiasm for the idea and asks for a hasty wedding; on the appointed day, however, she disappears and goes to live in the country disguised as a shepherd. Felice’s abandoned suitor Gasparo sets up a school of compliment to teach people refined behaviour. His clients include: Bubulcus, who wants to learn how to woo Hilaria and scorn Antonio; Delia, a chambermaid keen to brush up her etiquette: a judge who wants to learn how to address the jury; Medulla, a social-climbing widow; an old countryman who wants to turn his oafish son into a courtier; a quarrelsome gentleman; Ingeniolo, a justice’s clerk in love; and Jenkin, an amorous Welshman in search of poetry to turn a shepherdess’s head. The search for Selina sends several people off to the country, including her true love Infortunio. Antonio pretends to go too, and appears not to return; in fact he impersonates Selina and ‘marries’ Rufaldo. On the wedding night, the old man is horrified to learn that his new ‘wife’ will not sleep with him, and also has the physical strength to enforce ‘her’ will on him. Anxious not to lose ‘her’ altogether, he asks ‘her’ at least to live in his house and sleep with Hilaria; Antonio readily agrees. Bubulcus boasts to ‘Selina’ and Hilaria that he has killed the missing Antonio in a duel, stupidly forgetting that Selina is Antonio’s sister; ‘she’ fetches officers, and Bubulcus is arrested for murder. The real Selina has fallen in with a community of shepherds. Among them is her missing sister Felice, who uses herbal remedies to cure Infortunio of his love-melancholy for Selina.
