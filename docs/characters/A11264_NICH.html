What Corporall Oth? I am sorry we haue met with you ext our hearts; you are the man that we are forbidden to keepe company withall, wee must not sweare I can tell you, and you haue the name for swearing. O Simon, what an oth was there. How, Captaine Ydle, my olde Aunts sonne, my deere Kinsman in Capadochio. Well Corporall, Ile e'en along with you, to visit my Kinsman, if I can do him any good, I will,—but I haue nothing for him, Simon Saint Mary Oueris and Fraylty, pray make a lie for me to the Knight my Maister, old Sit Godfrey. Verily, and indeed la, Couzen?— Oh, I shall sowne! Why Co zen? you know tis written, thou shalt not steale? Masse I thinke it bee indeede, in what Chapter's that Couzen? The first of Charity, qua tha, that's a good iest, there's no such Chapter in my booke! That I will? I, that I will, Couzen. I, that would be good indeed? Kinsman? That were rare indeed la pray let me know how? An Actor? O no, that's a Plaier? and our Parson railes againe Plaiers mightily I can tell you, because they brought him drunck vpp'oth Stage once,—as hee will bee horribly druncke. I that, that. Nay grace of God Ile robbe him on't suddainlie? and hang it in the Rosemary banck, but I beare that minde Cou zen I would not steale any thing mee thinkes for mine owne Father. You see Couzen, I am willing to do you any kindnesse, alwaies sauing my selfe harmelesse? Why about your Neck, ist not sir. Maister, thats my Kinsman younder in the Buff-Ierkin— Kinsman, thats my Maister yonder ith' Taffetie Hatt—pray sa lute him intirely? O 'twas as delicious a Chaine a Gold, Kinsman you know, — And as for the Deuill Maister, he has no need on't, for you know he ha's a great chaine of his owne? O twill tickle you here abouts Coozen, because you haue not beene vsde toot. Beare him in, qua tha, pray call out the Maides, I shall nere haue the heart to doo't indeed la. Deare Maister, oh.

Plot — The Puritan, or The Widow of Watling Street (The Puritan Widow)
Lady Plus has just been widowed, but her son Edmond. does not grieve appropriately at the funeral. She mourns a good, liberal husband, who strove to make his family rich by fair means and foul; she swears not to remarry, and her elder daughter Frances renounces marriage too. Not so her younger daughter, Moll, who entertains a suitor. The other two Plus women are also being pestered by unwanted suitors, one of whom has bribed Lady Plus’s brother-in-law, Sir Godfrey, to recommend him. Captain Idle is taken to prison for highway robbery; there he is visited by his puritan kinsman Nicholas, one of. the Plus household’s servants, and also by Skirmish and Pieboard, two tricksters who plan to get him out. To that end, they persuade Nicholas to steal Sir Godfrey’s chain. Pieboard visits Lady Plus posing as a fortune teller, and tells her that her husband is in purgatory, from which he can be released only if both she and her daughters reverse their marital intentions (which he has overheard): she and Frances must marry, and Moll must not. As a puritan, Lady Plus doubts the theology of this, so he establishes his authenticity by predicting that, before the day is out, there will be bloodshed outside her door and Sir Godfrey will suffer a loss, with worse to follow for the women of the household. The first two events are prearranged: Nicholas steals Sir Godfrey’s chain, and Skirmish and Corporal Oath feign a street-fight which results in a wound for Oath and arrest for Skirmish. Pieboard gives Oath a medicine which is actually a sleeping potion, so that he appears to have been killed. Nicholas tells Sir Godfrey that Idle is a conjurer who can get his chain back for him; so Sir Godfrey arranges for Idle to be released and the charges dropped.
