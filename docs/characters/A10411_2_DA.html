Cruell Laurinda (if a word so foule Can have so faire a dwelling.) seale not up Thy eates, but let a pity enter there And find a passage to thy heart. And t'other, for old freindship Strangling his bitter Corrasive in his heart, Hath promis'd to desist from further suit. Then most unhappy me! And what I? Which hand am I? Then I am all despaire, no hope for me. Thy wife, Alexis? But how can it be Without a Husband? and I must be he. How without her heart? For that am I: besides you heard her say I was the right hand you the left, away, Desist Alexis, mine's the upper hand. But I am her desire, in that 'tis showne Her only wish is to make me her owne. 'Tis true, but I her fire. Yet when the flame is spent, The fire continues; Therefore me she meant. Alexis doe, so I her Maidenhead. Let's have another meeting, since her words Delude us thus, wee'le haue a pregnant signe To shew her mind. I'le the while retire Into the Temple, if I linger here I am afraid of meeting Amaryllis, Who with unwelcome love solicites me. Till then farewell. All but her to thee. The name of Rivall should not loose the Freind. Minds in love, Doe count their daies by minutes, measure howres By every sand that drops through the slow glasse; And for each vie a teare. Doe thou so too! Amaryllis, They cannot doe it: I have fixt my love So firme on my Laurinda, that for her I e're shall hate my selfe? For that I hate it! Nor can I, sister, dote like you on any That is the cursed brat of Lalage. Still idly you complaine To crosse mee, Amaryllis, but in vaine! My Alexis Well met, I'ave been at your cottage to seeke you. Let us goe seeke her, To have a finall judgement. Content. Here comes my joy or death. Laurinda now for mercy sake give period To our long miseries. Depriving one the comfort of his joy. I'le rather kisse An Ethiops crisped lip: imbrace a Viper! Deformity it selfe to her is fair. But to me an Antidote. And me she slew to raise me up againe: You shall not slight us thus, what doe you thinke Of mee? Shew by some signe which of us You have design'd for happinesse. Now happy Damon shee thy Garland weares That holds thy heart chain'd in her golden haires! Tis more to take then give. By this your share's the lesse, you but receive. Then shee did approve Of my affection best, shee took my love. Then let our speares Plead for us, Yet tis, Alexis, clean against our oath. So neither shall enjoy her. So both should loose their hope! Whose every breath has powre to stay our hands. Till then, Alexis, let me be thy friend. Tis fit, being Rivalls both in love and griefe. We both have vow'd to sacrifice our life, Resolve. The meaning of the riddle? Alexis, this plain signe confirmes her grant, She gave me waxe to seale the covenant. But by the waxe she saies that she from none But mee will take true loves impression. I see, Alexis, all Laurinda's bees Serve but to sting us both. Alexis come! Cure my feare. Wert thou but my Laurinda I were blest. Age, by no meanes to night: this night shee lodges With fair Laurinda, old Medorus daughter. O my torture! Fly hence as farre as hell, and hide thy head Lower then darknesse; would thou had'st been acting ▪ Incest or murder, when thou cam'st to pray: Thou hadst in any thing sinn'd lesse then this: Vnseasonable devotion! I'le not stand To her award, she is a partiall judge, And will decree unjustly. 'Tis a meere plot contriv'd Betwixt this cursed Nymph, and you ▪ Alexis. You tooke your time while I was sleeping. I doe not know, But there is some trick in't, and I'le appeale From her too partiall se … ence. I doe charge you to teare the paper, And refuse to judge between us. Now thou hast indeed a time wherein thou maist Revenge my scorne. Take it, but I 'le prevent thee. Laurinda, you may spare in this harsh language To utter your dislike: had you a beauty More then immortall, and a face whose glory Farre outshind Angels, I would make my choyce Here, and no where but here; her vertue now Moves a more noble flame within my brest Then ere your beauty did; I am enamour'd More of her soule, then ever yet I doted Upon your face: I doe confesse the fact; Pardon me vertuous maid, for though the action Be worthy death, the object most condemnes mee! Plot — Amyntas, or The Impossible Dowry
More than sixteen years before the start of the action, Claius stole Lalage, the girlfriend of Philaebus. Philaebus grew melancholy and his father Pilumnus prayed to Ceres for vengeance on Lalage, who duly died in childbirth. This didn’t help Philaebus, who pined to death on her grave. Ceres declared that she will remain angry until the blood of Claius both quenches and kindles fire; so Claius fled, pursued by Corymbus, determined to kill him and lift the curse. But Ceres also disapproved of Pilumnus’ vindictiveness, and further declared that his daughter Urania will have 1630 an unhappy marriage unless her fiancé Amyntas, Claius’ son, gives her as a dowry something he does not, may not, and cannot have. The riddle so occupied his mind that he went mad. All this strife convinces Medorus that the time is not right for his daughter Laurinda to fall in love. She is herself averse to committing herself in marriage (since marriages always seem to end in unhappiness), and cannot make up her mind between her two suitors, Damon and Alexis. Damon is also the object of Amaryllis’ affections, but he is only interested in Laurinda. The two suitors would like to settle matters by fighting a duel, but bloodshed is a capital crime in the sacred vale, so they make a suicide pact instead. Laurinda dissuades them from carrying it out, and promises that she will finally accept one of them tomorrow: all three must accept the decision of the first virgin they meet coming out of the temple in the morning. Amaryllis is in despair over Damon, and intends asking him to kill her if he cannot love her. Laurinda proposes a remedy, and they spend the night together. Claius returns to Sicily in disguise, hoping to use his medical knowledge to cure Amyntas. He succeeds, but advises Urania to take an oath of virginity to prevent Amyntas racking his brains into a relapse. During the night, Dorylas and his friends dress up as elves to scrump apples from an orchard owned by the fairy- obsessed Jocastus. When caught, Dorylas claims to be King Oberon, and ‘knights’ Jocastus. Damon and Alexis meet the virgins at the temple. The first one is Amaryllis in disguise, which pleases Alexis: he goes to fetch Laurinda. The angry Damon wounds Amaryllis, then flees. She writes a letter in her own blood, informing Laurinda of her decision, and claims that she accidentally gored herself on her own spear.
