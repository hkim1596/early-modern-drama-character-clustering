When Iulius Caeſar (whoſe remembrance yet Liues in mens eyes, and will to Eares and Tongues Be Theame, and hearing euer) was in this Britain, And Conquer'd it, Caſſibulan thine Vnkle (Famous in Caeſars prayſes, no whit leſſe Then in his Feats deſeruing it) for him, And his Succeſſion, granted Rome a Tribute, Yeerely three thouſand pounds; which (by thee) lately Is left vntender'd. I am ſorry Cymbeline, That I am to pronounce Auguſtus Caeſar ( Caeſar, that hath moe Kings his Seruants, then Thy ſelfe Domeſticke Officers) thine Enemy: Receyue it from me then. Warre, and Confuſion In Caeſars name pronounce I 'gainſt thee: Looke For fury, not to be reſiſted. Thus defide, I thanke thee for my ſelfe. Let proofe ſpeake. So ſir. Thankes, Royall Sir: My Emperor hath wrote, I muſt from hence, And am right ſorry, that I muſt report ye My Maſters Enemy. So Sir: I deſire of you A Conduct ouer Land, to Milford-Hauen. Madam, all ioy befall your Grace, and you. Your hand, my Lord. Sir, the Euent Is yet to name the winner. Fare you well. But what from Rome? When expect you them? This forwardneſſe Makes our hopes faire. Command our preſent numbers Be muſter'd: bid the Captaines looke too't. Now Sir, What haue you dream'd of late of this warres purpoſe. Dreame often ſo, And neuer falſe. Soft hoa, what truncke is heere? Without his top? The ruine ſpeakes, that ſometime It was a worthy building. How? a Page? Or dead, or ſleeping on him? But dead rather: For Nature doth abhorre to make his bed With the defunct, or ſleepe vpon the dead. Let's ſee the Boyes face. Hee'l then inſtruct vs of this body: Young one, Informe vs of thy Fortunes, for it ſeemes They craue to be demanded: who is this Thou mak'ſt thy bloody Pillow? Or who was he That (otherwiſe then noble Nature did) Hath alter'd that good Picture? What's thy intereſt In this ſad wracke? How came't? Who is't? What art thou? 'Lacke, good youth: Thou mou'ſt no leſſe with thy complaining, then Thy Maiſter in bleeding: ſay his name, good Friend. Thy name? Thou doo'ſt approue thy ſelfe the very ſame: Thy Name well ſits thy Faith; thy Faith, thy Name: Wilt take thy chance with me? I will not ſay Thou ſhalt be ſo well maſter'd, but be ſure No leſſe belou'd. The Romane Emperors Letters Sent by a Conſull to me, ſhould not ſooner Then thine owne worth preferre thee: Go with me. I good youth, And rather Father thee, then Maſter thee: My Friends, The Boy hath taught vs manly duties: Let vs Finde out the prettieſt Dazied-Plot we can, And make him with our Pikes and Partizans A Graue: Come, Athie him: Boy hee's preferr'd By thee, to vs, and he ſhall be interr'd As Souldiers can. Be cheerefull; wipe thine eyes, Some Falles are meanes the happier to ariſe. Away boy from the Troopes, and ſaue thy ſelfe: For friends kil friends, and the diſorder's ſuch As warre were hood-wink'd. It is a day turn'd ſtrangely: or betimes Let's re-inforce, or fly. Conſider Sir, the chance of Warre, the day Was yours by accident: had it gone with vs, We ſhould not when the blood was cool, haue threatend Our Priſoners with the Sword. But ſince the Gods Will haue it thus, that nothing but our liues May be call'd ranſome, let it come: Sufficeth, A Roman, with a Romans heart can ſuffer: Auguſtus liues to thinke on't: and ſo much For my peculiar care. This one thing onely I will entreate, my Boy (a Britaine borne) Let him be ranſom'd: Neuer Maſter had A Page ſo kinde, ſo duteous, diligent, So tender ouer his occaſions, true, So feate, ſo Nurſe-like: let his vertue ioyne With my requeſt, which Ile make bold your Highneſſe Cannot deny: he hath done no Britaine harme, Though he haue ſeru'd a Roman. Saue him (Sir) And ſpare no blood beſide. I do not bid thee begge my life, good Lad, And ye I know thou wilt. The Boy diſdaines me, He leaues me, ſcornes me: briefely dye their ioyes, That place them on the truth of Gyrles, and Boyes. Why ſtands he ſo perplext? Happy be you. Philarmonus. Read, and declare the meaning. WHen as a Lyons whelpe, ſhall to himſelfe vnknown, with out ſeeking finde, and bee embrac'd by a peece of tender Ayre: And when from a ſtately Cedar ſhall be lopt branches, which being dead many yeares, ſhall after reuiue, bee ioynted to the old Stocke, and freſhly grow, then ſhall Poſthumus end his miſeries, Britaine be fortunate, and flouriſh in Peace and Plen tie. Thou Leonatus art the Lyons Whelpe, The fit and apt Conſtruction of thy name Being Leonatus, doth import ſo much: The peece of tender Ayre, thy vertuous Daughter, Which we call Mollis Aer, and Mollis Aer We terme it Mulier ; which Mulier I diuine Is this moſt conſtant Wife, who euen now Anſwering the Letter of the Oracle, Vnknowne to you vnſought, were clipt about With this moſt tender Aire.

Plot — Cymbeline, King of Britain
Imogen has been the heir to Cymbeline’s kingdom since the disappearance of her infant brothers twenty years ago. Now her father intends her to marry her foolish stepbrother Cloten; but she pre-empts this by marrying Posthumus instead. Cymbeline objects to Posthumus, whom he took in as an orphan and brought up at court, and banishes him. The Queen pretends support for the couple, but is really the source of Cymbeline’s hostility; her objective is to bring the succession into her own family line, if necessary by destroying Imogen. Claiming to be studying toxicology, she asks Cornelius to give her some poison for experimental purposes. He mistrusts her motives: the ‘poison’ he supplies is really only a sleeping potion. She then gives it to Posthumus’ servant, Pisanio, claiming it to be a powerful medicine. Posthumus goes to Rome, leaving his bracelet behind with Imogen as a love-token, and taking her ring. There he boasts of his wife’s beauty and chastity, and bets Iachimo the ring that she will never succumb to his seduction. Iachimo comes to Britain, tells Imogen that Posthumus is behaving adulterously in Rome, and tries to persuade her to do the same. When she refuses, he claims he was only testing her, and asks her to take charge of a chest of valuables, partly paid for by Posthumus, and lodge them for the night in a safe place, her bedroom. He hides in the chest and emerges when she is asleep, giving him the opportunity to inspect the room, look at her in bed, and take Posthumus’ bracelet from her arm. Back in Rome, he claims to have enjoyed Imogen sexually, and the bracelet convinces Posthumus that he is telling the truth. Iachimo takes the ring and Posthumus sends two letters home: one to Imogen asking her to meet him at Milford Haven, the other to Pisanio ordering him to kill her on arrival there. Pisanio escorts her as planned, but then gives her boy’s clothes to enable her to escape from Britain; he also passes on the ‘medicine’ he had from the Queen, then writes to tell Posthumus that she has been killed as ordered. Cymbeline has neglected to pay the tribute to Rome agreed by his predecessors. Caius Lucius comes to demand it, but gets a dusty answer. Rome prepares its 1610 forces for an invasion. Imogen is found to be absent from court, and, stung by her contemptuous rejection, Cloten follows her wearing a suit of Posthumus’ clothes, intending to rape her and bring her back humiliated to court.
