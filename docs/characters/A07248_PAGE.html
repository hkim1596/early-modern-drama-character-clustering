Full speech

I wonder that My Lord so much affects him. In fire and water works, And deale in'em When the trade has given you over, as appeares (by the increase of your high forehead. I had had an ignorant nurse else I have tide sir My Ladies garter, and can ghesse. I would bring you on your knees, were I a woman. Feare not my brave Lord Your noble father; fighting is to him Familiar as eating. He can teach Our moderne Duellists how to cleave a button, And in a new way, never yet found out By old Caranza. Looke up deare Lady, sad newes never came sherd with loud applause. Thou art an asse, And this is a sweete burthen. It may be you 〈◊〉 both be got in court, Though bred up in the Citie, for your mothers, As I have heard lov'd the lobbie, and there nightly Are seen strange apparitions, and who knowes But that some noble fawne, heated with wine, And cloyde with partridge, had a kinde of longing To trade in sprats? this needs no exposition, But can you yeeld a reason for your wishes? You are fooles, And ignorant of your happinesse, ere I was Sworne to the pantofle, I have heard my tutor Prove it by logicke, that a servants life Was better then his masters, and by that I learne from him, if that my memory falle not, I'll make it good. Thus then from the king To the beggar, by gradation all are servants, And you must grant the slavery is lesse To studie to please one, then many. Well then, and first to you Sir, you complaine You serve one Lord, but your Lord serves a thousand, Besides his passions (that are his worst masters) You must humor him, and he is bound to sooth Every gri me Sir above him, if he frowne, For the least neglect you feare to loose your place, But if, and with all slavish observation, From the mignions selfe, to the groome of his close stoole, He hourly seekes not favour, he is sure To be eas' of his office, though perhaps he bought it. Nay more, that high disposer of all such That are subordinate to him, serves, and feares The fury of the many-headed monster, The giddy multitude And as a horse Is still a horse, for all his golden trappings, So your men of purchas'd titles, at their best are But serving-men in rich liveries. Why thou dull pate, As I could thee, of my tutor. I am cut of the governour. This is a pend speech, And serves as a perpetuall preface to A dinner made of fragments.

Plot — The Unnatural Combat
Malefort Junior, son of the Admiral, has committed treason by allying himself with pirates and threatening the ruin of Marseilles. The Admiral is suspected of complicity and imprisoned. Theocrine, his daughter, reluctantly offers herself to Beaufort Junior, son of the Governor of Marseilles, if he will only persuade his father to allow hers a hearing. Beaufort Senior can refuse his son nothing, so the Admiral gets his opportunity to protest his innocence. Malefort Junior ‘challenges his father to a duel over a wrongdoing which he will not specify. The Admiral refuses to be held accountable by his son, and accepts the challenge. Malefort Junior is killed: the threat to Marseilles is over, and Admiral Malefort is lauded a hero. Malefort suggests that Theocrine should marry Beaufort Junior; Beaufort Senior half-heartedly agrees and invites them to a nuptial feast. The Governor's table is open to any former soldier who needs a free ~ meal, a custom regularly invoked by Belgarde, who is unemployed in peacetime and angry at being denied - his -back pay. His plain- speaking wins him respect and a gift of money from the Governor. He spends it all on new clothes, but the signs of prosperity attract his creditors, and he is still unable to pay them. People are noticing Malefort’s excessive solicitude towards his daughter. This comes to a head when Beaufort Junior takes her off to woo her in private, with a priest ready to perform the marriage. Malefort reacts by calling off the marriage and taking Theocrine home. He harbours an incestuous passion for his daughter, who is very like his dead second wife. Struggling to conquer the urge, he confesses to his friend Montreville and asks him to abduct her: he cannot be tempted if he never sees her again. Beaufort Junior realizes something is wrong and has Belgarde muster troops to besiege Montreville’s fort. Montreville demands sexual favours from Theocrine, and reveals her father’s secret. Meanwhile Malefort reasons from classical precedent that incest is not wicked after all, and goes to fetch his daughter back.
