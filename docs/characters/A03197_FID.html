Heer's a fidling indeed, I thinke your tongue be made of nothing but fiddlestrings, I hope the fiddle must haue some rest as well as the fiddle-sticke: well Crowde, what say you to Fiddle now? It is indeed, Master. To goe to bed againe Sir. What a fidling doe you keepe, are you not ashamde to make such musicke; I hope sir, you will christen mee anew shortly, for you haue so worne this name, that ne're a wench in all the towne but will scorne to dance after my fiddle. Thats more than you know, Master. Youle be damn'd then, Master. I had rather your daughter lou'd me, I hold with you Master, if my yong mistresse would like so well of my musicke, that she would dance after no bo dies instrument but mine. A shame on you, I thought you would not heare on that side True, master; therin you say well. Who he sir, neuer regard him, I know the vildest thing by him, O tis abhominable! I tell you sir, twill make your haire to stand on end as stiffe as a Rubbing-brush, to heare his villanies. What's this you haue giuen me? Haue you any skill in Arithmeticke? Sir, I would haue you to multiply; could you not make this one shilling two or three? I would not be knowne to beg, but if out of your cunning you can doe this tricke of multi plication, I shall speake the better. So sir, this is multiplication, now sir, if you know the Rule of addition you are an excellent Scholler: can you not adde? An other shilling sir. Why then attend you Hilles and Dales, and stones so quicke of hearing, this Cripple is. An honest man, as any is in all the towne. I by this siluer, and as good a fellow as euer went vp on foure legges, if you would multiply till mid-night, I would neuer speake otherwise. The chaste Moone, why the Moone is not chaste. Why sir, ther's a man in the middle of her, how can she be chaste then? Gallants good-night; if time and place were in pros peritie, I were yours for an houres societie, I must after yon mulbery with my torch: adue deare hearts, adue. Exit. Porter, I am not for you, you see I am perambulating before a female. Speake in time then Porter, for otherwise I doe not loue to answer you, and bee as briefe as you can, good Por ter. Then you are an asse Porter, tis the faire Maide of the Exchange. God giue you the time of the day, pardon mee Gal lants, I was so neere the middle that I knew not which hand to take. And yet because I wil be sure to giue you a true salu tation, Cripple, quomodo vales? Good morrow Cripple, good den good maister Barnard, maister Bowdler, Bonos noches, as they say, good night; and thus you haue heard my manner of salutation. The best newes I know not sir, but the newest news is most excellent yfaith. Why this it is, the Serieants are watching to arrest you at maister Berries sute. Nay, I know not where; alas sir, there is no such mat ter, I did but say so much, to make you warme the handle of your rapier: But M. Bawdler, I haue good newes for you. How, russeting? You pippinmonger, Ile cut off your legs, and make you trauel so neere the mother earth, that euery boy shall be high enough to steale apples out of thy basket, call mee Rus setting? As I am a gentleman cripple, I meant him no harme, but the name of Russetting to maister Fiddle, that many times trauells vnder the arme in veluet, but for the most part in lea ther trussd with calue-skinne points, tis most tolerable, and not to be indured, flesh and bloud cannot beare it. Plot summary

Plot — The Fair Maid of the Exchange
Phyllis and Ursula are assaulted by two ruffians as they go to deliver wares to a customer at night. A cripple rescues them, and a second assault is foiled by the intervention of Frank Golding; the cripple promises to help Frank if ever he can. Frank's two brothers are both in love with Phyllis, and, though he scorns amorous passions, Frank has fallen for her too. Phyllis, however, loves the cripple, and woos him by giving him a handkerchief to embroider “for a friend’. Meanwhile, Bobbington poses as a sailor going on a voyage, and borrows money from Phyllis’s father, Flower, leaving a diamond ring with him as security. Ferdinand and Anthony overhear one another's plans to woo Phyllis, but neither knows that Frank is also a rival. Ferdinand writes to her; Anthony plans to intercept the letter and substitute his own, but Ferdinand disguises Frank as a porter and gets him hired as Anthony's messenger. Planning to double-cross them both, Frank enlists the cripple's help. The cripple supplies him with two rejection letters to take back to his brothers, as from Phyllis. They each enlist the help of one of Phyllis’s parents, ===== PAGE 378 ===== who then quarrel over which brother shall marry their daughter. _ Knowing Phyllis loves him but thinking himself unworthy, the cripple lends Frank his clothes. Frank courts Phyllis in disguise, and she gives him her ring; then. he brings the parents forged letters from his brothers withdrawing their suits. Each parent decides to:spite the other by offering Phyllis to Frank instead of the other’s favoured son-in-law. The thwarted brothers realize what has happened, but Phyllis asks to be allowed a say in her.own marriage — and chooses Frank. As the play ends, Flower is arrested for receiving stolen property — Bobbington’s ring — and sent for trial. In the sub-plot, Mall Berry is in love with Barnard, but suffers the unwanted attentions of Bowdler. Barnard is surety for a £100 debt to Mall’s father; the original borrower is dead and the debt about to fall due, but Berry refuses to allow Barnard any more time to find the money. Bowdler persists in his pursuit of Mall, using lines he remembers from Venus and Adonis. Berry engages sergeants to arrest Barnard. Mall agrees to marry Bowdler, but the cripple points out that, through a party wall, he has overheard her talking about Barnard in her sleep: he is the man she truly loves.
