What in a place ſo publique? A place more private would become my meſſage, And give it gentle hearing. From the yong Lord Skales. The Lord my Maſter, to whom the fame of Your faire Daughters beauty hath throng'd by infinite Tongues, hath in his breaſt Tooke ſuch a ſtrong impreſſion, that hee's pleaſ'd (So your conſent and his may paralell) To make her his faire Bride. Sir, I have full Commiſſion from his mouth To treat ſo much; and what I undertake He hath engag'd his honour to make good. Pray ſir, your anſwer. But Donna Anne, Madona, Madam, Lady, What breadth thoſe Titles beare? Me thinkes the motion might be faire and even, To make her miſtreſſe of her owne free thoughts And chuſe where ſhe beſt likes. Your Mother ſir, that muſt be. His Lordſhip askes no more. Sir, I ſhall. A bold fellow; give him ſcope, my Lord. Nay good my Lord have patience; heare him ſpeake. This fellowes bluntneſſe Doth ſomewhat better than at firſt, Whom wilt thou follow now? I doe not thinke but under that rough brow Is lodg'd an honeſt heart; they are beſt ſervants Whom want, penurious neede, and poverty Cannot fright from their Maſters. Ent. Chan. and his Wife. That meant by mee? But Trencher friend? But eate well. 'Tis moſt ſtrange: ſure 'tis ſome vex'd ſpirit that hath Deceaſ'd of late from out the houſe: who dyed laſt in that Chamber? Perhaps hers; who having hid ſome treaſure in her Life time muſt, till that be diſcovered, walke of force, The like I have read in Legends. But hath he done ſuch ſtrange things? Theſe things ſo well ſucceeding, pray reſolve us In what forme doth this Divell haunt your houſe. So I told you, ſure your Mothers. Your reaſon ſir? As you would purchaſe us your laſting friends. Ile aide him in the ſearch to make That ſweet concluſion. If his pockets lining ſuite but to the out-ſide Of his Cloaths, he hath ſmall need of them. Still, ſtill miſpriſion; might I adviſe you, proffer What you purpoſe, and if refuſ'd, ſo leave him. Sir, I had conference with him. By no entreaty or perſwaſive skill, Nay were he preſſ'd by menace or command, He vowes to have no hand in theſe deſignes; I could not make him thinke ſuch things can be; He counts them meere impoſtures, falacyes, Or, let a man receive them at the beſt, Illuſions of the Divell; that Ghoſts walke He ſaith directly 'tis impoſſible, And in that faith he'le dye; further diſcourſe I could by no meanes get him liſten to, but halfe diſpleaſ'd he left me. I know not how you could diſpoſe him better And empty all your wiſhes. The maid talkes idly. I am but one and I my ſelfe will venture.

Plot summary

Plot — A New Trick to Cheat the Devil
Anne Changeable wants to marry Slightall, with her father’s consent, but her mother persuades her that marriage to Lord Scales will be better for her social standing. Slightall takes rejection badly: he turns to misogyny and voluptuous hedonism. One of his servants, Roger, tries unsuccessfully to dissuade him from squandering his money, while the other, Geoffrey, helps make an arrangement with a usurer to supply him with a further £1,000 — the usurer’s objective being to acquire his land. Lord Scales visits Anne, and she finds that he is not a good kisser: Slightall is the better catch in every respect save his lack of a title; but when she tries to reactivate the romance, Slightall rejects her. He has been ruined by prodigality and pays off his servants; Geoffrey is taken on by Lord Scales, but Roger declines a similar offer. Anne insults everyone who persuaded her to jilt Slightall, but this only makes Lord Scales more attracted to her. Changeable promises Anne that he will get Slightall back for her. Driven out of his wits with melancholy, Slightall conjures up Beelzebub, and makes a contract: the devil will give him enough money to pay his debts in exchange for the right to take his soul when he no longer owes anyone anything. On the day his debt to the usurer falls due, he turns out to have ample money to pay, so the usurer gets his money back rather than the lands he coveted. Asked by Anne to give Slightall some financial support, Lord Scales finds his offer rejected with contempt. The Changeables’ house is troubled by a female spirit; Slightall agrees to help exorcise it by spending the night in the haunted room. When he does so, he is visited by the ghost in Anne’s form. Beelzebub comes for Slightall’s soul, but Slightall demonstrates that he is not yet clear of debt: he paid the usurer with money loaned by Beelzebub. The devil accepts the situation and offers to marry him to the she-devil who haunts the Changeables’ house. Anne’s mother and Lord Scales give their consent, and watch as a friar performs the ceremony. In fact it is not a ghost in Anne’s form, but Anne herself; and ‘Beelzebub’ was really Changeable: Anne has finally got her man back. In the sub-plot, two friars are detained until late by charitable visitation, and find themselves locked out of their abbey. They ask Nan to give them a bed for the night.
