Faith I'me almoſt extracted, I'me come to the Mercury already; there's nothing left but my wits: but what if I can get no cuſtomers now? Foh! that's a ſtinking trade. Oh, Aſtronomia! there's my chiefeſt griefe, I con feſſe; for as 'tis held policy in rich men to loue; ſo I feare it will proue ridiculous in me, if once I grow poore. Well, but what courſe ſhall I take, if I get mony? Oh! thou haſt a rare wit, my fine Phantaſtes! well, let's commit it to the heauens, and if my ſtars bleſſe me but to obtaine Aſtronomia; Ile count it as an enioying of the whole world, which I haue yet but ſeene. Sir, I can impart ſuch rarities of relation vnto you, as would amaze you; and yet they are familiar to a Trauai lour. In a City of Greece, I remember I ſaw the admired net, which Vulcan made to entangle Mars and Venus; and 'tis hang'd vp in a Temple dedicated to the ſame god, and by himſelfe was giuen thereunto, to the terror of all Cuckold-makers for euer. Hum—oh—true Sir, it was inuiſible, but, Now Sir— it is to be ſeene. Nay, Sir, in another place of Greece there is a round, cloſe Valley, incompaſſed with exceeding high Hills; only on one ſide there is a narrow entrance into it & through the middeſt of it runnes a delicate ſtreame, by the banke of which if a man ſtand, he ſhall as perfectly heare the Muſike of the Spheares, as if he were amongſt them: and the cauſe of this, by the inhabitants is thought to be the height of the Hills: which keeping-in the ſound, and bringing it down to the wa ter, does by an aëriall reſultancy produce a moſt reciprocall repreſentation of the diuine harmonie. Nay, Sir, moreouer it is ſo ſweet, that the hearer can neuer leaue hearing of his owne accord, but ſtands ſtill. Hum—faith I was told the deuice of that, but I haue forgot. Why, I haue ſeene white beares with faces would make you fall in loue with them. Nay, Sir, and theſe haue long tailes. 'Tis true; yet (if you marke it) 'tis broken. Againe, Sir, in my trauailes in Tuſcany, I beheld a moſt curious piece of Architecture; it was an hall built in the forme of a croſſe, that, which way ſoeuer the wind ſate, or the ſunne ſhin'd, a man might alwayes goe to one of the ends, and ſo decline the preſent violence of the ſeaſon: and as in an arbour vnto which the Sun has acceſſe, you ſhall ſee boughes at the top correſpondently repreſented on the ground in the ſhaddow: ſo whatſoeuer curious work was ſeene in the roofe of this building, the ſame vnderneath was expreſt in the Floore. Plot summary

Plot — Technogamia, or The Marriages of the Arts
Astronomia has several suitors: Geographus (whom her mother dislikes), Geometres (whom her friends favour), and Poeta (who has recently jilted Historia to pursue her). Against her kinsman Polites’ advice, Historia still wants to marry Poeta, consummating the long-standing association between history and the poetic imagination. Poeta quarrels with Grammaticus, and Logicus becomes involved in the ensuing affray. Unsure whether to fight a duel with Poeta or sue him for assault, Logicus sets both courses in train. Hearing that Poeta has been hurt in the fight, Historia sends to Medicus for help, but Magus, who is helping Geometres, sees an opportunity to dispose of one of his client’s rivals, and persuades Medicus to send poison instead. Medicus’ man, Sanguis, delivers the poison, but spots that it isn’t medicine. Poeta infers Historia's treachery, but persuades himself out of absolute misogyny by thinking of Astronomia. To reconcile Poeta with Logicus and Grammaticus, Ethicus arranges a banquet. Several of the wooers use magical help in their suits. Poeta meets two gipsies, who pick his- pocket and foretell that he will marry Astronomia. Using sieve- divination, Magus predicts that Geometres will marry Astronomia, and will achieve this mainly through hypnotism, with a little pharmaceutical help. (Geometres finds the third option, conjuration, too frightening to try.) However, when Astronomia drinks the supposed love philtre at Ethicus’ feast, it poisons her. Meanwhile, Poeta seems reconciled to his former adversaries, but gets drunk and tries to pick fights with Geographus and Grammaticus; Magus charms him to sleep. Causidicus accepts fees from both Logicus and Poeta, so when the case comes to trial, Polites dismisses him and forces the parties to speak for themselves. The trial is adjourned when news arrives that Astronomia, burning up with the poison, has fallen into a coma. Medicus attends her, and cures her by administering an enema. Meanwhile the gipsies and servants indulge themselves in country pastimes: morris dancing, barley-break, and riddles. Polites takes up Geographus’ and Historia’s love- suits: he persuades Astronomia’s mother that Geographus will be a worthy son-in-law, so long as he gets rid of his servant Phantastes, and the couple are married.
