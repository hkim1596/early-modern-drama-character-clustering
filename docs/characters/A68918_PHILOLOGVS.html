TOO true (alas) too true I ſay, was our Diuination, The whiche Mathaetes did foreſée, when laſt we were in place, For now (in deede) we féele the ſmart and horrible veration, Whiche Romyſh power vnto vs did threaten and manace: Wherfore, great néede we haue, to call to God alway for grace: For féeble fleſh is farre too weake, thoſe paynes to vndergo: The whiche all they that feare the Lord, are now appoin ted too. The Legate from the Pope of Roome, is come into our Coaſtes, Who doth the Sainctes of God eche where, with Tiranny oppreſſe, And in the ſame moſt gloryouſly himſelfe he vawnt and boaſt, The more one mourneth vnto him, he pittieth the leſſe, Out of his cruell Tyranny, the Lorde of Heauen me bleſſe: For hitherto, in bleſſed ſtate, my whole lyfe I haue ſpent: With health of body, wealth in Goddes, and minde alway content. Beſides, of friendes, I haue great ſtore, who do me firmely loue, A faithfull wife and children fayre, of wooddes and paſture ſtore, And diuers other thinges, whiche I haue got for my behoofe, Whiche nowe to be depraued off, would grieue my hart full ſore: And if I come once in their clawes, I ſhall get out no more. Vnleſſe I wyll renounce my fayth, and ſo their minde fulfyll, Whiche if I do, without all doubt, my ſoule for ay I ſpyll. For ſith I haue receiued once the firſt fruictes of my faith, And haue begon to ronne the courſe, that leadeth to ſaluation, If in the midſt therof, I ſtay or ceaſe, the Scripture ſayth, It booteth not that I began with ſo good preparation, But rather, maketh muche the more, vnto my condempnation: For he alone ſhall haue the Palme, whiche to the ende doth ronne, And he which plucks his hand fro ̄ Plough, in Heaue ̄ ſhal neuer com. Thoſe Labourers which hyred were in Vineard for to moyle, And had their Peny for their payne, they taried all whyle night, For if they ceaſſed had, when Sunne their fleſh with h … did droyſe, And had departed from their worke, they ſhould haue loſt by right, Their wages Peny: I like wiſe, ſhalbe depriued qaight Of that ſame Crowne, the whiche I haue in fayth longe looked for, But for this time, I wyll depart, I dare here ſay no more.

Plot summary

Plot — The Conflict of Conscience
Satan, concerned about the spread of godliness on Earth, procures Hypocrisy, Tyranny, and Avarice to counteract it by propagating vice through the Roman Catholic church. Philologus and Mathetes discuss the danger to Protestantism, and conclude that worldly torments are sent by God to test men’s faith. After arguing about their mutual precedence, the vices resolve to help the papal legation, and adopt innocuous aliases. Tyranny is commissioned as an inquisitor to hunt heretics, while Avarice accepts their bribes to save them from arrest. Caconos alerts the vices to the heresies of Philologus, who is arrested and tried by the Cardinal in consistory court. He is examined on the topics of papal authority and transubstantiation, and found guilty. His property is confiscated and his family’s distress used to put psychological pressure on him; he is also threatened with imprisonment, torture, and execution, but promised money and power if he recants. He does, persuaded by the glass of vanity given by Sensual Suggestion. Spirit and Conscience call on Philologus to renounce his apostasy and embrace martyrdom, but he rejects them. In prosperity, he is visited by Horror, who torments him with the knowledge of his own damnation. Theologus and Eusebius urge the continuing availability of God’s grace, but Philologus blasphemes and curses God. Despairing of salvation, he hangs himself. (In the B-text, he is given grace to repent at the last moment.)
