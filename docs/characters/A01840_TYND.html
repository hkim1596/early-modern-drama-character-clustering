O, Sir, but those that doe commend for feare, Doe in their hearts a secret hatred beare. Euer learne this; the truest praise indeed, Must from the heart, and not from words proceed. I feare some foule play: doth Aegystheus meane, Then totally for to inuest himselfe In Agamemnons seat? Where's young Orestes ? How took my daughter that? I feare heauen cannot looke with equall eyes Vpon so many deaths, but meanes to send Plague after plague; for in a wretched state, One ill begets another dismall Fate: But goe and tell my daughter I will come, And helpe to solemnize her nuptiall night: Her hasty wedding, and the old Kings neglect, Makes my coniecturall soule some ill suspect. Exeunt. O but pray Ioue the Fates now were not forc't, But deeds like words no man can e're recall, Bee't good or ill; once don, we must beare all. Come Father sit we downe, and make a feast, They set to the feast. To glad our hearts; Heauen still doth for the best. Who euer trusted much on fortunes gifts, On wife, on state, on health, on friends, on lands, May looke on Agamemnons comming home: Fortune me thinks ne're shew'd her power more, How quickly could she turn her Fatall sword Vpon his brest, that thought himselfe past harme, She that had vs'd death like an angry dogge, Holding him vp, when that he should haue bit, When al the game was past, and's fury laid, The King being past all danger, safe at home, Then he slip's coller, neuer vntill then; And fortune she stood hissing of him on, Till he had torne the good Kings soule away. Aegystheus, since the Gods haue bless'd you so, Haue care their blessings turne not to your woe. Your ioy, my daughters ioy, and my ioy too, Haue care it be preseru'd, and brought vp well: And take heed, sonne, of Agamemnons blood, Pierce not with enuy the Babes tender heart. I see't Aegystheus, and my ag'd blood grows warme, As if my selfe were a new father made, And all the blessings I can render it, Shal drop like golden showers on the head: Me thinks it doth recall my sliding age, And makes swift time retire backe againe: It doth vnfold those wrincles in my face, Which griefe and yeeres had fixed as their signes Vpon my brow, and now it shall be seene, Althoug my hayres are gray, my ioyes are greene. Then now is Argos Court like to some stage, When the sad plot fills it with murdred Trunckes, And none are left aliue but onely one, To aske the kinde spectators ( plaudite ) All else haue bid ( valete ) to the world, The man reseru'd for that, is Tyndarus, Who thus hath seen his childrens childrens end, His Grandchild, a bad sonne, a most deare friend; The Scene must now be ouerflow'd with grones, Each man sits downe to waile his priuate mones: One for the Queen doth weep, one for the King, All taste the bitter waters of this Spring: The Nurse bewails the child, that part she beares, All haue their subiects to bedew with teares; Each one yet haue but one; but all of mee, Challenge a part in griefes sad sympathy. 'Tis vile to hate a Father, but such loue, As breeds a hate to'th mother, worse doth proue: Our life consists of ayre, our state of winde, All things we leaue behind vs which wee find, Sauing our faults; witnesse Orestes here, VVho was his owne tormentor, his owne feare. VVho flying all, yet could not fly him selfe, But needs must shipwrack vpon murders shelfe: And so his brest made hard with miserie, He grew himselfe to be his enemy. Thus griefe and gladnesse still by turnes do come, But pleasure leastwhile doth possesse the roome. Long nights of griefe may last, but lo, one day Of shining comfort slideth soone away. He, whom all feare on earth, must feare a fate, For all our powers are subordinate: Three howres space thus well can represent, Vices contriu'd and murders punishment. A Monarchs life can in this little space Shew all the pompe that all the time doth grace His risings and his falls, and in one span Of time, can shew the vanity of man. For none of vs can so command the powers That we may say, to morrow shall be ours. Now Fortunes wheele is turn'd, and time doth call, To solemnize this friendly funerall. No force so great, no so disaster wrong, As can vnknit the bands which holdeth strong Vnited hearts: who since they thus are dead, One roome, one tombe shall hold them buried: And as these friends ioyn'd hands to beare their Fate; So we desire you to imitate. Plot summary

Plot — Orestes
Agamemnon returns home after ten years of war. When he takes his neglected wife Clytemnestra to bed, she and her lover Aegisthus murder him. Roused by bad dreams, his son Orestes finds the body in bed. Feigning innocence, Clytemnestra curses the murderer, while the surviving Trojan glories in the death of the enemy commander: Troy is avenged. Recognizing that he is in danger, Orestes goes into hiding with Pylades until the murderer is identified, and sends a report that, unhinged with grief, he threw himself off a cliff into the sea, and that Pylades also went over trying to stop him. Aegisthus, the next in line, ascends the throne and marries Clytemnestra. She bears him a son, establishing his dynasty, and in celebration he proclaims a general pardon for all crimes. Orestes and Pylades consult the witch Canidia, who shows them a vision of Clytemnestra and Aegisthus committing the murder. They return to court in disguise. Their plotting is overheard by Misander, and they kill him to preserve secrecy, but are immediately arrested. In judging the case, Aegisthus faces two problems: the crime is covered by the general pardon, and each defendant, seeking to save the other, claims that he alone was the murderer. Aegisthus decides not to pass a death sentence on either, and is further impressed when Pylades introduces Orestes as a doctor who has perfected the secret of rejuvenation. Aegisthus asks for a demonstration, using himself and Clytemnestra as the subjects. Agamemnon’s ghost appears to Orestes, urging him to get on with the revenge and take care not to spare his mother. Pylades obtains custody of Aegisthus’ son, who is in the care of Orestes’ sister Electra. When Orestes meets with the King and Queen for their ===== PAGE 235 ===== 1620 rejuvenation, he ties them up, kills their son in front of them, forces them to drink the blood, and finally kills them. He demands his restoration to the throne, but the lords are unwilling to crown a murderer, and instead enthrone Tyndarus, whose first act is to banish Orestes and his accomplices, further stipulating that nobody must give Orestes food. Pylades’ father Strophius dies and Electra kills herself. Orestes goes mad and thinks himself tormented by the Furies. He and Pylades kill one another in a suicide pact, and Tyndarus announces that they are to be buried together in a single tomb.
