Troilus and Cressida
Shakespeare, William · 1609 · tragedy · Lord Chamberlain's (Hunsdon's) Men · Globe;Outdoor Professional
In the besieged city of Troy, Prince Troilus is in love with Cressida, and secures the help of her uncle, Pandarus, as a go-between. Pandarus’ praise for Troilus’ virtues seems not to convince Cressida — because she is secretly interested in him already. Pandarus brings them together in the house of Cressida’s absent father, and they spend the night with one another. After seven years of war, the Greeks are weary and disunited. Achilles has retired to his tent with his friend Patroclus, and spends his time satirizing his fellow princes; Ajax emulates him with the coarse slave Thersites. The commanders offer the Trojans peace, without repayment of the war’s expenses, if they will return the abducted Helen. The Trojan princes consider the proposal: Hector wants to accept it, but Troilus and Paris win the debate with their arguments that this would be dishonourable; Cassandra prophesies the fall of Troy if Helen is not returned. Meanwhile, in an attempt to encourage Achilles into action, Hector sends the Greeks a challenge to single combat. Ulysses, who considers Achilles the key to victory, arranges for the combatant to be decided by lottery, and rigs it so that Ajax is chosen, then provokes Achilles to jealousy of that outcome. Cressida’s father, Calchas, has defected to the Greeks, and has long been trying to arrange for her to join him; but the Trojans have always refused. Cynthia's Revenge, or Maenander's Ecstasy
Stephens, John · 1613 · tragedy
PLOT The King of Sparta has decreed that henceforth only women need to worship Cynthia, goddess of change; men are to be exempt. Cynthia takes revenge. The King dies, but his son Menander begins his reign by confirming the edict and marrying Favorina, daughter of a senior counsellor. Malindo plots with Cassius, Governor of Epirus, to murder Menander and the peers as they watch a play, but Malindo’s letter falls into the hands of the playwright Perillus, who writes a special prologue to warn Menander. Malindo is beheaded, the Spartan army mobilized, and Cassius repelled. Malindo’s estate is given to Amilcar, son of the new favourite, Pheudippe, who is himself allowed to marry Lucilla, Menander’s sister. Reading Lucan tempts Pheudippe to aspire to be King, and these thoughts are reinforced by the invisible influence of Malindo’s ghost. Against his better judgement, Pheudippe confides in Lucilla, encouraging her jealousy that Favorina has supplanted her as the top woman at court. Their plan has three heads: Pheudippe will corrupt Laelio, Captain of the Guard, to assassinate Menander; Amilcar will kill Favorina; and they will secure control of the corn supply in order to starve the people into submission. The Muses' Looking Glass
Randolph, Thomas · 1638 · moral · King's Revels Company
Bird and Mistress Flowerdew, Puritan residents of Blackfriars, bring their wares to the theatre, but are appalled when Roscius tells them they have been ordered for use as props in a filthy stage play. Roscius argues that they themselves make a living from the vice of vanity, and invites them to see the play before they condemn it; he will give them a running commentary on its moral import. Despite Roscius’ high claims, Comedy and Tragedy seem initially more concerned about the order of their precedence; they agree to spend a year in competition, staging examples of their respective genres, and whichever is adjudged the better will thereafter have the theatre to herself. The play is a series of episodes showing specific virtues to be the mean: an excess or a deficiency constitutes a vice. A lack of courtesy makes Dyscolus churlishly ready to pick quarrels, whereas too much of it makes Colax sycophantic, to such an extent that he hangs around for the rest of the play flattering all the other vices. Aphobus is over-confident and Deilus cowardly, afraid of his own shadow: they have respectively too much fortitude and too little. Temperance is the next example, illustrated by Acolastus the epicure and Anaisthetus the ascetic, followed by liberality, missing in Aneleutherus the miser and carried to excess in Asotus the prodigal.
